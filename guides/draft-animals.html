Breeding Considerations

When selecting breeding stock, prioritize health, conformation, and temperament. Horses and cattle breed true to type. Mules are hybrids (horse + donkey) and are invariably sterile, making them excellent for work but requiring you to purchase or breed replacements. Donkeys produce fertile offspring but require careful matching for size compatibility.

2. Horse Care

Feeding & Nutrition

A working horse requires approximately 2.5‚Äì3% of body weight daily in feed . A 1,000 lb horse needs 25‚Äì30 lbs of forage (hay/pasture) plus 5‚Äì10 lbs of grain depending on work intensity.

Feed Guidelines

  • Pasture: Good quality pasture (clover, timothy, alfalfa mix) provides 2‚Äì3 lbs of feed per day per 1,000 lb horse. Rotational grazing maintains pasture quality and prevents parasites.
  • Hay: Provide 1.5‚Äì2% of body weight in hay daily (15‚Äì20 lbs for a 1,000 lb horse). Cut hay in mid-bloom stage for maximum nutrition. Store in dry conditions to prevent mold.
  • Grain Rations: A simple working ration consists of:
    • Oats: 40‚Äì50% (energy, palatability)
    • Barley: 20‚Äì30% (energy, weight gain)
    • Bran: 10‚Äì15% (fiber, digestion)
    • Oil/molasses: 5% (energy, palatability)
  • Water Requirements: Horses drink 10‚Äì12 gallons per day at rest; increase to 15‚Äì20 gallons when working hard. Provide fresh, clean water daily. In winter, offer slightly warm water to encourage consumption.
  • Salt: 1‚Äì2 ounces per day; provide salt block year-round or add to feed.
  • Supplements: Add a mineral mix containing calcium, phosphorus, and trace minerals. Ratio should be 1.2‚Äì1.5:1 (calcium:phosphorus).
Note: Never feed moldy hay or grain; mold can cause colic, respiratory disease, and liver damage. Introduce any new feed gradually over 7‚Äì10 days to avoid digestive upset.

Hoof Care & Farriery

Regular hoof care prevents lameness, infection, and structural damage. Neglected hooves lead to permanent disability within months.

Hoof Cleaning & Maintenance

Daily Hoof Cleaning Procedure

  1. Pick the hoof with a hoof pick, working from heel toward toe
  2. Remove all mud, manure, and packed debris
  3. Check for embedded stones, abscesses, or cracks
  4. Inspect the frog (spongy tissue) for rot or disease
  5. Rinse with water if available
  6. Look for heat, swelling, or discharge indicating infection

Trimming Schedule & Techniques

  • Frequency: Every 8‚Äì10 weeks in normal conditions; every 6 weeks during heavy work
  • Goal: Maintain a 45‚Äì50 degree angle (hoof-pastern angle) and level ground surface
  • Hoof Growth Rate: Approximately 3.5‚Äì4 inches per year (varies by genetics, nutrition, weather)
  • Tools Needed: Hoof nippers, rasp, hoof knife, measuring angle gauge

Horseshoe Dimensions & Installation

Standard horseshoe specifications (for average 1,000 lb horse):

  • Shoe Weight: 1.25‚Äì1.75 lbs per shoe (front lighter than hind)
  • Nail Size: #6 or #7 nails (about 2.5 inches long)
  • Nailing Pattern: 6‚Äì8 nails per shoe, arranged symmetrically:
    • 3‚Äì4 nails on outside (lateral) wall
    • 3‚Äì4 nails on inside (medial) wall
    • Never nail into heel or frog area
  • Nail Holes: Punched 1/4 to 3/8 inch above shoe edge
  • Shoe Fit: Must contact entire bearing surface of hoof wall; never rest on sole or frog
  • Reset Schedule: Every 6‚Äì8 weeks as hoof grows

Common Ailments & Treatment

Colic (Abdominal Pain)

Colic is the leading cause of death in horses. It ranges from mild impaction to severe displacement requiring surgery.

Types of Colic & Emergency Response

  1. Gas Colic (most common): Abdominal distension from fermented feed
    • Signs: Restlessness, lying down repeatedly, pawing, reduced appetite
    • Treatment: Walk continuously (prevents rolling, reduces pain); administer 1 pint of mineral oil via stomach tube or oral syringe; apply warm water enema if constipated
    • Prevention: Feed quality hay, provide water, avoid sudden feed changes
  2. Impaction Colic: Feed blockage, typically in colon
    • Signs: Decreased manure output, abdominal pain, firm abdomen
    • Treatment: Oil drench (2 quarts mineral oil), warm water enemas, continuous walking, fluids
    • Duration: May take 12‚Äì48 hours to resolve
  3. Displacement/Torsion (severe): Intestinal twist or movement
    • Signs: Severe pain unresponsive to treatment, increased heart rate (>60 bpm), sweating, shock
    • Action: This is a surgical emergency‚Äîno effective field treatment exists

Oil Drench Procedure (for impaction)

Safe Oil Administration

  1. Restrain the horse in a stocks or with an assistant
  2. Pass a soft rubber stomach tube (3/4" diameter, 54‚Äì60 inches long) through the nose into the esophagus
  3. Verify placement by listening for breath from tube end (should hear breathing)
  4. Administer 2 quarts (1.9 liters) of mineral oil slowly over 5‚Äì10 minutes
  5. Do not rush‚Äîoil inhalation is fatal
  6. Remove tube slowly and carefully
  7. Repeat every 12 hours if needed
  8. Monitor for diarrhea (sign of resolution)

Lameness & Foot Problems

  • Hoof Abscess: Infection in the hoof, causing sudden lameness
    • Cause: Puncture, crack, or bruise allowing bacteria entry
    • Signs: Severe lameness, heat in hoof, digital pulse pronounced
    • Treatment: Soak in warm Epsom salt water 20 minutes daily; open abscess at lowest point to drain; pack with iodoform gauze; wrap hoof
    • Timeline: Abscess typically ruptures and drains within 3‚Äì7 days
  • Thrush: Bacterial/fungal infection of the frog
    • Signs: Black discharge, foul odor, sensitive frog
    • Cause: Poor hoof cleaning, wet conditions, neglect
    • Treatment: Clean thoroughly; treat with copper sulfate solution or iodine; keep clean and dry
  • Rain Rot: Fungal/bacterial skin infection from prolonged moisture
    • Signs: Crusting, hair loss along back and rump
    • Treatment: Scrub with antimicrobial shampoo; dry thoroughly; improve shelter/drainage

Parasites & Internal Health

  • Internal Parasites: Strongyles, roundworms, tapeworms
    • Signs: Weight loss, dull coat, anemia, diarrhea, colic
    • Prevention: Rotate pastures every 4‚Äì6 weeks; remove manure regularly
    • Treatment: Deworm with fenbendazole or ivermectin paste in spring and fall; treat all horses simultaneously
  • External Parasites: Lice, mites, flies
    • Treatment: Dust with sulfur powder or spray with neem oil; ensure good nutrition to strengthen immune response

Dental Care

Horses' teeth grow continuously and can develop sharp edges that prevent proper chewing and cause performance problems.

Floating Teeth (Dental Rasping)

  • Frequency: Once yearly for adults; twice yearly for young and old horses
  • Signs of Sharp Teeth: Dropping feed, excessive salivation, head tossing, bit resistance, weight loss
  • Procedure:
    • Restrain head with cross-ties or stocks
    • Open mouth with a speculum (mouth gag)
    • Use a flat rasp in circular motions to smooth sharp points on molars
    • Check upper and lower teeth separately
    • Rinse mouth to remove debris
  • Age Determination by Teeth:
    • 0‚Äì2.5 years: Deciduous (baby) teeth; smooth, white
    • 2.5‚Äì5 years: Permanent teeth erupting; gradual replacement
    • 5+ years: All permanent teeth; examine wear patterns and cup depth
    • 7+ years: Dental stars appear (central dark points on chewing surface)
    • 15+ years: Hook formation on upper molars; extreme wear
    • Age can be estimated within ¬±2 years by dental examination

3. Ox Team Management

Training Young Steers

Oxen are castrated male cattle, superior to horses for raw power and feed efficiency. Training begins at 18‚Äì24 months and requires patience and consistency.

Voice Commands (Standard)

  • "Gee" ‚Äì Turn right (loud, sharp)
  • "Haw" ‚Äì Turn left (loud, sharp)
  • "Whoa" ‚Äì Stop (firm, authoritative)
  • "Come on" ‚Äì Forward (encouraging tone)
  • "Back" ‚Äì Reverse (rare, used for tight spaces)

Training Progression

  1. Halter Training (months 1‚Äì2): Lead with halter in open area; practice turning and stopping; reward with grain
  2. Yoke Introduction (months 2‚Äì3): Teach young steers to accept yoke (see below); pair with experienced trained ox for confidence
  3. Ground Driving (months 3‚Äì4): Drive from behind with reins; practice voice commands in open field
  4. Light Draft (months 4‚Äì6): Attach light drag (log or sledge); practice starting, stopping, turning
  5. Field Work (months 6‚Äì8): Begin light plowing or hauling under close supervision
  6. Full Duty (month 8+): Gradual increase in work load; can handle full-time work by 18‚Äì24 months age

Goad Use & Control

A goad (stick with pointed tip) provides immediate directional cues and reinforcement.

  • Light touch on right side ‚Üí "Gee" (turn right)
  • Light touch on left side ‚Üí "Haw" (turn left)
  • Touch on rear ‚Üí "Come on" (forward)
  • Never strike hard‚Äîgoads guide, not punish
  • Consistent application trains the animal to respond to light stimulation

Yoking Systems

Head Yoke (Bow Yoke)

Most common configuration, distributes load through neck.

  • Construction: Carved hardwood yoke (oak or hickory) fitted with iron reinforcements
  • Dimensions: 4‚Äì5 inches tall, 3‚Äì4 inches wide, 48‚Äì54 inches long
  • Bows: Two semi-circular wooden bows (padded with leather or cloth) that rest over animal's neck
  • Attachment: Wooden pins through yoke and bows; chains or wooden clevis pins at ends for draft equipment
  • Fit: Must be snug but not restrict breathing; test fit before extended use

Neck Yoke

Alternative design, distributes load differently.

  • Use: More common in Europe; some prefer for draft work
  • Advantage: Sits lower, may be more comfortable for some animals
  • Construction: Similar wood and iron construction as head yoke

Horn Yoke

Straps attach yoke to horns, used in some regions.

  • Advantage: Simple, can be used on various animals
  • Disadvantage: Uneven pressure, can damage horns with heavy pulling
  • Not Recommended: For serious draft work

Advantages Over Horses

  • Feed Cost: 30‚Äì50% cheaper than horses; thrive on poor pasture, straw, roughage
  • Hoof Structure: Hard, splayed hooves grip soft ground; rarely need shoeing
  • Strength: Can sustain heavier loads with steadier pulling; less prone to panic
  • Durability: Simpler digestive system, fewer health complications
  • Longevity: Productive into late teens with good care
  • Docility: Calmer than horses; less likely to bolt or react suddenly
  • Sustainability: Can be raised on-farm more readily; breeding is straightforward

4. Mule & Donkey Specifics

Mules: The Hybrid Advantage

Mules are the offspring of a male donkey (jack) and a female horse (mare). They exhibit hybrid vigor‚Äîsuperior traits from both parents.

Hybrid Vigor in Mules

  • Strength: Horse-like power with donkey-like endurance
  • Surefootedness: Exceptional balance and foot placement; prefer rocky, steep terrain
  • Heat Tolerance: Superior to horses; excellent for hot climates
  • Feed Efficiency: Thrive on rougher forage than horses; eat 10‚Äì20% less
  • Hoof Quality: Harder, more resilient than horse hooves; fewer shoes needed
  • Intelligence: Highly intelligent; excellent memory; learn commands quickly
  • Longevity: 25‚Äì35 year lifespan, productive into late years

The Sterility Paradox

Mules are sterile due to chromosomal incompatibility (horse: 64 chromosomes, donkey: 62, mule: 63‚Äîodd number prevents proper gamete formation). This means:

  • All mules must be purchased or bred, never reproduced in your herd
  • Replacement stock requires maintaining breeding jacks and mares
  • Rare documented cases of female mule fertility exist but are unreliable

Temperament & Training

  • More independent than horses, less responsive to punishment
  • Respond better to reward-based training
  • Excellent problem-solvers; learn from experience
  • Notorious for stubbornness, but actually exhibit caution (safety instinct)
  • Will refuse dangerous tasks‚Äîrare trait that can prevent accidents
  • Strong bonds with individual handlers; consistency important

Donkeys: The Economic Choice

Donkeys are underestimated as working animals. Standard donkeys (36‚Äì48" at shoulder) and large donkeys (48‚Äì56") offer different capabilities.

Donkey Strengths

  • Cost: Lowest purchase price ($300‚Äì1,000) and feed cost ($0.75‚Äì1.50/day)
  • Forage: Thrive on marginal pasture, weeds, woody browse
  • Terrain: Exceptional mountain climbers; safe on steep slopes
  • Pack Work: Excellent pack animals; can carry 20‚Äì30% of body weight
  • Intelligence: Highly intelligent; excellent memories; strong problem-solving
  • Lifespan: 25‚Äì35 years; highly economical lifetime investment
  • Hardiness: Extremely hardy; few health problems with basic care

Donkey Limitations

  • Limited pulling power (400‚Äì600 lbs) compared to horses or mules
  • Slower pace (1‚Äì2 mph for heavy work)
  • Poor for quick hauling or speed-dependent tasks
  • Better suited to light draft, packing, or herd guarding

Pack Donkey Specifications

  • Pack Capacity: 20‚Äì30% of body weight (donkey 800‚Äì1,000 lbs can carry 160‚Äì300 lbs)
  • Distance: 8‚Äì15 miles per day at easy pace with balanced load
  • Terrain: Mountain trails, steep slopes, rough ground
  • Load Distribution: Must be perfectly balanced; even distribution prevents back strain
  • Rest: 8‚Äì10 hours daily; feed and water access essential

5. Harness & Tack

Collar and Hames (Full Draft Harness)

The collar-and-hames system is the most efficient harness design for heavy draft work. It distributes force across the shoulder and chest, preventing injury.

Collar Design

  • Material: Leather (shaped and stitched) over wooden or metal frame
  • Inner Padding: Straw, hair, or felt (1‚Äì2 inches thick) to distribute pressure
  • Shape: Inverted U-shape; rests on shoulders, not on windpipe
  • Dimensions: 18‚Äì24 inches tall, varies by animal size
  • Fit: Critical‚Äîtoo tight restricts breathing; too loose causes chafing. Two-finger gap at sides when properly fitted

Hames Assembly

  • Material: Wood (ash or hickory) with iron reinforcements and brass or iron fittings
  • Design: Curved wooden pieces (left and right) that sit inside the collar
  • Attachment: Straps over collar; connected with metal bands at top and bottom
  • Draft Points: Iron rings or loops at bottom for trace chain attachment
  • Terrets: Metal loops on top hames for rein passage

Breast Collar

Lighter alternative to full collar-and-hames, suitable for lighter draft work.

  • Design: Single strap across chest; doesn't encircle neck
  • Material: Leather with padding
  • Draft Points: Metal rings on sides for trace attachment
  • Advantages: Easier to fit; allows more air circulation
  • Disadvantages: Less efficient force distribution; not suitable for heavy plowing or hauling
  • Best For: Light dragging, light wagon pulling, pack animals

Singletree & Doubletree (Eveners)

These devices balance pulling force between animals and implement.

Singletree (Single Animal)

  • Design: Wooden beam (20‚Äì30 inches long) with center pivot point
  • Materials: Hardwood (oak, hickory) with iron reinforcements
  • Draft Point: Center attachment for implement (plow, wagon, harrow)
  • Trace Rings: Left and right attachment points equidistant from center
  • Function: Balances pulling effort; allows slight movement to accommodate terrain

Doubletree (Two Animals)

  • Design: Larger wooden frame with center pivot and two singletrees (one per animal)
  • Dimensions: Total length 36‚Äì48 inches; singletrees 18‚Äì24 inches each
  • Movement: Each singletree can pivot independently, equalizing pull
  • Attachment: Center attachment to implement; left/right singletree pivots
  • Benefit: If one animal pulls harder, the evener distributes force equally

Bridle & Bit Basics

Simple functional bridle for draft work (not fine riding tack).

Basic Draft Bridle Components

  • Headpiece: Leather straps fitted over poll and behind ears
  • Noseband: Encircles muzzle; aids turning and stopping
  • Throatlatch: Strap under throat; prevents bridle from slipping forward
  • Bit: Metal mouthpiece; typical draft bit is snaffle (simple ring or jointed) or curb for more control
  • Reins: Leather straps attached to bit; typically 10‚Äì12 feet long when working multiple animals

Bit Selection for Draft

  • Snaffle Bit: Simple jointed or ring bit; mild, direct control; best for young or sensitive animals
  • Curb Bit: Leverage action; more stopping power; use for experienced animals only
  • Materials: Iron or steel; avoid copper (too soft for heavy work)
  • Size: Bit should fit snugly; corners of mouth just reach rein attachment rings

Saddle Basics (Utility Pad Saddle)

Pad saddles are simple, functional designs for utility packing and riding draft animals‚Äînot fine saddles.

  • Design: Wooden frame (arch) over canvas or leather panels
  • Padding: Heavy felt or hair padding (2‚Äì3 inches) for comfort and load distribution
  • Attachment: Girth (belly band) and britchen (rear strap) to stabilize
  • Capacity: Can support 20‚Äì30% of animal's body weight distributed across back
  • D-Rings: Metal attachment points for cargo lashing

6. Working Applications

Plowing

Plowing is the foundation of sustainable agriculture. Proper technique, implement adjustment, and animal conditioning determine success.

Walking Plow Components & Adjustment

  • Moldboard: Curved surface that turns over soil; angle affects soil movement (45‚Äì60 degrees typical)
  • Share: Cutting edge at bottom; keeps plow moving straight and clean
  • Landside: Side piece opposite moldboard; prevents sideways movement
  • Plow Beam: Main frame connecting components to draft attachment
  • Handles: Wooden grips for operator steering and depth control

Plowing Procedure & Depth Control

Basic Plowing Steps

  1. Hitch team to singletree or doubletree securely
  2. Mark first furrow line across field with stake or string
  3. Adjust plow depth to 6‚Äì8 inches (for initial turn; shallower for subsequent passes)
  4. Turn plow share slightly to left so right edge cuts furrow edge
  5. Walk team steadily; guide with verbal commands and light rein use
  6. Maintain consistent depth using depth-wheel or by hand pressure on handles
  7. Second furrow: keep left edge of moldboard in previous furrow; plow runs straight
  8. Continue until field is fully turned
  9. Rest animals every 4‚Äì6 furrows; provide water

Furrow Specifications

  • Depth: 6‚Äì8 inches typical (shallower for sandy soil, deeper for clay)
  • Width: 12‚Äì16 inches per furrow (depends on plow size and soil type)
  • Speed: 1.5‚Äì2.5 mph walking pace
  • Daily Capacity: Well-trained team: 1‚Äì1.5 acres per day (varies by soil condition)
  • Soil Type Adjustment:
    • Sandy soil: Run slightly deeper to increase moisture retention
    • Clay soil: Run shallower to avoid compaction and excessive effort
    • Wet soil: Wait until soil dries; plowing wet clay compacts soil permanently

Hauling & Wagon Work

Wagon Tongue & Hitching System

  • Wagon Tongue: Long pole extending forward for hitching draft animals
  • Length: 12‚Äì16 feet typical; must clear animals' hind legs
  • Hitching Point: Usually 3‚Äì4 feet from wagon bed (allows turning)
  • Clevis Pin: Large steel pin securing singletree to tongue; must be secure
  • Safety Chain: Secondary chain from singletree to wagon frame (backup in case pin fails)

Trace Chains & Load Distribution

  • Trace Chains: Connect draft animal harness to singletree; length 4‚Äì6 feet
  • Material: Iron or steel chain (3/8"‚Äì1/2" diameter); must be strong
  • Load Limits (safe):
    • Single horse: 1,500‚Äì2,000 lbs (with good footing and level ground)
    • Horse team (2): 3,000‚Äì4,000 lbs
    • Ox team (2): 4,000‚Äì6,000 lbs (slower, but steadier)
    • Mule team (2): 3,500‚Äì5,000 lbs
  • Wagon Bed Load: Distribute weight evenly; heavier load toward center
  • Ground Conditions: Wet ground reduces pulling capacity by 25‚Äì50%; avoid overloading

Logging

Skid Chains & Log Skidding

  • Skid Chain: Heavy chain attached to log (via chain around log or skidding hooks)
  • Diameter: 1/2"‚Äì5/8" steel chain; must support 2‚Äì3√ó estimated load weight
  • Attachment: Wrap chain 2‚Äì3 times around log; secure with chain binder or wrap-and-pin
  • Safety: Never stand behind chain under tension; chain breaks can be fatal

Logging Arch (High-Wheeler)

A logging arch lifts the heavy end of a log, reducing ground friction and protecting soil.

  • Design: Steel frame (A-frame or wheel-based) attached to singletree
  • Height: 4‚Äì6 feet; lifts log 2‚Äì3 feet off ground
  • Wheels: Large iron-rimmed wheels (2‚Äì3 feet diameter) for rolling over rough ground
  • Load Capacity: With arch, team can skid larger logs; reduces animal strain significantly
  • Benefit: Much easier on ground and animals than dragging logs flat

Pack Animals & Load Balancing

Pack Load Limits

  • Horse (1,000 lbs): 200 lbs maximum (20% body weight)
  • Mule (900 lbs): 225‚Äì270 lbs (25‚Äì30% body weight)
  • Donkey (600 lbs): 120‚Äì180 lbs (20‚Äì30% body weight, depending on size)
  • Duration: 8‚Äì12 miles per day at moderate pace
  • Terrain: Rough mountain terrain; slower pace needed on steep slopes

Pannier Construction

Panniers are paired containers hung on pack saddle for balanced loads.

  • Design: Wooden or canvas frames with side compartments
  • Dimensions: Roughly 18" tall √ó 12" wide √ó 10" deep per pannier
  • Material: Canvas, leather, or woven branches (for temporary)
  • Attachment: Lashed to pack saddle with rope or leather straps
  • Balance: Weight distribution critical‚Äîeach pannier must weigh within 2 lbs of other
  • Packing: Pack heavier items low; distribute side-to-side evenly

Water Pumping (Horse-Powered Pump)

A sweep (horizontal lever) or gin (vertical wheel) powered by walking animals can pump water without fuel.

Horse Sweep Pump System

  • Design: Horse walks in circle; connected beam (sweep) drives pump rod up and down
  • Mechanics: As horse walks outward, sweep raises pump rod; as it circles back, rod falls and pumps water
  • Capacity: 300‚Äì500 gallons per hour depending on well depth and setup
  • Well Depth Limit: Practical limit ~25 feet (atmospheric pressure limit ~33 feet)
  • Animal Requirement: One horse or mule can operate continuously; rotation rest every 2‚Äì3 hours

7. Breeding & Foaling

Heat Cycles & Breeding

Mare Heat Cycle

  • Season: Spring through fall (photoperiod-dependent); winter anestrus
  • Cycle Length: 21 days average (range 18‚Äì24 days)
  • Estrus Duration: 3‚Äì7 days (called "heat")
  • Signs of Heat: Swollen vulva, tail raised, urine squirting, backing toward stallion, rump swaying
  • Ovulation: Occurs 24‚Äì48 hours before end of heat; time breeding accordingly
  • Breeding Window: Last 2‚Äì3 days of heat period for best conception

Donkey & Mule Breeding

  • Donkey Cycle: Similar to mares but often has longer estrus (5‚Äì10 days)
  • Gestation Length: Donkey 12 months (vs. horse 11 months)
  • Mule Breeding: Mules are sterile; cannot breed true (rare documented fertile females, unreliable)
  • Mule Production: Requires jack (male donkey) √ó mare (female horse) mating; vice versa produces hinny (smaller, rarer)

Gestation & Foaling Preparation

Gestation Periods

  • Horse: 330‚Äì345 days (average 340 days)
  • Donkey: 365‚Äì375 days (12 months)
  • Mule: Sterile; N/A
  • Variation: Foals can be born 1‚Äì2 weeks "early" or "late" within normal range

Pre-Foaling Signs (Last 2‚Äì4 Weeks)

  • Udder Development: Swelling, teats enlarging to pea-sized bumps
  • Vulva Relaxation: Becomes puffy, elongated
  • Milk Leakage: "Waxing" (white wax crust on teat tips) occurs 24‚Äì48 hours before birth
  • Behavior Change: Mare becomes restless, pawing, lying down frequently
  • Colostrum: When large drops of milk leak, foaling is imminent (within 24 hours)
  • Pelvic Relaxation: Hip bones become more prominent; ligaments around tail loosen

Foaling Procedure & Assistance

Normal Foaling (Non-Emergency)

  1. Observation Phase: Mare lies down, gets up repeatedly; early labor lasts 1‚Äì4 hours
  2. Water Breaking: Amniotic sac ruptures; fluid releases. Active labor begins
  3. Expulsion Phase: Normal labor lasts 20‚Äì30 minutes
    • First presentation: white amnion sac visible at vulva
    • Foal front legs appear (one, then other)
    • Head/neck follows
    • Foal usually completes delivery in 10‚Äì15 minutes
  4. Placenta Delivery: Mare stands/moves; placenta passes within 1‚Äì3 hours. Inspect it's complete (looks like red tissue sac, should weigh 8‚Äì10 lbs)
  5. Clean Up: Allow mare and foal to rest; they'll bond. Wipe excessive fluids only if needed
  6. Foal First Nursing: Within 1‚Äì2 hours, foal should stand and nurse. Colostrum (first milk) is critical for immune antibodies
Emergency Warning Signs (Call veterinarian immediately): Labor lasting >2 hours without foal delivery, foal legs/position wrong, mare in shock (cold ears, pale gums, elevated heart rate), placenta not delivered within 3 hours, foal not standing within 2 hours of birth, foal not nursing within 2 hours.

Newborn Care (First 24 Hours)

  • Umbilical Cord: Breaks naturally as foal moves away from mare; do NOT cut unless bleeding profusely. Dip stump in iodine solution to prevent infection
  • Colostrum: Foal must nurse within 2 hours; colostrum provides maternal antibodies essential for survival
  • Meconium Passage: Foal should defecate (black/tarry stool) within 12 hours. If not, enema may be needed
  • Shelter: Provide dry, draft-free area; straw bedding; access to mare for nursing
  • Monitoring: Watch for normal nursing (every 1‚Äì2 hours for 20‚Äì30 seconds); weak nursing is concerning

Castration (Gelding)

Castrating colts eliminates behavioral problems and breeding risk. Ideally performed at 6‚Äì24 months.

Castration Method: Ligation (Safest Field Method)

  • Procedure: Testicle is isolated; blood vessels are tied with strong suture, then testicle removed
  • Timing: Spring or fall (avoid hot/wet conditions that promote infection)
  • Preparation: Colt fasts 12‚Äì18 hours; restraint essential (stocks or chemical sedation)
  • Recovery: 7‚Äì10 days in clean, dry pasture; monitor for infection and swelling
  • Aftercare: Keep wound clean; restrict exercise for one week

Rotational grazing maintains pasture health and reduces parasite burden.

  • Sections Needed: Minimum 4‚Äì5 rotational paddocks for effective parasite control
  • Rest Period: 3‚Äì4 weeks between grazing same paddock (longer in spring for regrowth)
  • Benefit: Reduces selective grazing (uneven wear); improves forage quality; lowers parasite load by 80‚Äì90%

9. Emergency Veterinary Care

Wound Treatment

Minor Cuts & Abrasions

Treatment Steps

  1. Restrain animal to prevent movement during cleaning
  2. Rinse wound thoroughly with clean water or saline solution to remove dirt and debris
  3. For deep wounds: use gentle irrigation (not high pressure, which embeds particles)
  4. If bleeding is heavy: apply direct pressure with clean cloth for 5‚Äì10 minutes
  5. Once clean and bleeding controlled: apply antibiotic ointment (iodine, zinc oxide, or commercial wound paste)
  6. Cover with sterile gauze if available; secure with bandage or wrap
  7. Monitor for swelling, heat, or discharge (signs of infection); change dressing daily

Serious Lacerations (Requires Stitching)

  • Decision: If wound edges are gaping (>1/4 inch separation) and bleeding is controlled, suturing is needed
  • Timing: Stitch within 6‚Äì12 hours of injury for best healing
  • Technique (field emergency):
    • If no veterinarian available: use sterile needle and thread (nylon or silk) to approximate edges
    • Place sutures 1/4 inch apart; knot securely but not so tight as to cut skin
    • Stitches should be removed in 10‚Äì14 days
    • Monitor for infection daily
  • Infection Prevention: Keep clean and dry; apply antibiotic ointment daily

Splinting & Fracture Stabilization

Leg Fracture Support

  • Goal: Immobilize the leg to reduce pain and prevent further damage pending professional treatment
  • Materials:
    • Splint material: cardboard tube, PVC pipe, wooden board (2√ó4), or thick plastic
    • Padding: cloth, straw, old clothing (2‚Äì3 inches thick)
    • Wrapping: elastic bandages, duct tape, or cloth strips
  • Application Steps:
    1. Apply thick padding along the leg (5‚Äì6 inches of cloth/straw)
    2. Place rigid splint on outside of leg (or on both sides for severe fractures)
    3. Wrap splint firmly with elastic bandage or duct tape (tight but not cutting off circulation)
    4. Support hoof with additional wrapping/padding underneath
    5. Check for swelling; adjust if leg becomes tighter than 2-finger spacing
  • Duration: This is temporary stabilization. Professional treatment (casting, stall rest) is needed for healing

Colic Management & Oil Drench

See Section 2 (Horse Care) for detailed colic treatment procedures.

Quick Colic Response Protocol

  1. Observe pain signs: is horse rolling, pawing, or relatively calm?
  2. Gas colic (mild): Walk horse continuously; do not let rest or roll
  3. Impaction (moderate): Walk horse; attempt oil drench (mineral oil, 2 quarts)
  4. Severe pain: Unresponsive to walking, elevated heart rate (>60 bpm), sweating‚Äîthis is surgical emergency; cannot be treated in field
  5. Monitor heart rate (normal: 30‚Äì40 bpm at rest, <60 during work); elevated rate indicates serious pain
  6. Monitor manure output; reduction may indicate blockage
  7. Continue walking for 1‚Äì2 hours; reassess response to treatment

Hoof Abscess Treatment

Abscess Drainage Procedure

Step-by-Step Treatment

  1. Restrain horse (stocks or cross-ties preferred)
  2. Clean hoof thoroughly with brush and water
  3. Locate abscess: squeeze hoof gently; find softest/hottest spot
  4. Open abscess at lowest point: use hoof knife to carefully open area; pus should drain
  5. If cannot locate: poultice hoof to bring abscess to surface (use clay poultice, Epsom salt paste, or hot water soak)
  6. Once drained: soak hoof in warm Epsom salt water (20 minutes daily) for 3‚Äì7 days
  7. Pack open hole with iodoform gauze or antibiotic ointment
  8. Wrap hoof with duct tape and cloth to keep clean and dry
  9. Remove packing and rewrap daily until hole closes (typically 7‚Äì10 days)
  10. Expect lameness to resolve within 2‚Äì5 days of drainage

Joint/Tendon Injuries

  • Signs: Heat, swelling, lameness, pain on flexion
  • First Aid: Cold water therapy (hose leg for 15‚Äì20 minutes, 3√ó daily) reduces swelling
  • Wrapping: Elastic bandage to provide compression; prevents excessive swelling
  • Rest: Confine to stall; complete stall rest for 4‚Äì8 weeks minimum depending on severity
  • Prognosis: Joint injuries are serious; recovery is slow and reinjury risk is high

Emergency Restraint & Handling

  • Stocks (Crush): Best restraint method; allows access to all body parts while preventing kicking/movement
  • Cross-Ties: Suitable for many procedures; prevents escape but allows some movement
  • Twitch: Squeeze on upper lip (nerve stimulation); calms animal for 10‚Äì20 minutes; use for minimal procedures only
  • Casting Harness: For procedures requiring full immobility (dental, severe lameness); dangerous‚Äîrequires expertise

Wagon Specifications & Design Principles

Utility Farm Wagon

A practical design for heavy hauling, built to withstand decades of use with proper maintenance.

Dimensions & Load Capacity

  • Bed Dimensions: 8‚Äì10 feet long √ó 4‚Äì5 feet wide √ó 18‚Äì24 inches tall sides
  • Total Height: 5‚Äì6 feet (important for clearance under trees, branches)

  • Empty Weight: 800‚Äì1,500 lbs (depends on construction quality/materials)
  • Safe Load Capacity: 2‚Äì4 tons (4,000‚Äì8,000 lbs) depending on ground conditions and draft animals

  • Tongue: Wooden beam (12‚Äì16 feet long, 6√ó6 inches); must be straight and strong

  • Axle Diameter: 1.25‚Äì1.5 inches for medium wagons; must support load + wheel weight
  • Bearings: Brass or bronze (wooden wagons); modern: sealed ball bearings

  • Hub: Elm or oak; 6‚Äì8 inches diameter, 4‚Äì6 inches wide
  • Spokes: Oak or hickory; 16‚Äì20 spokes per wheel for strength (more spokes = stronger)
  • Rim: Steel band shrunk hot onto wooden wheel; 0.5 inch thick typical

  • Width: 3‚Äì4 inches typical (wider wheels reduce ground pressure, better for soft soil)

Coach Design Principles (Light, Fast Transport)

Coach Specifications

Coaches prioritize speed and comfort over cargo capacity; used for passenger transport or light goods.

  • Body Style: Enclosed box suspended on springs/straps
  • Dimensions: 6‚Äì8 feet long √ó 5‚Äì6 feet tall √ó 4 feet wide (interior cabin)
  • Weight: 1,500‚Äì2,500 lbs (heavier than wagons due to fine construction)
  • Capacity: 4‚Äì8 passengers depending on comfort expectations
  • Load Limit: 1,500‚Äì2,500 lbs cargo (light draft animals sufficient: mules, light horses)
  • Speed: 6‚Äì10 mph cruising (much faster than cargo wagons)
  • Wheels: 42‚Äì48 inches diameter (lighter than cargo wagons; not overbuilt)

  • Leather Straps: Heavy leather slings support coach body; provide spring-like suspension
  • Steel Springs: Leaf springs (later improvement); reduce shock transmission
  • Axles: Thinner than cargo wagons; optimized for speed, not maximum load
  • Interior Cushioning: Leather seats with straw or hair stuffing; still rough by modern standards

Road Requirements & Maintenance

Road Surface Specifications

  • Width: Minimum 12 feet for two-way traffic (16 feet preferred)
  • Surface: Compacted earth (loose dirt is impassable); gravel layer 3‚Äì4 inches improves all-weather use
  • Drainage: Critical‚Äîwater-logged roads become impassable. Camber (slight hill in center) or side ditches required
  • Obstacles: Rocks, roots, and ruts must be removed regularly (weekly in heavy use seasons)
  • Gradient: Steep hills (>20%) are dangerous for wagons (braking failure risk); preferred slope <15%
  • Bridges: Must support full-loaded wagon weight; wooden bridges require regular inspection for rot

Road Maintenance Procedures

  • Crowning: Maintain center-high profile to shed water to sides
  • Clearing: Remove debris, vegetation weekly (prevents water pooling)
  • Rut Repair: Level deep wagon ruts; add gravel as needed
  • Seasonal: Spring: remove winter debris, repair frost heave damage; Fall: prepare for wet season with drainage improvement
