Fire Behavior

Understanding fire behavior enables prediction and effective suppression.

Fire Triangle Requirements

Fire requires three elements: heat, oxygen, and fuel. Removing any element extinguishes fire. Heat: temperature sufficient to ignite material. Oxygen: typically 16%+ in air. Fuel: any combustible material. Reducing any component inhibits fire spread.

Fire Spread Factors

  • Wind: Carries embers and flames ahead, accelerates fire. 10 mph wind can double fire spread rate.
  • Fuel Type: Fine materials (grass, twigs) burn faster than large logs. Dead wood burns faster than live wood.
  • Moisture: Wet materials burn slower. High humidity slows fire spread.
  • Topography: Fire spreads uphill faster (heat rises). Valleys channel wind and accelerate fire.
  • Temperature: High ambient temperature accelerates ignition of nearby materials.

Flame Characteristics

Flame height indicates fire intensity. Small flames (under 1 foot) = moderate intensity. Flames 4-8 feet = high intensity. Flames above 8 feet = extreme intensity - uncontrollable by hand crews. Wind affects flame appearance and direction dramatically - flames lean downwind, embers carry far ahead.

Smoke Patterns

Smoke behavior indicates air movement and fire strength. Light smoke = low intensity. Heavy, dark smoke = extreme intensity. Smoke column height indicates fire convection - tall columns mean strong updrafts, possible fire-generated winds. Smoke direction indicates wind direction.

Fire Extinguisher Types

Different extinguisher types suppress different fire classes. Using wrong type can spread fire or injure user.

Water Extinguishers

Most common, cheapest. Effective on Class A fires (wood, paper, cloth). NOT effective on electrical fires or liquid fires (spreads burning liquid). Water cools materials below ignition temperature. Pressurized water cools effectively and penetrates deep into material.

Foam Extinguishers

Effective on Class A and B fires. Creates blanket over fuel, smothering fire. Foam floats on liquid, preventing re-ignition. Cooling effect additional benefit. More expensive than water but more versatile.

Dry Powder Extinguishers

Effective on Class B and C fires. Works by interrupting chemical reaction of combustion. Does NOT cool materials well - can reignite if material still hot. Inert powder clogs vision and lungs briefly. Safety goggles recommended. Powder accumulates everywhere - messy.

Carbon Dioxide (CO2) Extinguishers

Effective on Class B and C fires (electrical, flammable liquids). Does not cool material - must cool separately or reignition occurs. CO2 displaces oxygen, extinguishing fire. Gas evaporates, leaving no residue. Expensive. High-pressure discharge can injure if directed at skin.

Wet Chemical Extinguishers

Specifically for Class K fires (cooking oils). Creates chemical reaction that cools and saponifies hot oil, preventing reignition. NOT suitable for other fire classes. Primarily for commercial kitchens.

Firebreaks and Containment

Firebreaks prevent fire spread by removing fuel or creating barriers.

Firebreak Construction

Firebreak is strip of cleared land, 10-30 feet wide depending on fire intensity expected. Remove all combustible materials: dead vegetation, leaf litter, fallen branches. Expose bare soil. Width of firebreak should equal 2-3x expected flame height.

Natural Firebreaks

Streams and bodies of water effectively stop fires. Rocky areas with minimal vegetation slow fire. Open meadows without dead fuel allow fire to spread slower. Strategic property placement relative to natural firebreaks aids fire safety.

Maintained Firebreaks

Keep properties around structures cleared of dead vegetation. Remove tree branches overhanging roofs. Maintain grass short (mowing reduces fire load). Remove dead trees. Space trees to prevent crown-to-crown fire spread (15+ feet between tree crowns).

Emergency Firebreak Creation

If fire approaching with no prepared firebreak: immediately clear 20+ foot swath of all dead vegetation. Even partial clearing helps. Coordinate with neighbors - multiple cleared areas more effective than single effort.

Structural Fire Response

Responding to fires in buildings requires rapid, safe action.

WARNING: In structural fires, prioritize personal and others' life safety above all property. Never re-enter building for any reason once evacuated. Structural fires can spread explosively - seconds make difference between safe and fatal.

Immediate Response

  1. Alert people in building - use alarm or loud calls
  2. Evacuate immediately - do not stop for belongings
  3. Feel doors before opening - hot doors indicate fire beyond
  4. Stay low below smoke (smoke contains fatal gases)
  5. Go to designated meeting point, account for all people
  6. Call emergency services (or send runner if no phone)

Fire Containment (Trained Personnel Only)

Trained firefighters with proper equipment may attempt suppression. Untrained individuals should evacuate. Fire doubles in size every minute if uncontrolled - minutes difference between manageable and catastrophic.

Extinguisher Use (Small Fires Only)

Use only if: fire small (contained in small area), single source, escape route clear, proper extinguisher type available, personally confident. Technique: approach from windward side, aim at base of flames, use smooth sweeping motion. Keep escape route behind you - never trap yourself.

Stop-Drop-Roll (for people)

If clothing ignites: stop running, drop to ground, roll to smother flames. Do NOT run - running increases oxygen, makes fire burn hotter. Cover face with hands while rolling. Seek water immediately after rolling to cool skin.

Wildfire Defense

Protecting structures and lives from advancing wildfire requires preparation and decisive action.

Pre-Wildfire Preparation

  • Clear 30-100 feet around structures of dead vegetation
  • Remove tree branches overhanging roofs
  • Use metal or tile roofing (not wood shingles)
  • Enclose eaves and vents with metal mesh (prevents ember entry)
  • Create defensible space: maintain green vegetation, water regularly
  • Keep gutters clear of leaves and debris
  • Have evacuation plan and go-bag ready

Evacuation Decision

Leave immediately when instructed by authorities. Do NOT wait for last minute - traffic jams kill. Take go-bag with essential documents, medications, valuables. Drive toward fire-free areas. If roads blocked, stay with vehicle (if metal-enclosed) - better than exposure.

Last-Minute Home Defense

If fire imminent and must stay: close all windows and doors, leave lights on (helps firefighters see you're there), close interior doors to create fire compartments, move away from perimeter walls. Fill pools, hot tubs, buckets with water if hose available. Move propane BBQs away from structures.

Ember Defense

Embers - small burning pieces carried by wind - ignite most homes, not flames. Embers enter through vents, windows, gaps in siding. Close windows, close vents with metal covers, seal gaps in siding. Standing water (pools, ponds) useful for firefighting helicopters and ground crews.

Smoke Management

Heavy smoke reduces air quality. Stay indoors, close windows, use HEPA filters if available. Wear N95 or P100 masks outdoors. Smoke exposure dangerous for elderly, children, and those with respiratory conditions. Evacuate rather than endure severe smoke.

Water Supply Systems for Fire Suppression

Adequate water supply critical for fire response.

Water Source Reliability

Municipal water systems may fail during large fires (loss of pressure from high flow demands). Private wells useless if power lost. Maintain independent water storage: tanks, pools, ponds accessible for firefighting. Water should be separate from drinking water supply (contamination from firefighting efforts).

Water Storage Tanks

Minimum 2,500 gallons recommended for structures. Tank elevated 8-10 feet (gravity provides pressure for hose use). Or use underground tank with pump. Tank sized for reasonable fire suppression (2,500-5,000 gallon capacity typical).

Hydrant and Hose Placement

Mark water sources clearly - firefighters need immediate access. Gates to water areas hinged to open fully (don't block access). Hoses maintained in good condition. Nozzles included for hose hookup to various sources.

Rural Water Systems

Ponds and natural water sources valuable if 20+ feet from flammable vegetation. Pump systems allow water to be drawn for firefighting. Stock in supplies: hoses, pumps, fittings. Coordinate with fire agencies on access and usage.

Fire Suppression Safety

Improper fire suppression efforts injure or kill responders.

WARNING: Untrained individuals should NEVER attempt structural fire suppression. Fires kill and injure unprepared people in seconds. Evacuation and calling professionals is the only safe response for untrained people.

Heat Dangers

Fire generates extreme heat (600-1000°C+). At 60°C, human skin cannot tolerate more than 5 minutes. Protective equipment (bunker gear) essential. Heat causes death rapidly - seconds of exposure to extreme heat causes fatal injuries.

Smoke Dangers

Smoke contains carbon monoxide (binds to hemoglobin, prevents oxygen transport), cyanide compounds, and particulates. Low oxygen in smoky areas causes unconsciousness. Smoke kills more people than flames. Escape smoke immediately - do not try to suppress fire from within heavy smoke.

Structural Collapse Risk

Fire weakens structural materials. Collapses kill firefighters and civilians. Structural failure unpredictable. Never remain in burning structures - floor can collapse, walls can fall. Evacuation only safe response.

Explosion Risk

Pressurized containers in fires explode violently. Propane, paint cans, compressed gases can detonate. Flying debris kills. Stay far from fire sources containing pressurized containers.

Personal Protective Equipment (PPE)

Proper firefighting PPE includes: heat-resistant coat and pants, boots, helmet with face shield, gloves, breathing apparatus. Incomplete PPE provides false sense of security - still lethal exposure risks. Only trained personnel with full PPE should attempt any suppression.
