Mass Casualty Incident (MCI) Overview

Critical Understanding

A Mass Casualty Incident overwhelms normal medical resources. Triage fundamentally changes during MCI‚Äîresources are rationed and allocation ethics shift from "save everyone possible" to "save the maximum number of lives."

What Constitutes an MCI?

An MCI occurs when the number of casualties exceeds available medical resources. Definitions vary by region, but generally:

  • Exceeds local hospital surge capacity
  • Requires activation of emergency operations plans
  • Demands mutual aid from neighboring jurisdictions
  • Involves 10+ casualties in austere/rural settings
  • Involves 30+ casualties in urban/medical settings

Phases of MCI Response

PhaseTimelinePrimary Actions
Activation0-15 minutesAlert, scene assessment, ICS activation, resource requests
Response15 min - 2 hoursPersonnel deployment, scene stabilization, triage initiation
Stabilization2-6 hoursPatient treatment, transport, field hospital operation
Recovery6+ hoursContinued operations, documentation, resource reallocation
Key Principle: The goal during MCI triage is not to provide optimal care to each patient, but to provide the greatest good to the greatest number under severe resource constraints.

START Triage System (Simple Triage and Rapid Treatment)

System Overview

START is the standard adult triage protocol used by most EMS systems. Each patient is evaluated in under 60 seconds using four assessment parameters. The acronym guides the assessment sequence:

  • S - Spontaneous breathing
  • T - Tourniquets (severe bleeding)
  • A - Altered mental status
  • R - Respiration rate
START Triage Decision Tree

Step-by-Step START Assessment

Step 1: Spontaneous Breathing Assessment

Position patient supine. Check for 10 seconds:

  • Breathing present: Proceed to Step 2
  • No breathing: Open airway by positioning (head tilt-chin lift). Recheck for 10 seconds
  • Still no breathing: Tag BLACK (unless resources unlimited, then may provide rescue breaths)

Step 2: Respiratory Rate Assessment

Count breaths for 15 seconds and multiply by 4 (or count for 60 seconds):

  • <10 breaths/min: Compromised oxygenation, Tag RED
  • 10-30 breaths/min: Normal range, proceed to Step 3
  • >30 breaths/min: Indicates distress/shock, Tag RED

Step 3: Severe Hemorrhage Control

Perform visual inspection and palpation:

  • Uncontrolled life-threatening bleeding: Apply tourniquet or direct pressure. Tag RED
  • Controlled or minor bleeding: Proceed to Step 4

Step 4: Altered Mental Status

Test patient responsiveness:

  • Ask: "What is your name?" or "Can you hear me?"
  • Altered response or unresponsive: Tag RED
  • Alert and oriented: Tag YELLOW
Time Budget: Maintain 30-60 seconds per patient. If taking longer, move to next patient and flag for reassessment. Speed is critical in MCI.

JumpSTART Triage (Pediatric Version)

Age-Specific Modifications

Children have different physiologic parameters than adults. JumpSTART modifies assessment thresholds and assessment methods to account for developmental stages (infants through adolescents).

JumpSTART Step-by-Step Process

Initial Assessment: Pediatric AVPU

  • A (Alert): Interacting appropriately with environment
  • V (Verbal): Responds to voice commands
  • P (Painful): Responds to pain only
  • U (Unresponsive): No response

Step 1: Assess Respiratory Status

  • Observe breathing for 15 seconds
  • Not breathing: Open airway gently and reassess
  • Still apneic: Check for palpable pulse; if absent tag BLACK
  • Breathing present: Proceed to assess rate

Step 2: Age-Specific Respiratory Assessment

Count breaths for 15 seconds and multiply by 4:

  • Infants (0-12 months): Normal 30-60, abnormal <30 or >60
  • Toddlers (1-3 years): Normal 24-40, abnormal <24 or >40
  • Preschool (3-6 years): Normal 22-34, abnormal <22 or >34
  • School age (6-12 years): Normal 18-30, abnormal <18 or >30
  • Adolescents (12+ years): Normal 12-20, abnormal <12 or >20

If respiratory rate abnormal: Tag RED and move to next patient

Step 3: Perfusion Assessment

Perform rapid capillary refill or assess for palpable pulse:

  • Capillary refill >2 seconds: Indicates poor perfusion, Tag RED
  • No palpable central pulse: Tag BLACK
  • Normal perfusion: Proceed to Step 4

Step 4: Mental Status

  • Use age-appropriate assessment (AVPU)
  • P or U (altered): Tag RED
  • A or V (appropriate): Tag YELLOW

JumpSTART Triage Tags for Pediatrics

RED (Immediate): Respiratory rate abnormal, poor perfusion, altered mental status, severe bleeding, or signs of shock
YELLOW (Delayed): Adequate breathing and perfusion, normal mental status, but with injuries requiring treatment
GREEN (Minor): Able to walk and follow commands, minimal injuries, good perfusion
BLACK (Expectant): No spontaneous breathing and no response to airway positioning, no palpable central pulse, profound injuries incompatible with survival
Special Note: In pediatric MCI, children who are not breathing should NOT receive rescue breaths during initial triage (resource constraints). They are tagged BLACK. Focus on green and yellow patients first.

Triage Categories and Definitions

RED - IMMEDIATE (Life-Threatening, Salvageable)

Tag Color: Red Triage Tag

Definition: Critical injuries requiring immediate intervention to prevent death. These patients can be saved with rapid treatment.

Common Presentations:

  • Uncontrolled severe bleeding
  • Airway obstruction or severe respiratory distress
  • Tension pneumothorax or open pneumothorax
  • Severe shock (pale, weak pulse, altered)
  • Altered mental status from head injury or toxin
  • Unstable pelvic or spinal injuries with circulatory compromise
  • Severe burns affecting >20% of body

Treatment Priority:

First priority for treatment. These patients go directly to the operating area or resuscitation zone. Limited interventions: airway management, hemorrhage control, shock management, pain relief.

Destination:

Trauma center or hospital with surgical capabilities. Transport immediately or as soon as resources available.

YELLOW - DELAYED (Serious, Stable)

Tag Color: Yellow Triage Tag

Definition: Serious injuries that are currently stable but require medical evaluation and treatment. Can tolerate delay of 1-4 hours without significant deterioration.

Common Presentations:

  • Moderate trauma without life threat (alert, stable vitals)
  • Controlled bleeding
  • Fractures (long bone, extremity) without shock
  • Moderate burns (<20% body surface)
  • Abdominal or chest injuries without respiratory distress
  • Impaled objects not in critical anatomical locations
  • Chemical exposure with mild symptoms

Treatment Priority:

Second priority. Treated after RED patients but before GREEN. Can be triaged to treatment area or temporary holding area. Periodic reassessment critical‚Äîsome will deteriorate to RED.

Destination:

Hospital admission required. May be able to use non-trauma center hospitals if resources allow.

GREEN - MINOR (Walking Wounded)

Tag Color: Green Triage Tag

Definition: Minor injuries that do not require immediate or urgent hospital care. These patients can walk or function relatively normally.

Common Presentations:

  • Abrasions, minor lacerations
  • Minor contusions or muscle strains
  • Minor fractures without neurovascular compromise (stable, alert)
  • Psychological distress without medical injury
  • Anxiety or hyperventilation
  • Minor burns affecting <5% of body
  • Able to self-ambulate and follow commands

Treatment Priority:

Lowest priority. Treated last. Can be directed to waiting/holding area with basic first aid. Often can be discharged with minor treatment. Reassess periodically.

Destination:

May be discharged home with instructions, treated at clinic, or hospitalized only if injuries worsen or resources available.

BLACK - EXPECTANT (Unsurvivable)

Tag Color: Black Triage Tag

Definition: Injuries deemed unsurvivable given current resources. Goal shifts to comfort care. Only triggered when resources are severely constrained.

Criteria for BLACK Tagging (MCI Only):

  • No spontaneous breathing after airway positioning
  • No palpable pulse (severe shock)
  • Profound head trauma with exposed brain matter
  • Massive trauma incompatible with life (>90% burns, crush injuries)
  • Severe injuries in austere/wilderness setting with no evacuation possible

Important Note:

BLACK tags should ONLY be applied in true MCI situations with severe resource constraints. In normal operations with adequate resources, all patients receive resuscitation attempts. In hospital settings with normal capacity, BLACK is rarely used.

Care Approach:

Comfort-focused care. Positioning for comfort, basic pain management if available, emotional support. Do not provide scarce resources (airway equipment, medications, personnel).

Reassessment: Triage is not static. Patients should be reassessed every 15-30 minutes as conditions change and resources become available. A YELLOW may become RED. A BLACK may be upgraded if resources suddenly available.

Field Hospital Setup and Organization

Field Hospital Organization Principles

A field hospital must be organized to maximize efficiency and patient flow. The layout should separate different functions and minimize cross-contamination.

Field Hospital Layout

Key Functional Areas

1. Triage Area (Entry Point)

  • Location: Single entry point, 50-100 feet from main hospital
  • Personnel: 1-2 triage officers, security to direct flow
  • Duration: Patients pass through in 60 seconds or less
  • Equipment: Triage tags, marking pens, clipboards, basic PPE
  • Function: Rapid assessment and tagging; separation of patients by category

2. Decontamination Area (If Needed)

  • Location: Upwind of hospital, separate from general area
  • Usage: Only if chemical, biological, or radiological exposure
  • Personnel: Trained hazmat team
  • Equipment: Shower tents, containment, PPE for responders
  • Process: Remove clothing, wash, issue hospital garments

3. RED Zone (Immediate Care)

  • Staffing: Highest concentration of personnel (doctors, nurses)
  • Equipment: Airway supplies, medications, monitors, ventilators if available
  • Tasks: Airway management, hemorrhage control, shock management, emergency surgery
  • Flow: Stabilized patients move to treatment area or transport

4. YELLOW Zone (Delayed Care)

  • Characteristics: Larger volume of patients, less acute interventions
  • Staffing: Moderate‚Äîmonitoring and reassessment
  • Tasks: Splinting, wound dressing, basic pain management
  • Monitoring: Reassess every 15-30 minutes for deterioration

5. GREEN Zone (Minor Injuries)

  • Characteristics: High volume, minimal supervision needed
  • Organization: Self-directed or community volunteer led
  • Functions: Basic first aid, reassurance, information
  • Discharge: Direct to home, clinic, or counseling as needed

6. Supply and Logistics

  • Centralized location: Easily accessible from all zones
  • Inventory: Medications, bandages, IV fluids, airway equipment, monitors
  • Personnel: Logistics officer tracking supply usage
  • Resupply: Coordinate with external resources and hospitals

7. Command Center (Incident Command)

  • Elevated location: Central overview of entire operation
  • Functions: Incident Commander, Operations, Logistics, Communications
  • Equipment: Radios, maps, patient tracking board, resource inventory

Safety Considerations

One-way flow: Design to prevent cross-contamination. Patients flow through triage ‚Üí treatment zones ‚Üí evacuation. Do not allow backward flow.

Personal Protective Equipment (PPE): Ensure adequate supply for all personnel. In potential contamination scenarios, enforce decontamination.

Waste Management: Designate biohazard waste areas separate from clean zones.

Patient Flow Management

Optimizing Patient Movement

In an MCI, rapid patient flow is critical. Bottlenecks cause harm‚Äîpatients deteriorate in waiting areas and resources are wasted. Effective flow management requires:

  • Clear decision points and designated paths
  • Adequate personnel at each stage
  • Constant reassessment and reallocation
  • Communication between zones
Patient Flow Model

Strategies for Maintaining Flow

Minimize Queue Times

  • RED patients: Minimize time in treatment zone; move to transport immediately after stabilization
  • YELLOW patients: Can tolerate 1-4 hours, but reassess at 30-min intervals
  • GREEN patients: Manage in groups; provide first aid and discharge rapidly

Dynamic Reallocation

  • Bottleneck at RED zone? Redirect personnel, set up additional treatment stations
  • YELLOW patients deteriorating? Move to RED zone; do not force treatment in YELLOW
  • Excess YELLOW capacity? Discharge GREEN candidates earlier

Continuous Monitoring and Reassessment

  • Every 15-30 minutes, reassess all patients in holding areas (YELLOW)
  • Move improved patients to GREEN or discharge
  • Upgrade deteriorating patients back to RED immediately
  • Document reassessment and any changes

Communication Systems

  • Radio communication between zones and command center
  • Regular updates (every 15 min) from each zone to IC on patient count and resource needs
  • Clear protocols for requesting additional supplies or personnel

Transport Coordination

  • Designate transport officer to coordinate with hospitals
  • Load-balance: Distribute patients to avoid overwhelming single facility
  • Use ground ambulances, helicopters, buses as needed
  • Track which patients go to which hospitals for continuity of care
Critical Principle: Movement is treatment in MCI. Keeping patients flowing through the system and to higher-level care is often more beneficial than prolonged treatment in field conditions.

Resource Allocation Under Scarcity

Ethical Framework

When resources are scarce, allocation shifts from "What can we do for this patient?" to "What should we do to maximize lives saved?" This is called utilitarian triage or crisis standards of care.

Resource Scarcity Scenarios

Scenario 1: Limited Medications (Pain Relief, Antibiotics)

Priority allocation:

  1. YELLOW patients who can be discharged after pain control
  2. RED patients requiring medication for stabilization
  3. GREEN patients for comfort only if available
  4. BLACK patients: minimal allocation (comfort-focused)

Scenario 2: Limited Ventilators (Respiratory Support)

Allocation criteria (Sequential Organ Failure Assessment or SOFA):

  • Highest priority: Salvageable RED patient with reversible respiratory failure
  • Second: YELLOW patient deteriorating to RED with respiratory compromise
  • Lowest: Patient with multi-organ failure or profound neurological injury (poor prognosis)

Patients not receiving ventilators are transitioned to comfort care (morphine, positioning).

Scenario 3: Limited Surgical Capacity

Triage for operating room:

  1. Salvageable patients with immediately life-threatening injuries (bleeding control)
  2. Patients with high likelihood of survival with surgery
  3. NOT: Expectant patients or those requiring extensive reconstruction

Scenario 4: Limited Transport

Evacuation priority:

  1. RED patients requiring definitive care not available in field
  2. YELLOW patients with deterioration risk
  3. BLACK patients and GREEN only if transport capacity available

Utility-Based Decision Framework

When making resource allocation decisions, use these factors:

Likelihood of Survival (Primary Factor)

  • High likelihood: Allocate resource
  • Medium likelihood: Consider patient's age and comorbidities
  • Low likelihood (<10%): Resource allocation unwarranted

Duration of Resource Need

  • Short-term need (hours) = allocate
  • Long-term need (days/weeks in field) = reconsider

Patient Age (Ethically Complex)

  • Some crisis protocols weight younger patients higher (more life-years remaining)
  • Others allocate by medical criteria only, age-neutral
  • Avoid age discrimination; use only in true crisis scenarios

Comorbidities and Life Expectancy

  • Pre-existing conditions affect post-injury prognosis
  • Severe chronic illness lowers expected survival from acute injury
  • Consider only if significantly affects chance of survival from current injury

Ethical Principles for Resource Allocation

  • Transparency: Explain allocation decisions to staff and patients when possible
  • Consistency: Apply same criteria to all patients (avoid bias based on social status)
  • Reversibility: Reassess decisions as resources become available
  • Documentation: Record rationale for significant allocation decisions
  • Accountability: Incident Commander bears responsibility for major triage decisions
Regional Planning: Many regions pre-establish crisis standards of care protocols before disasters occur. Familiarize yourself with your local/regional crisis standards documents, as they guide allocation decisions legally and ethically.

Incident Command System (ICS) Basics

ICS Overview

The Incident Command System (ICS) is a standardized approach to managing emergency incidents. It ensures clear command structure, communication, and resource allocation. ICS is used for MCIs as well as fires, hazmat spills, and other disasters.

Core ICS Principles

  • Unified Command: Agencies work under one command structure
  • Clear Chain of Command: Reporting lines are unambiguous
  • Span of Control: No manager oversees more than 5-7 personnel
  • Scalability: System grows with incident size
  • Modular Structure: Functions activate as needed
Incident Command System Organizational Chart

Key ICS Positions in MCI

Incident Commander (IC)

  • Responsibility: Overall authority and accountability
  • Main duties: Strategic decisions, resource allocation, big-picture priorities
  • Reports to: Municipal emergency management or state officials
  • Required training: ICS 100, 200, 300+
  • Medical role: Usually senior physician or EMS Chief

Operations Section Chief

  • Responsibility: Tactical execution of triage and treatment
  • Main duties: Oversees all treatment zones, personnel deployment, patient flow
  • Subordinates: Zone leaders (RED, YELLOW, GREEN), triage officer
  • Reports to: Incident Commander

Planning Section Chief

  • Responsibility: Information gathering and documentation
  • Main duties: Patient tracking, resource inventory, forecasting needs, action planning
  • Subordinates: Intelligence officer, documentation team
  • Reports to: Incident Commander

Logistics Section Chief

  • Responsibility: Resource management and support
  • Main duties: Medical supplies, personnel movement, transport coordination
  • Subordinates: Supply unit leader, transport unit leader, communications officer
  • Reports to: Incident Commander

Safety Officer

  • Responsibility: Worker safety and incident safety
  • Main duties: Hazard identification, PPE compliance, work environment safety
  • Authority: Can halt unsafe operations (direct line to IC)
  • Reports to: Incident Commander

Liaison Officer

  • Responsibility: Coordination with external agencies
  • Main duties: Interface with hospitals, other EMS agencies, National Guard, utilities
  • Reports to: Incident Commander

Public Information Officer (PIO)

  • Responsibility: Media relations and public communication
  • Main duties: Press releases, media briefings, rumor control
  • Reports to: Incident Commander

ICS in MCI Context

Establishing ICS Early

The first responder on scene should immediately establish ICS, even if alone:

  • Declare self as "IC"
  • Begin scene assessment
  • Call for appropriate resources
  • Establish command post location
  • Transfer command to senior authority as they arrive

Command Post Location

  • Upwind and uphill if possible
  • Safe from ongoing hazards
  • Visible to field personnel
  • Accessible to arriving personnel
  • Communication capability (radio, phone)

Operational Periods

In large MCIs, divide operations into periods (typically 12-24 hours each):

  • Start of each period: Briefing with all section chiefs
  • During period: Regular updates (every 15 min recommended)
  • End of period: Transition briefing to new command team

Span of Control

Maintain manageable spans of control:

  • IC should not directly supervise more than 5-7 section chiefs
  • Section chiefs should not directly supervise more than 5-7 unit leaders
  • Unit leaders should not directly supervise more than 5-7 personnel
  • Add intermediate layers (Strike Teams, Task Forces) if needed
ICS Training: FEMA offers free online ICS training (ICS 100, 200, 300, 400). These are valuable for all emergency responders. Many employment sectors now require ICS training for emergency management positions.

Documentation and Tracking

Why Documentation Matters in MCI

  • Patient care continuity: Hospital receives information about treatment provided
  • Legal protection: Records document decisions and actions
  • Resource tracking: Know what was used, what remains
  • After-action review: Improve future response based on data
  • Epidemiology: Track injury patterns, outcomes, effectiveness
  • Family reunification: Locate and identify patients

Triage Tags and Patient Identification

Standard Triage Tag Information

Each patient receives a triage tag with:

  • Patient identifier: Name (if known) or patient number
  • Triage priority: Color/category (RED/YELLOW/GREEN/BLACK)
  • Vital signs: Respiratory rate, pulse, mental status (if assessed)
  • Injuries noted: Obvious injuries, presumed injuries
  • Treatments given: Tourniquet, dressing, medication, airway
  • Reassessment times: When reassessed and any changes
  • Incident number: Links to overall MCI record
  • Time tagged: When patient entered the system

Standard Triage Tag Format

Most triage tags are tear-off style with 4 parts (one per category):

  • Large tear-off stub: Worn on patient's wrist or clothing
  • Smaller sections: Left with patient, given to transport, kept for field records

Documentation in Austere Conditions

In field conditions, documentation must be simple and rapid:

  • Use standard forms (laminated if possible)
  • Allow paper to get wet/dirty (use waterproof pen)
  • Include only essential information during active phase
  • Transfer detailed info to proper medical records later
Privacy and HIPAA: During MCI, patient privacy laws (HIPAA in US) are not suspended, but balance privacy with need to communicate patient information to receiving hospitals and family. Use unique identifiers if patient name unknown.

Psychological Aspects of Triage Decisions

Moral Injury Risk

Making triage decisions in MCIs can cause significant psychological stress on medical personnel. Decisions to withhold treatment, triage patients to BLACK category, or choose between two critical patients can result in moral injury, PTSD, or burnout if not managed.

Stress on Healthcare Workers

Common Psychological Reactions

  • Acute stress: Hypervigilance, difficulty concentrating, emotional blunting
  • Guilt: Feeling unable to save everyone despite impossible circumstances
  • Moral distress: Conflicting values (want to help all, must choose)
  • Compassion fatigue: Emotional exhaustion after prolonged empathetic engagement
  • PTSD: Intrusive memories, nightmares, avoidance behaviors
  • Substance abuse: Self-medicating for stress
  • Depression: Persistent low mood, hopelessness

Risk Factors for Psychological Injury

  • High number of pediatric casualties
  • Personal connection to victims
  • Prior trauma or PTSD
  • Lack of support or debriefing
  • Perceived failure to save lives
  • Moral conflict (withholding care from salvageable patients)
  • Extended operational period without relief

Supporting Staff Mental Health During and After MCI

During the Incident

  • Rotation schedules: Limit personnel to 2-4 hour shifts; rotate to lower-stress zones
  • Buddy system: Pair personnel; watch for signs of distress
  • Hydration and rest: Enforce mandatory breaks every 30-60 minutes
  • Clear decision-making framework: Reduce moral ambiguity; use established protocols
  • Leadership presence: Visible command presence reassures staff
  • Normalize emotions: Acknowledge that this is difficult; reactions are normal
  • Peer support: Trained peer support team available for immediate intervention

Immediate Post-Incident (First 24-72 Hours)

  • Structured debriefing: Organized group discussion (not free-form, which can increase symptoms)
  • Focus on facts: What happened, what was done, outcomes
  • Normalize reactions: Explain expected psychological responses
  • Coping strategies: Sleep, exercise, social support, limit media exposure
  • Avoid forced disclosure: Let people share voluntarily
  • Provide resources: Mental health hotlines, chaplain services, peer support contact info

Longer-Term Support
