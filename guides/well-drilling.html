1. Finding Water: Reading the Landscape

Locating groundwater is the critical first step. Water exists beneath most land surfaces, but depth and quantity vary dramatically. Understanding landscape indicators will guide your well placement for success.

Water Table Basics

The water table is the upper surface of groundwater. It rises and falls seasonally (higher in wet season, lower in dry season). In most regions, the water table is between 10-50 feet deep, though it can range from a few feet to hundreds of feet.

  • Shallow water table: Found in valleys, near streams, after rainy seasons (5-15 ft depth)
  • Deep water table: Found on hilltops, in arid regions, during dry seasons (50-200+ ft depth)
  • Seasonal fluctuation: Water table can rise 10-20 feet between dry and wet seasons

Scientific Landscape Indicators

Vegetation Patterns

  • Water-loving plants (willows, cottonwoods, poplars, reeds, cattails) indicate shallow water table within 5-15 feet
  • Deep-rooted trees (mesquite, acacia) reach down 30-50 feet, indicating deeper aquifers
  • Healthy grass coverage in otherwise dry areas suggests subsurface moisture
  • Alkali-tolerant plants (saltbush, greasewood) often grow where groundwater is near surface but mineral-laden
  • Absence of vegetation on hilltops indicates deeper water tables

Topography

  • Valley bottoms accumulate groundwater‚Äîwater table 5-20 feet deep
  • Slope bases where hillsides meet valleys have shallower water tables
  • Hilltops have deeper water tables‚Äîavoid for shallow wells
  • Flat terrain generally has moderate depth (15-40 feet)

Geology & Soil

  • Sandy or gravel soil holds water well‚Äîideal for wells
  • Clay soil is impermeable‚Äîwater may be deeper or require different methods
  • Rock outcrops indicate bedrock may be shallow; drilling more difficult
  • Alluvial deposits (river/stream sediments) are highly productive aquifers

Surface Water Features

  • Springs indicate groundwater is shallow and accessible
  • Wet spots or seeps show water table intersects surface
  • Streams flow from areas with shallow water table
  • Dry stream beds (wadis) contain underground water channels

Dowsing vs Scientific Methods

Dowsing (water witching) uses divining rods or pendulums to locate water. While traditional and culturally significant, scientific evidence does not support dowsing's effectiveness beyond chance. However, dowsers often have generations of local knowledge about where water exists‚Äîthis experiential knowledge is valuable, even if the mechanism isn't understood.

Scientific approach: Combine landscape observation with local knowledge. Ask neighbors where they found water, examine topographic maps, look at vegetation patterns, and check geological surveys if available.

Hybrid approach: Use both dowsing (which may access local knowledge) and landscape analysis. Site 3-4 potential drilling locations, then choose based on scientific indicators. This respects tradition while maximizing success probability.

Seasonal Considerations

  • Drill in wet season: Water table is highest; you see actual sustainable depth
  • Plan for dry season: Design well 10-20 feet deeper than current water table if possible
  • Test well stability: Return to a site 6 months apart to assess seasonal fluctuation
  • Record measurements: Note water level at different times to predict performance
Pro Tip: The best time to drill is 1-2 months after the wet season peaks. Water table is still high, but ground has stabilized for easier drilling.

2. Well Types: Choosing the Right Method

Different well types suit different depths, soil conditions, and available resources. Each has distinct advantages, costs, and maintenance requirements.

Dug Wells (Hand-Excavated)

Specifications

  • Diameter: 3-5 feet
  • Depth: 3-15 feet (rarely deeper)
  • Soil type: Clay, loam, soft rock
  • Yield: 50-500+ gallons per day
  • Construction time: 2-7 days for a team

Dug wells are the oldest and most practical method for shallow water. A person is lowered into the hole and digs/excavates with pick and shovel until hitting water. The shaft is lined with stone, brick, or concrete rings to prevent collapse.

Advantages

  • Minimal tools required (shovel, pick, rope)
  • Easy to maintain and repair
  • Can be enlarged if water demand increases
  • High yield if water table is shallow

Disadvantages

  • Limited depth (water table must be shallow)
  • Slow when hitting rock or gravel
  • Higher contamination risk (surface water can enter)
  • Labor-intensive and dangerous (cave-in risk)

Driven Wells (Sand Point / Well Point)

Specifications

  • Diameter: 1-2 inches
  • Depth: 25-50 feet (max without power)
  • Soil type: Sand, gravel, sandy loam
  • Yield: 200-1000+ gallons per day
  • Construction time: 4-16 hours

A sand point (small screened wellhead) is driven into the ground with a sledgehammer via a drive head. The point is connected to PVC or steel pipe that's driven incrementally deeper. Used when water table is 25-50 feet deep and soil is sandy.

Advantages

  • Much faster than dug wells
  • Works in sandy aquifers with minimal tools
  • Lower contamination (screen prevents large particles)
  • Portable‚Äîcan be relocated

Disadvantages

  • Doesn't work in rock or clay
  • Depth limited (heavier materials need power)
  • Screen clogs quickly in fine sediment
  • Requires straight, hammer-driven insertion

Bored Wells (Hand Auger)

Specifications

  • Diameter: 4-8 inches
  • Depth: 20-60 feet typical
  • Soil type: Soft soil, clay, sandy loam
  • Yield: 100-500+ gallons per day
  • Construction time: 3-14 days depending on depth

A hand auger (large screw-like bit) is twisted into the ground, with soil extracted after each rotation. Augers come in bucket, T-handle, or 2-person styles. As the auger deepens, extensions are added to the handle.

Advantages

  • Works in varied soil types
  • Moderate depth possible (30-60 ft)
  • Safer than dug wells (no confined space)
  • Relatively simple equipment

Disadvantages

  • Very labor-intensive
  • Difficult in coarse gravel or rock
  • Slow progress in deep holes
  • Auger can twist and break if soil resists

Drilled Wells (Percussion / Cable Tool)

Specifications

  • Diameter: 2-4 inches
  • Depth: 100-300+ feet possible with hand methods
  • Soil type: All types (clay, sand, rock)
  • Yield: 500-2000+ gallons per day
  • Construction time: 5-30 days for 100 ft well

A heavy chisel is repeatedly dropped on a rope, crushing rock and soil at the bottom. Debris is removed with a bailer (bucket with check valve). Extremely efficient for hard ground and great depths. Three primary hand methods: cable tool (traditional), sludger (mud pump), and Baptist well (rope-pump drilling).

Advantages

  • Reaches very deep aquifers (100-300 ft)
  • Works through rock and hard clay
  • Productive wells with large aquifer access
  • Hand methods require only rope and tools

Disadvantages

  • Very slow and labor-intensive
  • Requires discipline and consistent effort
  • Rope can fray or break under stress
  • Difficult to rescue equipment if stuck

Spring Development

Specifications

  • Yield: Highly variable (gallon to 1000+ gallons per day)
  • Depth: At or near surface
  • Construction: Spring box + pipework
  • Advantages: Gravity-fed, minimal pumping needed

Springs are natural groundwater emergence points. Development involves building a protective structure (spring box) around the emergence point and gravity-feeding water downhill through pipes to storage and use points. This is the most efficient system if a spring exists on your property.

Spring Development Steps

  1. Identify spring location: Find where water emerges
  2. Build spring box: Cement or stone enclosure to capture water
  3. Install intake pipe: Submersed in spring box, 1-2 inches diameter
  4. Add filter: Gravel and sand layers to remove particles
  5. Install overflow: Pipe to prevent box overflow
  6. Gravity distribution: Pipe water downhill to cistern or tap
Ideal Scenario: A spring 100 feet uphill from your home can deliver 50+ gallons per day by gravity alone‚Äîno pump needed.

3. Hand Drilling Methods: Deep Well Drilling Without Power

Hand drilling methods can reach 100+ feet deep using only simple tools, rope, and muscle power. All methods are based on pulverizing or loosening soil/rock and extracting debris. Choose based on soil type and depth target.

Sludger / Mud Pump Method

The sludger (also called mud pump or slush pump) is the fastest hand drilling method for soft ground. A tube with a valve at the bottom is repeatedly raised and lowered in the borehole. Each stroke sucks in loose material and water, which is then pumped out, deepening the hole progressively.

Sludger Construction

  • Body: 2-4" diameter steel or PVC pipe, 6-10 feet long
  • Rod: Inside the sludger, with handle at top for pumping
  • Bottom valve: One-way check valve (allows material in, prevents flow out)
  • Top opening: Open or with removable cap for discharge

Sludger Drilling Steps

  1. Start bore: Use hand auger to start 2-3 foot hole; diameter equals sludger diameter
  2. Insert sludger: Lower sludger into hole via rope or tripod
  3. Pump up-down: 1-2 person team rhythmically raises/lowers sludger handle (each stroke ~1 foot of motion)
  4. Check progress: Every 20-30 strokes, pull sludger out and empty debris into bucket
  5. Add water: Keep 1-2 feet of water in hole to suspend material
  6. Add casing: As hole deepens, install temporary or permanent casing to prevent collapse
  7. Continue pumping: Repeat stroke rhythm until target depth reached

Sludger Performance

  • Rate: 3-10 feet per day in soft clay/sand
  • Max depth: 100+ feet possible with determination
  • Soil suited to: Clay, silt, sand (NOT rock)
  • Water table: Works even below water table (uses muddy slurry)
  • Crew: 2-3 people (1-2 pumping, 1 managing)

Baptist Well Drilling (Rope-Pump Method)

Baptist well drilling uses a rope-pump action to lift a sand bucket from the borehole. This method works well in sandy soils and requires a simple framework. Named for its development in Baptist missionary regions where minimal infrastructure existed.

Baptist Well Drilling Steps

  1. Build frame: 12-15 foot tall tripod of wood logs with pulley at top
  2. Drill initial bore: Use hand auger to 3-5 feet
  3. Install rope: Single rope over pulley; one end has sand bucket, other is operator handle
  4. Lower bucket: Operator pulls down, bucket fills with sand/debris at bottom
  5. Raise bucket: Two workers pull up opposite end, lifting full bucket out
  6. Empty bucket: Dump material, repeat
  7. Monitor water: Keep standing water for bucket to fill into
  8. Add casing: Install pipe casing as depth increases (prevents collapse)

Baptist Well Performance

  • Rate: 2-5 feet per day in sand
  • Max depth: 80-150 feet possible
  • Soil suited to: Sand, sandy loam, gravel
  • Difficult in: Clay, rock (sand doesn't fill bucket well)
  • Crew: 2-3 people minimum (can work with 2)
  • Advantage: Simple materials, very teachable method

Percussion / Cable Tool Drilling

The cable tool method is the traditional deep-well drilling technique. A heavy chisel bit is repeatedly dropped from height via rope, shattering rock and soil. Debris is removed with a bailer (a tube with check valve that traps material when pulled up).

Cable Tool System Components

  • Bit: 3-4 inch wide chisel on the end of 1/2-inch rope; weighs 50-150 lbs
  • Rope: 1/2-inch manila or synthetic rope; strong enough for 200+ ft depth
  • Bailer: 2-3 inch diameter tube with check valve, 6-10 feet long; works like a pump
  • Derrick: Frame 30-40 feet tall for lifting chisel and bailer
  • Casing: Steel or PVC pipe pushed down as hole deepens; prevents collapse

Cable Tool Drilling Sequence

  1. Set up derrick: Rigid 30-40 ft structure with pulley at top
  2. Lift chisel: Team raises 100-150 lb chisel 2-3 feet via pulley
  3. Drop chisel: Release rope; chisel falls and smashes bottom
  4. Repeat drops: Lift and drop in rhythm; 30-50 strokes per cycle
  5. Bail debris: Every 30-50 drops, lower bailer, let it collect settled material, pull out
  6. Add water: Maintain standing water to suspend pulverized material
  7. Push casing: As hole deepens, drive casing sections down with drive head
  8. Continue: Repeat cycles until reaching target depth or water-bearing layer

Cable Tool Performance

  • Rate: 5-15 feet per day depending on soil hardness
  • Max depth: 200-400 feet possible with hand methods
  • Best in: Rock, hardpan, dense clay
  • Less efficient in: Soft sand and gravel (cave in)
  • Crew: 3-5 people (lift, release, bail, manage casing)
  • Traditional method: Most common before power drilling invented

Choosing Your Drilling Method

Method Best For Max Depth Speed Crew Size
Sludger Soft clay, silt, sand 100+ ft 3-10 ft/day 2-3
Baptist Well Sand, sandy loam 80-150 ft 2-5 ft/day 2-3

4. Well Casing & Screen: Protecting the Borehole

Well casing prevents the borehole from collapsing and keeps contamination out. Screening filters sediment while allowing water to enter. Proper construction ensures long-term well stability.

Casing Options

PVC Casing (Modern Standard)

  • Material: Rigid polyvinyl chloride pipe
  • Diameters: 2, 3, 4, 6 inches standard
  • Advantages: Inexpensive, corrosion-proof, easy to connect
  • Installation: Lowered or driven down hole; can be pushed simultaneously with drilling
  • Cost: $2-8 per foot depending on diameter

Steel Casing (High-Strength)

  • Material: Black steel or galvanized steel pipe
  • Diameters: 1-6+ inches
  • Advantages: Very strong; works for driven wells; can withstand heavy impacts
  • Disadvantages: Rusts without protection; more expensive than PVC
  • Use case: Driven wells where force is needed; deep wells

Hand-Made Clay Pipe (Traditional)

  • Material: Fired clay rings stacked and mortared
  • Diameters: 12-24 inches typical for dug wells
  • Advantages: Very long-lasting (centuries); uses local materials
  • Installation: Rings are lowered as excavation deepens
  • Challenges: Heavy; requires skilled labor to set; mortar can fail

Well Screen

The well screen prevents aquifer material (sand, gravel) from entering the pipe while allowing water to flow in. Without screening, your well will pump sand along with water, damaging pumps and clogging pipes.

Screen Types

Slotted PVC Screen
  • Construction: PVC pipe with factory-cut horizontal slots (0.01-0.05 inches)
  • Diameter: Matches casing (2-4 inches typical)
  • Length: 5-20 feet covering entire water-bearing layer
  • Cost: $5-15 per foot
  • Installation: Installed at bottom of well casing; water enters through slots
Wire-Wound Screen
  • Construction: Wire wrapped helically around profile bars
  • Slot size: Very uniform; ranges 0.010-0.050 inches
  • Durability: Excellent; long-lasting
  • Cost: $8-20 per foot
  • Use: High-yield wells; sandy aquifers
Hand-Made Gravel Screen
  • Construction: Perforated pipe surrounded by gravel pack
  • Method: Wrap perforated casing with cheesecloth, fill void with gravel
  • Cost: Minimal (scrap materials)
  • Effective: Works adequately in sandy soils

Screen Installation & Gravel Pack

Installing Well Screen and Gravel Pack

  1. Prepare screen: Insert slotted or wire-wound screen at bottom of casing (5-20 feet length)
  2. Position correctly: Screen should cover entire productive water-bearing layer
  3. Gravel pack layer 1: Pour coarse gravel (1/2 - 3/4 inch) around screen, filling void space 2-3 feet above screen top
  4. Transition layer: Place sand (fine gravel, 1/8 - 1/4 inch) above coarse gravel, 1-2 feet thick
  5. Seal transition: Add clay or bentonite above sand layer to prevent fine particles entering well
  6. Cement seal: Pour Portland cement from clay layer up to surface (inside casing); allows 24 hours to cure
  7. Install cap: Place sanitary well cap (vented, lockable) at surface

Well Seal at Surface

The surface seal prevents surface water, insects, and contaminants from entering the well. Proper sealing adds years to well life and maintains water quality.

Surface Seal Components

  • Sanitary cap: Metal or plastic, vented (air can enter/exit), lockable
  • Cement collar: 2-3 feet diameter, 6 inches tall around casing
  • Slope: Cement sloped away from well to shed surface water
  • Drainage: Gravel or drainage rock around cement to prevent pooling

Preventing Well Contamination

  • Distance from latrines: Minimum 100 feet downhill; 300+ feet uphill
  • Distance from livestock areas: 50+ feet
  • Distance from septic systems: 50-100 feet depending on soil permeability
  • Proper casing: No breaks or cracks; sealed at surface
  • Gravel pack: Prevents clay/silt from entering screen
  • Regular inspection: Check for cracks, damage, or signs of leakage

5. Pump Construction: Getting Water to the Surface

Several pump types can be hand-constructed or assembled from basic materials. The rope pump is the most practical for remote locations. All designs must create suction to lift water and overcome friction.

Rope Pump: The Most Buildable From Scratch

The rope pump is simple, effective, and can be built entirely from locally available materials. Water is lifted by pistons (valved cylinders) attached to a rope, which travels through a vertical pipe. As the rope moves up and down, pistons create suction and push water upward.

Rope Pump Components

Required Materials

  • Rope: 1/2 - 3/4 inch natural or synthetic; nylon, manila, or sisal
  • PVC pipe riser: 2-4 inch diameter; smooth interior
  • Pistons: 2-4 inch diameter rubber or leather cups; can be hand-made
  • Pulleys: 2-3 pulleys mounted on frame at top
  • Handle: Wooden or metal lever mechanism; 3-5 feet long
  • Frame: Wood or steel structure 10-15 feet tall

Rope Pump Detailed Dimensions

Component Specifications

  • Frame height: 12-15 feet (above ground)
  • Riser pipe: 2-4 inch diameter PVC or metal; smooth interior critical
  • Riser pipe length: Extends 5+ feet above highest water level to be lifted
  • Piston diameter: 2-4 inches (match pipe diameter closely)
  • Piston stroke: 2-3 feet per pump cycle (rope up/down cycle)
  • Rope diameter: 1/2 - 3/4 inch; heavy-duty
  • Pulley diameter: 12-18 inches (larger = less friction)
  • Handle length: 3-5 feet from pivot; mechanical advantage ~2:1
  • Piston spacing: 8-12 feet apart on rope (alternating suction)

Hand-Made Piston Construction

  • Option 1: Leather cup piston - Tanned leather disk (1/2 inch thick) wrapped around wood block; diameter = pipe diameter minus 1/8 inch for seal
  • Option 2: Rubber piston - Rubber ball or hemisphere with hole through center; rope passes through
  • Option 3: Cloth/rope piston - Tightly wrapped burlap or cloth around dowel; sealed with grease for water tightness

Hand-Made Leather Piston Steps

  1. Obtain leather: Tanned hide, 1/2 - 3/4 inch thick; cut 4-inch diameter disk
  2. Prepare dowel: 2-inch diameter wood dowel, 4 inches long; drill center hole for rope (1/2 inch)
  3. Wrap leather: Wrap leather disk around dowel, overlapping edges 1-2 inches
  4. Secure wrapping: Use rope or metal bands to compress leather tight around dowel
  5. Seal exterior: Coat with grease or pitch to waterproof
  6. Test fit: Piston should slide smoothly inside riser pipe with slight resistance

Rope Pump Assembly Steps

  1. Build frame: Construct sturdy wood/steel frame 12-15 feet tall; triangulate for stability
  2. Install pulley: Mount large pulley (12-18" diameter) at frame top via bearing
  3. Prepare rope: Cut rope length = well depth + 30 feet (extra for adjustment); tie loop at each end
  4. Attach pistons: Secure pistons to rope at 8-12 foot intervals using rope knots or bolts
  5. Install riser pipe: Lower riser pipe (2-4" diameter) into well beside production casing; smooth interior is critical
  6. Add check valve: Install one-way check valve at bottom of riser (allows water in, prevents backflow)
  7. Thread rope: Run rope over pulley; one end has pistons descending into well, other end rises for handle attachment
  8. Install handle: Attach lever handle to rope end; 3-5 feet long for mechanical advantage
  9. Seal top: Small cap or screen at riser pipe top to prevent debris/insects entering
  10. Test operation: Prime pump with water; push/pull handle in steady rhythm
Performance: A well-constructed rope pump lifts water from 30-100 feet deep. Two people alternating can lift 200-400 gallons per day. The rope pump is self-priming and extremely reliable.

Lever / Pitcher Pump

A traditional cast-iron or forged steel lever pump (also called a hand pump or pitcher pump) uses a lever to create suction and push water upward. These are excellent if cast iron components are available.

Lever Pump Mechanism

  • Lever arm: 18-24 inches long; mechanical advantage ~3:1
  • Piston chamber: Cast iron or bronze cylinder with check valves
  • Suction lift limit: ~25 feet (atmospheric pressure limit)
  • Production: 2-5 gallons per stroke; can pump 100-200 gallons per hour with moderate effort
  • Durability: Excellent; cast iron lasts decades
  • Maintenance: Occasional lubrication; leather washers may need replacement

Windmill Pump Connection

Windmills can power water pumps continuously when wind is available. A windmill drives a multi-blade rotor connected to a pump rod that operates a piston pump (usually suction lift type) below.

Windmill Pump System

  • Rotor blades: 6-12 feet diameter; catches wind to turn central shaft
  • Gearbox: Reduces rotor RPM (60-120 RPM) to pump RPM (40-60 strokes/min)
  • Pump rod: Reciprocating rod driven by gearbox; up/down 2-3 feet per second
  • Pump type: Suction lift pump (max 25 feet) or deep-well turbine
  • Tower height: 20-40 feet to access consistent wind
  • Production: 100-500 gallons per day depending on wind and depth

Solar Pump Basics

Solar-powered pumps use photovoltaic panels to power electric submersible or surface pumps. While requiring solar panels (technology-dependent), they provide reliable daytime pumping without ongoing fuel costs.

  • Panel size: 50-200 watts typical for moderate lifting
  • Pump type: Submersible electric pump (placed in well); 12V or 24V DC
  • Production: 100-500 gallons per day depending on sun and depth
  • Battery storage: Optional batteries allow nighttime pumping
  • Advantage: No fuel needed; quiet; reliable daytime operation

Hand Suction Pump (Max 25 feet)

A simple hand pump using atmospheric pressure to create suction. Limited to ~25 feet lift (the atmospheric pressure limit at sea level), but very simple to construct.

Simple Hand Suction Pump Steps

  1. Intake pipe: Install 1-2 inch diameter pipe into well, with lower end below water level
  2. Piston chamber: Install above water level; simple cylinder with moveable piston
  3. Inlet check valve: One-way valve at bottom of chamber (allows water in)
  4. Outlet check valve: One-way valve at top of chamber (water exits only on downstroke)
  5. Piston rod: Connect piston to hand lever via rope or mechanical linkage
  6. Operation: Pull lever up (piston rises, suction draws water through inlet); push down (pressure forces water through outlet)
Important: Suction pumps work only if water level is less than 25 feet below pump. Deeper wells require submersible pumps or rope pumps.

6. Water Quality: Testing and Maintaining Safe Water

Groundwater quality varies widely by location. Testing without laboratory equipment is possible using sensory and simple methods. Contamination prevention is far easier than treatment.

Testing Without Laboratory Equipment

Visual Inspection

  • Clarity: Water should be clear, not cloudy or turbid
  • Color: Clear or slightly yellow is normal; brown/orange suggests iron; dark suggests tannins or organic matter
  • Sediment: No visible particles; test by letting sample sit 1 hour‚Äîsediment indicates poor screening or well disturbance

Smell Test

  • Fresh/neutral: Ideal; no odor
  • Rotten egg / sulfur smell: Suggests hydrogen sulfide (H‚ÇÇS); indicates anaerobic conditions‚Äîpotentially dangerous
  • Musty / earthy smell: Suggests organic matter decay; usually not dangerous but indicates biological activity
  • Chemical / fuel smell: Contamination from petroleum, solvents, or chemicals‚Äîdo NOT drink

Taste Test (Small Sample Only)

  • Fresh / neutral: Safe-tasting groundwater
  • Salty / bitter: Dissolved minerals; not necessarily unsafe but often unpalatable
  • Metallic: Iron or other metals present; may indicate corrosion or contamination
  • Chemical taste: Do NOT drink; indicates contamination

Safety note: Never taste water suspected of contamination. Only taste from wells you trust or after boiling.

Common Contaminants and Indicators

Contaminant Appearance / Odor Health Risk Source
Iron Orange/brown tint; metallic taste Low (stains, not harmful) Natural; corrosion
Hydrogen Sulfide Rotten egg smell Medium (gas hazard) Anaerobic bacteria
Nitrates No visible sign High (infants) Latrines, manure
Bacteria / Pathogens No visible sign High Fecal contamination

Contamination Prevention: Proper Well Placement

Minimum Distances

  • Latrines / Pit toilets: 100+ feet downhill; 300+ feet uphill (slope matters critically)
  • Septic tanks: 50-100 feet; depends on soil permeability (clay is more protective than sand)
  • Livestock areas / manure piles: 50+ feet
  • Landfills / waste disposal: 300+ feet
  • Streams: 50+ feet; above highest flood level
  • Surface water: Never below lakes, ponds, or marshes

Topographic Considerations

  • Uphill from contamination: Wells placed uphill are safer (water flows downhill)
  • Hillside placement: Middle slope is ideal; higher contamination risk at valley bottoms (where contaminants accumulate)
  • Groundwater gradient: Determine flow direction (usually toward low points); place well uphill

Disinfection: Shock Chlorination

When a well is newly drilled, new casing is installed, or contamination is suspected, shock chlorination kills bacteria and pathogens using bleach. This is a one-time treatment.

Shock Chlorination Steps

  1. Calculate well volume: Internal diameter (feet) √ó depth (feet) √ó 0.5 = approximate gallons
    • Example: 2-foot diameter well, 50 feet deep = 2 √ó 50 √ó 0.5 = 50 gallons
  2. Calculate chlorine dose: Use 100-500 ppm (parts per million); for household wells, use 200 ppm
    • Bleach (5-6% sodium hypochlorite): 2 ounces per 100 gallons of well water
    • Example for 50-gallon well: 1 ounce of bleach
  3. Prepare chlorine: Mix bleach with 1 gallon of water in bucket
  4. Add to well: Pour bleach solution down well slowly
  5. Circulate: Run all taps and hoses for 5-10 minutes to distribute chlorine throughout system
  6. Wait: Let sit 12-24 hours; strong chlorine smell should be present
  7. Flush: Run taps again until chlorine smell is gone (may take 10,000+ gallons for large systems)
  8. Test: Sample water after 48 hours; should test free of bacteria
Safety: Bleach can damage metal and rubber components. Use rubber gloves; avoid skin contact. Never mix bleach with other chemicals (creates toxic gas).

7. Well Maintenance: Long-Term Reliability

Regular maintenance prevents well failure and maintains water quality. Most maintenance tasks are simple and can be done with basic tools.

Regular Inspection Routine

Monthly Inspection Checklist

  1. Check water level: Measure depth to water surface with tape/rope; compare to previous month
  2. Check water quality: Observe color, clarity, smell
  3. Inspect casing: Look for cracks, leaks, or rust stains
  4. Check cap: Ensure sanitary cap is in place and secure
  5. Test pump: Operate pump through full cycle; listen for unusual sounds
  6. Check discharge: Water should be clear; note any sediment or discoloration

Well Rehabilitation

Over time, wells may lose productivity due to screen clogging, sediment accumulation, or mineral buildup. Rehabilitation restores function.

Causes of Well Decline

  • Screen clogging: Fine sediment blocks water entry
  • Biofilm formation: Bacteria colonies coat screen and pipes
  • Mineral deposits: Iron or carbonate minerals coat interior surfaces
  • Sediment accumulation: Fine sand collects at well bottom, raising water level

Rehabilitation Methods

  • Bailing: Remove accumulated sediment at bottom using bailer bucket (effective but labor-intensive)
  • Surging: Vigorously move bailer up/down to agitate sediment and dislodge blockages
  • Backflushing: Reverse water flow (if pump allows) to dislodge particles from screen
  • Chemical treatment: Use dilute acid (for calcium deposits) or hydrogen peroxide (for biofilm) to dissolve deposits
  • Physical scrubbing: Lower wire brush down casing to scrub interior walls

Pump Repair and Maintenance

Rope Pump Maintenance

  • Inspect rope: Check for fraying, weak spots, rot; replace if damaged
  • Check pistons: Inspect leather/rubber for wear; replace if cracked or worn thin
  • Lubricate pulley: Grease bearing monthly to prevent friction
  • Check check valve: If pump loses prime, check valve may be stuck; clean by flushing

Lever Pump Maintenance

  • Lubricate joints: Oil pivot points monthly
  • Replace leather washers: Every 2-3 years; causes loss of suction
  • Check interior: If pump leaks or produces weak suction, internal parts may be worn
  • Winterization: In cold climates, drain pump before freezing

Water Level Monitoring

Track water level over time to predict well performance and plan for drought seasons.

Measuring and Recording Water Level

  1. Measure monthly: On same day each month if possible
  2. Use marked rope: Tie knot at water surface; measure rope length
  3. Record data: Date, depth, observations (water quality, pump performance)
  4. Track seasonal: Over 1-2 years, pattern will show wet/dry season changes
  5. Plan depth: Deepest dry season level tells you minimum pump intake depth
Data Example: Wet season (July) water level: 20 feet. Dry season (March) water level: 35 feet. Plan pump intake below 40 feet to ensure year-round water access.

8. Gravity-Fed Systems: Passive Water Distribution

Gravity-fed systems deliver water downhill without pumping, making them the most efficient and reliable solution when elevation change is available. Spring sources, hilltop cisterns, or raised storage tanks can feed gravity systems.

System Overview

  • Source: Spring, hilltop cistern, or elevated storage tank
  • Mechanism: Water flows downhill through pipe due to elevation difference
  • No pumping: Eliminates mechanical failure points; works continuously
  • Pressure: Elevation difference creates water pressure (1 psi per 2.31 feet)
  • Distribution: Single or multiple outlet points downhill from source

Pressure Calculations

Water pressure in a gravity system depends on elevation difference (head).

Pressure Formula

  • Pressure (psi) = Elevation difference (feet) √∑ 2.31
  • Example: Spring 100 feet above a tap = 100 √∑ 2.31 = 43 psi
  • Practical range: 10-50 psi is excellent; 5-10 psi is adequate; under 5 psi is weak
  • Minimum for household taps: 5 psi (though 20+ psi is more practical)
  • Pressure too high?: Add pressure-reducing valve to protect pipes/taps

Pipe Sizing for Gravity Systems

Correct pipe size balances flow rate against friction loss. Undersized pipe reduces flow; oversized pipe is wasteful and expensive.

Pipe Diameter Typical Flow Rate Suitable For Max Recommended Distance
1/2" 10-15 GPM Single household tap 50-100 ft
3/4" 20-30 GPM Single household; 2 taps simultaneous 100-200 ft
1" 40-50 GPM Household + livestock 200-400 ft
1.5" 80-100 GPM Multiple households/farms 400-800 ft

Spring Capture and Development

A spring is natural groundwater emergence. Proper capture prevents contamination and allows gravity distribution.

Spring Box Construction Steps

  1. Excavate area: Dig around spring emergence; clear soil 2-3 feet diameter
  2. Contain water: Build box from stone, concrete, or lined pit to catch spring water
  3. Filter layers:
    • Bottom layer: 6-12 inches coarse gravel
    • Middle layer: 4-6 inches sand
    • Top layer: 4-6 inches fine gravel/pebbles
  4. Intake pipe: Insert 1-2 inch diameter pipe 6-12 inches deep into spring box; cap at water surface
  5. Overflow pipe: Install overflow pipe at box top (prevents overflow/flooding)
  6. Seal edges: Clay or bentonite around box edges to prevent surface water entry
  7. Protective roof: Cover spring box with stone or concrete slab (prevents contamination)
  8. Vent opening: Small vent in roof to prevent pressure buildup

Storage Tank Sizing

An intermediate storage tank provides water buffer if spring flow is variable. Tank volume should handle daily demand plus safety margin.

Storage Tank Calculation

  • Daily demand: Estimate gallons per day for all users
  • Example: 5 people √ó 20 gallons per person per day = 100 gallons/day
  • Tank size: 1-3 days supply minimum; 100 gallons/day √ó 2 days = 200 gallon tank
  • Spring flow: Ensure spring produces more than daily demand (test over 24 hours)
  • Overflow management: Overflow should direct excess water safely (to garden, animals, etc.)

Gravity Distribution to Multiple Points

A gravity system can supply multiple taps, livestock troughs, or properties downhill.

Multi-Point Distribution Steps

  1. Main line: Run primary pipe from source downhill to lowest point
  2. Branch lines: Tee off main line to supply individual taps/troughs
  3. Isolation valves: Place ball valve at each branch for independent shutoff
  4. Faucet/tap: Install tap (gate valve + faucet) at each endpoint
  5. Air vent: Install small vent valve at high points to release trapped air
  6. Drain valve: Install drain at lowest point to empty pipes for winter
  7. Pressure relief: If pressure exceeds 80 psi, add pressure-reducing valve

Winter Protection for Gravity Systems

  • Drain system: Open drain valve in fall; let pipes empty before freezing
  • Insulation: Bury main lines below frost line; above-ground sections insulate with straw/foam
  • Heat tape: Optional electric heat tape around exposed sections prevents freezing
  • Continuous flow: Slow continuous flow (even in winter) prevents freezing in pipes

9. Cisterns & Rainwater Harvesting: Capturing Free Water

Rainwater harvesting supplements well water and reduces groundwater demand. A typical roof can collect substantial water during rainy seasons. Cisterns (storage tanks) store rainwater for use during dry periods.

Roof Catchment Calculation

The amount of water your roof collects depends on roof area and rainfall.

Rainwater Collection Formula

  • Water collected = Roof area (sq ft) √ó Rainfall (inches) √ó 0.6
  • Example: 1000 sq ft roof √ó 1 inch rain √ó 0.6 = 600 gallons
  • Annual potential: Vary by region
    • Arid region (10" annual rain): 6,000 gallons/year
    • Semi-arid (20" annual rain): 12,000 gallons/year
    • Humid region (50" annual rain): 30,000 gallons/year
  • 0.6 factor accounts for: Roof losses, gutter leaks, first-flush waste (30-40%)

First-Flush Diverter

Initial rainwater contains roof debris, dust, and contaminants. A first-flush diverter redirects this water away before filling the cistern.

First-Flush Diverter Design

  • Simple design: 50-100 gallon bucket in downspout before cistern
  • Mechanism: Float valve closes after bucket fills; overflow redirects to cistern
  • Divert amount: 50-100 gallons (about 0.5 mm rainfall across roof) is typical
  • Discharge: Diverted water can irrigate garden or livestock

Cistern Construction

Tank Material Options

Ferrocement Cistern (Buildable With Local Materials)
  • Material: Reinforced cement with wire mesh and mortar
  • Cost: Moderate; uses local materials
  • Construction: Build form, apply cement/wire/sand layers, cure
  • Lifespan: 20-40+ years if properly constructed
  • Size: Can be built in any size; 500-5000 gallons typical
Stone/Brick Cistern (Traditional)
  • Material: Mortared stone or brick
  • Cost: Moderate to high depending on materials
  • Durability: Excellent; centuries-old examples exist
  • Size: 500-5000+ gallons
  • Waterproofing: Interior surface must be sealed (lime wash, cement plaster, or bituminous coating)
Plastic/Fiberglass Tank (Purchased)
  • Material: Plastic or fiberglass
  • Cost: $500-3000+ depending on size
  • Durability: 10-30 years; UV degradation over time
  • Advantage: Ready-made; minimal assembly
  • Disadvantage: Transported, so less available in remote areas

Ferrocement Cistern Construction Steps

Building a 1000-Gallon Ferrocement Cistern

  1. Design form: Cylinder 5 feet diameter √ó 5 feet tall = ~1000 gallons
    • Volume (gallons) = Diameter¬≤ √ó Height √ó 5.87
    • 5¬≤ √ó 5 √ó 5.87 = 734 gallons; adjust height for 1000 gallon target
  2. Build form: Wooden or plastic cylinder form; support sides against outward pressure
  3. Install reinforcement: Horizontal and vertical wire mesh or rebar cage inside form
  4. Apply cement: First layer: 2-3 inches cement mortar (3:1 sand:cement)
  5. Add mesh: Press second wire mesh layer into wet cement
  6. Final coat: 2-3 inches final cement mortar; smooth interior surface
  7. Cure: Keep moist for 7-14 days (cover with plastic/wet burlap); prevents cracking
  8. Waterproof interior: Apply sealant (bituminous coating or cement-based waterproofing)
  9. Install inlets/outlets:
    • Inlet: 1-2 inch pipe with screen/strainer
    • Outlet: Pipe 2-3 feet above bottom (allows sediment to settle)
    • Overflow: Pipe at top; directs excess away
    • Drain: Lowest point for cleaning
  10. Install cover: Removable or hinged cover prevents contamination; includes vent
  11. Test fill: Fill slowly; check for leaks; patch any cracks with cement

Cistern Maintenance

  • Inspect regularly: Check for cracks, leaks, algae growth
  • Clean annually: Drain completely; scrub interior; remove sediment
  • Monitor water quality: If cistern algae or sediment develops, add cover/light-blocking material
  • Filter outlet water: Use fine mesh screen before household use (removes particles)
  • Winterization: In cold climates, drain and cover before freezing

Water Quality from Cisterns

Important: Rainwater in cisterns can contain biological contaminants (bacteria, protozoa). Best practice: Use cistern water for irrigation, animals, and non-potable uses. Boil or treat chemically (bleach) before drinking.
  • Animal watering: Safe for livestock (natural rainwater)
  • Garden irrigation: Ideal (reduces well demand)
  • Household cleaning: Safe if relatively clean
  • Drinking water: Requires treatment (boiling or bleach disinfection)

Multi-Tank Systems

For larger water demand, multiple cisterns can be connected in series or parallel.

  • Series: First tank fills, overflow feeds second tank (good for separating sediment)
  • Parallel: Multiple tanks fill simultaneously (increases storage without extra height)
  • Distribution: Gravity feed from highest tank downhill to lower tanks/taps
  • Seasonal use: Wet season charges cisterns; dry season draws down
