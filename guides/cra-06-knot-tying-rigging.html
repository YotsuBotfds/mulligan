Knot Fundamentals

Understanding knot principles enables learning any knot variation. Knots are classified by function: hitches (attach to objects), bends (join ropes), loops (create fixed circles), and specialty (specific purposes).

Knot Components:

  • Working end: Free end of rope being tied
  • Standing line: Main body of rope; carries load
  • Bight: U-shaped bend in rope without crossing itself
  • Loop: Bight that crosses itself; typically enclosed
  • Turn: Rope wrapped around object

Knot Strength Factors:

  • Rope type: Natural fiber generally 10-30% weaker than synthetic in knots (due to slipping)
  • Knot design: Different knots achieve 40-100% of rope breaking strength
  • Load type: Sudden shock loads break knots more easily than gradual tension
  • Friction: Rougher rope (natural fiber) holds knots better than smooth rope

Essential Knots

Bowline (Pronounced "BO-lin"):

Use: Create fixed loop at end of rope. Strength: 60-70% of rope breaking strength. Does not slip under load. Particularly useful when loop must be temporarily fixed, then released easily.

Tying: 1) Create loop in standing line; 2) Pass working end through loop; 3) Wrap around standing line; 4) Pass back through loop; 5) Tighten. Memory aid: "Rabbit comes out of hole, around tree, back down hole."

Clove Hitch:

Use: Attach rope to cylindrical object (pole, ring). Quick tie, easy to untie. Strength: 60-70%.

Tying: 1) Pass rope around pole from right; 2) Cross over standing line; 3) Pass around pole again (opposite direction); 4) Pass under first wrap; 5) Tighten. Second wrap is critical—must cross under first wrap or hitch will slip.

Square Knot (Reef Knot):

Use: Bind two ropes of equal diameter together. Fast to tie. Can slip under load; NOT recommended for critical applications. Useful for securing bundles or bandages.

Tying: 1) Cross right rope over left; 2) Tie simple knot; 3) Cross left rope over right; 4) Tie second simple knot (opposite hands). Result resembles "x" shape from end view.

Figure-Eight Knot:

Use: Stopper knot (prevent rope from slipping through hole). Creates bulge that catches. Strength: 65-75%.

Tying: 1) Make figure-eight shape with working end; 2) Cross over, under, over standing line; 3) Pass back through original loop; 4) Tighten. Resembles figure "8" when complete.

Attachment Knots

Taut-Line Hitch (Adjustable Loop):

Use: Create loop that slides when unloaded but jams when under tension. Excellent for tent guy-lines and adjustable loads.

Tying: 1) Make three wraps around standing line with working end; 2) Pass through loop from opposite direction as wraps; 3) Tighten wraps; 4) Loop will slide freely but jam under tension.

Timber Hitch:

Use: Attach rope to logs or heavy objects for dragging. Quick to tie and untie. Load capacity: excellent for objects 50kg+.

Tying: 1) Wrap working end around log 1.5 times; 2) Make wraps around working end with standing line (minimum 3 wraps); 3) Working end jams against log; 4) Hitch tightens under load and releases easily when load removed.

Trucker's Hitch (Mechanical Advantage Knot):

Use: Creates 2:1 mechanical advantage for tightening loads without tools. Excellent for securing cargo.

Tying: 1) Create small loop (bowline or fixed loop) in standing line at midpoint; 2) Pass working end under fixed anchor point; 3) Pass back up and through loop; 4) Create mechanical advantage by pulling down on working end (requires explanation of physics for full understanding—essentially creates pulley effect with rope).

Bends (Joining Ropes)

Sheet Bend (Becket Bend):

Use: Join two ropes of unequal diameter. Forms loop from thicker rope; passes thinner rope through loop. Strength: 50-60%.

Tying: 1) Create loop from thicker rope; 2) Pass thinner rope through loop; 3) Wrap around both strands of loop; 4) Pass under and back between loop strands; 5) Tighten.

Fisherman's Knot (Double Overhand Bend):

Use: Extremely secure joining of two ropes. Strength: 60-70%. Cannot slip once under load.

Tying: 1) Cross two ropes; 2) Tie overhand knot with first rope around second rope; 3) Tie identical overhand knot with second rope around first rope; 4) Pull both standing lines to tighten (creates two interlocked overhand knots).

Surgeon's Knot:

Use: Join two ropes quickly; similar to square knot but more secure. Strength: 60%.

Tying: 1) Cross right rope over left; 2) Tie double overhand knot (pass working end twice through loop); 3) Cross opposite and tie simple overhand knot; 4) Tighten.

Loops & Fixed Points

Butterfly Loop (Alpine Loop):

Use: Create strong fixed loop in middle of rope (no access to ends). Strength: 65-75%. Useful when only midpoint available.

Tying: Complex; requires practice. Creates loop by making series of wraps and tucks. Forms figure-eight shape from middle of standing line.

Overhand Loop:

Use: Simple fixed loop at rope end. Strength: 50-60%.

Tying: Make overhand knot using working end as loop rather than as simple knot. Quick and easy.

Running Bowline:

Use: Adjustable loop that slides until desired point, then locks under load.

Tying: Tie bowline with loop adjusted to desired size before load application. Loop tightens under tension but maintains position.

Splicing Techniques

Splicing joins rope ends permanently by weaving strands together. Creates stronger join than knots (70-90% rope strength retained).

Eye Splice (Creating Loop at Rope End):

  1. Unlay rope strands for 3-4cm at working end
  2. Form loop of desired size
  3. Weave each strand separately: pass over one strand, under next, over next, continuing in sequence
  4. Complete three passes minimum (five passes for maximum strength)
  5. Trim excess strands
  6. Result: permanent loop with strength equivalent to rope breaking strength

Short Splice (Joining Two Rope Ends):

  1. Unlay both ropes for 5-7cm
  2. Interlock strands (alternate strands from each rope)
  3. Tie temporary stopper knots in all strands
  4. Weave each strand over/under pattern from other rope
  5. Complete minimum three passes
  6. Trim strands and seal ends (burn synthetic fiber)

Splice Testing:

Properly spliced rope fails at standing line, not at splice. If splice pulls apart, technique was inadequate—more passes required.

Mechanical Advantage Systems

Block and tackle systems (pulleys) provide mechanical advantage for lifting/moving heavy loads with less force.

Mechanical Advantage Calculation:

Formula: Mechanical Advantage = Number of rope segments supporting load

Example: Single fixed pulley = 1:1 (no advantage). Two-pulley movable system = 2:1 (halves required force). Four-pulley system = 4:1 (quarters required force).

Simple Pulley Systems:

  • Fixed pulley: Changes direction only; no mechanical advantage
  • Movable pulley: Provides 2:1 advantage if rope attached to fixed point; load moved half distance of rope pulled
  • Compound pulley: Multiple movable pulleys; advantage multiplies (2x2x2 = 8:1 with three movable pulleys)

DIY Pulley Construction:

Salvage wheel/axle from machinery or construct from wood: wooden sheave (wheel) 15-20cm diameter, bearing on wood frame. Pulley efficiency 90-95% when properly constructed; metal pulleys 95-98% efficient.

Rigging Load Limits:

Always use safety factor: design system for 5x maximum expected load. Example: if lifting 100kg load, all components must handle 500kg minimum (rope, knots, pulley, anchor points).

Rigging Applications

Securing Loads on Vehicles/Sleds:

Use trucker's hitch with X-cross pattern over load. Secure both front and rear. Creates tension that prevents shifting. Check tension before traveling—vibration may loosen knots; retighten every 2 hours on long journeys.

Rescue Systems:

Create Z-pulley system for rescue: one pulley at load, one fixed, one at pulling point. Provides 3:1 mechanical advantage. Allows single person to raise 100kg+ loads. Essential for cliff rescue, well extraction, heavy equipment retrieval.

Sailing/Rigging:

Use bowline for main sheet (sail control line) attach point. Clove hitches for staying rope attachment to mast. Knowledge of proper rigging essential for sailing survival craft.

Camp/Shelter Rigging:

Use taut-line hitches for tent guy-lines (allows adjustment without retying). Use timber hitch for securing poles. Square knot for bundling gear.

Common Mistakes & Solutions

Mistake 1: Using Square Knot for Critical Applications

Error: Tying square knot to secure load or join critical cordage. Knot can slip under certain loads, particularly if ropes have unequal diameters or sudden loading occurs.

Solution: Reserve square knot for temporary/non-critical uses only. Use bowline, sheet bend, or fisherman's knot for critical applications.

Mistake 2: Inadequate Rope Length for Knots

Error: Tying knots with minimal rope length. Knots slide or fail under load.

Solution: Leave minimum 5-10cm working end after knot completion. Long working ends provide safety margin; can be trimmed after knot is proven solid.

Mistake 3: Improper Knot Tightening

Error: Leaving knots partially loose. Knot tightens unevenly under load; some strands bear most tension while others remain slack.

Solution: Tighten all components of knot sequentially. Tug working end, then standing line, checking that knot tightens evenly. Properly tied knot is visually compact and symmetric.

Mistake 4: Rigging System Overload

Error: Using pulley system without safety factor. System fails catastrophically when load near rope breaking strength.

Solution: Calculate maximum load realistically (including shock loading and unexpected jerks). Design system for 5x that load minimum. Inspect rope and rigging before critical use.
