Gravity-Fed Water Systems

The most reliable and sustainable method for moving water without external power. Water flows downhill naturally when source elevation is higher than destination. This section covers assessment, sizing, collection, and storage for gravity-fed systems.

Survey & Elevation

Before designing any gravity-fed system, you must survey the terrain to determine elevation differences between source and destination. This elevation difference directly determines water pressure available at the lowest point.

Elevation to Pressure Conversion: Every 1 foot of elevation = 0.43 PSI (pounds per square inch) 10 feet of elevation = 4.3 PSI 100 feet of elevation = 43 PSI Example: Spring at 200 ft elevation, house at 100 ft Elevation drop = 100 ft Available pressure = 100 √ó 0.43 = 43 PSI

Surveying Tools: A simple level can be made from a transparent tube filled with water and suspended between two points. Differences in water level at each end indicate elevation change. For precise measurements, use a transit level or smartphone level app combined with distance measurement.

Minimum Elevation Required: For basic gravity-fed systems, aim for at least 10 feet of elevation difference to ensure adequate pressure for household faucets. Springs higher than 50 feet elevation above the house provide excellent pressure (21+ PSI).

Pipe Sizing & Flow

Pipe diameter critically affects flow rate and friction loss. Water flowing through pipes experiences friction resistance that increases with velocity and pipe length. Larger diameter pipes reduce friction loss but cost more and require more material.

Pipe Diameter Typical Use Flow Rate @ 40 PSI Suitable Length
3/4" (19 mm) Main house supply 15-20 GPM Up to 200 ft
1/2" (13 mm) Branch lines to fixtures 8-12 GPM Up to 100 ft
1" (25 mm) Large supply mains 25-35 GPM Up to 300 ft
2" (50 mm) Community supplies 80-120 GPM 1000+ ft

Flow Rate Calculation: A household typically needs 50-100 gallons per day for basic needs (drinking, cooking, washing). Multiple simultaneous users may require 20+ GPM during peak times. If your spring produces 2-3 GPM, it can serve a single household year-round with proper storage.

Tip: To estimate spring flow, place a bucket under the spring's natural flow and time how long it takes to fill. A 5-gallon bucket filling in 2 minutes = 2.5 GPM flow rate.

Friction Loss: In long runs (200+ ft), friction loss becomes significant. Rule of thumb: friction loss increases with the 5th power of diameter, so using a 3/4" main instead of 1/2" reduces friction loss to about 1/10th. For runs exceeding 500 feet, consider using larger diameter pipe in the main supply line.

Water Collection Methods

Spring Box

A spring box is a concrete or stone structure built directly over a spring source to protect water from contamination while allowing natural flow.

  • Construction: Dig down to where water emerges from ground. Build a watertight box (concrete, stone and mortar, or wood with clay sealing) around the spring outlet.
  • Screened Intake: Install a fine screen (window screen or copper mesh) over intake to filter debris. Screen prevents insects and leaf material from entering the system.
  • Overflow Pipe: Extend a pipe from the highest point of the box to direct excess water away from the structure. This prevents saturation and maintains system integrity.
  • Cleanout Access: Include a removable cover for periodic maintenance and sediment removal. Spring boxes require annual cleaning.
  • Sizing: Box should hold 10-50 gallons of water to act as a small settling basin and buffer seasonal flow variations.

Stream Intake

For water sourced from flowing streams, a more elaborate intake system prevents sediment from clogging the main pipe.

  • Intake Pipe: Position intake near stream bottom (30-50 cm down) where flow is steadier and less turbulent than surface water.
  • Gravel Filter: Surround intake with layers of gravel (coarse on outside, progressively finer toward pipe) in a basket or cage. This pre-filters sediment before water enters the main line.
  • Settling Basin: Build a small basin downstream of intake (before main line) where water slows down and suspended solids settle. Basin should be accessible for sediment removal.
  • Screen Protection: Install a fine mesh screen around intake to prevent leaves, aquatic plants, and debris from clogging the entry point.
  • Backup Entry: Include a secondary intake slightly upstream as backup if primary intake clogs during high water periods.

Storage & Pressure

Storage tanks serve two critical functions: they buffer daily water demand against variable spring flow, and they create water pressure through elevation.

Elevated Tank Design

An elevated storage tank stores water at height, naturally creating pressure without pumps.

Water Pressure from Elevation: Height above faucet = Pressure at faucet 10 ft elevation = 4.3 PSI (adequate for gravity faucet flow) 25 ft elevation = 10.8 PSI (comfortable for showers) 50 ft elevation = 21.5 PSI (good for multiple simultaneous users)
  • Tank Placement: Position tank at least 10-15 feet above the highest fixture in the house for adequate pressure. Alternatively, build on hilltop or use tower structure.
  • Tank Material: Concrete (expensive but durable), welded steel (requires rust protection), wood staves with steel bands (traditional), or food-grade plastic (if scavenged).
  • Capacity: Size tank to hold 1-3 days of household use. For 4 people at 25 gallons per person per day, store 100-300 gallons. Larger tanks allow less frequent water collection.
  • Overflow & Drain: Include overflow pipe to prevent overfilling and sediment drain valve at lowest point for flushing sediment.
  • Access Cover: Provide sealed (not airtight) access cover for maintenance. Screened vent prevents insect entry while allowing atmospheric pressure equalization.
  • Inlet & Outlet: Inlet from source should enter at top of tank. Outlet to house should be 4-6 inches above tank bottom, preventing sediment from being drawn into house supply.

Pressure Dynamics

Pressure at any faucet depends on: (1) elevation of water surface above faucet, (2) friction losses in pipe running from tank to faucet, (3) any elevation drop in pipe run.

For a 1000-foot horizontal pipe run from elevated tank to house, friction loss might be 2-3 PSI. If tank is 30 feet above house faucet level, available pressure = (30 √ó 0.43) - 3 = 10.9 PSI, adequate for shower and washing.

Hydraulic Ram Pump

A hydraulic ram pump is a mechanical device that uses water flow and momentum (water hammer effect) to pump a small fraction of flowing water to a much higher elevation ‚Äî without external power input. This elegant system is ideal when elevation drop is available but source is lower than desired destination.

How It Works

The ram pump relies on the sudden stopping of water flow creating pressure surge (water hammer). This surge forces a small amount of water through a one-way valve into a pressure chamber, which then pushes water uphill.

  • Drive Pipe: Water flows downhill through a drive pipe (typically 1-2 inches diameter). The velocity builds as water drops elevation.
  • Impulse Valve: At the end of drive pipe, a swing or flip valve automatically closes when water momentum is maximum. This sudden closure creates a pressure surge.
  • Pressure Chamber: The surge pushes a check valve open, forcing water into an air-filled chamber. Air compresses, then pushes the water through an outlet pipe to higher elevation.
  • Delivery Pipe: Small diameter pipe carries the pumped water uphill. Delivery pipe can reach 50-100 feet above the ram location, depending on drive fall.
  • Waste Outlet: Most water (7 out of 8 parts) exits through the waste outlet with minimal pressure. Only 1 part is delivered to high elevation.

Performance & Sizing

Hydraulic Ram Efficiency: Drive Fall √ó Drive Flow = Delivery Height √ó Delivery Flow √ó Efficiency Typical efficiency: 60-75% Example: Drive fall: 10 feet Drive flow: 8 gallons per minute Delivery height: 100 feet (10√ó drive fall) Expected delivery: ~1 gallon per minute (8 √∑ 8) Waste calculation: 7 GPM wasted, 1 GPM delivered

Sizing Considerations:

  • Minimum Drive Fall: Requires at least 3 feet of elevation drop through drive pipe. More drop improves performance and pumping height.
  • Drive Flow: Needs continuous water supply from spring or stream. 2-3 GPM minimum. Higher flow rates can pump more water to higher elevations.
  • Delivery Capacity: Ratio of input to output is roughly proportional to ratio of drive fall to delivery height. Pumping water 100 feet high with only 10 feet of drive fall yields very low flow (perhaps 0.25 GPM from 8 GPM input).
  • Operating Limitations: Ram pumps are simple but somewhat inefficient by modern standards. They excel when water source is abundant and nearby, but delivery point is higher than natural gravity can reach.

Construction

A basic ram pump can be built with PVC pipe, a swing check valve, and an air-filled pressure chamber. Pre-made ram pumps are also commercially available. Key components:

  • Drive pipe: 1-2" diameter, 20-50 feet long, angled downward
  • Impulse valve: Commercial swing valve or fabricated flapper mechanism
  • Pressure chamber: Steel or PVC tube sealed at both ends, with check valve and air inlet
  • Outlet pipe: 1/2" to 1" diameter, sized for desired delivery flow
  • Foundation: Anchored securely to prevent vibration from damaging pipe joints
Note: Ram pumps produce rhythmic thumping sounds and some vibration. Position away from sleeping areas. Noise level drops when pump is properly tuned and sized for water supply.

Pipe Materials

The material chosen for water pipes depends on availability, durability requirements, water chemistry, temperature, and intended use. Different materials offer trade-offs between cost, longevity, workability, and water quality.

Bamboo Pipes

In tropical and subtropical regions where bamboo grows abundantly, whole bamboo stalks can serve as water pipes. Bamboo's natural hollow structure makes it ideal for this purpose.

  • Selection: Use mature bamboo (3-4 years old), as younger stalks are weaker. Choose straight sections without splits or cracks. Diameter ranges from 1-4 inches depending on species.
  • Preparation: Remove the septum (dividing wall) between sections by carefully pushing a long stick or auger through each chamber. Drill or burn out the barrier between segments to create continuous pipe.
  • Jointing: Connect sections using overlapping sleeves made from smaller diameter bamboo. A 12-inch sleeve covers the joint between two pipes.
  • Sealing: Seal joints with pine pitch or beeswax to make connections waterproof. Heat the sealant and work it into gaps. Some cultures use clay mixed with animal fat.
  • Lifespan: Bamboo pipes typically last 3-5 years before the material weakens and splits. They're suitable for temporary systems or where bamboo can be easily replaced.
  • Advantages: Abundant in certain regions, completely biodegradable, low cost, easily worked with simple tools.
  • Disadvantages: Short lifespan, prone to mold in wet climates, flexible (requires support), susceptible to insects and rodents.

Hollowed Log Pipes

Before plastic and metal pipes, water mains in colonial America and Europe were made from hollowed logs. This technique requires significant labor but produces durable pipes from readily available materials.

  • Material Selection: Use hardwood logs (oak, hickory, or elm) 6-12 inches in diameter. Softwoods rot quickly in water; hardwoods last 20-50+ years.
  • Hollowing Methods:
    • Auger Method: Use a long-shafted auger or bit (6-10 feet long) to bore through the length of the log. Multiple passes with larger bits enlarge the hole. Requires substantial muscle or animal power.
    • Fire Method: Build a controlled fire on the log surface above the center line, allowing heat to char the interior. Scrape charred material away in layers. Slow but effective; requires careful temperature control to avoid cracking the wood.
    • Combination Method: Use fire to char and soften interior, then use auger to remove material faster.
  • Jointing: Connect log pipes at ends by overlapping sections 6-12 inches. Seal overlap with cloth strips wrapped tightly and sealed with pitch.
  • Sealing & Maintenance: Treat interior with wood preservative (linseed oil or coal tar). Exterior should be kept dry to prevent rot. Inspect annually for leaks.
  • Longevity: 20-50 years or longer for hardwoods in cool, dry climates. Tropical or wet climates reduce lifespan to 10-15 years.
  • Advantages: Uses renewable material, high durability, large diameter capable of high flow, historically proven technology.
  • Disadvantages: Extremely labor-intensive, requires large mature trees, slow to construct, limited to gravity flow (low pressure tolerance).

Clay & Terracotta

Fired clay pipe sections have been used for thousands of years for water and sewage systems. Clay resists corrosion and is locally producible in many regions with clay deposits.

  • Manufacturing: Clay is shaped into pipe sections in molds, then fired in a kiln at high temperature (1000¬∞C+) to harden it. Sections are typically 12-24 inches long and 2-6 inches in diameter.
  • Joint Type: Bell-and-spigot joints use a flared end on one pipe fitting inside an enlarged bell end on the next pipe. This design resists joint separation.
  • Sealing: Joints are sealed with lime mortar (slaked lime mixed with sand) packed tightly around the joint. Some systems use hemp gasket material beneath the mortar for better water seal.
  • Installation: Lay pipes on a foundation of sand or gravel. Pack soil carefully around pipes to prevent settling. Slope at 1:100 to 1:200 for drainage pipes.
  • Durability: 50-100+ years in appropriate conditions. Acidic water or soil can slowly dissolve mortar joints. Alkaline or neutral water causes minimal degradation.
  • Best Uses: Drainage, sewerage, and vent pipes rather than potable water (due to porous nature). Some high-fired glazed terracotta is suitable for water supply.
  • Advantages: Long lifespan, locally producible, chemically inert, resistant to root penetration if jointed properly.
  • Disadvantages: Heavy and fragile, slow to install, requires skilled joint-sealing, poor for high-pressure systems, limited size range.

Copper

Copper is an excellent material for potable water systems. It resists corrosion in most water conditions, is non-toxic, and can handle both pressure and temperature variations.

  • Types: Copper pipe comes in rigid and flexible (soft) tubing. Rigid pipe is used for main runs; soft tubing for flexible connections and complex layouts.
  • Sizing: Common household sizes are 1/2", 3/4", and 1". Larger diameters (1.5"-2") used for main supply mains.
  • Joints - Soldering: Copper pipes are joined by soldering ‚Äî heating the joint and melting solder (tin/lead or lead-free) to flow into the joint gap. The solder creates a permanent watertight connection.
    • Process: Clean joint with sandpaper or chemical flux, apply flux paste, heat with torch until solder melts and flows into joint by capillary action. Allow to cool before pressure testing.
    • Solder Types: Traditional 50/50 tin/lead solder or modern lead-free solder (tin/silver/copper). Lead-free solder requires higher temperatures.
  • Joints - Flare Fittings: Tubing is flared at the end (bent into a cone shape) and connected with a nut and threaded fitting. This creates a pressure seal without soldering, useful for outdoor or temporary connections.
  • Bending: Soft copper tubing can be hand-bent for gentle curves or shaped with a tube bender (tool that prevents kinking). Hard copper requires either annealing (heating and cooling) or bending with mechanical bender.
  • Water Chemistry: In acidic water (pH < 6.5) or aggressive water (high chloride), copper can corrode and create blue-green staining. Use corrosion-resistant fittings or alternative materials in such conditions.
  • Cost & Availability: Copper is expensive and valuable (often scavenged). Limited in off-grid situations unless scavenged from buildings.
  • Lifespan: 50+ years in normal conditions. Can last 100+ years in non-aggressive water.

PVC & Plastic

PVC (polyvinyl chloride) plastic pipe is durable, lightweight, inexpensive, and widely available. If scavenged from construction sites, it can be a valuable resource.

  • Types: Schedule 40 (standard), Schedule 80 (thicker, higher pressure), and flexible PVC.
  • Joints - Solvent Welding: PVC pipes are joined using solvent cement that chemically bonds the plastic. Apply cleaner first, then solvent cement to pipe and fitting, push together, and hold for 30 seconds. Creates permanent water-tight joint.
  • Heat Bending: PVC can be heat-bent for curves using a heat gun or torch. Heat uniformly, support the pipe, and bend slowly to prevent kinking. Allow to cool in desired shape.
  • Advantages: Lightweight (easy to transport and install), corrosion-proof, inexpensive, good flow characteristics, simple jointing, widely available.
  • Disadvantages: Not suitable for hot water (softens above 140¬∞F), can degrade in UV sunlight (needs protective covering), brittle in cold temperatures, potentially toxic if water is heated excessively.
  • Potable Water: PVC is approved for potable water in most jurisdictions if potable-grade plastic is used and solvent welding is done correctly.
  • Lifespan: 50+ years in cool conditions away from sunlight. UV exposure reduces lifespan significantly.

Cast Iron

Cast iron pipe is heavy, durable, and commonly used in older plumbing and municipal water systems. It requires proper installation and maintenance.

  • Hub Joints: Cast iron pipes use hub-and-spigot joints. The hub (enlarged end) of one pipe receives the spigot (tapered end) of the next pipe.
  • Sealing Method - Lead: Joints were traditionally sealed by packing twisted hemp fibers (oakum) into the joint gap, then pouring molten lead over the oakum. The lead cooled, creating a permanent seal. This method is now considered unsafe due to lead toxicity.
  • Modern Sealing: Contemporary installations use rubber or neoprene gaskets with mechanical restraint systems instead of lead.
  • Corrosion Issues: Cast iron corrodes internally in acidic water, creating rust scale and discoloration. Exterior corrosion is visible as orange-red staining.
  • Weight: Cast iron is extremely heavy (3x heavier than copper), making handling and installation labor-intensive.
  • Lifespan: 75-100+ years in neutral water. Shorter in acidic or high-chloride water.
  • Best Uses: Drainage and vent systems, municipal water mains. Less common in modern small systems due to weight and corrosion concerns.

Fittings & Joints

Connecting pipes requires appropriate fittings and jointing techniques. The choice depends on pipe material, pressure rating, and available tools. Poor joints cause leaks and system failure.

Threaded Joints

Threaded connections use fittings with internal and external threads to connect pipes. This method works with metal pipes (copper, steel, brass).

  • Creating Threads: A pipe threading die is used to cut external threads on the pipe end. The die is rotated around the pipe while applying cutting oil. Multiple passes enlarge the thread to full depth. This requires a robust die holder or die stocks.
  • Thread Sealant: Threads are sealed with Teflon tape (plumber's tape) wrapped clockwise 3-5 times around the male thread, or thread-seal paste. This prevents leaks at the joint.
  • Fitting Types:
    • Elbows: 90-degree or 45-degree bends to change pipe direction
    • Tees: Branch connections allowing one pipe to split into two
    • Unions: Connectors allowing disconnection without unthreading both pipes
    • Couplers: Straight connectors for joining two pipes
  • Advantages: Reusable, allows easy disconnection, works with available tools, suitable for high pressure.
  • Disadvantages: Requires threading tool (expensive), prone to leaks if not sealed properly, cannot be easily modified once threads are cut.

Compression Fittings

Compression fittings create a seal by squeezing a soft ferrule (small ring) between the pipe end and the fitting body. No special sealing compound needed.

  • Components: Fitting body with internal threads, an adjustable nut, and a ferrule (usually brass or nylon) that seals against the pipe.
  • Assembly: Slide the nut and ferrule onto the pipe, insert into fitting, and hand-tighten the nut. The ferrule deforms as the nut tightens, creating a seal.
  • Advantages: Simple, no sealant needed, reusable connections, works with both rigid and flexible tubing.
  • Disadvantages: Can loosen over time with vibration, requires careful tightening (over-tightening splits ferrule, under-tightening leaks), limited to low-to-medium pressure.
  • Materials: Works best with soft metals (copper, brass) and plastic. More difficult with hard metals like steel.

Flare Fittings

Flare fittings are used primarily with flexible tubing (soft copper, refrigerant tubing). The tubing end is flared (spread into a cone shape) and held by a nut.

  • Creating a Flare: Tubing is inserted into a flaring tool that has cone-shaped dies. A screw tightens the die around the tubing, spreading the end into a flare. Different cone angles exist for different applications.
  • Assembly: Slide the nut over the flared end, then thread into the fitting. Tighten until snug. Flare fittings create high-pressure seals suitable for water systems.
  • Advantages: Reusable, high pressure rating, works well with soft tubing, simple assembly with basic tools.
  • Disadvantages: Requires flaring tool (specialized equipment), limited to tubing with sufficient wall thickness, tubing cannot be reused if flare is damaged.

Improvised Solutions

In off-grid or resource-limited situations, creative jointing methods using available materials can be employed. These methods work best for low-pressure gravity systems.

  • Wrapped Wire + Sealant: Tightly wrap galvanized or stainless steel wire around a pipe joint, then seal with putty, tar, or pitch. The wire reinforces the joint and holds the sealant in place. This method can temporarily repair leaks or create joints when no commercial fittings are available.
  • Carved Wooden Fittings: Hardwood can be carved into pipes and fittings for small-diameter systems. A hole drilled through a wooden block creates a simple T or elbow. Seal with pitch or beeswax. Best for gravity-fed systems only; wood cannot withstand pressure.
  • Leather Gaskets: Vegetable-tanned leather or animal hide can serve as gasket material. Layer leather around a joint and compress tightly. The leather swells slightly when wet, improving the seal. Used traditionally in low-pressure systems.
  • Tar & Rope: For large diameter pipes (logs or clay), wrap tarred rope or cloth tightly around the joint, then coat with pitch or tar. This flexible joint accommodates slight misalignment and thermal expansion.
  • Limitations: Improvised joints are suitable for gravity-fed systems (low pressure). They require regular maintenance and inspection. Best used as temporary solutions until proper fittings are available.

Indoor Plumbing

Interior plumbing distributes water throughout the house for cooking, washing, and sanitation. It also drains wastewater away. Design must account for water pressure, drainage slope, and trap requirements.

Sinks & Basins

Sinks are the fundamental water distribution and collection points in a house. Indoor water use begins and ends with sink drainage.

  • Installation: Sink is installed above a drainage point that directs wastewater outside or to septic system. Supply lines bring water up to the faucet (hot and cold separate). Drainage lines carry away used water.
  • Drainage Slope: All horizontal drain pipes must slope downward toward the exit at a minimum of 1/4 inch per foot (1:48 grade). Proper slope ensures water drains by gravity without pooling in low spots.
  • P-Trap Requirement: The drain line immediately below the sink includes a P-shaped pipe section called a P-trap. This trap is critical for sanitation and must always contain water.
  • How P-Trap Works:
    • Water flowing into the sink runs down through the drain and fills the lowest part of the P-trap, forming a water seal.
    • This water seal prevents sewer gases and methane from rising back up into the house through the drain.
    • When the next load of water enters, it pushes the sealed water ahead, and a new seal forms in the trap.
    • If a trap dries out (unused sink for weeks), the seal breaks and gases can enter the house. Occasionally running water in all drains keeps traps sealed.
  • Trap Depth: The water seal in a P-trap is typically 2-4 inches deep. This depth is sufficient to block gases from a septic tank or sewer while allowing water to flow freely.
  • Maintenance: Inspect P-trap connections periodically for leaks. Hair and debris accumulate in the trap and can be removed by unscrewing the clean-out plug at the trap bottom.

Hot Water Systems

Heated water for bathing and washing is essential for comfort and hygiene. In off-grid systems, heat must come from a wood stove, fireplace, or passive solar collection.

Thermosiphon Hot Water System

A thermosiphon system uses the natural convection of hot water rising to create circulation without a pump. Water heated in a coil near a heat source rises into a storage tank positioned above, creating continuous circulation.

  • Components:
    • Heat Coil: 1/2" or 3/4" copper tubing coiled around the firebox of a wood stove or inserted into a fireplace. Water flows through this coil and absorbs heat.
    • Storage Tank: Insulated tank positioned 3+ feet above the stove. Hot water rises from coil into the tank. Cold return line from tank bottom runs back to stove coil inlet.
    • Circulation Loop: Hot outlet line rises from coil to tank. Cold return line drops from tank to coil inlet. This creates a continuous convection loop.
  • Advantages: No pump required (works passively), reliable, simple design, uses existing heat source, provides hot water year-round if stove is in use.
  • Disadvantages: Slow heating in cool weather, requires stove to be actively burning, limited hot water capacity, risks of scalding if coil bursts inside stove, back-pressure issues if not properly designed.
  • Safety: Install pressure relief valve on storage tank. Tank must be rated for pressure or have open vent. If heating coil ruptures and steam forms, relief valve prevents explosion.
  • Piping Slope: All pipes from coil to tank must slope upward continuously (no dips). Return line from tank can be level or downward. Dips in the hot line trap air, breaking circulation.

Active Hot Water (Pump-Powered)

With a hand pump or small electric pump, the storage tank can be positioned anywhere. Pump pushes hot water from coil to tank, then distributes it on demand.

  • Advantages: Tank can be in any location, faster heat transfer, larger water volume can be heated.
  • Disadvantages: Requires pump energy (hand, wind, or solar), more complex, higher cost.

Passive Solar Collection

In sunny climates, black-painted tubes or flat-plate collectors on the roof can heat water using solar radiation. Water circulates through collectors, absorbing heat for storage.

Bathing Facilities

Bathing comfort and hygiene are important in sustainable living. Off-grid bathing relies on heated water and drainage infrastructure.

  • Shower vs. Bath: Showers use less water (5-10 gallons) than baths (30-50 gallons). For resource-limited situations, showers are preferable. A shower uses 2-3 gallons per minute with a gravity feed from tank at 20-30 feet elevation.
  • Shower Head: Can be as simple as a pipe with holes drilled in the end, or a purchased low-flow head. Height should be 6-7 feet above the floor for comfortable height.
  • Shower Stall: Contained area with sloped floor directing water toward a drain. Walls can be ceramic tile, linoleum, or plastic. All surfaces should slope toward drain at 1:4 to 1:6 grade.
  • Drain Requirements: Shower drain must be large enough to handle 3 GPM flow. 2" diameter drain is minimum. Slope to drain field at 1/4 inch per foot.
  • Water Heating: Heat storage tank with thermosiphon or solar coil. Temperature control at shower can be manual (mix hot and cold valves) or thermostatically controlled (if pump provides pressure).
  • Greywater Use: Shower water is relatively clean (greywater) and can be reused for irrigation or other purposes. See water-purification.html for treatment methods.

Toilet Systems

Sanitation and waste removal are critical in any dwelling. Water-based and waterless systems offer trade-offs in comfort, water requirements, and maintenance.

Water Closet (Flush Toilet)

Traditional flush toilets require reliable water supply and drain infrastructure.

  • Water Supply: 5-gallon tank refills each flush using gravity-fed water. Requires minimum 5 PSI pressure to fill tank quickly.
  • Flush Mechanism: Float valve automatically refills tank when water drops below threshold. Allows 1-2 gallons per flush.
  • Drain: Toilet discharge requires large-diameter pipe (3-4 inches) sloped properly. Connect to septic system or drain field.
  • Requirements: Toilet requires dedicated drain and significant water supply. Not suitable for extremely water-limited situations.
  • Septic System: See drainage section and sanitation.html for complete septic system design.

Composting Toilet

Composting toilets eliminate water use entirely, converting human waste to compost. See sanitation.html for detailed information.

  • Advantages: No water required, nutrient recovery, compact design suitable for remote locations.
  • Disadvantages: Requires active management (carbon addition, moisture control, periodic emptying), less socially acceptable in some areas, longer learning curve.

Aqueducts & Long-Distance Water

Transporting water from distant sources (spring, stream, well) to a dwelling requires pipes or channels designed to maintain flow and pressure over long distances. Several methods accommodate terrain variations.

Open Channel Aqueducts

An open channel (lined ditch or trough) allows gravity flow over distances while remaining simple to construct and maintain.

  • Slope Design: Channel must slope downward toward destination at a grade of 1:200 to 1:500 (0.2% to 0.5% slope). This gentle grade maintains steady flow while minimizing erosion.
  • Calculation: For a channel running 1000 feet, a slope of 1:200 means 5 feet of elevation drop total. Steeper slopes (1:100) increase erosion; gentler slopes (1:1000) collect sediment.
  • Lining Materials:
    • Clay Lining: Compacted clay layer 6-12 inches thick prevents seepage. Works if local clay is available and not excessively permeable.
    • Concrete: Poured or placed concrete creates durable, waterproof channel lasting 50+ years. Requires cement and skilled labor.
    • Plastic Sheeting: Flexible LDPE or HDPE plastic liners (0.5-1 mm thick) over compacted base. Durable if protected from UV and puncture.
    • Stone & Mortar: Stone laid and mortared creates traditional lined channel. More permeable than clay/concrete but culturally appropriate in some regions.
  • Width & Depth: For household use (50-100 GPM), channel 12-18 inches wide and 12 inches deep provides adequate capacity. Depth should be 1/3 to 1/2 the width for optimal flow.
  • Sediment Management: Include settling basin at intake. Periodically flush sediment from channel by opening bypass valve.
  • Advantages: Simple design, easy to inspect and maintain, gravity only (no pumps), tolerates sediment.
  • Disadvantages: Exposed to contamination and algae growth, seepage losses (unless well-lined), freezes in cold climates, requires maintenance from erosion and debris.

Inverted Siphon

An inverted siphon carries water across a valley or over an obstacle by running the pipe downward then upward again. Atmospheric pressure pushes water up the far side.

  • How It Works: Water enters the siphon at high elevation. Gravity pulls it downward through the low section. Atmospheric pressure on the water surface at the inlet pushes water up the far side of the pipe. This works up to about 30 feet of elevation gain on the far side.
  • Limitations: Siphon height (from inlet water surface to top of descending pipe) must be less than 30 feet. Air pockets in the pipe break the siphon. Pressure rating of pipe limits depth the siphon can descend.
  • Pipe Size: Use large diameter pipe (2-4 inches) to keep velocity low and minimize friction loss. Velocity should be 2-3 feet per second for reliable siphon operation.
  • Priming: When first filling siphon, the pipe must be completely filled with water (no air bubbles). Air vents at high points are sometimes needed during initial filling, then plugged.
  • Maintenance: Sediment accumulates in the low point and must be periodically flushed. Install cleanout valve at lowest point accessible for sediment removal.
  • Applications: Ideal for crossing streams, valleys, or terrain obstacles when source is higher than destination by only modest elevation.

Elevated Aqueduct

An aqueduct bridge or elevated channel carries water across terrain without descending into valleys. Romans used this method extensively.

  • Construction: Built from stone arches, wood pilings, or iron trusses to support the channel at constant elevation above ground. The structure must be strong enough to support water weight.
  • Channel Design: Same open channel design as ground-level aqueduct (lined, sloped at 1:200-1:500).
  • Advantages: Maintains constant elevation over obstacles, impressive engineering appearance, long lifespan if well-built.
  • Disadvantages: Extremely expensive and labor-intensive, requires significant engineering knowledge, requires durable building materials, large scale project unsuitable for small systems.
  • Realistic Use: Most off-grid systems cannot justify elevated aqueducts. Inverted siphons or ground channels are more practical.

Settling Basins & Filtration

Long-distance water transport allows time for sediment settling and filtration, improving water quality naturally.

  • Settling Basin: Large shallow basin where water slows down (residence time 4-8 hours). Suspended sediment settles to the bottom. Sand filters at basin outlet remove remaining particles.
  • Sand Filter: Layers of sand (coarse to fine) through which water percolates slowly. Removes turbidity and some bacteria. Must be periodically backflushed to clean.
  • Multiple Basins: Using three settling basins in series (water flows from one to the next) provides superior filtration through time and settling depth variation.

Drainage & Sewage

Removing wastewater from the dwelling is as important as bringing clean water in. Drainage systems must manage both greywater (from sinks, showers) and blackwater (from toilets). See sanitation.html for complete sewage treatment design.

French Drain

A French drain is the simplest subsurface drainage system, suitable for greywater dispersal in suitable soil conditions.

  • Construction: Dig a trench 12-24 inches deep and 12-18 inches wide. Line trench bottom with landscape fabric. Fill with gravel (1-2 inches at bottom, progressing to finer gravel at top). Lay perforated drainage pipe (4-6 inches diameter) in the center. Top with more fabric to separate gravel from soil. Cover with soil.
  • Function: Water seeps through the perforated pipe and gravel, dispersing into surrounding soil. Fabric prevents soil from clogging gravel.
  • Length Required: For 50-100 gallons per day of greywater, a 50-100 foot drain is typically adequate, depending on soil permeability.
  • Soil Requirements: Works best in sandy or loamy soil with good drainage. Clay soil causes water to pool; test soil permeability before installation. Dig 12-inch deep test hole and time how long it takes to drain 12 inches of water. Acceptable permeability: drain within 4-8 hours.
  • Maintenance: Inspect cleanout access points annually. Remove sediment buildup if present.
  • Limitations: Not suitable for blackwater (toilet waste). Nutrient-rich greywater may promote algae growth in drain area.

Septic System Basics

A septic system treats blackwater (toilet waste) and greywater by separating solids and allowing biological decomposition. See sanitation.html for detailed design and maintenance.

  • Septic Tank: Large buried tank (500-5000 gallons) receives all wastewater. Solids settle to the bottom as sludge. Scum (grease/oils) floats to the top. Liquid (effluent) exits to drain field after settling.
  • Drain Field: Network of perforated pipes laid in gravel-filled trenches in well-draining soil. Effluent percolates through gravel and soil, where biological and chemical processes treat it before groundwater contact.
  • Bacteria Action: Anaerobic bacteria in tank break down solids. Aerobic bacteria in drain field soil further decompose remaining organics. This biological treatment happens passively over time.
  • Design Basics: System sizing depends on number of people and daily water use. Typical: 1 person = 50-100 gallons per day. Tank retention time: 3-5 days minimum. Drain field area varies by soil permeability.
  • Maintenance: Do not use bleach or harsh chemicals (kills bacteria). Pump sludge every 3-5 years. Avoid driving over tank or drain field.

Grease Trap

Kitchen wastewater contains grease and oils that can clog drain fields. A grease trap pre-treats kitchen waste before it enters the main drainage system.

  • Design: Simple tank receiving kitchen sink drain. Grease and oils cool and solidify, floating to the top. Clear liquid exits the bottom. Baffles direct flow, maximizing settling time.
  • Sizing: Intercept volume should equal 3 minutes of peak kitchen flow. For typical household (3-5 people), 50-75 gallon capacity is adequate.
  • Maintenance: Grease must be manually removed every 2-4 weeks (more frequently if heavy cooking). Dispose of solidified grease in trash, not down drain.
  • Installation: Place between kitchen sink and septic tank or drain field. Should be accessible for cleanout.
Important: Blackwater (toilet waste) contains pathogens and must be properly treated. Never discharge septic tank effluent directly to surface water. Always use an adequate drain field in appropriate soil. Improper sewage handling contaminates groundwater and causes disease.

Pump Systems

When gravity is insufficient, pumps move water uphill or against pressure. Pump selection depends on lift height, flow rate, and available power source. See well-drilling.html for detailed information on well pumps and deep-water extraction.

Lift Pump (Suction)

A lift pump draws water up from a source lower than the pump location using suction created by reducing pressure inside the pump.

  • Maximum Lift: Limited to about 25 feet maximum vertical lift. This theoretical limit exists because atmospheric pressure can only push water up about 34 feet in a perfect vacuum. Practical maximum is 20-25 feet due to friction losses and air leakage.
  • Types:
    • Hand Pump: Reciprocating piston or diaphragm driven by human power. Can pump 5-15 GPM depending on design and operator effort. No external power required.
    • Ram Pump: Uses flowing water momentum as power source. See gravity-fed section for details.
    • Windmill Pump: Piston pump driven by wind-powered rotor. Suitable in windy locations; low maintenance once installed.
    • Electric Pump: Motorized pump requiring electricity. Most efficient for reliable pumping but dependent on power source.
  • Applications: Suitable for shallow wells (25 ft or less), surface water (streams, ponds), and pressure boost situations.
  • Advantages: Simple, reliable, can be hand-powered, durable with little maintenance.
  • Disadvantages: Limited lift height, requires priming with water to begin operation, less efficient than force pumps at high pressure.

Force Pump

A force pump pushes water against pressure, capable of delivering water to any height (within pump structural limits). Unlike lift pumps, force pumps can be positioned at or below the source level.

  • Pressure Capability: Force pumps deliver pressure-rated discharge. Simple hand-operated force pump produces 50-100 PSI. Animal-powered force pump produces 100-200 PSI. Motorized force pump can exceed 200 PSI.
  • Lift Capacity: Can pump water uphill 100+ feet if pump has sufficient pressure rating. Pressure = (lift height in feet √ó 0.43 PSI/ft) + friction losses.
  • Hand-Operated Force Pump: Uses reciprocating lever or handle to drive piston. Operator effort determines flow rate. Typical: 10-20 GPM with sustained effort.
  • Animal-Powered Force Pump: Ox, horse, or mule-powered pump via gears or cams. Provides continuous, steady pumping without human labor. Efficiency higher than hand-pumping.
  • Electric Motor Force Pump: Motorized centrifugal or displacement pump. Most efficient for high-volume pumping. Requires reliable electricity supply.
  • Applications: Deep wells, high-elevation destinations, pressurized systems, high-volume requirements.
  • Advantages: Unlimited lift height, high pressure possible, compact design, can be powered by various sources.
  • Disadvantages: More complex than lift pumps, requires more maintenance, higher cost.
