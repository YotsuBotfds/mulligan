Why Cordage & Baskets Matter

Cordage and basketry are among humanity's oldest technologies , dating back tens of thousands of years. Archaeological evidence shows basketry artifacts from 12,000 years ago, and rope fibers from well before that. These skills were foundational to civilizations and remain essential for survival and practical living.

Fundamental Uses

  • Carrying & Transport: Baskets, net bags, and pack frames enable humans to move goods. Without baskets, agricultural surplus cannot be gathered or stored.
  • Storage: Baskets protect food from pests and moisture. Coiled baskets can be made waterproof with plant resins or tar.
  • Fishing & Hunting: Nets, fish traps, and snares require cordage and basketry. These provide reliable protein sources.
  • Construction & Structural Support: Rope binds structures, suspends loads, and secures joints. Without cordage, complex structures fail.
  • Agriculture & Processing: Winnowing baskets separate grain from chaff. Rope and cordage tie bundles and hang crops for drying.
  • Clothing & Shelter: Fiber arts create nets, rope, and textiles for clothing. Basketry woven tight can shed water.
Key Insight: A person skilled in cordage and basketry can solve resource problems across farming, hunting, construction, and trade. These are among the most versatile skills in an offline economy.

Processing Plant Fibers

  1. Harvesting: Cut stems or peel bark in growing season. For inner bark, fell small trees or peel from saplings in spring when sap is rising.
  2. Retting (if needed): Soak fibers in water (2-3 weeks) to decompose the pectin binding fibers to woody core. This is critical for stalks like hemp, nettle, and dogbane. Skip for bark and leaves.
  3. Stripping & Beating: After retting, beat fibers against wood to loosen and separate. Roll between hands or strip with fingers. Remove outer bark and woody debris.
  4. Drying: Lay fibers flat in dry, shaded location. Do not sun-dry prolonged‚Äîthis yellows and weakens fibers. Dry quickly with good air circulation.
  5. Storage: Store in cool, dry place protected from insects. Keep in bundles or braids to prevent tangling. Moisture invites mold; excessive heat causes brittleness.

Animal Fibers

Sinew

Tendon and ligament from large animals (elk, deer, moose, bison). Sinew is incredibly strong and flexible when properly prepared. Used for bow strings, fishing line, repairs, and binding.

  • Extraction: Remove from legs and spine during butchering. Clean meat away and dry completely in sun or smoke.
  • Preparation: Soak dried sinew in water to soften. Pound gently with mallet to separate into fibers. Split into desired thickness.
  • Strength: Single sinew fiber is stronger than plant cordage of equal diameter. When twisted into cord, sinew approaches the strength of thin rope.

Rawhide Strips

Raw animal hide cut into strips. Becomes hard when dry, pliable when wet. Used for bindings, snowshoe webbing, and leather cord.

Horsehair & Wool

Horsehair is coarse, strong, water-resistant‚Äîgood for brushes, ropes, and sieve cloth. Wool is warm, felts easily, used in textiles and padding.

Cordage Making Techniques

Two-Ply Twist (Reverse Wrap) ‚Äî The Fundamental Technique

The two-ply reverse wrap is the foundational cordage technique used across cultures for thousands of years. It creates a strong, balanced cord from individual fibers. This is the fastest way to produce usable rope by hand.

Mechanics & Hand Movements

  1. Prepare two bundles of fibers: Take two equal quantities of processed fiber (dried plant strands, bark, or sinew). Keep them roughly the same thickness. Slightly dampen if they are brittle‚Äîa little moisture makes fibers more pliable.
  2. Start the twist: Hold one bundle loosely in your left hand, one in your right, at about shoulder height. The two bundles should be roughly parallel, touching at your center.
  3. Right-hand twist: Roll the right-hand bundle away from your body between your right thumb and fingers, twisting it clockwise. The fibers spiral rightward. Do 2-3 full rotations.
  4. Left-hand wrap: While the right bundle is twisted, bring your left bundle up and over the top of the right bundle (this is the "reverse wrap"). Push it down and forward on the near side, then wrap it under the right bundle, coming back around behind to the far side.
  5. Lock the wrap: This counter-clockwise left-hand wrap is the key: it locks the right-hand twist in place. The two opposing spirals create a balanced, non-twisting cord.
  6. Repeat: As the right bundle twists and the left wraps, step backward slowly or let the cord hang. The twisting motion travels down the length. Maintain consistent tension.
  7. Keep fibers bundled: As fibers in each bundle are consumed, splice new fibers into that bundle (see Splicing Fibers below) to maintain length and thickness.
  8. Finish the cord: When you reach the desired length, tie off the ends with simple overhand knots, or fuse by slightly dampening and rolling between palms to bond fibers.
Tip: Practice with fairly long fibers (8-12 inches). As you master the motion, you'll develop rhythm and speed. One experienced person can produce 100+ feet of usable cordage per hour using this technique.

Three-Ply Braiding

Three-ply cordage is stronger and less prone to tangling than two-ply. It requires three bundles instead of two.

Method

  1. Prepare three equal fiber bundles.
  2. Hold one bundle in each hand, and one in your mouth or have an assistant hold it.
  3. Cross the right bundle over the center, then the left over the new center. Repeat: right, left, right, left...
  4. Alternatively, secure the three bundles to a fixed point (tree, post) and braid them while walking backward, maintaining even tension.
  5. Three-ply braid is simpler to learn than reverse wrap but slower to produce. Use for specialty cordage or when assistance is available.

Splicing Fibers for Continuous Length

When fibers in your bundle run out during twisting, splice in new fibers to extend your cord seamlessly.

Splicing Technique

  1. Identify the exhausted fibers: As you twist, one bundle will begin to thin. Stop and add new fibers.
  2. Overlap new fibers: Cut or tear 4-6 new fiber strands. Place them alongside the old, overlapping by 3-4 inches.
  3. Wrap the joint: Use a thin scrap of twine to wrap tightly around the overlap zone 3-4 times. This binds old and new together.
  4. Continue twisting: The wrapped joint will pass through your hands. The twist and wrap make the splice nearly as strong as the rest of the cord.
  5. Practice makes perfect: The splice will feel rough initially but becomes invisible as you twist. Thin cordage makes splicing easier than thick.

Strength Testing & Rule of Thumb

Testing cordage strength is critical before trusting it with loads. Use this formula for estimation:

Cord Strength = Fiber Strength √ó Ply Count √ó 0.7

The 0.7 factor accounts for friction loss, knot weakness, and splicing inefficiency.

Testing Method

  1. Hang a known weight: Tie your cordage to a fixed point (tree branch). Tie a bag or bucket to the other end.
  2. Add weight incrementally: Fill the bag with sand or rocks, adding weight slowly. Mark or note each increment.
  3. Record breaking point: When the cord breaks, you have its actual breaking strength.
  4. Safety factor: Never load cord above 1/5 its breaking strength for dynamic loads (suspension, pulling). Use 1/3 for static loads.
  5. Document results: Keep notes on cordage type, fiber, ply count, and tested strength. This builds knowledge for future projects.
Safety: A cord that breaks unexpectedly can snap back with force. Perform testing in a clear area away from the face and body.

Rope from Cordage: The Rope Walk

Once you can produce cordage, you can produce rope by combining multiple cordage strands. A rope walk (or ropeyard) is a long, straight space where rope is laid up (twisted) by combining cords.

Basic Rope Making

  1. Prepare 3-4 strands of cordage, each the length you want for your finished rope (rope shrinks ~10% in lay-up).
  2. Secure the three cordage strands to a fixed point (a stake, tree, or person holding them).
  3. Attach the three strands to a simple hand-crank or spindle device (see Rope Walk Construction below).
  4. Turn the crank, which twists the three cordage strands together. This is the same reverse-wrap principle but on a larger scale.
  5. As you turn the crank, walk backward slowly, keeping tension even on all three strands.
  6. The crank does the twisting; your job is to maintain even tension and feed the strands smoothly into the lay.
  7. When finished, the rope will have a slight curl or "lay"‚Äîthis is normal and expected.

Rope Walk Construction

A rope walk can be as simple as a long, straight path with a rotating spindle. Here's the basic setup:

  • Fixed end: A sturdy post or stake where the three cordage strands are secured.
  • Rotating spindle: A hand-crank device with three hooks or eyes, one for each cordage strand. The crank can be turned by one person.
  • Space: At least 30-50 feet of clear, straight ground. Longer rope walks allow for longer rope production in a single lay-up.
  • Simple spindle: A wooden frame with an axle and hand crank. Three eyebolts or hooks around the circumference hold the cords. As the crank turns, all three cords twist together.
  • Tension device (optional): A weight or spring at the fixed end maintains tension as the rope shortens during lay-up.
Efficiency Note: A basic rope walk allows one person to produce rope 3-4 times faster than hand-laying. Two or more people (one at the spindle, one at the fixed end for tension) can work faster and produce longer, more uniform rope.

Basketry Techniques

Overview

There are four primary basketry methods. Each produces different basket types and strengths.

Coiled Basketry

Coiled basketry is the oldest method, dating back 12,000+ years. A rope or bundle of fibers forms a continuous spiral. A second fiber (often thin, flexible material) wraps around and binds each coil to the previous one.

Structure

  • Foundation: A rope or bundle of plant fibers (straw, grass, plant stems) forms the core. This is the "coil."
  • Wrapping: A binding fiber (thin bark, rush, or split material) wraps tightly around each coil and catches the coil below, binding them together.
  • Starting: Coil the foundation fiber into a flat spiral (like a rope on the ground). Use the wrapping fiber to bind each loop to the previous one.
  • Building up: After the base is several inches wide, start angling the coils upward to form bowl walls.
  • Finishing: When the basket reaches desired height, trim the foundation and wrap the edge securely.

Advantages

  • Watertight: Coiled basketry can be sealed with plant resin or tar to hold liquids.
  • Flexible size: Can be made any size by simply extending the coil.
  • Durable: The multiple wrap points distribute stress. Coiled baskets last decades with care.
  • Beautiful: Pattern variations in wrapping create visual texture.

Plaited (Woven) Basketry

Plaited basketry uses flat materials woven in an over-under pattern. It's fast and works well with bark strips, rush, or yucca fibers.

Method

  1. Warp: Lay flat strips parallel to form the foundation (these are the warp).
  2. Weft: Weave another set of strips over and under the warp at right angles (these are the weft).
  3. Pattern: Basic plaiting is "over one, under one." Variations create diagonal or complex patterns.
  4. Tension: Pull each weft row tight to compress the weave. Loose plaiting produces floppy baskets.
  5. Edges: Bend the warp strips up at the edges to form sides. Continue weaving to close sides and form the basket.

Advantages

  • Fast‚Äîmuch quicker than coiling.
  • Works with stiff materials (ash strips, split bamboo).
  • Clean, geometric appearance.

Twined Basketry

Twined basketry combines warp and weft, but instead of alternating over/under, two weft strands twist around each warp element. This is stronger than plaiting and produces a tighter fabric.

Method

  1. Set up warp: Arrange vertical warp strips or fibers in a circle (for baskets) or parallel (for baskets with flat base).
  2. Two-weft twist: Use two long flexible fibers. Cross them in front of each warp element. The two fibers swap sides with each warp: left fiber goes to right, right goes to left. This creates a twist.
  3. Tightness: Pull the twist firmly to compress the weave. The twisted fibers lock around the warp, holding very firmly.
  4. Rows: Move upward, twining row after row. Each row binds the warp securely.
  5. Finish: Bend warp over and tuck into the top rows, or finish with a woven band.

Advantages

  • Very strong‚Äîthe twist locks the weave.
  • Can use thin, round materials (vines, roots).
  • Flexible basket walls (less rigid than plaiting).
  • Used by many cultures for fish traps and storage baskets.

Wicker Weaving (Round Rods)

Wicker uses round flexible rods (willow, hazel, cane) in a simple over-under weave, similar to plaiting but with round materials.

Method

  1. Frame: Form a frame of stouter rods (warp rods) arranged radially (for round baskets) or parallel (for rectangular).
  2. Weaving: Weave finer, more flexible rods through the frame, alternating over and under.
  3. Height: Build upward, row by row. The rods compress and conform to the frame shape.
  4. Finish: Bend the top warp rods over and weave them back down to form a rim.

Advantages

  • Professional appearance‚Äîthis is the style of commercial wicker furniture.
  • Sturdy and lasts decades.
  • Works well for decorative and functional baskets.

Essential Basket Projects

Gathering Basket

An open, sturdy basket for collecting wild foods, plant materials, or firewood.

  • Size: 12-18 inches diameter, 8-12 inches deep.
  • Material: Twined or plaited with round or flat materials. Willow rods or ash strips work well.
  • Features: Open top (no lid), reinforced rim, sturdy handles or straps for carrying.
  • Advantage: Multiple baskets allow division of harvested materials by type.

Storage Basket with Lid

A sealed container for dry goods, seeds, or preserved foods. Protects contents from pests and moisture.

  • Size: 12-24 inches diameter, 10-18 inches tall.
  • Material: Coiled basketry provides excellent watertight seal.
  • Lid construction: Weave a flat lid with overlapping sides to seal the opening. Optional: line with cloth or plant resin.
  • Sealing: Coat interior with animal fat, beeswax, or plant resin to prevent moisture absorption and rodent penetration.
  • Organization: Multiple sizes allow hierarchical storage (grain in large basket, seeds in smaller).

Fish Trap / Creel

A conical or funnel-shaped trap for catching fish. Fish enter a narrow opening and cannot easily escape.

  • Design: Twined basketry in a funnel shape, 18-30 inches long, 12-18 inches diameter at opening.
  • Entrance: A narrow funnel narrows to a 1-2 inch opening in the lower section.
  • Bait chamber: A separate compartment for bait suspended inside the trap.
  • Material: Willow, hazel, or split bamboo for durability in water.
  • Maintenance: Check traps daily. Soak periodically to prevent drying and cracking.

Pack Basket (Burden Basket)

A large, rigid basket for carrying heavy loads on the back or strapped to a frame.

  • Size: 18-24 inches square, 18-24 inches tall.
  • Material: Stout willow rods or ash strips in wicker weave. Rigid materials essential.
  • Straps: Woven handles or leather straps for wearing on the back.
  • Capacity: Can carry 30-60 pounds depending on construction and user strength.
  • Versatility: Pack baskets serve as backpacks, cargo containers, and storage in the home.

Winnowing Basket

A shallow, wide, flat-bottomed basket for separating grain from chaff by tossing and wind.

  • Size: 24-36 inches diameter, 4-6 inches deep.
  • Material: Plaited or twined with thin, flexible materials. Woven very tightly.
  • Design: Slightly rounded or tilted to allow grain to roll toward the user.
  • Method: Fill basket with grain and chaff. Toss upward so chaff blows away in breeze while grain falls back.
  • Construction: Must be sturdy enough to withstand repeated tossing and impact with grain.

Bee Skep

A traditional dome-shaped hive made by coiling straw. Modern beekeepers still use skeps in some regions.

  • Size: 14-18 inches diameter, 16-20 inches tall (dome shape).
  • Material: Long straw (wheat, rye, or grass) coiled very tightly and bound with thin twine or raffia.
  • Construction: Start with flat base coil, then angle upward to form domed roof. Finish with a peaked top.
  • Entrance: A small hole near the base for bee access. Can be blocked with mud or cork in winter.
  • Note: Skeps are labor-intensive to construct and require skill. Start with simpler baskets first.

Net Making

Mesh Knot (Net Knot)

The fundamental knot for net-making. It creates interlocking loops that form a mesh fabric.

The Sheet Bend (Half Hitch Variant)

  1. Use a net needle (see below) loaded with cordage.
  2. Form a loop with one running line.
  3. Pass the needle through the loop and wrap around both the loop and the running line.
  4. Pull tight‚Äîthe knot locks the two lines together.
  5. Repeat to form rows of interlocking loops.

Creating Uniform Mesh

A gauge stick (wooden dowel) determines mesh size. All knots are tied around the gauge stick, ensuring consistent spacing.

  • Gauge stick: A wooden rod, 1/2 to 2 inches wide, depending on desired mesh size.
  • Loading cordage: Thread cordage around the gauge stick. Each loop represents one mesh opening.
  • Tying: Tie knots at each loop, always maintaining the gauge stick to keep spacing consistent.

Net Needle & Shuttle

A net needle is a simple tool for loading, holding, and tensioning cordage during net knitting.

Construction

  • Shape: Flat, elongated shuttle about 3-6 inches long and 1-1.5 inches wide.
  • Material: Wood (maple, birch) or bone is traditional. The needle must be smooth to avoid snagging cordage.
  • Notches: Two notches or grooves on the ends hold the cordage securely during loading and knitting.
  • Loading: Wind cordage around the needle in a figure-eight pattern. This keeps cordage under tension and prevents tangling.

Using the Needle

  1. Load cordage on the needle using figure-eight wrapping.
  2. Hold the gauge stick in your left hand, vertical.
  3. Pass the needle and cordage through each loop on the gauge stick.
  4. Tie the mesh knot with cordage from the needle.
  5. Slide the knot down the gauge stick and repeat.
  6. As cordage deploys, reload the needle when needed.

Net Types & Applications

Fishing Gill Net

A large, rectangular net with mesh openings sized to catch specific fish species. The fish's gills snag in the mesh.

  • Size: 20-50 feet long, 6-10 feet tall.
  • Mesh: Typically 1-3 inches depending on target fish.
  • Deployment: Suspended vertically in water using floats on top and weights on bottom.
  • Time: Set gill nets in evening, check at dawn.

Cast Net

A weighted conical net thrown by hand. Sinks and encloses fish, then is drawn up.

  • Size: 6-12 feet diameter when thrown.
  • Design: Mesh narrows from wide opening to funnel base. Weights distributed around circumference.
  • Skill: Requires practice to throw properly, but very effective for schooling fish.
  • Advantage: Portable, reusable, works in shallow and deep water.

Dip Net

A small net on a handle for scooping individual fish or dipping in shallow water.

  • Size: 1-3 feet diameter mesh.
  • Handle: 4-8 feet long, wood or bamboo.
  • Use: Scooping spawning fish, harvesting trapped fish in temporary weirs, hand-gathering.

Hammock

A netted bed suspended between two trees or posts. Also serves for storage and gear suspension.

  • Size: 4-6 feet long, 2-3 feet wide.
  • Mesh: 1-2 inches for comfort.
  • Rigging: Suspended with cordage. Can be made completely portable.

Seine Net

A large team-fishing net. Two lines are anchored and pulled from opposite banks or shores to encircle fish.

  • Size: 30-100+ feet long, 6-12 feet deep.
  • Method: Requires multiple people. One line is anchored, the other is pulled in a semi-circle to encircle fish, then both lines are drawn closed.
  • Efficiency: Can catch large numbers in a single deployment.

Wicker & Furniture

Willow Cultivation & Coppicing

Coppicing is the practice of cutting a willow tree at the base so it regrows multiple shoots annually. A coppiced willow can provide harvestable rods for decades or centuries.

Establishing a Coppice Stool

  1. Young willow: Plant or select a willow tree, 2-4 inches diameter.
  2. First cut: In late winter (February-March), cut the entire tree to within 2-3 inches of the ground.
  3. Regrowth: The stool (cut base) produces 5-15 new shoots that spring. These are called "withes."
  4. First season: Let the shoots grow unimpeded. They will reach 4-8 feet by autumn.
  5. Annual harvest: Each late winter, cut the shoots. The stool produces new shoots the next spring.
  6. Longevity: A well-maintained coppice stool can produce for 100+ years. Some European stools are medieval.

Best Willow Species

  • Salix alba (White Willow): Large, fast-growing, produces long straight rods. European standard.
  • Salix viminalis (Basket Willow): Specifically bred for basketry. Highly flexible, fine diameter.
  • Salix purpurea (Purple Willow): Smaller rods, very pliable. Good for fine work.
  • Local species: Many willows work. Test locally available species for flexibility and strength.

Willow Rod Harvesting & Preparation

Timing

Harvest withes (rods) in late winter before spring growth starts. The rods are fully mature, have less water content, and will dry well.

Stripping & Drying

  1. Fresh harvest: Cut rods are green (full of water). They can be used fresh for flexible basketry.
  2. Drying: Stand rods upright in bundles in a dry, shaded location (barn, shed). Allow 4-8 weeks to dry completely.
  3. Peeling (optional): For decorative pale rods, peel the bark after light soaking. This is labor-intensive.
  4. Storage: Dried rods keep 1-2 years in dry conditions. Bundle tightly to prevent warping.
  5. Reconditioning: To use dried rods, soak in water for several hours to soften and restore flexibility.

Chair Construction

Willow or hazel rods provide the frame for simple chairs. This is an intermediate basketry project.

Basic Design

  • Frame: Four sturdy legs, connected by horizontal rods forming a square seat base and a back frame.
  • Seat weave: Use flexible rods to weave the seat in a twill or simple over-under pattern.
  • Back: Similar weave forms the backrest.
  • Joinery: Rods are lashed or woven together at junctions. Cordage binding adds strength.
  • Height: Typically 16-18 inches from floor to seat, with back rising 12-18 inches above seat.

Fencing & Hurdles

Hurdles are portable woven fence panels used to corral animals, protect crops, or divide space.

Construction

  1. Frame: Stout rods form a rectangular frame, typically 4-6 feet wide and 3-4 feet tall.
  2. Weave: Flexible rods are woven through the frame in an over-under pattern, creating a lattice.
  3. Durability: Well-made hurdles last 10-20 years. Damage is easily repaired by re-weaving sections.
  4. Portability: Hurdles can be carried by two people and quickly arranged and rearranged.
  5. Multiple uses: Pens for animals, wind breaks, snow fencing, drying racks for harvested crops.

Lobster & Crab Pots

Basket-like traps for crustaceans. Similar to fish traps but designed for specific behaviors of crabs and lobsters.

Design

  • Shape: Often rounded or barrel-shaped, with funnel entrances leading inward.
  • Bait chamber: Central compartment holds bait that attracts crustaceans.
  • Material: Willow or hazel rods, or hardwood slats. Must withstand saltwater and repeated hauling.
  • Buoy line: Rope with floating buoy marks the pot location and allows hauling.
  • Maintenance: Scrape and repair regularly. Soaking prevents wood from drying and becoming brittle.

Knot Integration

This guide covers the fundamental knots specific to basketry, cordage, and netting. However, cordage work also requires knowledge of general-purpose knots for securing loads, rigging, and repairs.

Essential Knots for Cordage Work

Refer to knots-rigging.html for detailed instructions on:

  • Square Knot: Joining two cordage ends of equal diameter.
  • Reef Knot: Secure binding that doesn't slip.
  • Clove Hitch: Securing cordage to posts or rods.
  • Bowline: Creating a fixed loop at the end of cordage.
  • Timber Hitch: Securing bundles and loads.
  • Taut-line Hitch: Adjustable loop for tensioning.
  • Splicing: Joining rope ends permanently for strength and appearance.

Net-Specific Knots

Two knots dominate net-making:

  • Mesh Knot (Sheet Bend variant): The primary net knot. Creates interlocking loops. Described in detail in Section 6 (Net Making).
  • Lark's Head Knot: Attaches cordage to floats or weights on the net's top and bottom edges. Simple loop-and-pull knot.

Cordage-to-Basket Lashing

When binding basket components with cordage, use a simple figure-eight lashing :

  1. Wrap cordage tightly around the joint point (where two rods meet).
  2. Cross the cordage in an X pattern four times.
  3. Pull tight and finish with a square knot.
  4. Trim excess and tuck the knot into the basketwork so it doesn't snag.
Practice Tip: Master the basic knots with larger cordage first (1/4 to 1/2 inch diameter). Once comfortable, practice with smaller material for fine work.

Material Preparation Calendar

Timing is critical for harvesting basketry and cordage materials. Different fibers have optimal harvest windows based on growth stage and water content.

SPRING (March - May)
  • Inner bark (bass, elm, willow, cedar): Peak harvest window. Sap is flowing; bark separates easily from wood. Fell trees or peel from saplings. Process immediately while fresh.
  • Young willow withes: New growth is tender and pliable. Can be used fresh without drying.
  • Nettle (before flowering): Begin retting fresh nettle stalks. Processing takes 2-3 weeks.
  • Cattail shoots: Young shoots are tender. Harvest for immediate use or dry for storage.
EARLY SUMMER (June - July)
  • Flax & hemp: Harvest when flowers begin to fade but seeds are not yet hard. Rett for 1-2 weeks.
  • Dogbane & milkweed: Plants are fully grown. Cut stalks and begin retting.
  • Cattail leaves: Harvest mature leaves. Still flexible and good color. Dry for basket weaving.
  • Willow rods (green harvest): Can harvest shoots mid-summer for immediate use. They're still flexible.
  • Sinew collection: Hunt or butcher to collect fresh sinew. Process and dry immediately.
LATE SUMMER / EARLY FALL (August - September)
  • Yucca fibers: Mature leaves are stiff and strong. Harvest and process (fibers naturally separate).
  • Hazel & willow rods: Second growth rods are maturing. Can harvest but still somewhat green.
  • Drying season: This is ideal time to finish drying all retting fibers in warm, dry conditions.
  • Storage preparation: Bundle and store all dried materials for winter and spring use.
FALL / EARLY WINTER (October - November)
  • Willow coppice rods: Rods are mature and hard. Perfect for weaving furniture and large baskets. Harvest before frost.
  • Hazel rods: Similar to willow. Best harvested after first frost for maximum strength.
  • Straw (for skeps & coiling): Harvest wheat, rye, or other grain straw after threshing. Long, intact straw is ideal.
  • Cane & bamboo: In temperate zones, trim and bundle for drying.
WINTER (December - February)
  • Willow coppice cutting: Late winter (February-March) is the optimal time to cut coppice stools. Cut before spring growth starts.
  • Planning & preparation: Process stored materials. Prepare new plantings of basket willow or other fiber sources.
  • Tool making & repair: Make net needles, gauge sticks, and basketry forms during slow season.
  • Fiber splitting & cleaning: Indoor work with dried materials. Peel bark, split ash strips, prepare cordage bundles.
Planning Tip: Manage multiple material sources so some are always in harvest, drying, or storage phase. This ensures year-round supply for basketry and cordage work.
