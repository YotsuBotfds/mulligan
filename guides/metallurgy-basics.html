Atomic Structure and Bonding

Metals are elements with loosely-bound outer electrons. Metallic bonding: electrons delocalized in "electron sea," metal atoms as cations in lattice. This electron mobility enables: (1) electrical/thermal conductivity (electrons flow), (2) malleability (atoms can shift positions without breaking bonds), (3) ductility (large deformation before fracture).

Strength depends on: (1) atomic bonding strength (varies by metal), (2) crystal defects (dislocations impede movement), (3) grain size (smaller grains more strength but less ductility), (4) alloy composition (alloying atoms modify bonding).

Crystal Structure

Metals solidify into crystalline lattices. Common types:

Body-Centered Cubic (BCC)

Iron (below 912°C), chromium, tungsten. Atom at cube center + 8 at corners. Coordination number 8. Properties: high strength, brittle at low temperature, ductile at high temp. Lattice parameter ~2.9Å for iron.

Face-Centered Cubic (FCC)

Iron (912-1394°C, called austenite), copper, aluminum, nickel. Atoms at cube corners + face centers. Coordination 12. Properties: ductile, good toughness, lower melting point. High solubility for carbon in FCC iron (why austenite can dissolve 2% carbon while ferrite holds <0.02%).

Hexagonal Close Packed (HCP)

Magnesium, zinc, titanium. Atoms in hexagonal layers. Coordination 12. Properties: variable - some HCP metals brittle, others ductile depending on slip planes.

Lattice defects: (1) point defects (vacancies, substitutional atoms), (2) line defects (dislocations - responsible for plastic deformation), (3) planar defects (grain boundaries). Dislocation motion enables work hardening (cold work strengthens metal by creating many dislocations).

Carbon solubility: Austenite dissolves 2.06% C at 1130°C. Ferrite dissolves only 0.022% C at room temp. This huge difference drives martensitic transformation: austenite (carbon dissolved) cooled rapidly → martensite (strained lattice, carbon forced into ferrite structure = very hard but brittle).

Annealing and Recrystallization

Annealing removes work hardening (cold work) and internal stress by slow heating and cooling.

Process

  1. Heat metal to 600-700°C (below critical point Ac1 for steel), hold 30-60 minutes
  2. Cool slowly (furnace cool, hours) to room temperature

At elevated temperature, atoms have energy to move, dislocations annihilate, new strain-free grains form. Result: soft, ductile metal, suitable for further forming.

Recrystallization Temperature

Depends on metal: iron ~600°C, copper ~250°C, aluminum ~150°C. Absolute rule: recrystallization temperature ≈ 0.4 × melting temperature (Kelvin). Below this temp, work hardening retained; above this temp, metal progressively anneals.

Grain Growth Control

Excessive heat (above 800°C) causes grain growth (small grains merge into larger ones). Large grains = weak, brittle. Optimal annealing: 650-750°C for steel, long enough for recrystallization, short duration to prevent grain growth.

Quenching and Hardening

Quenching cools austenite rapidly, suppressing diffusion. Carbon atoms "locked" in iron lattice as martensite (highly strained, very hard).

Mechanism

At 912°C, steel is soft (austenite). Upon rapid cooling, austenite cannot transform to equilibrium ferrite + cementide (transformation requires atomic diffusion, which is slow at low temperature). Instead, FCC lattice (austenite) deforms to BCC structure (martensite) without carbon leaving. Result: highly strained BCC lattice with dissolved carbon = martensite, extremely hard (62-65 HRC typical).

Trade-off: water cools fastest (best hardness) but fast cooling causes internal stress, uneven cooling (surface vs. interior), risk of cracking. Oil better compromise - still hard, less distortion. Air slowest, minimal distortion but lower hardness.

Practical Quenching

Small items (tools <2cm thick): water quench acceptable. Larger items (bars >5cm): oil quench (prevents cracking). Monitoring: color of steel during cooling - bright red/orange = still hot, straw color = starting to cool. Upon reaching final color, immediately quench.

Tempering for Toughness

Martensite is extremely hard but brittle. Tempering reheats quenched steel to lower temperature (200-400°C), relaxing internal stress and improving toughness. Partial decomposition of martensite occurs - some carbon precipitates as cementide particles, reducing hardness slightly but greatly improving toughness.

Hardening mechanism: Alloying atoms substitute for iron in lattice. Larger/smaller atoms create lattice strain, impeding dislocation movement = increased hardness. Some elements form intermetallic compounds (Fe-V, Fe-Mo) contributing to hardening.

Testing Methods

Tensile Test

Standard test: pull sample until breaks, measure: (1) yield strength (stress at plastic deformation start), (2) ultimate tensile strength (maximum stress), (3) elongation (ductility - % stretch before break). Equipment: simple mechanical setup with calibrated weight (no electricity needed in survival scenario). Results indicate material quality.

Hardness Test

File test: Harden steel file, attempt to scratch sample. If file skids (doesn't cut), sample is harder than file (>65 HRC typical). If file cuts easily, sample softer.

Sclerometer: Spring-loaded hardness tester (if available). Records rebound height of falling ball - higher rebound = harder material. Correlates to Rockwell hardness.

Bend Test

Heat sample to different temperatures, quench, then attempt to bend. Harder sample cracks upon bending (brittle). Properly tempered sample bends without breaking. Bend angle before fracture correlates to toughness.

Impact Test

Drop weight from standard height onto sample. Hardened brittle steel shatters; properly tempered steel dents/bends. Ductile iron/wrought iron doesn't break. Qualitative assessment of impact resistance.

Failure Modes and Prevention

Brittle Fracture

Cause: Over-hardened (not tempered enough), low temperature, high stress concentration. Martensite is brittle - needs tempering. Cold weather increases brittleness (atoms have less thermal motion).

Prevention: Temper to appropriate hardness. Avoid sharp corners (stress concentration points). Keep tools warm in cold climates. Avoid impact loading of hardened tools.

Fatigue Failure

Cause: Repeated cycling of stress (even below yield strength) causes microscopic crack growth. Eventually crack reaches critical size, sudden failure.

Prevention: Keep stress below fatigue limit (typically 50% of yield strength for steel). Remove surface defects (stress concentrators). Smooth transitions in cross-section. Case hardening (hardened surface layer, tough interior) resists fatigue well.

Corrosion

Cause: Iron oxidizes in wet/salt environment: 4Fe + 3O₂ + 6H₂O → 4Fe(OH)₃ (rust). Rust expands, damages coating, exposes fresh metal.

Prevention: Protective coating (oil, paint, chrome plating). Stainless steel (>12% chromium) resists corrosion (chromium oxide layer protects). Proper storage (dry environment). Rust removal - remove oxide mechanically (wire brush, sandblasting) and recoat immediately.
