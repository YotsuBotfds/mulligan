Plain Bearings and Bushings

The simplest bearing type: a shaft rotating inside a stationary hole. Radial load capacity depends on bearing surface area and material friction properties.

Design Considerations

  • Length-to-diameter ratio: L/D of 0.5-2.0 typical; longer bearings reduce side loading
  • Surface finish: Smooth surfaces (Ra <0.8 Œºm) reduce friction and wear
  • Clearance: Small clearance (0.5-2 mm for 50 mm diameter) allows oil film formation
  • Cooling: High-speed operation generates heat; requires circulation or natural dissipation

DIY Plain Bearing Construction

  1. Start with a steel or aluminum outer tube (inner diameter =shaft diameter + clearance)
  2. Bore the inner surface smooth with a lathe; finish to <0.8 Œºm surface finish
  3. Insert a bronze or babbit liner (if using a composite bearing)
  4. Machine an oil grooves on the inner surface (spiral or axial grooves) to distribute lubricant
  5. Drill oil inlet holes and outlet holes to allow lubrication flow
  6. Test fit with the shaft; verify smooth rotation with minimal drag

Babbit Bearing Production

Pouring a babbit liner into a bearing shell:

  1. Prepare a bearing mold: Split outer shell with a mold core (shaft) inserted
  2. Heat the outer shell to 150-200¬∞C to prevent rapid cooling (which causes cracks)
  3. Heat babbit ingot to 260-290¬∞C until molten (do not exceed 315¬∞C or tin will oxidize)
  4. Pour molten babbit into the mold cavity slowly to avoid air bubbles
  5. Cool slowly; allow 8-12 hours for complete cooling
  6. Remove the mold core and finish the bearing surface if needed
  7. The bearing is now ready for oil lubrication and use

Ball Bearing Manufacturing Essentials

Ball bearing assembly: inner race, outer race, balls, and retaining cage

Production Steps

  • Forge or cast steel balls (high-carbon steel AISI 52100)
  • Spherical grinding to precise diameter (¬±0.001 inch tolerance)
  • Hardening by quenching and tempering (Rc 58-65)
  • Lapping to final diameter and surface finish (Ra <0.2 Œºm)
  • Manufacture races by turning and grinding
  • Assemble cage, insert balls, and press races together

Steel Ball Grinding and Hardening

Grinding Procedure

  1. Start with forged or cast steel ball blanks (slightly oversized, ~10% larger than final size)
  2. Place balls in a spherical grinding machine with a rotating grinding wheel (silicon carbide or diamond)
  3. Feed balls against the wheel gradually to remove material uniformly
  4. Cool with cutting fluid (water-soluble oil or synthetic)
  5. Check diameter periodically with precision calipers; final diameter target ¬±0.0005 inch
  6. Remove balls when size achieved; allow to cool before next process step

Hardening and Tempering

Quenching procedure:

  1. Heat balls to 800-850¬∞C (bright cherry red) in a furnace
  2. Soak for 15-20 minutes to ensure uniform temperature throughout
  3. Quench rapidly in cold water or oil; this creates a hard martensite structure
  4. Ball becomes very hard but brittle; proceed to tempering

Tempering (stress relief):

  1. Reheat quenched balls to 180-250¬∞C (pale yellow color) in oven
  2. Soak for 1-2 hours; this relieves internal stresses introduced during quenching
  3. Cool slowly in air; final hardness Rc 58-65
  4. Final hardness balances high strength (from quenching) with low brittleness (from tempering)

Inner and Outer Race Manufacturing

Turning

  1. Start with cylindrical steel stock (AISI 52100, 80-100 mm diameter typical)
  2. Mount in lathe chuck; turn OD to slightly oversized
  3. If making outer race: bore hole then turn OD grooves; if inner race: turn OD grooves on solid bar
  4. Create ball-seating grooves: radius-shaped cavity where balls rest (radius = 5-10% larger than ball radius)
  5. Surface finish is critical: Ra <0.4 Œºm for best rolling performance

Grinding the Grooves

  1. Use a grinding wheel shaped to the groove profile
  2. Feed the wheel carefully into the race groove
  3. Remove 0.2-0.5 mm of material to achieve desired groove shape and finish
  4. Cool with cutting fluid; inspect with profile gauge for accuracy

Hardening Races

Races are hardened identically to balls: quench at 800-850¬∞C, temper at 180-250¬∞C to achieve Rc 58-65.

Lubrication Theory and Film Formation

Hydrodynamic (Thick Film) Lubrication

At high speeds or with viscous oils, a complete oil film separates surfaces:

  • Film thickness > 1 micrometer; surfaces do not touch
  • Friction from oil viscosity, not metal-to-metal sliding
  • No wear occurs (ideal condition)
  • Pressure builds up in converging oil wedge; supports load

Boundary Lubrication

At low speeds, with thin lubricants, or high loads, metal contact occurs:

  • Film thickness <0.1 micrometer; surfaces separated by molecular layers only
  • Friction depends on surface chemistry (adsorbed oil molecules)
  • Wear occurs; life of bearing is limited
  • Antiwear additives reduce friction and prolong life

Bearing Operating Regimes

  • Rolling bearings (high speed, low viscosity): Hydrodynamic film; load carried by pressure in oil wedges between balls and races
  • Plain bearings (low speed, high viscosity): Boundary-to-mixed lubrication; high friction, requires careful design and monitoring
  • Water-lubricated bearings (very low friction): Hydrodynamic, but water has low viscosity; requires large clearances and specific geometry

Lubricant Production from Accessible Materials

Animal Fat Rendering

Traditional method still effective for producing basic lubricant:

  1. Render animal fat (beef tallow, lard) by heating to 120-150¬∞C with slow stirring
  2. Heat melts fat and separates water/protein (rendered fat is clear)
  3. Cool and filter through cheesecloth; solid wax/fat separates from liquid oil
  4. Use clear rendered oil as bearing lubricant (works for low-speed bearings)
  5. Viscosity at 40¬∞C: ~150-500 cSt depending on animal and processing

Making Grease from Tallow + Lime

Thickened lubricant for slow-speed bearings:

  1. Mix rendered tallow (90% by weight) with calcium hydroxide (slaked lime, 10%)
  2. Heat mixture to 80-100¬∞C with vigorous stirring for 30-60 minutes
  3. The lime forms soap-like structure, thickening the oil
  4. Cool to room temperature; resulting grease is suitable for low-speed bearings
  5. Consistency: similar to butter; appropriate for applications where oil leakage must be prevented

Mineral Oil as Lubricant

If access to petroleum refining is available, mineral oil fractions are superior lubricants:

  • ISO VG 32 (32 cSt @ 40¬∞C): Light bearing oil; for high-speed, precision bearings
  • ISO VG 46 (46 cSt @ 40¬∞C): Standard for most industrial bearings
  • ISO VG 100+ (>100 cSt): Heavy oils for slow-speed, heavily-loaded bearings

Fitting and Tolerance Standards

ISO Tolerance Grades

Bearing fits are standardized to ensure proper function:

Fit Type Inner Race Tolerance Outer Race Tolerance Application
Slip fit (loose) g6 G7 Shafts that require easy removal; rotating inner race on stationary shaft
Running fit (normal) k6 H7 Normal operating condition; standard for fixed inner race
Press fit (tight) m6 H6 Stationary inner race; prevents rotation on shaft
Force fit (very tight) p5 H5 Rigidly fixed; requires press to assemble/disassemble

Practical Tolerancing

  • Measure shaft with precision calipers or micrometer to 0.01 mm tolerance
  • Inner race bore should be 0.0-0.2 mm larger than shaft (k6 fit = 0.002-0.008" for 50 mm shaft)
  • Outer race diameter should be 0.1-0.3 mm smaller than housing bore (H7 fit = 0.002-0.010" clearance for 100 mm housing)
  • Align races concentrically using test rotation; if bearing binds, one race is misaligned

Bearing Failure Modes and Prevention

Failure Mode Cause Prevention
Spalling (flaking) Surface fatigue from repeated rolling contact stress Reduce load; increase bearing size; ensure proper lubrication (hydrodynamic film)
Brinelling (denting) Static load or impact crushes balls into races, creating permanent deformation Avoid shock loads; use soft impacts during installation; use preload bearings
Cage failure Cage becomes loose or breaks; balls can skid and overlap Ensure proper cage design; adequate lubrication (reduces ball skidding); monitor bearing for noise
Wear/scoring Boundary lubrication; metal-to-metal contact over time Ensure adequate lubrication; use oils with antiwear additives; increase bearing size if overloaded
Corrosion/rusting Water contamination or poor storage Use sealed bearings; store in dry environment; apply preservative oil if storing long-term

Wooden and Ceramic Bearing Alternatives

Lignum Vitae (Ironwood) Water-Lubricated Bearing

A traditional bearing that requires only water for lubrication, ideal for water-powered machinery:

  • Material: Lignum vitae wood (Guaiacum officinale), self-lubricating with natural resins
  • Bore a hole through the wood block slightly larger than the shaft
  • Allow water to flow continuously through the bearing (water acts as lubricant)
  • Very low friction under water lubrication; high load capacity despite being wood
  • Replacement interval: 1-2 years under continuous operation

Ceramic Ball Bearings

Experimental alternative with advantages:

  • Silicon nitride (Si‚ÇÉN‚ÇÑ) balls: 40% lighter than steel, lower density creates less centrifugal force
  • Lower friction, cooler running, higher speed capability
  • Extremely hard (harder than steel); resistant to wear and corrosion
  • Disadvantage: Expensive and brittle; prone to catastrophic failure under shock loads
