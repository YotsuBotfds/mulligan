Septic Systems

Self-contained wastewater treatment system combining settling, biological decomposition, and soil filtration.

Septic Tank Function

Wastewater enters tank where solids settle (becoming sludge) and fats float (becoming scum). Bacteria decompose material anaerobically (without oxygen). Clarified liquid flows to drain field. Tank provides 24-48 hour retention time (allows solids to settle). Properly functioning tank removes 30-40% of contaminants.

Septic Tank Sizing

Size based on number of residents and daily wastewater flow. Typical: 4-6 people = 1000-1200 gallon tank. Rule of thumb: 150 gallons per person per day × 3 days retention = tank size. Undersized tank fails rapidly (solids overflow to drain field). Oversized tank unnecessary (high cost with marginal benefit).

Drain Field Design

Trenches 2-3 feet deep, 2-3 feet wide, 50+ feet long total (multiple parallel trenches). Lined with sand and gravel. Perforated pipes distribute effluent into trenches. Soil naturally filters remaining contaminants. Bacteria and protozoa trapped by soil. Chemical contaminants absorbed. Process removes 95%+ of pathogens if functioning properly.

Drain Field Spacing

Minimum 100 feet from water wells. Minimum 50 feet from surface water sources. Locate downhill from drinking water sources. Avoid wet areas, flood zones, rocky sites (prevent proper filtration). Soil percolation test determines suitability: 1 inch drop per hour optimal (faster = risk of poor filtration, slower = system won't drain).

System Failure Signs

  • Toilets backing up or draining slowly
  • Grass growing greener over drain field (excess nutrients)
  • Wet spots or standing water in yard
  • Odors near tank or drain field
  • Multiple toilets draining at different rates

System Maintenance

Pump tank every 3-5 years (removes accumulated sludge). Never use septic tank additives (kill beneficial bacteria). Conserve water (reduces system load). Avoid dumping chemicals, grease, non-degradable materials. If system fails, must replace (expensive - proper design prevents failures).

Constructed Wetlands

Artificial wetland systems provide advanced treatment through plant uptake and biological processes.

Wetland System Design

Wetland consists of shallow basin (1-2 feet deep) filled with substrate (sand, gravel, soil) planted with wetland vegetation (cattails, reeds, bulrushes). Wastewater distributed at one end, collected at other end. Roots and substrate provide large surface area for bacteria and filtering. Completely natural process, no chemicals or electricity required.

Treatment Process

Sediment settles quickly. Bacteria decompose organic material. Plant roots absorb nitrogen and phosphorus (major water pollutants). Substrate filters suspended solids. Pathogens reduced by competition from beneficial organisms and unfavorable pH. Process highly effective if properly designed and maintained.

Capacity and Sizing

Wetland requires 2-5 square feet per person (much larger than septic systems but far superior treatment). For 4-person household: 8-20 square feet minimum (roughly 3x4 to 4x5 feet). Larger wetlands allow greater water retention, better treatment.

Plant Selection

Cattails: most effective, aggressive growers, 4-6 feet tall, excellent nutrient removal. Reeds: similar effectiveness, handle variations better. Bulrushes: good for cooler climates. Native plants preferred (better adapted, self-sustaining). Avoid invasive species.

Maintenance

Remove accumulated sludge every 5-10 years (less frequent than septic systems). Harvest plants annually (removes nutrients). Monitor water level (maintain 6-12 inches depth). Wetland essentially maintenance-free once established. No chemical inputs needed.

Climate Considerations

Cold climates: wetlands still function but bacteria slower (system must be larger). Frozen wetlands partially disrupt function but restart in spring. Very cold climates (below -15°C regularly) may require subsurface flow design (pipes under wetland prevent freezing).

Composting Toilets

Toilets that compost human waste with carbon material (sawdust, peat) into usable compost.

Composting Toilet Types

Waterless design: no water used, waste falls into composting chamber. Separator designs: separate urine from feces (urine reused, feces composted). Continuous designs: waste composted directly in chamber. Batch designs: full chamber sealed for composting (takes weeks), then starts new chamber.

Operation

After each use, add carbon material (sawdust, peat moss, leaves) to cover waste. Carbon provides bulking (creates air spaces) and absorbs moisture and odors. Regular stirring (if accessible) accelerates decomposition. Process takes 6-12 months for complete decomposition (depends on design and conditions).

Composted Product

After complete composting, product resembles dark soil. Does NOT smell. Not suitable for direct food contact (safety concerns despite processing) but excellent for ornamental plants, trees, landscaping. Can be further aged in piles if concerned about pathogen remaining.

Advantages

No water required (ideal for arid regions, areas without water systems). No septic or drain field (saves space, cost). Nutrients recovered (not lost to environment). Portable (can move if needed). Self-contained (functions independently).

Disadvantages

Requires user participation (adding carbon, odor management). Less effective with high-moisture waste (diarrhea overwhelms carbon). Ongoing supply of carbon material required. Final product handling questionable for food crops. Requires large chamber volume (space constraints).

Maintenance

Regular carbon addition (critical). Monitor moisture level (too wet = anaerobic and smelly, too dry = slow decomposition). Manage odors with additional carbon, ventilation fans. Empty/rotate chambers periodically. Final compost removal and disposal.

Greywater Systems

Treating and reusing water from sinks, showers, washing machines (greywater) instead of sending to treatment.

Greywater Sources and Quality

Sinks, showers, washing machines, baths = low-contaminant greywater (suitable for reuse). Toilets = blackwater (NOT greywater, untreatable without advanced systems). Typical greywater contains soap residue, food particles, hair, bacteria. Treatment before reuse removes most contaminants.

Simple Greywater Treatment

  1. Settling tank: allows solids to settle and be removed (24-hour retention)
  2. Sand filter: greywater passes through sand, removing particles and bacteria
  3. Storage tank: treated water stored for irrigation use

Reuse Applications

Toilet flushing: 2-3 gallons per flush (biggest water use in homes). Landscape irrigation: flowers, shrubs, non-edible plants. Car washing: recirculate multiple times before disposal. Emergency water (after boiling) if needed. Most efficient reuse: toilet flushing.

Restrictions

Never use untreated greywater for drinking or food crops. Salt and soap in greywater accumulate in soil (damages plants long-term). Bacteria may survive short treatment (especially important for food crops). Best for non-edible ornamental plants and toilet flushing.

Greywater Plumbing

Separate lines from black water. Direct sink/shower water to greywater tank. Pump or gravity-flow to reuse point. Overflow goes to septic or sewage system. Requires dual plumbing (complex in existing homes, easier in new construction).

System Sizing

Typical household: 50-100 gallons greywater daily. Landscape irrigation needs: 5-20 gallons daily (depending on climate, vegetation). Excess greywater must go somewhere (overflow, drain field, or storage tank for future use).

Pathogen Testing

Fecal coliform count indicates sanitary quality. Raw sewage: 100,000,000+ per 100mL. Safe discharge: less than 200 per 100mL (EPA standard). Simple test strips available for sanitary facilities. Professional lab testing more accurate but expensive.

DIY Testing Methods

Clarity test: filter sample, compare to clean water (cloudiness indicates solids). Odor test: properly treated effluent has no fecal smell (smell indicates incomplete treatment). Simple coliform test kits available (color change indicates presence). Professional testing more reliable but DIY tests give useful indication.

Testing Frequency

New system: test monthly first year (detect problems early). Established systems: test quarterly or annually (verify continued function). More frequent testing if problems suspected. Test after heavy rain or heavy use (stresses systems).

Pathogen Reduction in Sewage Treatment

Various treatment processes reduce pathogens to safe levels.

Pathogen Types

  • Bacteria: E. coli, Salmonella, Vibrio. Killed by chlorine, heat, UV, biological competition, or environmental stress (pH extremes, starvation).
  • Viruses: Hepatitis A, Rotavirus, Norovirus. Harder to kill than bacteria. Heat and chlorine effective. Some survive environmental stress.
  • Protozoa: Giardia, Cryptosporidium. Very resistant to chlorine. Killed by boiling, UV, filtration through sand/gravel with adequate particle size.
  • Helminths: Worm parasites. Settled in tanks. Killed by long-term composting, heat, or chlorine.

Treatment Mechanisms

Settling: heavy particles (including some parasites) settle by gravity. Biological treatment: bacteria consume pathogens (predation and competition). Filtration: particles and pathogens trapped in sand/gravel. pH extremes: acid or alkaline conditions kill some pathogens. Time: pathogens die naturally given sufficient retention time (days to months).

Multi-Barrier Approach

No single treatment 100% effective. Multiple barriers (settling, filtration, biological treatment) together achieve high confidence. Septic + soil filtration removes 99%+ pathogens. Constructed wetlands similar effectiveness. Boiling or UV disinfection provides final barrier if needed.

Reduction Targets

For safe environmental discharge: 99% pathogen reduction acceptable. For potable water: 99.99%+ reduction required. For irrigation reuse: 95%+ reduction minimum. Treatment design determines achieved reduction level.

System Maintenance and Troubleshooting

Proper maintenance prevents system failures and extends lifespan.

Septic System Maintenance

  • Pump tank every 3-5 years (remove sludge accumulation)
  • Monitor water use (excessive water overloads system)
  • Avoid flushing non-degradable items (toilet paper OK, but avoid wipes, feminine products, diapers)
  • Avoid dumping chemicals (cleaning products, medications, pesticides kill bacteria)
  • Protect drain field from compaction (don't park on it, avoid excessive watering)
  • Inspect for failures (wet spots, odors, backup)

Constructed Wetland Maintenance

  • Harvest plants annually (removes excess nutrients)
  • Remove accumulated sludge every 5-10 years
  • Monitor water level (maintain shallow water depth)
  • Check for standing water or pooling (indicates clogging)
  • Prevent erosion at inlets/outlets

Composting Toilet Maintenance

  • Add carbon material after each use (critical)
  • Monitor moisture level (add dry material if too wet)
  • Ventilate if odors develop (increase airflow)
  • Empty full chambers for composting
  • Rotate to new chamber periodically

Septic Tank Sizing

Septic tank must be sized to household occupancy. Too small causes overflow and inadequate treatment. Sizing formula:

Septic Tank Capacity:
1,000 gallons (minimum) for 1-3 persons
+250 gallons for each additional person

Examples:
1 person: 1,000 gallons
3 people: 1,000 gallons
4 people: 1,250 gallons
6 people: 1,500 gallons
8 people: 1,750 gallons

Tank Configuration

  • Depth: 4-5 feet typical (allows settling and anaerobic treatment)
  • Length-to-width ratio: Roughly 2:1 (longer tank = better settlement)
  • Material: Concrete, fiberglass, or plastic. Concrete most durable (40+ years); plastic shorter lifespan (20-30 years).
  • Compartments: Ideal design includes 2 chambers (first chamber for solids settlement, second for further treatment). Single chamber acceptable for small systems.

Drain Field (Leach Field) Design

Soil Percolation Testing

Soil must drain slowly enough to treat effluent but fast enough to prevent ponding.

Percolation Test Procedure:
1. Dig test hole 12 inches deep in proposed drain field location
2. Fill hole with water; allow to drain completely
3. Refill and measure drop rate over 30 minutes
4. Calculate: inches per minute

Interpretation:
1 inch/minute = Sandy soil (IDEAL for septic)
0.5-1 inch/minute = Loamy soil (GOOD)
0.1-0.5 inch/minute = Clay-loam (ACCEPTABLE)
<0.1 inch/minute = Clay or hard soil (NOT SUITABLE for conventional drain field)

Drain Field Layout

  • Area calculation: 1-1.5 sq ft of drain field per gallon per day of household flow. Average household: 100-150 gallons/day, requiring 100-225 sq ft drain field.
  • Minimum width: Trench system minimum 2 feet wide, deeper systems minimum 3 feet wide.
  • Depth: Trench bottom 18-36 inches below surface (varies by soil and frost depth).
  • Spacing: Parallel trenches spaced 6-8 feet apart (center-to-center).
  • Slope: Slight slope (0.5% to 2%) ensures gravity drainage without pooling.

Material Specification

  • Gravel layer: 4-6 inches of 1-2 inch diameter gravel at bottom
  • Perforated pipe: 4-inch diameter PVC or clay tile with 0.5-inch holes spaced every 6-8 inches
  • Top gravel: 2-3 inches above pipe
  • Filter fabric: Geotextile fabric between gravel and native soil prevents soil infiltration
  • Cover: 12+ inches of native soil on top (allows soil treatment and vegetation rooting)

Composting Toilet Carbon-to-Nitrogen Ratios

Successful composting requires proper balance of carbon (brown) and nitrogen (green) materials to accelerate decomposition and pathogen kill.

Ideal C:N Ratio = 25:1 (by weight)

This means for every 25 pounds of carbon material, add 1 pound of nitrogen material.

Carbon Sources (Browns):
- Sawdust: excellent, abundant
- Wood shavings: good
- Dry leaves: moderate
- Straw (not hay): moderate
- Paper/cardboard: poor (takes longer to decompose)

Nitrogen Sources (Greens):
- Human feces (source of nitrogen)
- Urine (high nitrogen)
- Grass clippings: when available
- Food scraps: occasional
- Coffee grounds: small amount

Composting Toilet Operation

  • After each use: Add carbon material (handful of sawdust/shavings). This immediately covers feces, controls odor, starts C:N balance.
  • Container: 5-gallon bucket with tight-fitting lid, changed weekly.
  • Decomposition location: Pile outdoors or in dedicated compost bin.
  • Moisture: Add water if pile becomes too dry (should feel like wrung-out sponge).

Humanure Pathogen Kill Timelines

Hot Composting (Accelerated)

  • Requirement: Maintain temperature 131°F (55°C) for minimum 3 consecutive days throughout pile
  • Why: Pathogens (E. coli, viruses, parasites) are killed by sustained heat
  • Achievement: Requires proper C:N ratio (25:1) and regular turning to generate heat and prevent cool spots
  • Timeline: 3-6 months from peak heat to finished compost
  • Monitoring: Compost thermometer essential; if temperature drops below 131°F, extend treatment time or add more nitrogen-rich material

Cold Composting (Passive)

  • No temperature requirement: Relies on time instead of heat
  • Minimum duration: 1 year minimum; 2 years preferred to ensure pathogen die-off
  • Risk: Longer timeline increases probability of environmental contamination if pile breaches
  • Use case: Appropriate when energy for turning/monitoring unavailable; requires secure location away from groundwater

Critical Safety

  • DO NOT: Use uncomposted humanure for edibles (vegetables, fruits). Only use fully composted material (1+ year or 131°F for 3 days).
  • Safety margin: Use on non-edible plants (ornamentals, trees) until 2+ years have passed.
  • Application: Finished compost spread on soil surface; do not incorporate into eating surface (salads, berries at ground level).

Well Contamination Radius

Septic systems can contaminate groundwater through soil. Safe distances prevent cross-contamination.

Minimum Safe Distances:
From septic TANK to well: 50 feet
From drain FIELD to well: 100 feet

These assume flat terrain and normal soil. Reduce if terrain slopes downhill from septic to well.

Soil Type Effects

  • Sandy soil: Contamination spreads faster; increase distances by 50%
  • Loamy soil: Standard distances acceptable
  • Clay soil: Contamination spreads slowly; standard distances acceptable (may reduce slightly)

Well Testing

  • Baseline test: Before relying on well, test for bacteria (E. coli, total coliform) and nitrates
  • Annual testing: Minimum; quarterly in high-risk areas
  • After septic pumping: Test well 1-2 weeks after pumping truck accesses area
  • Results interpretation: Any coliform bacteria = contamination; shut down well and treat (boil/filter) until resolved

Biosand Filter Construction

Biosand filter uses layers of sand to remove pathogens and sediment from contaminated water. Effective for treating surface water or septic effluent.

Layer Specifications

Biosand Filter Layers (top to bottom):

Top layer (protective): 5cm gravel (pea-sized, prevents sand disturbance)

Coarse sand layer: 5cm (1-2mm diameter particles)

Fine sand layer: 40cm (0.15-0.35mm diameter particles - the critical layer)

Bottom layer: 5cm gravel (drainage layer, prevents clogging)

Total depth: ~55cm (21 inches)

Filter Housing

  • Material: Concrete or plastic box, minimum 20-30 cm (8-12 inches) diameter/width for adequate sand volume
  • Input: Top opening for pouring water
  • Output: Outlet pipe at bottom for filtered water collection
  • Biofilm formation: Fine sand layer develops biofilm (algae and bacteria) that actively filters particles and kills pathogens after 1-2 weeks of operation

Operation

  • Initial flushing: Filter first 30-50 gallons of water (discard) to develop biofilm and remove fine sand particles
  • Flow rate: Gravity-fed, approximately 0.5-2 liters per hour depending on sand granulometry
  • Maintenance: When flow slows, remove top 1-2cm sand layer (clogged with particles) and replace with clean sand
  • Effectiveness: Removes 90%+ of sediment and pathogens; not effective for viruses or chemical contaminants

Slow Sand Filters

Similar to biosand but larger scale, slow sand filters treat larger volumes of water.

Design Specifications

  • Recommended flow rate: 0.1-0.4 meters per hour (roughly 1-4 inches per hour)
  • Sand bed depth: 1-2 meters (3-6 feet)
  • Surface area: 1 m² per 20-50 people (depending on water quality)
  • Sand specifications: Uniform fine sand (0.15-0.3mm effective size, uniformity coefficient <3)

Biofilm Development

  • Schmutzdecke (filter skin): Top 10-20cm develops biologically active layer of algae, protozoa, and bacteria
  • Formation time: 4-8 weeks before full effectiveness
  • Function: Actively kills pathogens through predation (protozoa eat bacteria) and competition

Maintenance

  • Head loss: As filter clogs, water level rises above sand; indicates need for cleaning
  • Cleaning: Remove top 1-2cm of clogged schmutzdecke and sand; backwash if necessary
  • Storage: Supply tank above filter maintains head pressure for gravity flow

Greywater vs Blackwater Treatment

Greywater (Low-Risk Wastewater)

Greywater from sinks, laundry, and showers contains minimal pathogens (no feces).

  • Treatment requirement: Minimal; filtration adequate for irrigation
  • Simple system: Drain to settling basin (removes particles), then use gravity drip irrigation for plants
  • Benefit: Reduces freshwater demand for landscape irrigation by 30-50%
  • Limitation: Should NOT be applied to vegetables eaten raw (salads); safe for ornamentals and trees

Blackwater (High-Risk Wastewater)

Blackwater from toilets contains fecal matter and high pathogen load.

  • Treatment requirement: Extensive; must include septic tank and drain field (or constructed wetland) before environmental release
  • Direct irrigation: NEVER use untreated blackwater for irrigation; extreme disease risk

Constructed Wetlands for Sewage Treatment

Constructed wetlands use plants and microorganisms in shallow water systems to treat wastewater. Effective, low-energy, and creates wildlife habitat.

Design Type: Horizontal Subsurface Flow (Most Common)

  • Configuration: Shallow basin (1-2 feet deep) filled with gravel substrate through which water flows horizontally
  • Plants: Wetland species (cattails, rushes, reeds) rooted in gravel; oxygen transfer through root zones
  • Sizing: Surface area 5-10 m² per person (larger in cold climates)
  • Treatment mechanism: Combination of microbial decomposition, plant uptake, and physical settling

Treatment Performance

  • BOD removal: 85-95% (biochemical oxygen demand; measure of organic pollution)
  • Nitrogen removal: 50-80% (through plant uptake and denitrification)
  • Pathogen removal: 90-99% (through biofilm action and settling)
  • Phosphorus removal: 50-70% (adsorption and plant uptake)

Construction

  1. Excavate basin: Shallow pond shape, depth 18-24 inches
  2. Liner: Impermeable layer (plastic, clay, or bentonite) prevents groundwater contamination
  3. Gravel: 12-18 inches of 1-2 inch diameter gravel substrate
  4. Plants: Install rhizomes or root divisions of wetland species; spacing 12-18 inches
  5. Water input: Septic tank effluent or other pretreated wastewater distributed across inlet
  6. Outlet: Overflow weir at far end allows treated water to discharge or percolate

Advantages

  • Low operating cost (no electricity, minimal maintenance)
  • Wildlife habitat (birds, amphibians, insects)
  • Aesthetic value (landscape feature)
  • Resilience (tolerates variable flow and loading)

Limitations

  • Requires relatively flat, available land (space-intensive)
  • Longer residence time than mechanical treatment (days vs hours)
  • Mosquito habitat if not designed carefully (maintain proper water depth, aeration)
  • Cold climate performance reduced (extended treatment time)
