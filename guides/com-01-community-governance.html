Why Governance Matters

Individual survival skills mean little without functioning community. The most skilled individuals cannot survive alone long-term; they need others for trade, defense, reproduction, and psychological support. However, groups without clear governance descend into conflict, resource hoarding, and violence. Functional governance is the difference between communities that thrive and those that collapse into chaos.

Governance is not about control—it's about coordination. A well-governed community can:

  • Make decisions quickly and fairly
  • Resolve conflicts without violence
  • Allocate scarce resources equitably
  • Coordinate labor and production
  • Maintain morale and social cohesion
  • Adapt to changing conditions
  • Preserve knowledge and tradition
Without governance: Groups splinter over resource disputes, decisions are made by whoever is loudest or most violent, talented individuals are exploited, and knowledge is lost when key people die.

Group Sizing & Communication Dynamics

Group size fundamentally changes how governance works. Communication complexity increases geometrically as group size grows. A group of 5 can operate through face-to-face discussion; a group of 150 requires formal hierarchy.

The Dunbar Number

Research suggests humans can maintain stable social relationships with roughly 150 people through personal contact. Groups larger than this require formal structure and mediation. Smaller groups rely on reputation and personal relationships.

Decision-Making Models

Consensus

How it works: Discussion continues until all members agree or can live with the decision. No vote; seeks genuine consensus.

Advantages: High buy-in, quality decisions, develops shared understanding.

Disadvantages: Slow, requires skilled facilitation, vulnerable to blocking by individuals or coalitions.

Best for: Groups of 5-20 with enough time and good communication skills.

Voting (Majority Rule)

How it works: Proposal is discussed, then voted on. Majority (>50%) wins.

Advantages: Fast, clear winner, scalable.

Disadvantages: Minorities feel excluded, encourages tribalism, can produce unstable decisions if coalition shifts.

Best for: Groups of 40+ where speed matters.

Council-Based

How it works: Selected representatives (council) meet regularly to make decisions. They consult with community members between meetings.

Advantages: Combines speed with representation, allows specialization, scales well.

Disadvantages: Requires good representatives, can become disconnected from community.

Best for: Groups of 75-300 people.

Autocratic (One Leader)

Verdict: Not recommended for survival communities. Concentrates power, breeds resentment, fails catastrophically if leader dies or becomes incapacitated. Only acceptable in extreme crisis (immediate threat) and only as temporary measure.

Leadership Structure

Core Leadership Roles

  • Coordinator/Chief: Facilitates decisions, represents community externally, breaks deadlocks. Not a dictator; authority comes from community trust.
  • Resource Manager: Tracks inventory, allocates materials, prevents hoarding. Accountable to community through regular reporting.
  • Conflict Resolution: Mediates disputes, maintains justice system, ensures fairness.
  • Defense/Security: Organizes defense, maintains external security, coordinates disaster response.
  • Health/Welfare: Manages medical resources, coordinates care for sick/injured, tracks nutrition.
  • Knowledge/Education: Preserves knowledge, teaches skills, maintains records.

In small communities (<20 people), these roles are part-time and often combined. As groups grow, they specialize and become full-time.

Rotation & Term Limits

Best practice: Term limits (1-2 years) with rotation of leadership roles. This prevents entrenchment of power, develops leadership capacity across the community, and ensures fresh perspectives. Leaders should be rotatable back into general population afterward.

Conflict Resolution Process

The Three-Step Model

Step 1: Direct Discussion

Two disputing parties attempt to resolve the issue themselves. Facilitator may help, but parties try to find agreement without community involvement. Most conflicts resolve here if both parties are motivated.

Step 2: Mediation

If Step 1 fails, a neutral third party (mediator) meets with both sides, listens to each perspective, identifies common ground, and proposes solutions. The mediator doesn't decide; they help parties decide.

Step 3: Community Hearing

If mediation fails, the conflict goes to the council or community assembly. Both parties present evidence and arguments. Community decides on resolution (restitution, behavior change, exclusion, etc.).

Principles for Fair Resolution

  • Transparency: Disputes are handled openly, not secretly. Everyone knows what happened and why the decision was made.
  • Proportionality: Penalties match the severity of the offense. Stealing a meal is not the same as premeditated violence.
  • Restorative: Focus on fixing the harm, not punishing the person. When possible, the offender repairs damage or compensates the victim.
  • Appeal mechanism: Decisions can be appealed if new evidence emerges or process was unfair.
  • Record-keeping: All significant disputes are documented, creating a record for future reference and consistency.

Resource Allocation

Principles

  • Equity over equality: Different people have different needs. A pregnant woman needs more calories than a child. The elderly may need more firewood for warmth.
  • Transparency: Everyone knows allocation criteria. No secret hoarding or favoritism.
  • Contribution-based: Those who contribute more labor get recognition, though basic needs are met for all.
  • Redundancy: No single person controls a critical resource.

Allocation Methods

  • Need-based: Each person gets what they need to survive and function. Requires trust and accurate information about needs.
  • Equal share: Everyone gets the same. Simple, but ignores individual differences. Works well for basic necessities (food, shelter) in egalitarian communities.
  • Contribution-based: More work or higher-skill work earns more resources. Incentivizes productivity but can create inequality.
  • Lottery: For scarce, desirable items, drawing lots ensures fairness. Removes accusations of favoritism.
  • Trading/barter: Resources are privately owned and exchanged. Requires established prices/values and prevents hoarding through social pressure.

Rationing in Scarcity

If supplies cannot meet demand, implement formal rationing. Examples:

  • Per-capita rations: Each person gets X calories/day of food
  • Ration cards: Coupons distributed monthly; destroying a coupon when used prevents double-claiming
  • Priority ranking: Essential workers (medical staff, security) get more; children and elderly get standard; able-bodied adults get reduced rations
  • Duration: Rationing is temporary and reviewed monthly; removes as soon as possible to prevent resentment

Justice & Law System

Core Principles

Any justice system needs basic principles to be legitimate:

  • Rule of law, not people: Everyone—including leaders—is subject to the same rules. No exemptions for powerful.
  • Presumption of innocence: A person is innocent until evidence proves guilt. Burden of proof is on the accuser.
  • Right to defense: Accused person can present their side and challenge evidence.
  • Proportionality: Punishment fits the crime. Execution is not appropriate for theft.
  • Finality: Decisions are final unless new evidence emerges. Endless retrials prevent closure.

Execution as Last Resort

Execution should be reserved for the most serious crimes (murders, rape, treason) in settled communities with robust due process. It should never be used for theft, shirking, or lesser offenses. In resource-scarce situations, imprisonment is not practical; exile or lifetime labor servitude are alternatives.

Elections & Accountability

Electoral Process

If using elected representatives or leaders:

  • Criteria for candidacy: Minimum age, community residency, mental competency, no disqualifying crimes
  • Nomination: Self-nomination or community nomination. Avoid hereditary succession.
  • Voting: Each adult member gets one vote per office. Secret ballot prevents coercion.
  • Frequency: Elections every 1-2 years. More frequent = instability; less frequent = entrenchment
  • Term limits: Maximum of 2-3 consecutive terms. Prevents permanent power concentration.

Accountability Mechanisms

  • Regular reports: Leaders report to community monthly on decisions and activities.
  • Public debate: Community members can question leaders' decisions publicly.
  • Recall: If a leader is clearly failing or abusing power, community can call for recall vote. If 2/3 vote for recall, leader is removed.
  • Transparency: Major decisions (resource allocation, conflicts, changes to law) are made publicly, not in secret.

Documentation Systems

Governance only works if decisions are documented. Oral tradition alone creates disputes over "what was decided."

Essential Records

  • Meeting minutes: Date, attendees, topics discussed, decisions made, how votes went. File in chronological order.
  • Laws/Rules: Written code of conduct. Lists offenses and penalties. Updated when community changes rules.
  • Conflict resolution: Date, parties involved, dispute, resolution, penalty if applicable.
  • Resource allocation: Monthly records of inventory, distribution, any special circumstances.
  • Elections: Candidates, voting results, terms of office.

Record Preservation

  • Keep multiple copies (at least three) in different locations. One fire shouldn't destroy history.
  • Use durable paper (archival quality) and permanent ink.
  • Assign one person responsibility for records. Their job is to maintain, copy, and protect historical documents.
  • Create a summary ledger of major decisions for easy reference.
