Tooth Anatomy

Tooth cross-section: Enamel (hard outer), dentin (middle), pulp (nerve chamber), root
Structure Composition Function
Enamel Calcium phosphate crystals, ~96% mineral Protective outer layer, hardest substance in body
Dentin Tubular structure, ~70% mineral, 30% organic Main tooth structure, softer than enamel, conducts pain
Pulp (nerve) Blood vessels, nerves, connective tissue Nourishes tooth during development; pain sensation
Cementum Similar to dentin, ~50% mineral Covers root, anchors periodontal ligament
Root Dentin core with cementum coating Anchors tooth to jaw bone via ligament

Common Dental Emergencies and Initial Assessment

Emergency Symptoms Immediate Action
Toothache (pain) Sharp/dull ache, worsens with heat/cold NSAIDs (ibuprofen 400-600 mg), cold compress on cheek, avoid hot foods
Abscess (infection) Swelling, pus, fever, severe pain Antibiotics if available, warm salt rinses, pain relief; drain if safe
Avulsion (knocked out) Tooth completely out of socket Reimplant within 30 min if possible; store in milk or saliva if not
Fracture/crack Chip or crack visible, sharp pain Cover sharp edge with temporary cement; avoid that side when chewing
Loose/extruded tooth Tooth pushed partway out, visible displacement Gently push back into socket; stabilize with splint or bite guard

Tooth Extraction Technique

Tooth extraction is invasive and carries risks of nerve/blood vessel damage, excessive bleeding, jaw fracture, and infection. Only attempt if the tooth cannot be saved and professional dental care is unavailable for extended period. Proper anesthesia and sterile technique are essential.

Extraction Instruments Diagram

Pre-Extraction Examination

  1. Use mouth mirror or flashlight to visualize the tooth
  2. Check for bone loss (X-ray ideal but not always available); loose teeth with severe bone loss are easier to extract
  3. Assess mobility by grasping with fingers/gloved hands; healthy teeth are firmly anchored
  4. Take tooth radiographs if possible to identify root anatomy (one vs. multiple roots affects technique)

Extraction Instruments

Instrument Use DIY Alternative
Forceps (universal or root-tip) Grasp and luxate tooth Pliers (smooth-jaw surgical pliers preferred)
Elevators Separate tooth from ligament Flat-blade screwdriver or chisel-tipped metal pick
Scalpel Incise and reflect gum tissue Sharp blade or scalpel knife
Periosteal elevator Lift periosteum from bone Smooth metal spatula

Extraction Procedure

  1. Administer local anesthesia (inferior alveolar nerve block for lower teeth; infraorbital block for upper front)
  2. Test anesthesia: gently probe gum with explorer; patient should not feel pain
  3. If gum is loose/inflamed, make small incision to reflect soft tissues slightly
  4. Place elevator under the tooth at the gum line; apply gentle rocking motion to loosen periodontal ligament
  5. Grasp tooth firmly with forceps at the crown (just below gum line)
  6. Use controlled rocking/rotational motion‚Äîavoid jerking; apply steady pressure
  7. Upper teeth: rotate and elevate upward; lower teeth: elevate and rotate
  8. Once tooth is mobile, remove with steady traction
  9. Apply gauze and direct pressure to control bleeding; patient bites down for 15-30 minutes
  10. Inspect socket; remove any root fragments or debris
Tooth extraction: elevator loosens attachment, forceps applies traction

Local Anesthesia Administration

Inferior Alveolar Nerve Block (Lower Teeth)

Anesthetizes lower jaw and teeth on one side (most common for lower extractions)

  1. Position patient supine or semi-reclining; head tilted back slightly
  2. Identify the anterior border of mandibular ramus (rough vertical edge of jaw)
  3. Find the lingula (small bony landmark on inner surface of ramus, at midheight)
  4. Insert needle at lingula level, on inner surface of ramus, at approximately 90¬∞ angle to jaw
  5. Inject 1.0-1.5 mL of 2% lidocaine; needle should contact bone at correct depth
  6. Withdraw 0.5 mm and inject remaining solution
  7. Patient may feel slight tingling in lower lip and tongue
  8. Wait 3-5 minutes for full effect before proceeding

Anesthetic Agents

Agent Concentration Duration Notes
Lidocaine 2% solution 30-60 min Most common; fast onset
Procaine 1-2% solution 15-30 min Shorter duration; less effective
Bupivacaine 0.5% solution 90-120 min Long duration; slower onset

Dosage Calculations

Maximum safe dose of lidocaine: 7 mg/kg body weight (up to 500 mg total)

  • 2% lidocaine = 20 mg/mL
  • For 70 kg adult: maximum 490 mg = 24.5 mL of 2% solution (typically only 1-2 mL needed per block)
Overdose of local anesthetic causes CNS toxicity (tremor, seizures, loss of consciousness) and cardiac dysrhythmias. Inject slowly and aspirate before injecting to avoid intravascular injection. Have emergency equipment available (airway, oxygen, epinephrine if possible).

Temporary Fillings

Zinc Oxide + Eugenol (ZOE) Cement

Simple, biocompatible temporary restoration. Eugenol (clove oil) is the active ingredient.

Preparation: Mix zinc oxide powder with eugenol liquid (3:1 powder to liquid by weight) until putty-like consistency. Apply to cavity with small instrument; fill completely to prevent food trapping.

Working time: 10-15 minutes before hardening

Removal: Hardens in 24-48 hours; can be removed with explorer

IRM (Intermediate Restorative Material)

Reinforced zinc oxide-eugenol; stronger and longer-lasting than simple ZOE

Preparation: Mix powder and liquid according to package; fill cavity

Durability: 6-12 months if properly sealed

Temporary DIY Fillings

  • Calcium hydroxide (slaked lime) mixed with water: Paste form; biostimulative (promotes healing). Mix until thick consistency; apply to cavity.
  • Oil of cloves (eugenol) + zinc oxide powder (from calamine lotion): DIY ZOE substitute; mix to putty consistency
  • Gutta-percha (natural rubber) softened in warm water: Moldable temporary sealant; place in cavity while warm
  • Beeswax mixed with resin: Temporary sealant; mold when warm

Abscess Drainage and Management

A dental abscess is a localized infection (usually from untreated decay or trauma). Symptoms: pain, swelling, pus, fever, difficulty swallowing.

Safe Drainage Procedure

  1. Examine visually to confirm pus-filled swelling (yellow/white point on gum)
  2. Sterilize area and instrument (scalpel or needle) with alcohol
  3. Make small incision (3-5 mm) at the white point where abscess is closest to surface
  4. Apply gentle pressure around abscess to encourage pus drainage
  5. Allow pus to drain completely (may take 5-10 minutes)
  6. Insert small rubber drain (tubing) or leave incision open to continue drainage
  7. Instruct patient to rinse with warm salt water (1 teaspoon salt in 8 oz water) 3-4 times daily
  8. Prescribe antibiotics if available (amoxicillin 500 mg TID, or clindamycin if penicillin allergy)
  9. Follow up in 2-3 days; if abscess has not improved or is worsening, extract the causative tooth
Do not attempt drainage if: - Swelling extends to floor of mouth or neck (airway emergency) - Patient has fever >101¬∞F and spreading swelling (possible sepsis) - Swelling extends to eye area (serious orbital cellulitis) These require hospitalization and IV antibiotics immediately.

Infection Management

Antibiotics for Dental Infection

Antibiotic Dose Frequency Notes
Amoxicillin 500 mg Every 8 hours x 7 days First-line for dental infections
Clindamycin 300-450 mg Every 6-8 hours x 7 days For penicillin allergy; good tissue penetration
Azithromycin (Z-pack) 500 mg day 1, then 250 mg daily 5 days total Alternative; shorter course

Supportive Care

  • NSAIDs: Ibuprofen 400-600 mg every 6 hours (reduces inflammation and pain)
  • Warm salt rinses: 4-6 times daily (promotes drainage, reduces pain)
  • Soft diet: Avoid hot, hard, crunchy foods
  • Hydration: Drink plenty of water to support immune function
  • Rest: Allow adequate sleep for healing

Jaw Fracture Stabilization

Jaw fractures cause severe pain, malocclusion (teeth don't meet properly), difficulty swallowing, and risk of airway obstruction.

Recognition

  • Swelling and pain at the fracture site
  • Teeth do not occlude normally (bite is off)
  • Difficulty opening mouth fully
  • Numbness of lower lip (inferior alveolar nerve compression)

Barton Bandage (Simple Stabilization)

A figure-8 bandage that supports the jaw:

  1. Use elastic bandage or strips of cloth (3-4 inches wide)
  2. Start at lower jaw, pass under chin
  3. Cross diagonally up one side of face, over the head
  4. Return diagonally down the opposite side, under chin again
  5. Repeat 2-3 times to create figure-8 pattern
  6. Secure with tape or ties; tension should support jaw without cutting off circulation
  7. Ensure teeth can still be slightly apart (allows swallowing, prevents aspiration if vomiting)

Care Instructions

  • Soft diet only; liquid nutrition for severely restricted opening
  • Pain relief: NSAIDs or acetaminophen
  • Ice on swelling: 15 minutes on, 15 minutes off for first 48 hours
  • Maintain oral hygiene: Gentle rinses only, no vigorous brushing
  • Seek professional care as soon as possible for X-rays and proper fixation

Preventive Dental Care

Daily Oral Hygiene

  • Brushing: 2-3 times daily with fluoride toothpaste (1450 ppm fluoride); use soft bristles
  • Flossing: Daily to remove food and plaque from between teeth
  • Rinsing: Warm salt water after meals helps remove debris
  • Avoid sugary foods: Bacteria ferment sugars, producing acid that demineralizes enamel

Fluoride Application

Fluoride strengthens enamel by forming fluorapatite, which is more acid-resistant than normal hydroxyapatite.

  • Topical: Fluoride toothpaste (1000-1450 ppm) or rinse (0.05% daily or 0.2% weekly)
  • Professional: Sodium fluoride gel (1.1%) or acidulated phosphate fluoride (1.23%) applied annually
  • Systemic (children): Fluoridated water (1 ppm) during tooth development

Professional Cleaning

Tartar (calcified plaque) can only be removed professionally. Buildup leads to gum disease and tooth loss. Professional cleanings recommended every 6-12 months.

Improvised Dental Tools

Dental Elevators (Extraction Tools)

  • Material: Flat-head screwdriver (1/4" to 1/2" width)
  • Preparation: File smooth all edges; remove sharp burrs that would cut gums. Edges should be rounded, not sharp.
  • Technique: Insert under tooth at gumline, gently lever to break periodontal ligament. Rock side-to-side, not back-and-forth, to avoid crushing tooth.
  • Alternative: Chisel (old or broken, filed smooth); butter knife (though wider and less precise).

Improvised Forceps

  • Material: Locking pliers (vise-grips) or standard pliers with smooth jaws
  • Modification: If teeth rough, file smooth or wrap jaws with cloth to avoid slipping and crushing tooth.
  • Grip: Grasp tooth crown (visible part), not root. Root is not accessible and is fragile.
  • Extraction: Rock gently side-to-side to expand socket, then steady upward pull. Do NOT twist or pull at angles‚Äîrisk of breaking tooth and leaving root.

Anesthesia: Clove Oil (Eugenol)

  • Agent: Eugenol, a natural compound in clove oil, is a local anesthetic that numbs tissue on contact.
  • Application: Soak cotton ball in clove oil, apply directly to affected tooth and surrounding gum. Repeat as needed.
  • Onset: 2-5 minutes for effect; lasts 15-30 minutes.
  • Limitation: Numbs surface only; does not penetrate deep tooth pulp. Helpful for pre-extraction anesthesia but not sufficient for major procedures.
  • Side effects: Rare if applied topically; excessive swallowing of clove oil can cause upset stomach.

Tooth Powder Recipe

Basic Tooth Powder:
Baking soda: 2 parts (sodium bicarbonate‚Äîmild abrasive, slightly alkaline, deodorizing)
Fine salt: 1 part (abrasive, antimicrobial)
Optional: Crushed charcoal: 1/4 to 1/2 part (absorbs staining compounds, slightly more abrasive)

Mix thoroughly. Store in sealed container.
Usage: Damp toothbrush, apply 1/4 teaspoon powder, brush gently.
Effectiveness: Removes surface staining and plaque. Less fluoride benefit than modern toothpaste, but adequate for mechanical cleaning.

Chew Sticks for Tooth Cleaning

Miswak (Salvadora persica)

  • Traditional use: Used for 7,000+ years in Middle East and Africa for tooth cleaning.
  • Properties: Contains natural antimicrobial compounds and mild abrasive fibers.
  • Preparation: Cut fresh twig (pencil-thick), chew one end until fibers fray into brush-like form.
  • Usage: Rub fray-end against teeth in vertical motions. Can be softened by soaking in water.
  • Effectiveness: Removes plaque and debris; naturally fights bacteria. Clinical studies show effectiveness similar to modern toothbrushes.

Willow Twigs

  • Alternative: If miswak not available, fresh willow twigs work similarly.
  • Selection: Young, tender twigs; avoid old, brittle wood.
  • Preparation: Cut 6-inch sections, chew one end until fibers separate into brush.
  • Properties: Willow contains salicin (salicylic acid precursor), which provides slight analgesic and antimicrobial benefit.

General Chew Stick Technique

  • Chewing motion: Gently work one end with teeth to fray and soften fibers; do not bite hard enough to splinter.
  • Brushing: Once fray-end is prepared, rub against each tooth surface: outer surfaces, inner surfaces, and biting surfaces.
  • Vertical strokes: Brush downward from gum toward tooth tip; this motion helps clean under gumline.
  • Frequency: Use after meals; takes 5-10 minutes per session.
  • Lifespan: Single chew stick lasts 2-3 days with regular use; then discard and prepare new stick.
