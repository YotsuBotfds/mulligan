Distillation Principles

Distillation separates liquids by boiling point differences. Heating mixture vaporizes lower-boiling component preferentially. Vapor rises through column, cools, condenses to liquid, and is collected. Key principle: Raoult's Law - partial pressure of component in vapor phase proportional to its mole fraction in liquid, adjusted by volatility (vapor pressure). This preferential enrichment allows separation.

Efficiency depends on: (1) column height/packing (more surfaces for equilibration), (2) boiling point difference (>30°C ideal, <20°C difficult), (3) heating rate (slow distillation = better separation), (4) reflux ratio (vapor recondensing and dripping back improves purity).

Distillation Apparatus

Simple Pot Still

Minimal setup: roundbottom flask on heat source, thermometer inserted through stopper, condenser tube attached to outlet, collection vessel below condenser outlet. Heat flask, vapor rises, travels through condenser (cooled by air or water circulation), condenses to liquid, drips into collection vessel.

Condenser design: Liebig condenser most effective - inner tube carries product vapor (cooled), outer jacket circulates cold water (or ice in survival conditions). Length 30-60cm. Alternatively, air-cooled: coil of copper/glass tubing allows vapor to cool while traveling. Less efficient than water-cooled but functional without running water.

Fractional Distillation Column

For better separation: insert packing into column above boiling flask. Packing increases surface area for vapor-liquid equilibration. Materials: glass marbles, ceramic pellets, copper gauze coils, or wooden dowels (less effective but available). Height 20-60cm. Larger diameter (5-10cm) reduces pressure drop. Packing creates 50-100 theoretical plates of separation (simple pot still = ~1 plate).

Ethanol Production

Ethanol is produced via fermentation of sugars using yeast (Saccharomyces cerevisiae). Fermentation: C₆H₁₂O₆ → 2C₂H₅OH + 2CO₂. Feedstock: molasses, sugar cane juice, fruit juice, grains (malted to convert starch to sugar).

Fermentation

  1. Preparation: Mix 100kg sugar (or molasses) with 400L water, create solution ~20% solids (specific gravity ~1.08)
  2. Sterilization: Heat to 70°C for 30 minutes, cool to room temperature (kills wild bacteria, leaves yeast viable if added)
  3. Inoculation: Add baker's yeast (500g dry yeast) or starter culture from previous fermentation
  4. Conditions: Maintain temperature 18-25°C (mesophilic yeast), provide slight aeration (for yeast growth first 24h, then anaerobic), prevent contamination
  5. Fermentation time: 1-3 weeks until no more CO₂ evolution (fermentation complete)
  6. Product: ~10% ethanol by volume (depending on initial sugar concentration and yeast health)

Distillation of Fermented Liquid

Fermented liquid contains ~10% ethanol + water + yeast byproducts. Distill at low temperature (~78°C, ethanol boiling point):

  1. Place fermented liquid in boiling flask (do NOT exceed 3/4 full)
  2. Heat slowly until vapor appears (around 65°C thermometer reading)
  3. Once vapors reach 78-80°C, condense and collect. This is "heads" (first portion) - contains unpleasant compounds (methanol, acetaldehyde). Discard first 5-10% of distillate
  4. Main fraction: 78-80°C collection = ethanol-rich product, ~50-70% pure ethanol
  5. As temperature rises above 85°C, collect separately (tails) - mostly water, can be redistilled

Redist illation improves purity: collect only 78-80°C fraction in second distillation, yields ~90-95% ethanol (pure ethanol is 100%, boils at 78.4°C, azeotropic mixture with water at 95.6% ethanol).

Denaturant

If producing for fuel/solvents (not consumption): add denaturant to prevent drinking and avoid excise tax (historical). 5% methanol or 2% gasoline renders ethanol unpalatable. Mix thoroughly after final distillation.

Turpentine from Pine Resin

Turpentine is mixture of terpenes (C₁₀H₁₆) obtained from pine tree resin. Boiling point ~155°C. Applications: paint thinner, solvent, fuel additive.

Collection & Processing

  1. Resin collection: Cut diagonal scar into pine tree bark (blue pine, longleaf pine best), collect resin that flows in cups/trays below scar. Resin contains ~20% volatile terpenes (turpentine), 80% resin acids (rosin)
  2. Distillation: Heat resin in still to 150-180°C. Turpentine vapors condense, rosin (higher boiling point) remains in flask. Collect vapor at 155-165°C
  3. Yield: ~20kg turpentine per 100kg crude resin
  4. Purity: After distillation, product is pure turpentine. Rosin residue useful for friction applications (violin bows, bowling pins)

Acetone Production

Acetone (CH₃COCH₃, boiling point 56°C) produced via fermentation (acetone-butanol-ethanol fermentation) or dry distillation of calcium acetate.

Dry Distillation Method (Simpler)

  1. Materials: Calcium acetate (produced from vinegar + limestone), heat source 450°C+
  2. Preparation: Mix 200g calcium acetate powder with 50g sand, load into metal tube closed at one end
  3. Heating: Heat tube to 450-500°C in furnace/hot coal bed. Calcium acetate decomposes: Ca(CH₃COO)₂ → CaCO₃ + CH₃COCH₃↑
  4. Collection: Vapors pass through condenser cooled by water/ice. Acetone condenses below 56°C
  5. Yield: ~80% of theoretical (calcium acetate mass × 0.74)
  6. Purity: ~95% acetone, 5% water. Redist illation improves to 98-99%

Calcium Acetate Preparation

From vinegar (5% acetic acid) + limestone (CaCO₃): Mix 1000g vinegar (50g acetic acid) with 200g limestone, allow reaction to complete (vigorous bubbling as CO₂ releases), evaporate water over low heat. Calcium acetate crystallizes. Dry at 100°C. Theoretical yield: ~28g calcium acetate from 100g vinegar + appropriate limestone.

Fractional Distillation

Separation of multiple components with boiling points within 20-30°C range.

Example: Mixture of Ethanol (78°C) + Ethyl Acetate (77°C)

Very close boiling points make separation difficult. Solution: tall packed column (50-100cm), slow heating rate (increase temperature 2-3°C per hour), high reflux ratio (allow vapor to recondense and redrop multiple times through packing).

Procedure: Heat to 76°C, collect slowly. At 76-77°C, ethyl acetate preferentially condenses (higher molecular weight, slightly higher bp). At 77-78°C, mixture emerges. At 78°C+, pure ethanol. By slowing heating and collecting fractions every 0.5°C rise, achieve separation into 90%+ components.

Column Performance

Efficiency measured as "theoretical plates" (simplification: each theoretical plate is one equilibration step). Packed column ~1-2 theoretical plates per 10cm height. To achieve 10-plate separation (roughly 99% vs. 1% component concentration), need 100cm+ column height with efficient packing.

Safety and Hazards

Ethanol vapor: Flammable at concentrations >3.3% in air (lower explosion limit). Above 19% in air, not flammable (too lean). Maintain ventilation, avoid open flame during distillation. Use electric heat (safer than gas/coal for solvent work).

Acetone: Extremely flammable, boils at 56°C (low temperature). Vapor highly flammable even at room temperature. NO open flames, use electric heating only. Condenser cooling critical (ice bath essential) to prevent vapor escape.

Turpentine: Flammable, but higher boiling point (155°C) means vapor less volatile at room temperature. Heat source must be oil bath or electrical (not direct flame touching still).

Methanol poisoning: If fermentation contaminated or heads distillate consumed, methanol causes blindness/death. Never taste distillate to check. Always discard heads (first 5-10% of distillate) - contains methanol/acetaldehyde.

Apparatus integrity: Cracks in condenser allow hot vapor escape. Inspect glassware for cracks before use. Pressure buildup in sealed still is dangerous - always use open-top collection flask or pressure relief valve.

Critical Safety: Solvent distillation produces flammable vapors. Work outdoors or in well-ventilated fume hood only. No open flames within 5 meters. Have fire extinguisher (Class B: flammable liquid) nearby. Shut down immediately if fire detected - let fire burn or use CO₂ extinguisher, water ineffective for organic solvent fires.

Testing Purity

Boiling Point Test

Pure ethanol boils at 78.4°C. Measure boiling point of your product: heat slowly, record thermometer reading when vapor forms (first drops condense). 78-79°C = good purity (>90%). 75-78°C = some water present (85-90% ethanol). <75°C = mostly water, needs redist illation.

Density Test

Pure ethanol has density 0.789 g/cm³. Measure using hydrometer or calculation: weigh 100mL of product, divide mass by 100mL. 0.79 g/cm³ = ~90% pure. 0.80 g/cm³ = ~85% pure. 0.82 g/cm³ = ~80% pure. Formula: ethanol% ≈ 100 × (1 - (measured density - 0.789) / 0.211).

Flame Test

Pure ethanol burns with colorless/pale blue flame. Water-containing ethanol burns with yellow-orange flame (slower, cooler reaction). Not quantitative but qualitative indication of purity.

Ethanol: Most versatile. Dissolves many organic compounds, mixes with water, non-toxic at low concentrations. Used as preservative (kills bacteria in tinctures), fuel for lamps/vehicles (mixed 10% with gasoline), solvent for plant extracts.

Acetone: Strong solvent for polymers/resins, dissolves many adhesives. Used in cleanup (excellent degreaser). Flammability risk makes handling difficult.

Turpentine: Traditional paint thinner, wood cleaner. Can harm skin with prolonged exposure - ventilate well. Some people sensitive to pine allergens.

Ethanol Proof Ratings and Practical Distillation Limits

Alcohol proof is defined as twice the alcohol percentage by volume (%ABV). Standard distillation cannot exceed approximately 95-96% ethanol (190-192 proof) because ethanol and water form an azeotrope—a mixture that cannot be further separated by simple distillation.

The Ethanol-Water Azeotrope

At 95.6% ethanol and 4.4% water, the vapor composition equals the liquid composition—a point where no further separation occurs through heating. This azeotropic point is why home distillation plateaus at approximately 190 proof (95% ABV). Attempting further distillation only recycles the same mixture without increasing ethanol concentration.

Practical Distillation Strategy

For Tinctures: 80-100 proof is sufficient and easier to achieve. Heating to lower proof (faster process) is acceptable since you only need good solvent properties, not maximum alcohol concentration.

For Disinfection: Target 140 proof (70% ABV). Interestingly, pure 190-proof ethanol is less effective as a disinfectant than 70% ethanol because pure alcohol denatures the outermost proteins too quickly, preventing penetration into cell membranes. The 30% water content allows slower denaturation and better cell penetration.

For Fuel: Target 160-190 proof. The higher the concentration, the better the combustion. Must be completely anhydrous (water-free).

Maximum Achievable: Home distillation with a reflux condenser typically yields 160-180 proof without heroic efforts. Reaching 190 proof requires very efficient condensation and repeated distillation passes.

Preventing Methanol in Home Distillation

When distilling fermented liquids (wine, beer, fruit mash), methanol (wood alcohol) can form. Methanol boils at 148°F (64°C), slightly lower than ethanol (173°F / 78°C). In industrial distillation, the "heads" (first fraction, containing methanol) are separated and discarded. In home distillation:

  • Discard the first 5-10% of distillate (the "heads")
  • Collect the main body (the "hearts")
  • The final fraction (the "tails") contains undesirable heavier compounds
  • Only use the "hearts" for consumption or high-quality applications
