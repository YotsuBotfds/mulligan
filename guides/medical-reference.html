First Aid Essentials

Wound Assessment and Cleaning

Initial Wound Assessment:
  1. Stop the bleeding (see bleeding control section)
  2. Examine wound for debris, foreign objects, or contamination
  3. Assess wound depth: superficial (epidermis only), partial thickness (dermis involved), or full thickness (extends to muscle/bone)
  4. Note wound edges: clean cut (lower infection risk) vs. jagged/crushed (higher risk)
  5. Check for signs of infection: pus, redness, warmth, foul odor, red streaking toward heart
  6. Determine if professional medical care is needed based on depth, contamination, and location
Wound Cleaning Procedure:
  1. Wash hands thoroughly with soap and clean water
  2. Use clean gloves if available
  3. Irrigate wound with clean water (boiled and cooled if off-grid) using a syringe or clean cloth
  4. For contaminated wounds, irrigate with high pressure (20cc syringe with 18-gauge needle creates appropriate pressure)
  5. Remove visible debris with clean tweezers (sterilized in boiling water if possible)
  6. If wound appears infected or has dead tissue (necrotic), gently scrub with soft brush or cloth to remove bacteria
  7. Rinse thoroughly until water runs clear
  8. Pat dry with clean cloth (don't rub)
  9. Apply antiseptic (iodine, chlorhexidine, hydrogen peroxide, or alcohol if nothing else available)

Bleeding Control - The Cascade Method

Step 1: Direct Pressure (Most Effective)
  1. Apply firm, direct pressure with clean cloth or gauze
  2. Maintain pressure for 10-15 minutes without checking the wound
  3. If blood soaks through, apply additional cloth without removing first layer
  4. Once bleeding stops, leave cloth in place and secure with bandage
  5. Elevate the injured area above the heart if possible
Step 2: Pressure Points (If Direct Pressure Fails)

Apply pressure to artery between wound and heart:

  • Arm bleeding: Brachial artery - press inside upper arm between shoulder and elbow against bone
  • Leg bleeding: Femoral artery - press in groin crease where leg meets body
  • Hand/forearm: Radial and ulnar arteries - press inside wrist
  • Neck: Carotid artery - press gently on side of neck (single hand only, don't compress both sides)
Step 3: Tourniquet Application (Last Resort for Limb Bleeding)

When to use: Severe limb bleeding that won't stop with direct pressure, or when manual pressure cannot be maintained

  1. Place tourniquet 2-3 inches above the wound (between wound and heart)
  2. If wound is on hand/foot, place just above ankle or wrist
  3. Tighten until bleeding completely stops and pulse below tourniquet is no longer palpable
  4. Mark tourniquet with time applied (critical for later treatment)
  5. Reassess every 30 minutes - loosen if bleeding has completely stopped
  6. Maximum safe time: 2 hours in cool environment; less in warm weather (tissue damage begins)
  7. If tourniquet must stay on longer than 2 hours, gradually loosen every 15 minutes for 30 seconds to allow some circulation
Note on Tourniquets: Modern data shows that properly applied tourniquets are safer than previously thought. They should be used for life-threatening limb hemorrhage when direct pressure is insufficient.

Burn Treatment by Degree

Burn Degree Depth Appearance Treatment
1st Degree Epidermis only Red, painful, no blistering (like sunburn) Cool water (10-20 min), moisturize, pain relief. Heals in 3-7 days.
2nd Degree Epidermis + dermis Red/blotchy, painful, fluid-filled blisters Cool water, don't pop blisters, sterile dressing, pain relief. Risk of infection. Heals in 2-3 weeks.
3rd Degree Through dermis to subcutaneous tissue White/charred, painless (nerve damage), leathery appearance Cover with clean cloth, elevate if possible, seek immediate professional help. May require grafting.
Burn Treatment Protocol:
  1. Remove person from heat source
  2. Cool the burn: immerse in cool water (not ice) for 10-20 minutes
  3. Remove restrictive items (rings, bracelets) before swelling occurs
  4. For 1st degree: apply moisturizer, take pain relief medication
  5. For 2nd degree: do NOT pop blisters; apply sterile non-adhesive dressing; elevate if possible; watch for infection
  6. For 3rd degree: cover with clean dry cloth; elevate above heart; give pain relief; seek professional help immediately
  7. For extensive burns (>10% body surface): treat for shock, keep person warm, provide fluids if conscious
Burn Surface Area Estimation (Rule of 9s): Head/neck = 9%, each arm = 9%, chest = 9%, abdomen = 9%, each leg = 18%, back = 18%. Burns >15% need professional care. Burns >20% are life-threatening.

Fracture and Sprain Management

RICE Protocol (Rest, Ice, Compression, Elevation):
  1. Rest: Stop activity immediately, keep limb immobile
  2. Ice: Apply cold (wrapped ice pack or cold water) for 15-20 minutes, repeat every 2-3 hours for first 48 hours
  3. Compression: Wrap with elastic bandage to reduce swelling
  4. Elevation: Keep injured limb above heart level
  5. Continue for 48 hours or until swelling plateaus
Fracture vs. Sprain: Sprains involve ligament damage (joint injury), fractures involve bone breaks. Both cause pain, swelling, and limited mobility. When in doubt, treat as fracture and immobilize until professional evaluation.

CPR Procedure (Hands-Only for Untrained Responders)

Cardiopulmonary Resuscitation - Adult:
  1. Check responsiveness: tap shoulders, shout "Are you okay?"
  2. If unresponsive and not breathing normally, begin CPR immediately
  3. Position person on firm, flat surface
  4. Place heel of hand on center of chest between nipples
  5. Place other hand on top, lock fingers together
  6. Keep arms straight, lean forward
  7. Push hard and fast on chest: compress at least 2 inches deep at rate of 100-120 compressions per minute
  8. Allow chest to recoil completely between compressions
  9. Continue without interruption until: emergency help arrives, patient begins breathing, you are exhausted
  10. With rescue breaths: After 30 compressions, give 2 breaths (30:2 ratio)
CPR - Child (1-8 years):
  1. Use same hand placement as adult
  2. May use single hand if child is very small
  3. Compress at least 2 inches or 1/3 of chest depth
  4. Use rescue breaths: after 30 compressions, give 2 gentle breaths covering mouth and nose
  5. Continue until help arrives or signs of life return
CPR - Infant (under 1 year):
  1. Check responsiveness and breathing
  2. Place 2 fingers (index and middle) on breastbone just below nipple line
  3. Compress 1.5 inches at rate of 100-120 per minute
  4. After 30 compressions, give 2 gentle breaths (infant-mouth-to-mouth-nose)
  5. Continue until emergency help arrives

Choking Response

Conscious Adult - Heimlich Maneuver:
  1. Ask "Are you choking?" - if they can't cough/speak, act immediately
  2. Stand behind the person
  3. Place fist just above navel, below ribcage
  4. Grasp fist with other hand
  5. Perform quick, hard, upward thrusts into abdomen
  6. Repeat thrusts until object is dislodged or person becomes unconscious
  7. If person becomes unconscious, begin CPR
Infant Choking (under 1 year):
  1. If infant cannot cry/cough, give 5 back blows: hold infant face-down on your forearm, support chin, deliver sharp blows between shoulder blades
  2. Then give 5 chest thrusts: turn infant face-up, place 2 fingers on lower breastbone, deliver upward thrusts
  3. Repeat cycle until object is expelled or infant becomes unresponsive
  4. Do NOT perform abdominal thrusts on infants (risk of organ damage)

Shock Recognition and Treatment

Signs of Shock:
  • Pale, cool, clammy skin
  • Rapid weak pulse (>100 bpm)
  • Rapid, shallow breathing
  • Anxiety, confusion, or altered mental status
  • Nausea or vomiting
  • Decreased urine output
Shock Treatment Protocol:
  1. Lay person flat
  2. Elevate legs 12 inches above heart level (unless head/spinal injury suspected)
  3. Keep person warm with blankets but avoid overheating
  4. Control any bleeding with direct pressure
  5. Provide fluids if conscious and can swallow (sips of water)
  6. Monitor vital signs: pulse, breathing, consciousness
  7. Do not give food or drink if surgery may be needed
  8. Seek professional medical help immediately
  9. Monitor for deterioration; be prepared to give CPR

Making Herbal Antiseptics & Pain Relief

Natural Antiseptics from Plants

Raw Honey Poultice
  • Properties: Raw honey kills bacteria through hydrogen peroxide production and osmotic effect
  • Application: Apply directly to clean wound, cover with cloth. Change dressing daily or when honey becomes diluted.
  • Effectiveness: One of the best natural antiseptics. Studies show it's effective against MRSA and other resistant bacteria.
  • Caution: Do not use on infants under 1 year (botulism risk). Process honey from reputable sources.
Garlic Preparation
  • Active compound: Allicin, produced when garlic cells are crushed
  • Fresh preparation: Crush 3-4 garlic cloves, add to 1 cup boiled water (cooled), soak wound 2-3 times daily
  • Poultice: Crush garlic, mix with honey or lard, apply to wound. Pungent smell is normal.
  • Effectiveness: Broad-spectrum antibacterial activity. Works against common wound pathogens.
  • Caution: Can irritate sensitive skin; dilute if irritation occurs
Thyme Tea Wash
  • Preparation: Steep 2 tablespoons dried thyme in 1 cup boiling water for 15-20 minutes, strain and cool
  • Use: Wash wound 3-4 times daily, or soak cloth and apply as compress
  • Properties: Thymol is a powerful antimicrobial. Also reduces inflammation and promotes healing.
  • Advantage: Gentle, can be used on sensitive skin without irritation
Plantain Poultice
  • Preparation: Pick young plantain leaves, chew or crush to release juices, apply directly to wound
  • Properties: Promotes blood clotting, reduces inflammation, antimicrobial compounds
  • Traditional use: Called "nature's bandaid" - draws out infection, promotes healing
  • Best for: Infected wounds, splinter removal, bee stings
Charcoal & Clay Paste
  • Preparation: Crush activated charcoal (from burned wood), mix with bentonite clay and water to create paste
  • Use: Apply thick layer to infected wound, cover with cloth, change daily
  • Properties: Absorbs toxins and bacteria. Draws out infection. Charcoal is antimicrobial.
  • Caution: Stains skin/cloth dark color; temporary effect

Herbal Pain Relief

Willow Bark Decoction (Natural Aspirin)
  • Active ingredient: Salicin, converted to salicylic acid in body (same compound as aspirin)
  • Preparation: Harvest inner bark from willow branches (white, young growth). Dry and chop finely.
  • Decoction: Boil 1 tablespoon dried bark in 1 cup water for 5-10 minutes, strain, drink warm
  • Dosage: 1 cup 2-3 times daily for pain and inflammation. Effects take 15-30 minutes.
  • Caution: Do not use if allergic to aspirin. Not suitable for pregnancy. Can cause stomach upset; take with food.
Turmeric & Ginger Paste (Anti-inflammatory)
  • Ingredients: 1 tablespoon turmeric powder + 1 tablespoon fresh ginger paste + honey to bind
  • Internal use: Mix with warm milk/water, drink 1-2 times daily
  • External use: Apply directly to inflamed areas, cover with cloth
  • Properties: Curcumin in turmeric is potent anti-inflammatory. Ginger adds pain relief.
  • Timeline: Effects build over 3-5 days with regular use. Most effective for chronic pain.
Arnica Compress (Bruise & Muscle Pain)
  • Preparation: Steep dried arnica flowers (1 tablespoon per cup) in boiling water 10-15 minutes
  • Use: Soak cloth in cooled liquid, apply as compress to bruised/sore areas
  • Properties: Reduces bruising, inflammation, speeds healing of muscle injuries
  • Caution: Never ingest arnica (toxic). Use only topically. Do not apply to broken skin.

Wound Care & Closure

Advanced Wound Cleaning

Irrigation Levels by Contamination:
  1. Clean wounds: Gentle irrigation with clean water, 500-1000 mL
  2. Contaminated wounds: Forceful irrigation with 1-2 liters clean water using 20cc syringe and needle
  3. Heavily contaminated/dirty wounds: High-volume irrigation (3+ liters), consider mild soap solution followed by clean water rinse
Debridement (Removing Dead/Damaged Tissue):
  1. Clean hands and use sterile gloves if available
  2. Identify dead tissue: gray/black color, loss of sensation when touched
  3. Use sterile scissors or scalpel (boiled metal if necessary) to carefully trim away necrotic tissue
  4. Trim only obviously dead tissue - err on side of caution
  5. Irrigation after debridement to remove tissue fragments
  6. Monitor for infection in following days

Suturing and Wound Closure Techniques

When to Suture vs. Leave Open:
Scenario Recommendation Rationale
Clean wound, less than 6-8 hours old Can suture Low infection risk if properly cleaned
Dirty/contaminated wound, more than 12 hours old Leave open (delayed closure) High infection risk; allow cleaning/treatment first
Heavily infected wound with pus Leave open, drain if needed Suturing traps infection
Bite wounds (animal or human) Usually leave open High bacterial contamination, anaerobic bacteria
Puncture wounds Leave open unless superficial Deep punctures risk abscess formation
Suturing Materials and Techniques:
  1. Materials: Use non-absorbable suture (silk, nylon) for skin closure, 2-0 to 4-0 gauge for most wounds
  2. Needle selection: Curved needle for skin (easier to use than straight)
  3. Preparation: Sterilize needle and thread in boiling water (10 minutes) if not pre-sterilized
  4. Hand position: Hold needle in middle with needle driver, don't touch needle tip
  5. Stitching technique: Pierce skin 2-3mm from wound edge, cross wound, exit 2-3mm from opposite edge
  6. Stitch spacing: Place stitches 5-10mm apart depending on wound location and tension
  7. Knot tying: Tie with square knots (right-left-right for 4-knot security)
  8. Stitch removal timing: Face = 3-5 days; scalp/arm = 7 days; leg/body = 10 days; joint areas = 14 days
Suturing Complications: If wound becomes increasingly red, swollen, warm, or produces pus after closure, the sutures may need removal to allow drainage. This indicates infection.

Butterfly Closures and Steri-Strips

When to Use:
  • Small clean cuts less than 1/4 inch deep
  • Wounds where minimal scarring is desired
  • Areas of high mobility (joints) - provides flexibility
  • Patients who prefer not to have stitches
Application:
  1. Clean and dry wound thoroughly
  2. Cut steri-strip or create butterfly from medical tape
  3. Pull wound edges together
  4. Apply strips perpendicular to wound (across, not along)
  5. Leave in place 3-5 days, replace as needed
  6. Gradually allow strips to peel off naturally

Wound Packing

For Deep Wounds or Cavities:
  1. Clean wound thoroughly with irrigation
  2. If left open to heal, pack with sterile gauze to prevent premature surface closure
  3. Use iodoform-impregnated gauze if available (antimicrobial), or plain sterile gauze
  4. Pack loosely - not tight - to allow drainage and gas exchange
  5. Change packing daily or every other day
  6. Watch for signs of infection: increased drainage, odor, fever
  7. Gradually stop packing as wound fills from bottom up

Infection Signs and Monitoring

Early Infection Signs (Act Immediately):
  • Increasing redness around wound edges
  • Warmth and swelling
  • Pus drainage (yellow/green/bloody)
  • Foul or unusual odor
  • Pain that increases after initial 24-48 hours
  • Red streaking extending from wound toward heart (lymphangitis)
Managing Wound Infection:
  1. If sutures present, remove them to allow drainage
  2. Increase irrigation and cleaning frequency to 2-3 times daily
  3. Apply warm compresses to encourage drainage
  4. If abscess forms (collection of pus), may need drainage procedure
  5. Start antibiotics if available: oral amoxicillin-clavulanate 500/125 mg three times daily, or doxycycline 100 mg twice daily
  6. Monitor for spreading infection/cellulitis (red streaking, fever, chills)
  7. Seek professional care if infection worsens or doesn't improve in 48-72 hours

Bandaging Techniques

Proper Bandaging Method:
  1. Layer 1 - Contact layer: Sterile non-adhesive dressing directly on wound
  2. Layer 2 - Absorption layer: Sterile gauze pad(s) to absorb drainage
  3. Layer 3 - Compression layer: Elastic bandage wrap or gauze roller to hold in place
  4. Tension: Tight enough to control bleeding/swelling but not cut off circulation (you should fit one finger under the bandage)
  5. Coverage: Extend bandage 2 inches beyond wound edges
  6. Secure: Use medical tape, clips, or tie ends
  7. Changing: Change daily or when soaked through, unless healing well then every 2-3 days
Signs of Poor Bandaging: Numbness, tingling, increased pain, color change of limb beyond bandage (pale or blue), or inability to move fingers/toes indicates the bandage is too tight - loosen immediately.

Common Illness Treatment

Fever Management

Fever Treatment Protocol:
  1. Identify source: Determine if fever is from infection, inflammation, or medication reaction
  2. Physical cooling: Remove excess clothing and blankets
  3. Hydration: Drink plenty of water, electrolyte solutions (see ORS recipe below)
  4. Cool compresses: Apply to forehead, under arms, groin (major blood vessels)
  5. Sponging: Use lukewarm water (not cold) on skin to encourage heat loss through evaporation
  6. Medication: Acetaminophen 650-1000mg every 4-6 hours OR ibuprofen 400-600mg every 6-8 hours
  7. Monitoring: Check temperature every 2-4 hours
  8. When to worry: Fever >104¬∞F (40¬∞C), fever in infant, persistent fever >7 days, fever with stiff neck or rash
Note on Fever: Low fever is body's defense mechanism. Only treat if uncomfortable or if fever becomes dangerously high. Many infections resolve within 3-7 days with supportive care alone.

Dehydration Treatment

Rehydration Solution (ORS) Recipe:
  1. Boil 1 liter of clean water and cool to room temperature
  2. Add 6 teaspoons (30ml) of sugar or honey
  3. Add 1/2 teaspoon (2.5g) of salt
  4. Add 1/4 teaspoon (1.25g) of salt substitute (potassium chloride) if available
  5. Stir until dissolved
  6. Solution is good for 24 hours at room temperature, longer if refrigerated
Dehydration Management by Severity:
  1. Mild (dry mouth, thirst): Drink sips of water frequently, ORS solution
  2. Moderate (dry mouth, weakness, dizziness, decreased urination): ORS solution 50-100ml per kg body weight over 4 hours, then maintenance (5-10ml/kg for each loose stool)
  3. Severe (confusion, extreme weakness, no urine output): Need IV fluids - seek immediate professional help. Until then, give ORS solution in small frequent doses
  4. Continue giving fluids after symptoms improve to prevent relapse
  5. Gradually return to normal diet once able to eat

Respiratory Infections

Common Cold/Upper Respiratory Infection:
  • Symptoms: Runny nose, sneezing, sore throat, mild cough, congestion, malaise
  • Treatment: Rest, fluids, throat lozenges, honey for cough, saline nasal drops, fever reduction
  • Duration: 3-7 days, no antibiotics needed (viral)
Bronchitis:
  • Symptoms: Persistent dry or productive cough (>1 week), chest discomfort, fatigue, shortness of breath
  • Viral treatment: Rest, fluids, cough suppressant at night, expectorant during day, honey, steam inhalation
  • Bacterial signs: Colored sputum (yellow/green), fever, progression - may need antibiotics (amoxicillin 500mg three times daily)
  • Recovery: 2-3 weeks typical
Pneumonia (Requires Antibiotics):
  • Symptoms: High fever, chest pain with breathing, productive cough, shortness of breath, fatigue
  • Treatment: Amoxicillin 500mg three times daily OR doxycycline 100mg twice daily for 7-10 days
  • Supportive care: Fluids, rest, fever management, oxygen if available
  • Warning signs: Severe shortness of breath, confusion, blue lips - seek professional help immediately

Gastrointestinal Illness

Acute Gastroenteritis (Food Poisoning/Viral Gastro):
  1. Initial phase: Rest digestive system - no solid food for first 6-12 hours
  2. Fluids: Sip water, broth, ORS solution frequently in small amounts
  3. Anti-nausea: Ginger tea, peppermint tea, avoid strong smells
  4. Medication: Bismuth subsalicylate (Pepto-Bismol) or loperamide (Imodium) for diarrhea - BUT avoid if fever or bloody stools present
  5. Gradual feeding: After 12-24 hours, introduce bland foods (crackers, rice, toast, broth)
  6. Recovery timeline: Most viral gastro resolves in 24-48 hours; bacterial may last 3-5 days
  7. Antibiotics: Only if bacterial (prolonged illness, bloody stools, fever) - ciprofloxacin 500mg twice daily or doxycycline 100mg twice daily
Severe Dehydration Signs: No urination for 8+ hours, extreme weakness, confusion, rapid heartbeat, dry mouth and eyes. This is life-threatening in children and elderly - aggressive rehydration needed.

Urinary Tract Infections

Symptoms:
  • Dysuria (painful urination)
  • Frequency (frequent urination, especially at night)
  • Urgency (sudden strong need to urinate)
  • Suprapubic pain (pain above pubic bone)
  • Fever, chills, lower back pain (indicates upper UTI/pyelonephritis)
UTI Management:
  1. Prevention: Drink plenty of water, urinate frequently, wipe front to back (females), void after intercourse
  2. Home care: Increased fluid intake, cranberry juice (mild benefit), heat application to lower abdomen
  3. Antibiotics: Ciprofloxacin 250mg twice daily for 3 days (uncomplicated) or 7-14 days if complicated/pyelonephritis
  4. Alternative antibiotics: Trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole DS one tablet twice daily for 3-7 days
  5. Pain management: Acetaminophen or ibuprofen
  6. When to treat urgently: Fever >101¬∞F, flank pain, nausea/vomiting (upper UTI) - need antibiotics immediately

Skin Infections

Cellulitis (Spreading Bacterial Skin Infection):
  • Signs: Red, warm, swollen, tender area; may have fever, red streaking (indicates serious)
  • Treatment: Antibiotic immediately - amoxicillin-clavulanate 500/125mg three times daily OR doxycycline 100mg twice daily for 7-10 days
  • Care: Elevate affected area, warm compresses, clean gently daily
  • Warning: Red streaking toward heart indicates systemic infection - urgent medical attention needed
Abscess (Localized Collection of Pus):
  • Signs: Localized swelling, fluctuance (feels like fluid inside), warmth, pain
  • Early treatment: Warm compresses may allow drainage, antibiotics
  • If need drainage: See field surgery section for incision and drainage procedure
  • Do NOT squeeze: Risks pushing infection deeper
Fungal Infections (Tinea, Athlete's Foot, Candida):
  • Signs: Itching, redness, white coating or scaling
  • Treatment: Antifungal cream (miconazole, clotrimazole) applied twice daily for 2-4 weeks
  • Keep dry: Good hygiene, keep area clean and dry
  • Oral for severe: Fluconazole 150mg as single dose (if available) for yeast infections

Dental Emergencies

Tooth Pain Management:
  1. Immediate relief: Ibuprofen 600-800mg or acetaminophen 650-1000mg
  2. Topical: Clove oil (eugenol) on affected area - natural anesthetic
  3. Salt water rinse: Dissolve 1/2 teaspoon salt in 8oz warm water, rinse 2-3 times daily
  4. Cold compress: Apply to cheek for 15 minutes at a time to numb pain
  5. Avoid: Very hot/cold foods, hard foods, chewing on affected side
  6. If abscess: Oral antibiotics - amoxicillin 500mg three times daily for 7 days
Knocked Out Tooth (Avulsed Tooth):
  1. If tooth is intact and fresh, recovery is possible with prompt action
  2. Handle by crown only, not the root
  3. Rinse tooth gently with saline (don't scrub)
  4. Try to place back in socket - bite gently on gauze to hold in place
  5. If can't place, keep tooth in saline or milk in a container
  6. Seek emergency dental care as soon as possible (best within 1 hour)

Pulse (Heart Rate)

How to measure:

  • Use radial artery: place 2-3 fingers (index, middle, ring) on inner wrist below thumb
  • Or carotid artery: place 2 fingers on side of neck between muscle and trachea
  • Count beats for 60 seconds, or count for 15 seconds and multiply by 4
  • Note regularity: regular or irregular rhythm
  • Note strength: strong, weak, or thready (barely palpable)

Respiration Rate

How to measure:

  • Watch chest/abdomen rise and fall (one complete breath = one in + one out)
  • Count for 60 seconds at rest
  • Note effort: labored (difficult) or easy
  • Note pattern: regular or irregular
  • Assess depth: shallow, normal, or deep breathing

Blood Pressure

Manual Measurement (without machine):

  • Approximate systolic: locate radial pulse, apply pressure to arm until pulse disappears, note when it returns = systolic
  • Full reading requires stethoscope and cuff (may not be available)
  • Estimate as normal (pulse palpable), low (weak/absent pulse), or high (flushed face)

Temperature

Measurement Methods:

  • Oral: Thermometer under tongue, 3 minutes, no hot/cold drinks first
  • Axillary (armpit): Less accurate, add 1¬∞F to reading
  • Rectal: Most accurate, use 1 minute
  • No thermometer: Feel forehead and compare to yourself; can feel for fever

Capillary Refill Test

Indicator of Circulation and Shock:
  1. Press on fingernail or skin until it blanches (turns white)
  2. Release pressure
  3. Normal: color returns in <2 seconds (good circulation)
  4. Delayed: returns in >2 seconds (indicates poor circulation, shock)

Pupil Response Test

Indicator of Neurological Status:
  1. In dark environment, observe pupil size
  2. Shine light in one eye
  3. Normal: pupil constricts (gets smaller) in 1-2 seconds
  4. Both pupils should respond equally (consensual response)
  5. Abnormal: dilated pupils (pain, intoxication, serious head injury), pinpoint pupils (opioid use, stroke)
  6. Unequal pupils: possible head injury or stroke

Field Surgery Basics

Critical Legal and Ethical Note: Field surgery should ONLY be attempted when professional medical help is unavailable and the condition is immediately life-threatening. Use of these procedures without proper training and in situations where medical help is available could result in serious harm or legal consequences.

When Surgery is Necessary in Field Conditions

Situations Warranting Surgical Intervention:
  • Uncontrolled life-threatening hemorrhage not responding to pressure/tourniquets
  • Tension pneumothorax (collapsed lung with breathing difficulty)
  • Airway obstruction that cannot be cleared by other means
  • Compartment syndrome (muscle swelling cutting off circulation)
  • Large abscess with systemic infection/sepsis
  • Foreign body deeply embedded causing major bleeding or organ dysfunction
  • Bowel perforation with peritonitis

Abscess Drainage (I&D - Incision and Drainage)

Procedure for Simple Abscess Drainage:
  1. Preparation: Sterilize area with iodine or alcohol. Gather sterile supplies: scalpel, gauze, drainage material
  2. Anesthesia: If available, inject local anesthetic (lidocaine) around abscess perimeter
  3. Incision: Make small incision (0.5-1cm) over point of maximum fluctuance (feels like fluid)
  4. Direction: Incise along skin lines when possible to minimize scarring
  5. Expression: Gentle pressure to express pus (may be copious)
  6. Irrigation: Flush cavity with saline to remove debris
  7. Drainage: May leave small drain (gauze wick) if abscess was large
  8. Dressing: Sterile gauge packing if leaving open to drain
  9. Follow-up: Change dressing daily, watch for signs of continued infection

Wound Exploration and Foreign Body Removal

Removing Embedded Objects:
  1. Do NOT remove: Objects impaled in chest, abdomen, or large vessels - stabilize and seek help
  2. Assess: Determine depth and proximity to vessels/nerves
  3. Anesthesia: Local or regional anesthesia if available
  4. Exposure: Gently open wound to visualize object without traumatizing tissue
  5. Remove: Slow, careful extraction along the angle it entered - pulling at wrong angle causes more damage
  6. Hemorrhage: If bleeding occurs, apply direct pressure
  7. Irrigation: Thoroughly irrigate wound after removal
  8. Closure: Close wound or leave open depending on contamination/infection risk

Emergency Airway Management

Jaw Thrust Maneuver (if neck injury suspected):
  1. Position behind patient's head
  2. Place hands on either side of patient's face
  3. Use thumb to hold mouth open while jaw is thrust forward
  4. This opens airway without moving neck
Head Tilt-Chin Lift (if no neck injury):
  1. Place hand on forehead, tilt head back gently
  2. With other hand, lift chin forward
  3. This straightens airway
Emergency Cricothyrotomy (Last Resort for Complete Airway Obstruction):
  1. When: Complete airway obstruction (choking not relieved, severe facial trauma, tongue obstruction) AND cannot intubate
  2. Anatomy: Locate cricoid cartilage (hard ring below Adam's apple)
  3. Incision: Make small horizontal incision through skin and membrane between cricoid and thyroid
  4. Insertion: Insert tracheostomy tube or needle (if available) into opening
  5. Breathing: Person should be able to breathe through opening
  6. Stabilize: Secure tube to prevent dislodgement
  7. Seek help: This is emergency measure only - get to professional help immediately

Improvised Surgical Instruments

Sterilization Methods (Off-Grid):
  • Boiling: 20+ minutes in water kills most pathogens
  • Fire: Hold metal instruments in flame for 30 seconds
  • Pressure cooker: 15-20 minutes at high pressure (most effective if available)
  • Alcohol: 70% isopropyl for 5+ minutes (not complete sterilization but helps)
Improvised Tools:
  • Scalpel: Sharp knife blade, razor blade, or glass shard (sterilized)
  • Forceps: Tweezers, pliers (sterilized) for holding/removing objects
  • Needle/thread: Sewing needle and thread (sterilized) for suturing
  • Syringe: Improvise with bulb or plunger-type syringe for irrigation
  • Tourniquet: Belt, cloth strip, or cord
  • Dressings: Clean cloth, boiled and dried

Orthopedic Emergencies

Splinting Techniques for Different Bones

General Splinting Principles:
  1. Immobilize the joint above and below the fracture
  2. Remove tight clothing/jewelry before swelling occurs
  3. Check circulation: can feel pulse, normal color/temperature distal to injury
  4. Check sensation: person can feel distal to injury
  5. Use padding to protect bony prominences
  6. Apply ice after splinting to reduce swelling (20 min on, 20 min off)
  7. Elevate when possible
  8. Do NOT move the fracture during splinting
Arm Fracture Splinting (Upper Arm/Humerus):
  1. Use sling to immobilize arm across chest
  2. Can add padding and wrap arm to body for additional support
  3. Keep elbow at 90 degree angle
  4. Check circulation in hand regularly
Forearm/Wrist Fracture Splinting:
  1. Align forearm in neutral position (palm down)
  2. Splint from just below elbow to beyond fingertips
  3. Use padded board, roll of cloth, or cardboard
  4. Immobilize with sling and wrap around body
  5. Keep wrist elevated
Hand Fracture Splinting:
  1. For individual finger: tape to adjacent finger (buddy tape), or use small splint
  2. Keep hand elevated in functional position (as if holding golf ball)
  3. Apply ice, elevate
Leg Fracture Splinting (Femur/Thigh):
  1. This is a major fracture with severe bleeding risk
  2. Apply traction splint if possible (realigns bone, reduces bleeding and pain)
  3. Position person on back, measure from groin to 4 inches past heel
  4. Wrap traction splint around leg, create anchor at ankle using cloth loops
  5. Apply gentle longitudinal traction to realign limb
  6. Without traction splint: immobilize with padded boards on both sides of leg, secure at multiple points
  7. Monitor circulation: check foot color, temperature, pulse at ankle
Knee Fracture/Dislocation:
  1. Immobilize knee in position found - do NOT try to straighten
  2. Use pillow or padding under and around knee
  3. Wrap with elastic bandage for support
  4. Elevate leg
  5. Apply ice
Ankle Fracture Splinting:
  1. Remove shoe carefully
  2. Apply ice before swelling becomes severe
  3. Use pillow splint: wrap pillow around ankle and foot, secure with cloth
  4. Or use rigid splint: padded board on both sides of ankle
  5. Elevate foot above heart
  6. Do NOT try to straighten ankle if deformed

Reduction of Simple Dislocations

Critical: Only attempt reduction if: (1) you have some training, (2) professional help is not available, (3) there is no associated fracture, (4) you have adequate pain relief. Get X-ray confirmation when possible after reduction.
Shoulder Dislocation (Anterior - Most Common):
  1. Check first: X-ray if possible to rule out fracture
  2. Anesthesia: Give strong pain relief before attempting reduction
  3. Technique: Patient lying on back, bend elbow to 90 degrees, gently rotate arm outward (external rotation) until shoulder pops back into socket
  4. Alternative: Some methods use gentle traction on arm while internally rotating
  5. Success: You will feel/hear the shoulder slip back into place
  6. After: Immobilize in sling for 2-4 weeks, ice, pain relief
  7. Complications: Stop immediately if severe pain, signs of nerve/blood vessel damage
Finger Dislocation (Most Common Hand Dislocation):
  1. Anesthesia: Give pain relief - even local if available
  2. Technique: Apply gentle longitudinal traction to finger while straightening it
  3. Direction: Usually finger is bent backward - apply traction and gently flex forward
  4. Success: Feel/see the joint slide back into place
  5. After: Buddy tape to adjacent finger, ice, elevation
  6. Check: Make sure finger looks straight and aligned

Sling Construction

Improvised Arm Sling:
  1. Take cloth strip (scarf, bandage, or torn sheet) about 40-50 inches long
  2. Place one end at shoulder, bring diagonally across chest
  3. Support arm at 90 degree elbow angle
  4. Bring cloth under elbow and around to opposite shoulder
  5. Tie at shoulder, ensuring arm is fully supported
  6. Adjust so no pressure on neck vessels or nerves
  7. Arm should be at heart level or above

When NOT to Move a Patient

Conditions Requiring Spinal Immobilization:
  • Trauma to head, neck, or back
  • Loss of consciousness or altered mental status
  • Severe pain in neck or back
  • Inability to move arms or legs
  • Tingling or numbness in extremities
  • Any mechanism of injury that could cause spinal damage

Action: Keep person still, support head and neck, seek immediate professional help. Moving a person with spinal injury can cause paralysis.

Environmental Emergencies

Hypothermia (Core Temperature Below 95¬∞F/35¬∞C)

Hypothermia Stages and Treatment:
  1. Mild (90-95¬∞F): Shivering, confusion, slurred speech. Warm gradually (blankets, warm drinks), avoid rapid rewarming
  2. Moderate (82-90¬∞F): Shivering stops, drowsiness, poor coordination, possible irregular heartbeat. Handle gently (rough movement can cause cardiac arrhythmia). Insulate from ground, apply heat packs to groin, armpits, neck
  3. Severe (68-82¬∞F): Unconscious, barely perceptible pulse/breathing, possible rigid muscles. Do NOT attempt to rewarm in field - could cause afterdrop (further cooling when peripheral blood returns to cold core). Insulate, monitor vitals, get to professional help with care
  4. Profound (<68¬∞F): Appears dead (no pulse, no breathing, rigid). Do NOT give up - with proper rewarming, people can recover from severe hypothermia. Transport carefully, gentle rewarming only in hospital with ECMO capability if available
Rewarming Procedure (Mild to Moderate):
  1. Move to shelter, remove wet clothing
  2. Apply passive external rewarming: blankets, insulation from ground
  3. Active external rewarming: heat packs, warm (not hot) water bottles placed at core (groin, armpits, neck)
  4. For mild: warm drinks if alert and can swallow
  5. Avoid hot drinks, alcohol, exercise (these cause rapid heat loss from core)
  6. Monitor core temperature if possible
  7. Continue warming for several hours
  8. Watch for afterdrop: further drop in core temperature after rewarming starts
Hypothermia Saying: "It's not over until it's over" - severely hypothermic patients can recover with no brain damage after prolonged rewarming. Continue resuscitation attempts.

Heat Stroke and Heat Exhaustion

Heat Exhaustion (Core Temp 98.6-104¬∞F):
  • Symptoms: Heavy sweating, weakness, dizziness, nausea, fast pulse, cool/clammy skin, muscle cramps
  • Treatment: Move to cool area, drink water, cool body with wet cloths, elevate legs
  • Recovery: Usually improves within 30 minutes with cooling and fluids
Heat Stroke (Core Temp >104¬∞F) - MEDICAL EMERGENCY:
  • Symptoms: No sweating, hot/dry skin, severe weakness, confusion, possible seizures, altered consciousness
  • Difference from heat exhaustion: Lack of sweating and altered mental status are key - body's cooling system has failed
  • Treatment: Immerse in cold water if possible (most effective), or apply ice packs to neck, armpits, groin (major blood vessels)
  • Fluids: Only if alert and can swallow
  • Urgency: Medical emergency - seek help immediately, death risk is high

Drowning Response and Recovery

Drowning Rescue and Resuscitation:
  1. Safety first: Don't become another drowning victim - extend object or reach from shore if possible rather than entering water
  2. Remove from water: Get person out as quickly as possible
  3. Airway: If not breathing, begin rescue breaths immediately (water in lungs doesn't prevent breathing rescue)
  4. CPR: Start chest compressions if no pulse
  5. Hypothermia: Drowning in cold water may cause hypothermia - follow hypothermia protocol for rewarming
  6. Recovery position: If breathing but unconscious, position on side to allow water drainage
  7. Seek medical help: Even if person seems to recover, secondary drowning (pulmonary edema from water in lungs) can occur hours later - get professional evaluation

Snakebite Protocols

Snakebite First Aid:
  1. Prevention: Most important - wear boots, watch where hands/feet go, leave snake alone
  2. Immediately after bite: Remove jewelry, immobilize limb, place below heart level
  3. Venom extraction: Do NOT cut, suck, or use tourniquet (these cause more harm)
  4. Cleaning: Gently wash bite with soap and water
  5. Immobilization: Splint the limb - venom spreads with movement
  6. Monitoring: Mark the swelling with pen and time - swelling should not progress beyond marked area in 15 minutes if dry bite (20% of bites are dry)
  7. Pain management: Opioids if available for pain
  8. Antivenom: If available and patient is progressing (worsening swelling, systemic symptoms), antivenom is most effective within first few hours
  9. Observation: Monitor for systemic symptoms: bleeding, neurologic symptoms, shock
  10. Seek professional help: Medical care should be sought - even dry bites need monitoring

Insect Sting Reactions

Local Sting Reaction:
  1. Remove stinger if visible (scrape off, don't pinch)
  2. Clean area with soap and water
  3. Ice for pain and swelling
  4. Hydrocortisone cream for itching
  5. Antihistamine if itching severe
  6. Avoid scratching to prevent infection
Anaphylactic Reaction to Sting (Medical Emergency):
  1. Signs: Difficulty breathing, swelling of mouth/throat, hives, dizziness, abdominal pain
  2. Use epinephrine immediately: EpiPen 0.3-0.5mg IM into outer thigh
  3. Position: Lie flat with legs elevated (unless having respiratory difficulty)
  4. Follow-up medications: After epinephrine, give antihistamine (diphenhydramine 50mg) and corticosteroid if available
  5. Repeat epinephrine: If symptoms don't improve in 5-15 minutes, give second dose
  6. Seek emergency help: Anaphylaxis can progress, medical care is essential

Altitude Sickness

Acute Mountain Sickness (AMS) - Up to 8,000 feet:
  • Symptoms: Headache, nausea, fatigue, dizziness, shortness of breath
  • Prevention: Ascend gradually, stay hydrated, avoid alcohol
  • Treatment: Descend if symptoms severe, rest, oxygen if available, acetaminophen for headache
  • Resolution: Usually improves within 24 hours with acclimatization
High Altitude Cerebral Edema (HACE) - Serious:
  • Symptoms: Severe headache unresponsive to pain relief, confusion, ataxia (poor coordination), altered mental status
  • Treatment: DESCEND IMMEDIATELY - this can be fatal
  • Oxygen: If available, give supplemental oxygen
  • Dexamethasone: 4mg every 6 hours if available (corticosteroid to reduce brain swelling)
  • Seek emergency care: Medical evacuation needed

Lightning Strike

Lightning Strike Treatment:
  1. Safety: Don't approach if lightning storm still active - wait until all clear
  2. Check responsiveness: Person may appear dead but can recover
  3. CPR: If no pulse/breathing, begin CPR immediately and continue for extended period
  4. Entry/exit wounds: Check for burn marks where current entered and exited
  5. Spinal precautions: Potential for spinal injury from lightning or fall - immobilize spine
  6. Cardiac monitoring: Arrhythmias may occur - monitor heart rate and rhythm if possible
  7. Fluid resuscitation: May need significant fluids (more than usual burn)
  8. Seek medical help: All lightning strikes need professional evaluation

Childbirth & Women's Health

Normal Labor Stages

Stage 1 - Labor (Contractions and Cervical Dilation):
  • Early/Latent Phase: Contractions 15-30 minutes apart, mild to moderate intensity, woman can walk and talk. May last 6-8 hours or longer
  • Active Phase: Contractions 3-5 minutes apart, intense, woman focuses on breathing. Lasts 3-5 hours. Cervix dilates from 4-7cm
  • Transition Phase: Contractions 2-3 minutes apart, very intense, overwhelming sensation. Lasts 15 minutes to 1 hour. Cervix dilates to 10cm (fully dilated)
  • Duration: First time mothers average 12-14 hours; subsequent pregnancies may be faster
Stage 2 - Pushing (Delivery of Baby):
  • Duration: 20 minutes to 2 hours (shorter in experienced mothers)
  • Process: Woman feels urge to push with contractions, pushes with each contraction
  • Baby descent: Crowning (top of head visible), then full emergence
  • Assistance: Guide head out gently, help body follow
Stage 3 - Afterbirth (Delivery of Placenta):
  • Duration: 5-30 minutes after baby is born
  • Contractions: Usually milder contractions help expel placenta
  • Signs: Increased vaginal bleeding, urge to push, placenta appears at introitus
  • Delivery: Placenta comes out naturally with gentle traction on umbilical cord
  • Cord clamping: Wait 30-60 seconds before clamping to allow blood transfer to baby

Emergency Delivery Procedure

Assisting With Delivery:
  1. Preparation: Wash hands, sterilize any instruments, gather clean cloths and materials
  2. Position: Mother on back or semi-sitting, legs elevated, perineal area accessible
  3. Crowning: When head is visible, guide gently with downward and backward motion
  4. Head delivery: Support head as it comes out, check for umbilical cord around neck (nuchal cord) - if present, gently slip over head
  5. Body delivery: With next contraction, guide body out by gently supporting shoulders
  6. Suction: Gently clear mouth/nose of mucus if possible
  7. First breaths: Dry baby immediately, stimulate to cry
  8. Cord clamping: Wait 30-60 seconds, then clamp/tie cord in two places, cut between

Cord Care and Placenta Delivery

Cord Management:
  1. If no clamps available: tie cord with sterile string/cloth in two places, cut between with sterile scissors or blade
  2. After cutting, leave stump 1-2 inches long
  3. Keep cord stump clean and dry
  4. Fold diaper below cord to air-expose it
  5. Cord separates naturally in 5-14 days
  6. Watch for signs of infection: redness, swelling, discharge with foul odor
Placenta Delivery:
  1. After baby is born, continued contractions help expel placenta
  2. Do NOT pull on cord excessively (risk of retained placenta or uterine inversion)
  3. Gentle traction on cord with other hand supporting uterus from above
  4. When you feel it separate, placenta should come out
  5. Inspect placenta for complete delivery (should be intact with all lobes)
  6. If placenta retained after 30 minutes, may need manual removal or medical intervention

Postpartum Hemorrhage

Critical: Postpartum hemorrhage is leading cause of maternal mortality. Recognize and treat aggressively.
Recognition and Treatment:
  1. Normal bleeding: Heavy flow (soaking pad in 1-2 hours) is normal for first 24 hours
  2. Hemorrhage signs: Soaking more than one pad per hour for more than 2 hours, dizziness, weakness, rapid pulse, cold/clammy skin
  3. Causes: Uterine atony (uterus not contracting), retained placenta, tissue tears, infection
  4. Massage: Firmly massage uterus (just below navel) in circular motion to encourage contraction
  5. Medications: Oxytocin 10 units IM if available (causes uterine contraction), methergine if available (ergot derivative)
  6. Fluids: Start IV fluids (saline) if available to maintain circulation
  7. Packing: If bleeding won't stop, pack vagina with clean cloth/gauze
  8. Seek emergency help: Hemorrhage is medical emergency - professional care critical

Breastfeeding Basics

Initiation and Basics:
  • First feeding: Begin within first hour of birth if possible
  • Position: Baby's mouth should cover entire areola (dark area), not just nipple
  • Frequency: Feed 8-12 times per day initially (2-3 hours apart) or on demand
  • Duration: 10-20 minutes per side, empty one breast before switching
  • Milk production: Increases with frequent emptying
  • Colostrum: First milk is yellowish, nutrient-rich, laxative effect helps baby pass meconium
Common Problems:
  • Sore nipples: Ensure proper latch, air dry between feeds, lanolin cream
  • Engorgement: Breasts hard and painful - express some milk to soften, frequent feeding
  • Mastitis: Infection of breast tissue - firm, red, painful area, fever. Treat with heat, frequent feeding, antibiotics if available
  • Low milk supply: Increase frequency of feeding, ensure deep latch, hydration

Common Pregnancy Complications

Preeclampsia (Pregnancy-Induced Hypertension):
  • Signs: Blood pressure >140/90, swelling (especially face/hands), headache, visual disturbances, upper abdominal pain
  • Serious: Can progress to eclampsia (seizures) or HELLP syndrome
  • Treatment: Bed rest, monitor blood pressure, antihypertensive medications if available
  • Urgent: Delivery of baby is definitive treatment - seek obstetric care
Gestational Diabetes:
  • Screening: Glucose screening at 24-28 weeks
  • Management: Diet control (low sugar), monitor blood glucose if possible
  • Risk: Large babies, delivery complications
  • Resolution: Usually resolves after delivery
Placental Abruption (Premature Separation):
  • Signs: Vaginal bleeding, abdominal pain, uterine tenderness, shock symptoms
  • Severity: Can vary from minor to life-threatening
  • Treatment: Seek emergency care - may need emergency delivery to save mother and baby
Placenta Previa (Placenta Covering Cervix):
  • Signs: Vaginal bleeding (painless) in second/third trimester
  • Management: Pelvic rest, avoid penetrating exams (risk of hemorrhage)
  • Delivery: Requires cesarean section - vaginal delivery would cause severe hemorrhage

Sanitation & Disease Prevention

Latrine Construction for Off-Grid Living

Pit Latrine Construction:
  1. Location: At least 50 meters (150 feet) downhill from water source, away from living areas
  2. Dig pit: 1-2 meters deep (or until hits bedrock/water table)
  3. Diameter: 1-1.5 meters wide
  4. Superstructure: Build simple structure with seat/platform over pit (wood frame with hole)
  5. Privacy: Enclose with cloth/wood walls
  6. Covering: Keep seat covered when not in use (prevents flies, odor)
  7. Maintenance: Add ash or lime periodically to reduce odor and pathogens
  8. Closure: When full (3-5 years), fill with soil and build new latrine elsewhere
Composting Toilet (Better Option):
  1. Container: Large bucket or drum with seat attached
  2. Material: After each use, add 2-3 handfuls of carbon material (dry leaves, wood chips, sawdust)
  3. Moisture: Keep contents slightly moist (like wrung-out sponge)
  4. Aeration: Ensure air flow (compost needs oxygen)
  5. Decomposition: Over 6-12 months, waste breaks down into harmless compost
  6. Emptying: Remove finished compost (safe for garden but not vegetables you eat raw)
  7. No flies: Regular covering with carbon prevents odor and insects

Hand Hygiene Critical Points

When to Wash Hands:
  • Before preparing food
  • Before eating
  • After using toilet
  • After contact with feces (diaper change, latrine use)
  • Before treating wounds or handling medical supplies
  • After handling infected materials
  • After animal contact
Proper Hand Washing (Without Running Water):
  1. Pour water (boiled and cooled if necessary) over hands
  2. Apply soap (if available) or ash/sand (abrasive, can substitute)
  3. Scrub all surfaces: palms, backs, between fingers, fingernails, wrists
  4. Scrub for at least 20 seconds
  5. Rinse with clean water until soap is gone
  6. Dry with clean cloth (cloth should be washed regularly)
  7. If no water: alcohol-based sanitizer works, or use clean cloth to wipe dirt off first then sanitize

Water Safety and Purification

Water-Borne Diseases:
  • Bacterial: Cholera, typhoid, dysentery, E. coli
  • Viral: Hepatitis A, polio (rare), norovirus
  • Parasitic: Giardia, cryptosporidium, hookworm
  • Chemical: Heavy metals, arsenic (source-dependent)
Water Purification Methods (Multi-Barrier Approach Best):
  1. Boiling: Most reliable. Boil for 1 minute (3 minutes if at high altitude) kills all pathogens. Cool and store in clean container
  2. Filtration: Sand and gravel filter removes sediment and some bacteria; doesn't kill all pathogens
  3. Chemical: Bleach (2-8 drops per liter depending on clarity) kills bacteria/viruses but not parasites. Wait 30 minutes
  4. Iodine: Iodine tablets or liquid kills most pathogens but not cryptosporidium. Follow product instructions
  5. Combination: Filter first (removes sediment), then boil or chemical treatment most effective

Quarantine Procedures

Isolating Contagious Illness:
  1. Isolation room: Separate sick person from others if possible (one room, separate entrance if available)
  2. Bedding: Dedicate clean bedding; wash separately after illness
  3. Food/drink: Use separate dishes, utensils; wash in hot water with soap
  4. Hygiene: Sick person washes hands frequently; helpers wear masks if available
  5. Coughing/sneezing: Cover with tissue or elbow, dispose properly
  6. Duration: Respiratory illness - isolate for 5+ days or until fever-free for 24 hours without medication
  7. Gastrointestinal: Isolate until diarrhea resolves; strict hand hygiene critical
  8. Return to activity: When well enough and no longer contagious

Basic Epidemiology Concepts

Disease Transmission Routes:
  • Respiratory: Coughing, sneezing, talking (droplets travel ~6 feet)
  • Fecal-oral: Poor hygiene, contaminated water/food
  • Contact: Touching infected person or contaminated surface
  • Vector: Insect bites (mosquito, tick)
  • Bloodborne: Blood or body fluid contact
Prevention Strategies:
  • Hand hygiene: Single most important prevention measure
  • Clean water: Prevents waterborne diseases
  • Food safety: Cook meat thoroughly, avoid cross-contamination
  • Sanitation: Proper latrine/waste disposal
  • Respiratory hygiene: Cover coughs, separate sick people
  • Vaccination: If available, prevents many diseases
  • Pest control: Reduce insect/rodent populations
Disease Outbreak Response:
  1. Identify sick person(s) and symptoms
  2. Isolate from others if contagious disease suspected
  3. Trace contacts: who has been in close contact in last 1-14 days (depends on disease)
  4. Monitor contacts for symptoms: twice daily temperature and symptom checks
  5. Keep contacts quarantined for incubation period (varies by disease)
  6. Treat symptomatic cases with available medications/supportive care
  7. Enhance hygiene measures: handwashing, food safety, sanitation
  8. Seek professional help if outbreak overwhelms local capacity

Vector-Borne Disease Prevention

Mosquito-Borne Illnesses (Malaria, Dengue, Zika, Yellow Fever):
  • Prevention: Eliminate standing water (breeding grounds), use screens, sleep under nets
  • Protective wear: Long sleeves/pants during dawn/dusk (peak mosquito hours)
  • Repellent: DEET, picaridin, or lemon eucalyptus oil
  • Recognition: Fever, chills, body aches, headache
  • Malaria treatment: Artemether-lumefantrine or other antimalarial if available
Tick-Borne Illnesses (Lyme disease, Rocky Mountain Spotted Fever):
  • Prevention: Avoid tall grass/brush, wear protective clothing, check body for ticks
  • Tick removal: Use tweezers, pull straight out (don't twist), save for identification
  • Lyme disease signs: Bullseye rash, flu-like symptoms, joint pain
  • Early treatment: Doxycycline 100mg twice daily for 14-21 days (most effective if started early)

Recognizing and Treating Infection

Early Signs of Infection (First 48 Hours Post-Injury)

  • Redness: Pink to red skin surrounding wound, spreading outward
  • Warmth: Area feels warm to touch compared to surrounding skin
  • Swelling: Increasing puffiness around wound (can worsen over hours)
  • Pus: Yellow/greenish drainage (indicates bacterial infection)
  • Odor: Foul smell from wound (suggests bacterial overgrowth)
  • Pain: Throbbing or progressive pain despite treatment
  • Red streaking: URGENT: Red lines extending from wound toward heart indicate spreading infection

Systemic Infection Signs (Spreading Beyond Local Wound)

  • Fever: Temperature above 100.4¬∞F (38¬∞C), usually appearing 12-24 hours after infection begins
  • Chills: Body shaking, often precedes fever spike
  • Swollen lymph nodes: Lumps in neck, armpit, or groin on same side as infected wound
  • Fatigue: Overwhelming tiredness, weakness
  • Headache: General malaise, joint aches
  • Nausea/vomiting: Indicates significant infection
  • Rapid pulse: Heart rate above 100 bpm at rest
  • Mental changes: Confusion, delirium (dangerous sign - means infection is severe)

Infection Management by Stage

Stage 1: Early Localized Infection (First 24-48 hours)
  1. Increase wound cleaning frequency to 4-6 times daily
  2. Use hot water (as hot as can tolerate without burning) with honey, garlic, or thyme wash
  3. Apply raw honey poultice and cover; change 2-3 times daily
  4. Drain any visible pus: gently express by pressing around wound margins (allows drainage)
  5. Elevate infected area above heart level to reduce swelling and improve drainage
  6. Internal treatment: Garlic + turmeric internally 2-3 times daily for antibacterial effect
  7. Monitor closely: If not improving in 24 hours, escalate treatment
Stage 2: Spreading or Worsening Infection
  1. More aggressive drainage: If safe, open any abscess pocket to allow pus drainage
  2. High-dose herbal treatment: Garlic 4-5 cloves crushed 3-4 times daily, turmeric + ginger paste 3 times daily
  3. Continue frequent wound washing (4-6 times daily minimum)
  4. Poultice changes every 4-6 hours
  5. Cold compress 15-20 min at a time if fever develops (to help regulate body temperature)
  6. Increase fluids significantly (prevents dehydration from fever)
  7. If fever develops, monitor vital signs every 4 hours
  8. Watch for red streaking (emergency sign)
Stage 3: Systemic Infection (Fever + Spreading Redness)
  1. This is a medical emergency. Try everything possible to slow infection
  2. Keep infected area immobilized to slow spread
  3. Aggressive internal treatment: Maximum herbal antibacterials
  4. If amputation is necessary for survival: Have realistic discussion about what signs indicate this
  5. Maintain hydration and nutrition despite nausea
  6. If no improvement in 48-72 hours despite aggressive treatment, consider amputation (slow progression of infection to vital organs is fatal)

Oral Rehydration Solution - Exact Ratios

Critical for survival: In off-grid conditions with diarrhea, vomiting, or extreme heat, oral rehydration solution (ORS) is life-saving. It replaces water AND electrolytes lost through fluid loss.
WHO Standard Oral Rehydration Solution Recipe (Low-Osmolarity)
  • Salt (sodium chloride): 2.6 grams per liter (or 1/2 teaspoon per liter)
  • Sugar (glucose): 13.5 grams per liter (or 2.5 tablespoons per liter)
  • Potassium chloride (salt substitute): 1.5 grams per liter (optional but ideal if available)
  • Water: 1 liter (clean, boiled if possible)
  • Optional flavoring: Orange juice or lemon juice (also provides potassium)
Field-Expedient ORS (No Measuring Equipment)

In 1 quart (liter) clean water:

  • Salt: Pinch in 3 fingers (about 1/2 teaspoon)
  • Sugar: 2-3 big handfuls or approximately 2-3 tablespoons
  • Stir thoroughly until dissolved

Administration Protocol

  • Mild dehydration: Drink 50 mL (3 tablespoons) every 5-10 minutes, conscious and can swallow
  • Moderate dehydration: Drink 100 mL (6 tablespoons) every 10-15 minutes
  • Severe dehydration with vomiting: Drink 5-10 mL (1-2 teaspoons) every 2-3 minutes, very slowly to prevent vomiting
  • Continue until: Urine becomes pale/clear, thirst is quenched, person feels better
  • Duration: May need to continue ORS for 4-24 hours depending on fluid loss cause

Alternative Fluid Sources (When ORS Not Available)

  • Coconut water: Natural electrolyte balance, excellent if available
  • Broth with salt: Contains salt and some nutrients
  • Sugar water with salt pinch: Better than plain water
  • Avoid: Plain water only (dilutes remaining electrolytes), caffeine/alcohol (increases loss)

Mass Casualty Triage (START Protocol)

Simple Triage and Rapid Treatment (START) System Overview

The START protocol is the most widely used mass casualty triage system in emergency medicine. It requires minimal training, no equipment, and takes approximately 60 seconds per patient. The system is based on three assessment parameters: ability to walk, respiratory status, and perfusion/mental status. In disaster situations with 50+ patients and limited resources, START triage can dramatically improve survival rates by ensuring critical patients receive immediate care.

START Triage Color Categories

‚ñ† RED (IMMEDIATE/EMERGENCY)

Life-threatening injuries requiring immediate intervention. Respiratory distress, uncontrolled bleeding, altered mental status, severe injuries. These patients have the highest mortality rate if not treated within minutes. Examples: tension pneumothorax, severe head injury with altered consciousness, massive hemorrhage, airway compromise. Expected to consume 40-50% of immediate care resources in typical mass casualty events.

‚ñ† YELLOW (DELAYED)

Serious injuries that can wait 1-4 hours. Moderate bleeding controlled with basic pressure, moderate shock, fractures, moderate burns (10-20% body surface), altered mental status that improves with positioning. These patients are stable enough for evacuation in groups. Can tolerate temporary delays in definitive care. Expected to comprise 30-35% of triage categories.

‚ñ† GREEN (MINOR/WALKING WOUNDED)

Minor injuries with no systemic complications. Lacerations, minor fractures, abrasions, minor burns (<10% body surface). These patients can walk and follow commands. They can often assist with scene evacuation and care for themselves. Comprise 50-60% of typical mass casualty incidents. Can be held in waiting areas for hours to days.

‚ñ† BLACK (EXPECTANT/DECEASED)

Non-salvageable patients or those already deceased. Unsurvivable injuries (catastrophic head trauma, massive truncal injury incompatible with life), or when resources are overwhelmed and a patient requires intensive care that cannot be provided. In mass casualty events with extremely limited resources, some potentially salvageable patients may be triaged black to preserve resources for those with higher survival probability. This is an ethically difficult but sometimes necessary decision.

Resource Allocation Under Constraints

Priority treatment sequence when supplies are limited:

  1. RED patients first (40-50% of resources): Focus on life-saving interventions: airway management, hemorrhage control, tension pneumothorax decompression, shock management. In a typical mass casualty event with 100 patients, ~15-20 will be RED.
  2. YELLOW patients second (30-35% of resources): Once RED stabilized, move to delayed care: wound care, splinting, pain management, continued monitoring. ~30-35 patients will be YELLOW.
  3. GREEN patients last (15-20% of resources): Minimal supplies needed; focus on organization and reassurance. ~50-55 will be GREEN.

Resource rationing decisions:

  • If IV fluids limited: RED patients only; others treated with oral hydration if possible
  • If antibiotics limited: Prioritize HIGH infection risk (open fractures, penetrating trauma, immunocompromised RED patients)
  • If pain medications limited: RED and severe YELLOW; GREEN managed with ice, elevation, reassurance
  • If bandaging supplies limited: Control bleeding first, then use alternative materials (cloth scraps, leaves) to maintain infection barriers

Reverse Triage (Return to Duty Assessment)

In mass casualty events, especially in operational/military settings, determining which patients can return to duty soonest optimizes remaining personnel:

  • Return to duty within hours: Minor lacerations (closed), minor sprains without swelling, minor burns <5% body surface, psychological distress responding to reassurance
  • Return to duty next day: Healing minor fractures with proper splinting, contusions, minor respiratory symptoms resolving
  • Return to duty after days-weeks: Moderate injuries no longer bleeding, stabilized fractures, resolved infection symptoms
  • Permanently unable to return: Severe disability from amputation, severe burns >30%, permanent neurological injury, psychological trauma requiring ongoing support

Psychological First Aid for Triage Workers

Triage work is emotionally exhausting. In mass casualty events, triage officers may experience secondary trauma, especially when making BLACK category decisions. Support measures:

  • Rotation: Limit triage duty to 2-3 hours maximum; rotate triage officers to prevent decision fatigue
  • Buddy system: Pair triage officers; they watch each other for signs of distress
  • Decision support: Use written protocols; reference the algorithm instead of relying on judgment alone during crisis
  • Emotional decompression: After shift, allow 15-30 minutes to transition: remove gloves, wash hands, sit quietly, talk through difficult cases
  • Reassurance: Remind officers that triage decisions are made with available information; no one person can save everyone
  • Follow-up support: In weeks following, expect some officers to experience nightmares, guilt, or hypervigilance; normalize this and provide connection to mental health resources

Epidemic & Pandemic Response

Recognizing Outbreak Patterns

Early detection of outbreaks is critical for containment. Before modern surveillance systems, communities identified outbreaks by recognizing unusual patterns of illness.

Key outbreak indicators:

  • Temporal cluster: Multiple cases of same disease appearing within days (not weeks/months)
  • Geographic cluster: Cases concentrated in specific area or institution (school, market, gathering place)
  • Demographic cluster: Specific age group or population affected (children, elderly, occupation-specific)
  • Rapid increase in cases: Doubling of cases every 2-7 days suggests active spread

Calculate attack rate (indicates outbreak severity):

Attack Rate (%) = (Number of people who became ill / Total people exposed) √ó 100

Attack rate interpretation:

  • <1%: Sporadic cases, endemic level
  • 1-5%: Small outbreak, limited spread
  • 5-20%: Moderate outbreak, active transmission
  • >20%: Severe outbreak, rapid person-to-person spread

Example calculation: If a school has 500 students and 45 develop diarrhea in one week, the attack rate is (45/500) √ó 100 = 9%. This 9% rate indicates moderate outbreak, suggesting a contamination event (water, food) or infectious agent spread.

Infection Control Without Modern Supplies

Improvised PPE (Personal Protective Equipment)

Face coverings (for respiratory protection):

  • Cloth masks: Layer 3-4 pieces of tight-weave cotton (bed sheets, T-shirts); effectiveness ~50-60% vs. N95's 95%, but infinitely better than bare face
  • Makeshift N95: Multiple cloth layers + one layer of silk or chiffon (electrostatic properties); more filtration than plain cloth
  • Mask fastening: Use ear loops from cloth strips, elastic bands, or tie around head; mask must seal edges to be effective
  • Duration: Replace when damp (sweat/breath moisture reduces effectiveness); reusable masks should be washed daily in hot water
  • Fit testing: Mask is effective only if properly sealed; check seal by inhaling sharply (should feel air resistance, not leaks at edges)

Eye protection (goggles/face shields):

  • Improvised goggles: Clear plastic bags/wrapping secured around eyes with elastic bands; protects from splashing of body fluids
  • Face shields: Clear plastic sheets (from windows, packaging) attached to a headband; covers entire face
  • Alternative: Swim goggles if available; not ideal but better than nothing for aerosol exposure
  • Cleaning: Wipe with cloth after patient contact; disinfect with available solutions (see below)

Gowns/protective clothing:

  • Washable gowns: Cotton or plastic aprons worn over clothing; change between patients or when visibly soiled
  • Plastic alternatives: Heavy plastic sheeting can be fashioned into simple garments or worn as overlay
  • Sleeve protection: Forearms are frequent contact points; wrap with cloth or plastic secured with tape
  • Foot protection: If handling highly contaminated materials, wrap feet with plastic bags secured with elastic bands

Gloves:

  • Reusable options: Rubber household gloves (dish gloves) can be washed and reused; most durable option
  • Cloth options: Tightly woven cotton gloves provide minimal protection but better than bare hands
  • Plastic alternatives: Plastic bags as glove liners with cloth gloves on top; secure with tape at wrist
  • When gloves unavailable: Focus intensely on hand hygiene instead; gloves provide false sense of security if hands not washed after glove removal

Hand hygiene with limited water

Hand washing protocol (requires ~5 liters/person/day):

  1. Rinse hands with small amount of water (cup, not running)
  2. Apply soap (bar, powdered, or soap made from ash + oil)
  3. Rub vigorously: palms, between fingers, back of hands, wrists (20-30 seconds)
  4. Rinse with second cup of clean water
  5. Dry on clean cloth or in air

Soap alternatives when soap unavailable:

  • Ash soap: Combine wood ash with grease/oil in 1:2 ratio; let sit 1-2 weeks; strains off top layer (actual soap); effective but harsh
  • Vinegar rinse: After soap/water wash, final rinse with diluted vinegar (1 part vinegar to 4 parts water) increases antimicrobial effect
  • Saltwater wash: 1 teaspoon salt per cup of water; antimicrobial but less effective than soap
  • Alcohol-based: Any >60% alcohol (spirits) as hand sanitizer when water unavailable; less effective than washing but better than nothing

Water conservation strategies:

  • Hand sanitizer stations at entrance to patient areas: smaller volume needed
  • Footbaths at exits: single bucket can do multiple people (change water every 4-6 people)
  • Prioritize handwashing at critical times: before eating, after toilet, before/after patient contact
  • Dry hand rubbing with cloth between water-based washes (removes visible dirt before water wash)

Airflow direction (negative pressure concepts):

Common Epidemic Diseases and Field Identification

Cholera

Transmission: Contaminated water/food (faecal-oral route). Extremely rapid onset and severe dehydration.

Field identification:

  • "Rice-water" stools: Watery diarrhea with white/gray flecks, looks like rice water; unique to cholera
  • Rapid onset: Diarrhea + vomiting can start within 12 hours of ingestion
  • Severe dehydration signs: Sunken eyes, dry skin that doesn't bounce back when pinched, weak/rapid pulse, low BP
  • Shock risk: Can progress to severe shock and death within 18-24 hours if untreated
  • Fluid loss: Up to 1 liter/hour; most rapid fluid loss of any diarrheal disease

Treatment: ORS (see previous section) is 99% effective. Give frequently in small amounts. If patient can't keep fluids down, IV fluids preferred (if available), otherwise continue ORS every few minutes. Zinc supplementation (if available) prevents future infections. Antibiotics (doxycycline if available) reduce duration.

Typhoid Fever

Transmission: Contaminated food/water (Salmonella bacteria). Slower onset than cholera.

Field identification:

  • Rose spots rash: Faint pink/red spots on trunk (pathognomonic sign); appears week 2
  • Stepwise fever pattern: Fever increases daily in steps over 3-4 days, then plateaus at 103-104¬∞F (39-40¬∞C)
  • Headache, body aches: Severe, often more prominent than respiratory symptoms
  • Abdominal involvement: Constipation (early) or diarrhea ("rose spots" in stool), tender abdomen
  • Delirium ("rose spots" in behavior): Confusion, delirium, muttering; appears in untreated cases by week 3
  • Splenomegaly: Enlarged spleen common; can perforate (surgical emergency)

Treatment: Antibiotics essential (ciprofloxacin, ceftriaxone if available). Without antibiotics, mortality ~20%. With antibiotics, mortality <1%. Supportive care: fluids, acetaminophen for fever. Isolation for 1-2 weeks (until fever resolves and diarrhea stops).

Dysentery (Bloody Diarrhea)

Two types with different field presentations:

  • BACILLARY dysentery (Shigella):
    • Rapid onset (12-72 hours)
    • Frequent small bloody stools (20-30+/day)
    • Tenesmus (painful straining)
    • High fever (103-104¬∞F), severe cramping
    • Often improves in 5-7 days without treatment
    • Can spread rapidly in crowded conditions
  • AMOEBIC dysentery (Entamoeba histolytica):
    • Slower onset (7-21 days)
    • Fewer, larger bloody/mucoid stools (3-8/day)
    • Less fever, milder cramping than bacillary
    • Can have intestinal ulceration complications
    • Recurrent symptoms if untreated; can become chronic
    • Risk of amoebic liver abscess (fever, RUQ pain, hepatomegaly)

Treatment: ORS for both. Bacillary: antibiotics (ciprofloxacin, azithromycin) if available; supportive care. Amoebic: specific anti-amoebic drugs (metronidazole preferred if available); luminal agent (paromomycin) to eliminate cysts. Stool examination (if microscopy available) can distinguish, but field treatment same: ORS + antibiotics if available.

Influenza & Pneumonia

Field identification of influenza:

  • Sudden onset fever (101-103¬∞F), chills, malaise
  • Myalgias (muscle aches) prominent; worse than common cold
  • Headache, cough often appears after fever
  • Respiratory symptoms: cough (may become productive), sore throat
  • Duration: 3-7 days for uncomplicated illness

Complications (watch for): Pneumonia (fever not improving by day 5, worsening cough, shortness of breath), secondary bacterial pneumonia.

Field identification of pneumonia:

  • Productive cough (sputum production)
  • Shortness of breath; fast breathing (>30/min)
  • Chest pain, especially with deep breathing
  • Crackles/rales on lung auscultation (if stethoscope available)
  • Fever often present

Treatment (influenza/pneumonia): Antiviral drugs (oseltamivir/Tamiflu) most effective if given within 48 hours; supportive care, fluids, rest. For suspected bacterial pneumonia: antibiotics (amoxicillin, azithromycin, or fluoroquinolone if available). Observe for deterioration; respiratory support (supplemental O2 if available) for hypoxia.

Measles

Field identification:

  • Prodrome phase (3-4 days): Fever, cough, runny nose, conjunctivitis ("3 Cs: cough, coryza, conjunctivitis")
  • Koplik spots (pathognomonic): White spots on inside cheek (like grains of salt) appearing 2-3 days before rash
  • Rash phase: Starts on face, spreads downward over 3-4 days; flat red spots, maculopapular; blanches with pressure
  • Fever pattern: High fever (104-105¬∞F) with rash
  • Symptoms worsen with rash: Cough, eye discharge peak with rash onset

Complications: Otitis media (ear infection), pneumonia, encephalitis (rare but serious), subacute sclerosing panencephalitis (SSPE, very late complication).

Treatment: Supportive care (fever management, fluids); vitamin A supplementation (if available: 200,000 IU daily for 2 days, especially in children under 5 and malnourished). Antibiotics if secondary bacterial infection develops. Isolation: 4 days after rash onset (or 5 days if immunocompromised).

Pertussis (Whooping Cough)

Field identification:

  • Catarrhal phase (1-2 weeks): Mild URI symptoms, runny nose, mild cough; looks like common cold
  • Paroxysmal phase (weeks 2-6+): Severe coughing fits, followed by inspiratory "whoop" (high-pitched breathing in)
  • Coughing fits: 5-10 rapid coughs followed by whoop; can last hours, causing exhaustion
  • Post-tussive vomiting: Coughing so severe it triggers vomiting
  • Apnea: Young children may have breathing pauses instead of whoop

Complications: Pneumonia (especially in infants), seizures, encephalopathy, death (rare with supportive care, but ~1% in untreated infants).

Treatment: Antibiotics early (macrolides: azithromycin/erythromycin) shorten duration; if started in catarrhal phase, prevents paroxysmal phase. Once coughing started, antibiotics less effective but still reduce spread. Supportive care: keep airway clear, oxygen if hypoxic, monitor for apnea. Isolation: 21 days from cough onset (or 5 days after starting antibiotics).

Diphtheria

Field identification:

  • Pseudomembrane: Gray/white thick membrane on throat/tonsils; adherent (doesn't wipe off easily)
  • Respiratory form: Sore throat, low-grade fever; membrane can obstruct airway
  • Cutaneous form: Skin ulcers with gray membrane
  • Nasal form: Nasal discharge, crusting (less severe)
  • Systemic toxin effects: Myocarditis (heart inflammation, arrhythmias), neuropathy (esp. cranial nerves causing paralysis)

Treatment (urgent): Antitoxin (diphtheria antitoxin - if available, give immediately; doesn't reverse but prevents further toxin damage). Antibiotics (penicillin/erythromycin) kill bacteria. Supportive care: monitor airway (may need emergency tracheostomy if obstruction), manage myocarditis (rest, monitor heart), manage neuropathy (supportive care). Isolation: until 2 consecutive negative cultures after completion of antibiotics.

Malaria (if in endemic area)

Field identification:

  • Fever cycles: Patterns vary by Plasmodium species: every 48 hours (tertian = P. vivax/ovale), every 72 hours (quartan = P. malariae), irregular (P. falciparum)
  • Fever pattern: Shaking chills followed by high fever (104-105¬∞F), followed by sweating; then afebrile period
  • Associated symptoms: Headache, myalgias, weakness, sometimes nausea/vomiting
  • Jaundice: May develop with severe disease or hemolysis
  • Splenomegaly: Enlarged spleen common
  • Severe malaria: Cerebral malaria (seizures, coma), severe anemia, acute kidney injury, pulmonary edema

Treatment: Antimalarial drugs critical (chloroquine, artemisinin derivatives if available; resistance patterns vary by region). Supportive care: fever management, fluids, transfusion if severe anemia. Monitor for complications. Prevention: mosquito prevention (nets, repellent, cover up at dusk/dawn).

Water and Food Safety During Outbreaks

Water safety:

  • Boiling: Most reliable method; boil 1 minute (3 minutes at high altitude >6,500 ft) kills most pathogens. Cholera killed in seconds, typhoid needs sustained boiling.
  • Bleach treatment: 2 drops of household bleach per liter of clear water; let sit 30 minutes before use. For turbid water, use 4 drops.
  • Solar disinfection: Fill clear bottles with water, leave in sun 6+ hours (UV radiation kills many pathogens); works in clear sunny climates
  • Filtration first: Always filter/settle water to remove visible particles before bleaching or boiling (chemicals/heat work better on clear water)

Food safety:

  • High-risk foods during outbreaks: Raw vegetables/fruits (if water source contaminated), shellfish (filter feeders concentrate pathogens), undercooked meat/eggs
  • Safe preparation: Wash hands thoroughly before eating; cook meat/eggs fully; peel fruits/vegetables or cook them
  • Storage: Food poisoning risk increases if food not kept hot (>140¬∞F) or cold (<40¬∞F); in epidemic, avoid leftover food when possible
  • High-transmission foods: Foods prepared by infected food handler without gloves/handwashing; communal serving spoons increase risk

Body Handling and Burial Protocols for Infectious Deaths

In severe epidemics, deaths may occur faster than standard funeral practices allow. Respectful, safe handling prevents further transmission:

  • PPE for handlers: Full protection (gown, gloves, mask, eye protection) when handling deceased from infectious disease
  • Body washing (if practiced): Use gloved hands; minimize splashing; water used should be disinfected after use
  • Body wrapping: Wrap in impermeable material (plastic sheet, thick cloth) to contain body fluids; secure with cloth ties, not pins/open
  • Burial location: >2 meters deep (below groundwater level); >30 meters from water source; avoid contaminating wells/streams
  • Mass burial pits (if necessary): Dig trenches 2+ meters deep; bodies placed in rows; layer of soil (30 cm) between each row if multiple burials; 1+ meter of soil cover minimum
  • Cremation (if available): Safest option for highly infectious deaths; requires fuel source and trained personnel
  • Documentation: Record burial location (GPS coordinates if possible); helps families locate graves later; prevents contaminated site reuse

Contact Tracing Without Technology

Identifying and monitoring people exposed to infected individuals slows disease spread. In off-grid settings without digital databases:

  • Interview infected person: Ask detailed history: where have they been last 2 weeks? Who did they spend significant time around? When did symptoms start? When were they most contagious (typically peak illness)?
  • Identify contacts: Create list of exposed people; prioritize close contacts (family, healthcare workers, frequent physical contact)
  • Contact quarantine list: Write on paper (with copies) names, addresses, last known location of each contact
  • Physical monitoring: Assign person to visit contacts daily, check temperature if possible, ask about symptoms
  • Documentation: Keep contact list, monitor records (who was checked, when, symptoms present)
  • Duration: Monitor for incubation period of specific disease (2-14 days for most respiratory illnesses, up to 21 days for some)
  • Information privacy: Keep contact lists confidential; breach of privacy can cause social stigma/violence against infected persons

Epidemic Curve Tracking (Paper-Based)

Tracking number of new cases over time shows outbreak progression and effect of interventions. Creates visual record without computers:

How to create and use an epidemic curve:

  1. Set up paper graph: Draw x-axis (days) and y-axis (number of new cases). Use graph paper if available; if not, ruled lines work.
  2. Daily data collection: Each day, count NEW cases diagnosed that day (not cumulative total). Record number.
  3. Plot on graph: Mark point or draw bar at appropriate day and height. Use different colors if possible: red (rising cases), yellow (plateau/intervention), green (declining).
  4. Mark interventions: Draw vertical line when quarantine started, water source changed, etc. Helps correlate interventions with case trends.
  5. Interpretation:
    • Rising curve: Outbreak still growing; transmission not controlled
    • Peak then decline: Interventions working or outbreak burning out (fewer susceptible people left)
    • Plateau: Cases staying steady; suggests ongoing transmission despite intervention
  6. Use for decisions: Curve shows whether to escalate interventions, maintain current level, or wind down precautions

Oral Rehydration Solution (ORS) ‚Äî EXACT Ratios

CRITICAL: Wrong ORS ratios cause MORE harm than no treatment. Use these EXACT proportions only.

WHO/UNICEF Standard ORS Formula (EXACT):
Sodium Chloride (salt): 2.6 grams per liter
Potassium Chloride: 1.5 grams per liter
Glucose (dextrose): 13.5 grams per liter
Trisodium Citrate dihydrate: 2.9 grams per liter
Total osmolarity: 245 mOsm/L (this specific value is critical)

Simplified ORS Recipe (Field Conditions)

When precise measurements unavailable:
1 liter clean water
6 teaspoons sugar (or honey)
1/2 teaspoon salt (table salt, not iodized)
1/4 teaspoon potassium chloride (salt substitute) ‚Äî if available

Mixing procedure:
1. Measure exactly. Use teaspoon as standard measure.
2. Dissolve salt and potassium in small amount of warm water first.
3. Add sugar to salt solution; stir until completely dissolved.
4. Add to remainder of water; stir well.
5. Test: solution should taste slightly salty and slightly sweet (like tears).
6. If tastes too salty: add more water. If too sweet: add salt.

ORS Administration

  • Mild dehydration (5% fluid loss): 50 mL/kg over 4 hours = rehydrate over morning or afternoon
  • Moderate dehydration (10%): 100 mL/kg over 4 hours
  • Severe dehydration (15%+): IV fluids required; ORS insufficient. If no IV access, small frequent sips ORS + seek medical help urgently
  • Ongoing losses: 10 mL/kg for each diarrheal stool or vomiting episode
  • Frequency: Small frequent sips (teaspoon every 5‚Äì10 minutes) better tolerated than large amounts at once
  • Monitoring: Urine output should resume within 6‚Äì8 hours of starting ORS. If not, increasing dehydration‚Äîescalate treatment
ORS Success Indicator: After 4 hours of ORS, patient should urinate. This is the sign hydration is improving. No urine = inadequate replacement, increase ORS intake or consider IV if available.

Tourniquet Application ‚Äî Time Limits

CRITICAL: Tourniquet time limits DIRECTLY affect amputation risk:

0‚Äì2 hours: Minimal tissue damage. Tourniquet safe, reversible.
2‚Äì6 hours: Significant tissue damage begins. Muscle breakdown occurs (rhabdomyolysis). Reversible with aggressive IV fluids post-release.
6‚Äì8 hours: Severe tissue necrosis. Amputation likely if limb salvage attempted. Permanent nerve damage probable.
8+ hours: Amputation nearly certain. Limb non-viable; irreversible damage to muscles, nerves, blood vessels.

RULE: >2 hours tourniquet time = likely permanent disability or amputation
If tourniquet must remain >6 hours: Plan for amputation.

Tourniquet Release Protocol

  • Monitor for Reperfusion Injury: When tourniquet released, acidic, damaged muscle tissue re-enters circulation. Flush kidneys aggressively with IV fluids to prevent acute kidney injury.
  • IV Hydration: Start large-bore IV immediately before releasing tourniquet (if time permits). Goal: urine output 200‚Äì300 mL/hour for first 6 hours post-release.
  • Urine Monitoring: If urine dark (cola-colored), rhabdomyolysis severe‚Äîcontinue aggressive IV fluids.
  • Sodium Bicarbonate: If available, add 50‚Äì100 mEq to IV fluids to alkalinize urine and prevent kidney damage from myoglobin precipitation.

Frostbite Rewarming ‚Äî EXACT Temperature Protocol

CRITICAL: Incorrect rewarming temperatures cause additional tissue damage.

Rewarming water temperature: 104‚Äì107¬∞F (40‚Äì41.7¬∞C) ‚Äî exactly
NOT hotter (damages tissue further)
NOT at body temperature (too slow, continued freezing can occur)

Duration: Continue rewarming 15‚Äì30 minutes until flushing (redness) returns to frostbitten area. Do NOT reheat if refreezing likely.

Post-rewarming: Blisters will form within 24 hours (normal). Do not drain. Elevate extremity, give pain medication (aspirin to prevent thrombosis), tetanus prophylaxis.

Cricothyrotomy (Emergency Surgical Airway)

Indication: Complete airway obstruction that cannot be relieved by other means (choking, anaphylaxis with severe swelling, etc.). This is the LAST RESORT to save life when patient would otherwise die.

Anatomical landmarks:
1. Palpate thyroid cartilage (Adam's apple) at front of neck
2. Feel below it for cricoid cartilage ring (feels like firm ridge)
3. The space between thyroid and cricoid is the cricothyroid membrane (target site)

Procedure (if trained and absolutely necessary):
1. Stabilize larynx with one hand (thumb and finger on sides of thyroid cartilage)
2. With other hand, make 1‚Äì1.5 inch horizontal incision through skin over cricothyroid membrane
3. Puncture cricothyroid membrane horizontally with knife or needle
4. Insert hollow tube (pen barrel, small breathing tube, or similar) through opening into trachea
5. Tape tube in place; breathing should resume
6. This buys time‚Äîrequires hospital transfer ASAP

Complications: Tracheal stenosis, voice changes, infection. Only perform when death is otherwise certain.

Chest Tube Insertion (Pneumothorax/Hemothorax)

Anatomical landmarks for safe insertion:
1. Palpate ribs and spaces between them (intercostal spaces)
2. Target: 4th or 5th intercostal space, along midaxillary line (mid-armpit level)
3. Insert needle/catheter above the rib (to avoid blood vessels running below each rib)
4. Push through chest wall until "pop" felt (entering pleural space)
5. If air rushes out: pneumothorax confirmed. If blood drains: hemothorax.
6. Secure catheter; attach drainage tube to one-way valve or water seal bottle
7. Monitor: air/fluid should drain; lung should re-expand

One-way valve (improvised): Can improvise with needle, tubing, and bottle of water (water seal allows air to escape but not re-enter). This maintains negative pressure in pleural space.

Heimlich Maneuver vs Back Blows

INFANT (under 12 months): Do NOT use Heimlich. Use back blows + chest thrusts:
1. Hold infant face-down on forearm
2. Support infant's jaw and chest with hand
3. Deliver 5 firm back blows between shoulder blades with heel of other hand
4. If object not dislodged, flip infant to back (supporting head)
5. Place 2 fingers on breastbone below nipple line
6. Deliver 5 firm chest thrusts (same depth as CPR compressions)
7. Repeat back blows + chest thrusts until object cleared or emergency help arrives

CHILD (1‚Äì8 years): Heimlich with modifications:
1. Stand behind child
2. Place heel of one hand on abdomen between navel and ribcage
3. Place other hand on top
4. Deliver quick, upward thrusts (gentler than for adults)
5. Repeat until object expelled

ADULT: Standard Heimlich:
1. Stand behind victim
2. Wrap arms around waist
3. Make fist above navel, below rib cage
4. Grasp fist with other hand
5. Press into abdomen with quick, upward thrusts
6. Repeat until object expelled or victim becomes unresponsive (switch to CPR)
