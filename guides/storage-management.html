Core Principles

Proper storage protects materials from degradation, contamination, theft, and hazards. Key factors: temperature control, moisture protection, light exclusion (for chemicals, fuels, medications), ventilation (for volatile materials), and physical security.

The Rule of 4 Enemies

  • Heat: Accelerates degradation, promotes mold, reduces shelf life
  • Light: Degrades oils, medicines, and some foods through photochemical reactions
  • Moisture: Promotes mold, rust, clumping, and microbial growth
  • Oxygen: Causes oxidation (rancidity in oils, rust in metals)

Food Storage

Canned Goods

  • Temperature: 50-70°F optimal. Avoid freeze-thaw cycles (cracks seals).
  • Shelf life: High-acid (pickles, tomatoes): 1-2 years. Low-acid (beans, meat): 3-5 years.
  • Inspection: Monthly check for rust, swelling, leaks. Discard compromised jars.
  • Rotation: FIFO (First-In-First-Out) discipline—use oldest cans first.
  • Labeling: Contents, processing date, and expiration estimate.

Dried Goods (Beans, Lentils, Pasta)

  • Moisture: Must be below 10%. High moisture promotes mold.
  • Container: Sealed containers (glass jars, metal cans, mylar bags with oxygen absorbers).
  • Location: Cool (60-75°F), dark, away from direct sunlight
  • Shelf life: 2-5 years if kept dry
  • Pest prevention: Store in food-grade plastic buckets with tight-fitting lids or use oxygen absorbers

Salt & Sugar

  • Salt: Indefinite shelf life if kept dry. Absorbs moisture easily; store in sealed containers.
  • Sugar: Indefinite shelf life. Prone to crystallization if exposed to humidity; store sealed.
  • Stock heavily: In collapse scenarios, salt and sugar become currency-valuable for food preservation and trade.

Oils & Fats

  • Rancidity: The enemy of stored oil. Oxidation causes off-flavors and potential toxins.
  • Storage: Cool (50-60°F), dark, sealed containers. Protect from light and oxygen.
  • Shelf life: Refined oils: 1-2 years. Unrefined oils: 6-12 months. Solid fats (lard, tallow, coconut oil): 2-3 years if sealed and cool.
  • Testing: Rancid oil has sharp, unpleasant odor. Discard if uncertain.

Seeds (Grain & Vegetable)

  • Viability: Seeds lose germination ability over time. Optimal storage is cool (32-40°F) and dry (5-10% moisture).
  • Containers: Sealed glass jars with oxygen absorbers or airtight metal cans
  • Shelf life: Most vegetable seeds: 2-5 years at room temperature; 5-10 years refrigerated. Grain for food: 5-10 years. Grain for planting: may lose viability after 3-5 years.
  • Testing: Before major planting, do a germination test (sprout a small batch to verify viability)

Fuel Storage

Wood (Firewood)

  • Moisture: Green wood (freshly cut) contains 50%+ moisture and burns poorly. Dry wood (6-12 months seasoning): ideal.
  • Storage: Stack on pallets or raised structures (off ground) in a sheltered location
  • Volume: Estimate 3-5 cords per person for a full winter (cord = 128 cubic feet)
  • Protection: Cover top to shed rain; allow air circulation on sides

Coal

  • Storage: Dry location, covered, off ground. Coal absorbs moisture. Cover with tarp or metal roofing.
  • Safety: Coal dust is respiratory hazard. Store away from living areas. Ensure good ventilation.
  • Types: Bituminous (common, smoky) vs. anthracite (less smoke, hotter burn). Anthracite preferred if available.
  • Quantity: Similar heating value to wood; estimate 1.5-2 tons per person for winter

Liquid Fuels (Gasoline, Diesel, Kerosene)

CRITICAL SAFETY: Liquid fuels are volatile and flammable. Improper storage causes explosions, fires, and poisoning. Follow ALL safety protocols.
  • Containers: ONLY approved fuel cans (metal or specialized plastic). NEVER store in glass or regular plastic bottles.
  • Location: Outside, away from buildings, electricity sources, and ignition points. Minimum 30 feet from residences.
  • Ventilation: Fumes are heavier than air; pool at ground level. Ensure proper ventilation.
  • Temperature: Keep cool (below 85°F). Excessive heat increases pressure and evaporation.
  • Shelf life: Gasoline: 3-6 months without stabilizer; 1-3 years with fuel stabilizer. Diesel: 6-12 months; biodiesel shorter. Kerosene: 1-2 years.
  • Rotation: Use and replace regularly. Old fuel gums up engines.
  • Labeling: Clearly label all containers with fuel type and date stored

Ammunition Storage

  • Temperature: 60-75°F. Avoid extremes. Temperature fluctuations cause condensation inside cartridges.
  • Humidity: Keep dry. 30-50% relative humidity ideal. Moisture causes corrosion and misfires.
  • Containers: Sealed metal ammo cans with desiccant packets (silica gel or calcium oxide)
  • Location: Cool, dry, secure location. Away from fuel and other explosives.
  • Rotation: Oldest ammunition should be used first (FIFO). Fire test a few rounds periodically to ensure viability.
  • Inspection: Visually inspect for corrosion, discoloration, or deformation. Discard suspicious cartridges.
  • Security: Ammunition should be locked up, especially if children are present.

Chemical Storage

  • Segregation: Keep incompatible chemicals separate (acids from bases, oxidizers from fuels, toxic from non-toxic)
  • Containers: Store corrosives in appropriate containers (glass for acids, some plastics for bases). Never store in metal if reactive.
  • Ventilation: Adequate ventilation in storage areas. Some chemicals emit harmful fumes.
  • Inventory: Label everything clearly. Maintain a list of what's stored, quantities, and purposes.
  • Safety: Store poisons separately under lock if children are present.
  • Spill kits: Keep absorbent materials and neutralizers nearby. Sand, diatomaceous earth, and baking soda can help manage spills.

Water Storage

  • Containers: Food-grade containers only. Glass or BPA-free plastic.
  • Treatment: Add 2 drops of unscented household bleach per liter for long-term storage to prevent algae and microbial growth
  • Light protection: Keep in dark location. Light promotes algae growth and photochemical reactions.
  • Location: Cool (50-70°F), protected from contamination. Cisterns and tanks should be covered and screened against insects and animals.
  • Cleaning: Disinfect storage containers annually. Drain completely, rinse with bleach solution (1:100 ratio), rinse thoroughly with clean water.
  • Rotation: Rotate water every 6-12 months if stored without treatment. With bleach treatment, can store 1-5 years.
  • Volume: Minimum 1 gallon per person per day for 2 weeks. Better: 1 gallon per person per day for 3 months.

Pest Prevention in Storage

Rodent Control

  • Prevention: Seal cracks and gaps larger than 1/4 inch. Elevate goods on pallets (12+ inches off ground). Remove food sources outside storage.
  • Monitoring: Look for droppings, gnaw marks, nesting material. Set snap traps or electronic sensors.
  • Treatment: Snap traps (quick, reusable) or poison (slower; dead bodies may smell). Cats (natural predators; supplement with other methods).

Insect Control

  • Prevention: Proper moisture (<12% for grain), cool temperature, sealed containers, clean storage areas
  • Monitoring: Pheromone traps detect early infestations. Check grain regularly for insects or webbing.
  • Treatment: Food-grade diatomaceous earth, heat or freeze treatment, or controlled fumigation

Bird Control

  • Prevention: Fine-mesh screens on vents and openings. Remove roosting perches and nesting sites.
  • Deterrents: Reflective tape, noise devices, or predator decoys (owls, hawks). Effectiveness varies.

Rotation Schedules

FIFO (First-In-First-Out) Implementation

  • Date everything when it enters storage. Use clear labels with month/year.
  • Place new stock behind older stock on shelves
  • Use shelf tags showing "Use by" dates in order
  • Conduct monthly FIFO audits to ensure compliance
  • Train all staff/family on the principle; make it habitual

Sample Rotation Schedule

  • Canned goods: Check monthly, rotate every 3 months
  • Dried goods: Inspect quarterly, open containers annually to check for pests
  • Oils: Use within 1-2 years; replace annually
  • Flour/grains: Rotate every 6 months; older flour should be used for cooking, newer stored for seed
  • Water (treated): Rotate every 12 months
  • Ammunition: Fire test samples annually; rotate stock if older than 5 years

Root Cellars & Underground Storage

Principles of Underground Storage

Below the frost line (typically 60-120 cm deep), soil temperature remains relatively constant year-round at 10-15°C. A root cellar exploits this natural temperature stability to store produce for 4-8 months without refrigeration.

Site Selection & Excavation

  • Location: North-facing hillside is ideal. Avoid low ground where water collects. Entrance should face north or northeast.
  • Water table: Dig a test hole to confirm the water table is at least 60 cm below the planned floor level.
  • Soil type: Clay soils hold moisture but drain poorly. Sandy soils drain well but need structural support. Loam is ideal.
  • Size: A family of 4 needs approximately 2.5m x 3m floor space with 1.8-2m ceiling height.

Construction Methods

  • Hillside cut (best): Excavate into a hillside. Walls are earth-backed on three sides. Only front wall and door need construction.
  • Underground box: Dig a pit, build walls from stone, concrete block, or timber. Lay a heavy beam roof covered with plastic sheeting, then 60+ cm of soil.
  • Above-ground with earth berming: In areas with high water tables, build at ground level and mound earth around it.

Ventilation & Humidity Control

  • Two-pipe system: Install an intake pipe near the floor and an exhaust pipe near the ceiling on opposite ends. Cool air enters low, warm air exits high.
  • Temperature target: 0-5°C for most root vegetables, 10-15°C for squash, onions, and garlic.
  • Humidity target: 85-95% for root vegetables, 60-70% for onions, garlic, and squash.

Storage Guidelines by Crop

  • Potatoes: Store in darkness at 4-7°C, high humidity. Do not wash before storing. Keeps 4-6 months.
  • Carrots, beets, turnips: Pack in damp sand or sawdust. 0-4°C, 90-95% humidity. Keeps 4-5 months.
  • Apples: Store separately from vegetables (emit ethylene gas). Wrap individually in newspaper. 0-4°C. Keeps 2-5 months.
  • Onions and garlic: Cure for 2 weeks before storage. Hang in mesh bags. 60-70% humidity.
  • Winter squash: Cure at 25-30°C for 10 days. Store at 10-15°C. Keeps 3-6 months.
  • Cabbages: Hang upside down from ceiling hooks or wrap in newspaper. 0-4°C. Keeps 3-4 months.
