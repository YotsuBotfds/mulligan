1. Fiber Sources

Animal Fibers

Wool

The most widely available and forgiving fiber for hand spinners. Comes from sheep and has natural crimp, elasticity, and water resistance due to lanolin content.

  • Staple Length: 2-6 inches (varies by breed)
  • Micron Count: 15-30 microns (finer = softer, lower count = pricklier)
  • Elasticity: Excellent - can withstand significant stretching
  • Moisture Absorption: Can absorb 30% of weight in water
  • Best Uses: General yarns, felting, insulation
  • Breeds: Merino (fine, 18-24Î¼), Romney (medium, 28-32Î¼), Corriedale (medium, 24-30Î¼), Shetland (medium, 20-25Î¼)

Alpaca

Luxury fiber from South American camelids. Softer than wool, exceptionally warm, with little lanolin and naturally occurring colors.

  • Staple Length: 3-5 inches
  • Micron Count: 18-24 microns (very soft)
  • Elasticity: Lower than wool - requires proper spinning technique
  • Luster: Natural sheen, 36 natural colors
  • Care: Requires gentle handling to prevent pilling
  • Best Uses: Fine garments, baby items, specialty yarns

Angora (Rabbit)

From angora rabbits. Extremely soft, warm, and fluffy with a halo effect when spun.

  • Staple Length: 2-3 inches
  • Micron Count: 12-16 microns (very fine)
  • Fiber Density: Very fine, requires binding with other fibers
  • Production: 4-8 ounces per rabbit per harvesting cycle
  • Challenge: Slippery fiber, difficult to spin alone
  • Best Uses: Blended with wool for fuzzy yarns, specialty items

Silk

Protein fiber produced by silkworm cocoons. Most lustrous of all natural fibers with exceptional strength.

  • Fiber Type: Continuous filament (unlike staple fibers)
  • Strength: Stronger than steel of same diameter
  • Sericulture: Cocoons are boiled to harvest 300-900 yard continuous filament
  • Cultivated Forms: Bombyx (mulberry silk, white), Tussar (wild, golden), Eri (peace silk, doesn't kill pupa)
  • Processing: Sericin (gum) must be degummed to achieve softness
  • Best Uses: Luxury garments, scarves, mixed with wool

Plant Fibers

Cotton

Seed hair fiber. Cool, breathable, but requires significant processing to remove oils and prepare for spinning.

  • Staple Length: 0.75-2 inches
  • Strength: Stronger when wet than dry (unlike wool)
  • Cellullose Content: 90% pure cellulose
  • Processing: Ginning removes seeds, cleaning removes dust, carding aligns fibers
  • Absorbency: Highly absorbent - excellent for dyeing
  • Best Uses: Summer clothing, dishcloths, thread
  • Challenge: No crimp means less elasticity than wool

Flax / Linen

Bast fiber from flax plant stem. Strong, cool, produces smooth lustrous yarn. Requires extensive processing.

  • Fiber Location: Between outer bark and woody core
  • Staple Length: 10-20 inches (long staple length)
  • Strength: Much stronger than cotton, improves when wet
  • Processing Steps: Harvesting, retting (breaking down pectin), scutching (beating), hackling (combing)
  • Retting Time: 2-6 weeks in water or field
  • Best Uses: Fine smooth yarns, summer textiles, canvas
  • Challenge: Time-intensive processing, stiff until softened

Hemp

Bast fiber similar to flax but coarser. Exceptionally strong, durable, and sustainable.

  • Staple Length: 15-60 inches (longest of plant fibers)
  • Strength: 3 times stronger than cotton
  • Durability: Resistant to decay, UV damage, mold
  • Processing: Similar to flax (retting, scutching, hackling)
  • Growth: Fast-growing, minimal pesticide needs
  • Best Uses: Durable textiles, rope, canvas, rustic items
  • Note: Coarser than flax, requires blending for comfort

Nettle Fiber

Bast fiber from stinging nettle plants. Can be harvested wild, excellent sustainability.

  • Harvesting: Cut stems in late summer
  • Processing: Retting (4-8 weeks), scutching, hackling
  • Fiber Quality: Fine, soft, lustrous
  • Strength: Comparable to flax
  • Sustainability: Renewable, requires no cultivation
  • Best Uses: Fine garments, blended yarns

Milkweed Pod Fibers

Fluffy seed pod hairs. Lightweight, warm, but challenging to process and spin.

  • Harvest: Mature dry pods only
  • Fiber Character: Hollow, crimped, fine
  • Challenge: Very slippery, needs careful handling
  • Preparation: Cannot be wet processed easily
  • Best Uses: Insulation, blended with wool, stuffing

Processing Raw Fiber

Washing Fleece

Remove lanolin, dirt, and vegetable matter before processing. Critical first step.

  • Temperature: Use hot water (140-160Â°F / 60-70Â°C). Do NOT shock with temperature changes
  • Detergent: Use plain soap, olive oil castile soap, or wool wash. Avoid harsh detergents
  • Soaking: Submerge wool for 20-30 minutes. Gentle pressing only - no agitation
  • Rinsing: Repeat rinses until water runs clear. Last rinse should be cooler water
  • Drying: Squeeze gently, spread on screen or towel in shade. Air dry completely before carding
  • Yield: Expect 30-50% weight loss from lanolin removal
  • Plant Matter: Pick out obviously contaminated areas before washing

Carding (Opening Fibers)

Align short staple fibers in preparation for spinning. Breaks up clumps and removes remaining debris.

  • Hand Carding: Two hand carders with wire cloth surfaces
  • Process: Load fiber on one carder, brush back and forth with other carder 5-10 times until fibers are parallel and fluffy
  • Roll Out: Gently roll carded fiber into loose roll or batt
  • Drum Carder: Mechanized option for larger volumes (hand-crank or electric)
  • Result: Soft, open fiber ready for spinning
  • Working Direction: Carders have wire grain direction - work with the grain

Combing (Hackling)

Separate long staple fibers and arrange in parallel order. Used for long fibers like flax, hemp, and long-staple wool.

  • Tools: Hackle (wooden board with metal spikes) or hand combs
  • Short Fibers: Remove short fibers (noil) that fall through
  • Preparation: Produces smoother, more lustrous yarn than carding
  • Best For: Flax, hemp, worsted spinning
  • Drawback: Labor-intensive, removes some fiber as waste
  • Result: Top (combed long fibers in parallel arrangement)

2. Spinning

Drop Spindle

Simple Drop Spindle Construction

Easiest and cheapest way to spin. Can be made from basic materials.

  • Shaft: Wooden stick, 6-10 inches long, 3/8 inch diameter
  • Whorl: Heavy disk attached near bottom (wood, ceramic, clay - 1-2.5 inches diameter)
  • Hook or Notch: At top to hold and guide yarn
  • Weight: Total 0.5-2 ounces (heavier for coarser fibers, lighter for fine)
  • DIY Version: Wooden dowel + clay disk + hook works well
  • Balance: Should spin freely with minimal wobble

Drop Spindle Operation

  • Setup: Wind some previously spun yarn on spindle as base
  • Attaching Fiber: Tie prepared fiber to base yarn or attach to spindle
  • Spinning Motion: Spin spindle (clockwise for Z-twist, counterclockwise for S-twist)
  • Drafting: Let twist move up into fiber while controlling fiber length with fingers
  • Rhythm: Spin, draft, wind on. Usually 1-2 feet per spindle rotation
  • Winding: Once spindle reaches floor, wind yarn around shaft, rewind below
  • Learning Curve: Most spinners achieve proficiency in 5-10 hours practice

Spinning Wheel Basics

Wheel Components

  • Wheel: Large spinning wheel (12-24 inch diameter) turned by foot treadle
  • Treadle: Foot pedal connected to wheel with crank mechanism
  • Flyer: Rotating hook device that inserts twist into fiber
  • Bobbin: Removable spool where yarn winds
  • Drive Belt: Connects wheel to flyer, transfers rotation
  • Orifice: Opening where yarn passes through flyer
  • Maiden Posts: Support frame for flyer and bobbin

Wheel Operation

  • Treadle Speed: Usually 1 push per 2 yarn sections produced
  • Flyer Rotation: Creates twist in yarn
  • Bobbin Rotation: Wind speed determines how fast yarn is wound on
  • Drive Ratio: Wheel rotations per treadle push. Common ratios: 4:1 to 20:1
  • Tension: Brake or tension band controls how tightly yarn winds (singles to ply balance)
  • Types: Wheel at top (Saxony), wheel to side (Norwegian), double-drive vs single-drive

Spinning Techniques

Short Draw (Woolen Spinning)

Fiber is drafted a short distance (1-3 inches) into the twist zone. Creates airy, elastic yarn.

  • Process: Fiber enters twist, gets twisted, then drafts back as twist enters
  • Twist Direction: Twist stays in drafted fiber, supports as it's spun
  • Best For: Woolen yarn, carded fibers, textured effects
  • Result: Lofty, bouncy, less dense yarn
  • Fiber Position: Both hands in front of orifice
  • Speed: Generally faster than long draw

Long Draw (Worsted Spinning)

Fiber is drafted a longer distance (6-12+ inches) before twist catches it. Creates smooth, strong yarn.

  • Process: Fiber is drafted first, then twist is inserted into drafted length
  • Twist Direction: Drifts back through drafted fibers to insert twist
  • Best For: Combed fibers, worsted yarn, fine consistent yarn
  • Result: Dense, smooth, strong yarn
  • Hand Position: Back hand in supply, front hand drafts forward
  • Control: Requires more skill but produces professional results
  • Speed: Generally slower but requires less re-working

Plying (Combining Singles)

Twist multiple single strands together to create stronger, more balanced yarn.

  • Two-ply: Twist 2 singles together (opposite direction from singles twist)
  • Three-ply: Combines 3 singles for even more strength and drape
  • Twist Direction: If singles are Z-twist, ply with S-twist (and vice versa)
  • Tension: Keep plying tension consistent to avoid bias or unevenness
  • Ply Twist: Less twist needed than in singles (about 1/3 to 1/2)
  • Advantages: Stronger yarn, balanced tension, hides imperfections
  • Two-Fold / Three-Fold: Terms for 2-ply and 3-ply yarns

Measuring Yarn

Wraps Per Inch (WPI)

Quick method to determine yarn thickness and grist.

  • Tool: Wrap spun yarn around ruler or measuring tape with no gaps and no overlap
  • Count: How many wraps fit in 1 inch of ruler

Setting Twist

Finishing spun yarn to lock in twist and prevent unraveling.

  • Washing: Hot water wash sets twist in animal fibers
  • Drying: Hang under weight (full sink, water bottle) while drying to prevent tangling
  • No Agitation: Gentle submersion only - no squeezing
  • Temperature Shock: Cold final rinse helps set twist
  • Weight Drying: Prevents yarn from curling or felting
  • Completion: Yarn should feel stable and not unwind from itself

3. Weaving Fundamentals

Basic Weave Structures

Plain Weave

Most fundamental weave. Warp and weft alternate over and under each row.

  • Pattern: Over 1, under 1, alternating
  • Strength: Very strong, excellent edge definition
  • Properties: Even texture, little bias
  • Uses: Basic fabrics, stable structures
  • Variations: Basket weave (over 2, under 2)

Twill Weave

Diagonal pattern created by offsetting the over-under sequence each row.

  • Pattern: Over 2, under 2 (offset each row)
  • Diagonal Direction: Z-twill (left) or S-twill (right)
  • Properties: More fabric weight, less stretchy, visible diagonal
  • Uses: Durable fabrics, herringbone patterns, denim
  • Thread Count: Typically fewer threads per inch than plain weave

Satin Weave

Long floats create smooth lustrous surface. Generally over 4 or more.

  • Pattern: Over 4 (or more), under 1, offset each row
  • Float Length: Creates smooth surface on one side
  • Properties: Lustrous, less durable than plain/twill, shows dirt
  • Uses: Formal wear, linings, decorative items
  • Durability: Not recommended for heavy wear

Weaving Terminology

Warp & Weft

  • Warp: Vertical threads strung on loom. Tension-bearing, critical for structure
  • Weft: Horizontal threads woven through warp. Creates fill and pattern
  • Warp Tension: Must be even and consistent for good results
  • Selvedge: Self-finished edge where weft returns (no finishing needed)
  • Shed: Opening between upper and lower warp threads where weft passes through
  • Pick: Single passage of weft across loom (one row of weave)

Setting Up a Loom

Basic Setup Steps

  • 1. Prepare Warp: Calculate length needed, measure out and bundle
  • 2. Attach to Loom: Tie warp bundle to warp beam (back) and cloth beam (front)
  • 3. Thread Heddles: Pass warp threads through heddles in pattern for weave
  • 4. Create Shed: Lift heddles to separate warp into alternating layers
  • 5. Insert Reed: Thread warp through reed (comb-like) for spacing and beaters
  • 6. Tension Check: All threads should have even tension, feel slightly springy
  • 7. Weave Test: First several inches check alignment and spacing

Beating & Weaving

Proper Beating Technique

  • Beat: Use beater (reed) to push weft firmly into woven fabric
  • Consistency: Even pressure each beat creates consistent cloth
  • Tension Balance: Pull weft to maintain straight selvedges
  • Slay Angle: Beater angle affects beat force and fabric handle
  • Rhythm: Shed, pick (insert weft), beat, repeat
  • Over-Beating: Creates dense, stiff fabric
  • Under-Beating: Loose, weak fabric that falls apart

4. Loom Construction

Simple Frame Loom

Building a Basic Frame Loom

Simplest loom to construct. Ideal for learning weaving and creating small pieces.

  • Frame: Wood (4x4 posts or 2x4 boards) or metal frame 12-24 inches square
  • Nails or Pegs: Spaced evenly (1/4 to 1/2 inch apart) along top and bottom
  • Spacing: Space determines thread spacing (common: 10-20 threads per inch)
  • Warping: Tie warp to first nail, wind back and forth, tie off last nail
  • Heddles: Use stick or shed stick to create separate sheds
  • Weft Threading: Use needle or shuttle to weave weft through shed
  • Beating: Use flat stick to beat each weft row

Rigid Heddle Loom

Rigid Heddle Basics

Modern loom design allowing quicker weaving than frame looms.

  • Heddle: Board with holes and slots for warp threads
  • Holes: Threads pass straight through (stay in back shed)
  • Slots: Threads pass through (alternate between front and back)
  • Width: Common sizes: 10, 15, 20 inches
  • Threading: Alternating hole-slot-hole creates shed
  • Lifting: Raise heddle to create shed, lower for reverse
  • Advantage: Quick shed creation, suitable for small to medium weaving

Rigid Heddle Setup

  • 1. Measure Warp: Calculate length needed (same as other looms)
  • 2. Warp Onto Pegs: Wind warp back and forth on warping pegs or board
  • 3. Bring to Loom: Carefully transport warp bundle to loom
  • 4. Thread Heddle: Thread first thread through hole, next through slot, alternating
  • 5. Attach to Beam: Tie both warp ends to front and back beams
  • 6. Apply Tension: Adjust tension screws until even tension across all threads
  • 7. Test Sheds: Heddle up, heddle down - should create clear separation

Backstrap Loom

Backstrap Loom Construction

Ancient, portable loom used throughout the world. Excellent for scarves and narrow pieces.

  • Warp Beam: Wooden bar or dowel 12-36 inches long
  • Cloth Beam: Smaller bar to wrap finished cloth
  • Strap: Webbing or rope attached to cloth beam, worn around weaver's back
  • Heddles: Looped strings (cotton, nylon) threading warp for sheds
  • Shed Sticks: 2-3 sticks creating additional sheds
  • Shuttle: Small tool to pass weft through
  • Tension Source: Weaver's body provides tension via backstrap

Inkle Loom (Band & Strap Loom)

Inkle Loom Basics

Specialized loom for creating narrow decorative bands and straps.

  • Size: Typically 2-4 inches wide, 12-36 inches long
  • Pegs: Top and bottom rails with evenly spaced pegs
  • Heddle: Looped or threaded to create sheds
  • Threading: Warp winds back and forth creating many passes
  • Advantage: Creates bands with patterning and multiple colors
  • Speed: Quick to setup and weave
  • Products: Bookmarks, trim, straps, decorative bands

Heddle Making

Creating String Heddles for Rigid Heddle Looms

  • Material: Cotton string, yarn, or dental floss
  • Heddle Height: 2-3 inches long (depends on warp thickness)
  • Loop Size: Slightly larger than warp thread diameter
  • Making: Form loop around heddle bar, tie off securely
  • Spacing: Match rigid heddle slot spacing (typically 1/4 or 1/8 inch)
  • Strength: Must support warp tension without breaking
  • Test: Pull gently to ensure knots hold

Warping Techniques

Warping Peg or Board Method

  • Setup: Pegs or nails at top and bottom, distance apart = warp length
  • Wind: Tie warp end to top peg, wind around top-bottom alternately
  • Counting: Count passes for thread count
  • Even Tension: Maintain consistent tension while winding
  • Careful Removal: Bundle warp carefully to prevent tangling
  • Advantage: Easy to see total length, good for learning

Direct Warping on Loom

  • Method: Wind warp directly onto loom beams
  • Advantage: Faster for production, no transport issues
  • Challenge: Tension control during winding
  • Spacing: Use reed or guide to maintain correct spacing
  • Tension: Keep consistent tension throughout winding

5. Knitting & Crochet

Knitting Basics

Cast On Methods

Foundation row for knitting. Different methods create different properties.

Long-Tail Cast On: Most common and versatile. Creates balanced, elastic edge. Uses single strand from ball plus tail length.
  • Long-Tail: Elastic edge, professional look, requires measuring tail
  • Thumb Cast On: Simple alternative, slightly less elastic
  • Knitted Cast On: When you don't have a long tail to measure
  • Cable Cast On: Strong, firm edge, more rows of setup
  • Provisional Cast On: Temporary loops for picking up later (for seamless design)
  • Ribbed Cast On: Creates ribbed edge, slightly firmer
  • Stretchy Cast On: Maximum elasticity for cuffs and edges

Basic Stitches

Knitting Stitches

Knit Stitch: Right needle enters left needle from left to right, wraps yarn, pulls through. Creates "V" pattern on fabric face.
Purl Stitch: Right needle enters left needle from right to left, wraps yarn, pulls through. Creates bumpy pattern on fabric face.
  • Knit (K): Basic stitch, creates smooth "V" texture, produces stockinette stitch when alternating rows
  • Purl (P): Reverse of knit, creates bumps when on fabric face
  • Garter Stitch: Knit all rows - creates ridged horizontal pattern
  • Stockinette Stitch: Knit front, purl back rows - smooth front, bumpy back
  • Ribbed Stitch: Alternate knit and purl columns - elastic, vertical lines
  • Seed Stitch: Alternate knit and purl each row - bumpy texture, flat, reversible

Increasing & Decreasing (Shaping)

  • Increase: Adding stitches to make fabric wider
  • M1 (Make One): Lift yarn between stitches, knit through back loop
  • Yarn Over: Wrap yarn around needle, creates lacy hole
  • Knit Front & Back: Knit in same stitch twice
  • Decrease: Removing stitches to make fabric narrower
  • K2tog (Knit 2 Together): Knit two stitches as one, leans right
  • SSK (Slip, Slip, Knit): Slip 2 stitches, knit together, leans left
  • P2tog: Purl two stitches together (for purl rows)

  • Tension: Keep stitches loose enough to work into, tight enough to control

Dye Extraction Process

Basic Extraction Steps

  • 1. Prepare Plant Material: Fresh or dried dye plants. Dried plants often more potent
  • 2. Extract: Simmer plant material in water 30 minutes to 1 hour
  • 3. Cool: Let bath cool to room temperature
  • 4. Strain: Remove plant material through fine strainer
  • 5. Dye: Add pre-mordanted fiber to dye bath
  • 6. Heat Slowly: Bring to 185-195Â°F, hold 30-60 minutes
  • 7. Cool: Let cool in dye bath (often improves color)
  • 8. Rinse: Rinse in warm, then cool water until clear
  • 9. Dry: Squeeze gently, air dry away from direct sun

Vat Dyeing (Indigo & Woad)

Indigo Vat Basics

Indigo requires special reduction process to become soluble and take up by fibers.

  • Indigo Reduction: Uses reducing agent (sodium dithionite, thiourea dioxide) to break indigo into soluble form
  • Vat Color: Reduced vat is pale yellow-green (indigo is colorless when reduced)
  • Oxidation: Fiber exposed to air develops blue color (indigo oxidizes back to blue)
  • Multiple Dips: Deeper blues require repeated dips (oxidation between each)
  • Temperature: Indigo vat must stay 160-180Â°F (cooling slows process)
  • Chemistry: pH around 10-12 (alkaline) maintained with washing soda or lye
  • Safety: Indigo vats can be maintained for months - save for repeated use

pH Effects on Color

Acid vs Alkaline Dyes

Many plants produce different colors depending on pH of dye bath.

  • Acidic (pH 3-5): Add vinegar or citric acid to bath
  • Neutral (pH 6-7): Plain water, no additives
  • Alkaline (pH 8-12): Add soda ash (washing soda) or lye to bath
  • Madder: Acidic bath = red, Neutral = orange, Alkaline = purple-red
  • Weld: Yellow across pH range, slightly greenish in alkaline
  • Cochineal: Red in acidic, becomes purplish in alkaline
  • Testing: Test small amounts in different pH to see color shifts

Fastness Properties

Light Fastness & Wash Fastness

  • Light Fastness: How well color resists fading from sunlight exposure
  • Excellent: Indigo, madder, logwood, weld
  • Good: Woad, cochineal, walnut
  • Fair: Marigold, some yellows
  • Wash Fastness: How well color survives washing
  • Excellent: Indigo, madder, cochineal, logwood
  • Good: Weld, woad, walnut
  • Fair: Marigold, many delicate flowers
  • Improvement: Tannin pre-mordant and iron after-bath improve both fastnesses

7. Felting

Wet Felting Process

Creating Felt Sheets

Wool fibers are matted together using heat, water, and agitation to create non-woven fabric.

  • Fiber Preparation: Carded wool or loose fibers work best. Separate locks into thin layers
  • Laying Out: Place fibers on flat surface, building layers in alternating directions
  • Density: Use enough fiber to create sheet desired thickness (light = less dense, thick = more dense)
  • Wetness: Spray with hot soapy water until thoroughly saturated but not dripping
  • Agitation: Gentle rubbing and pressing at first, then more vigorous agitation
  • Rolling: Roll up and roll back and forth to continue felting
  • Testing: Pick at edge - if fibers don't separate, felting is complete
  • Shrinkage: Felt shrinks 25-40% depending on agitation intensity

Wet Felting Steps

  1. Card wool: Create fluffy carded batts or roving
  2. Divide into layers: Pull apart into thin, manageable layers
  3. Layer on foam: Place first layer on felting mat (bamboo or bubble wrap)
  4. Alternate direction: Each layer in different direction (horizontal, vertical, diagonal)
  5. Repeat layers: 3-6 layers typically create good felt
  6. Hot soapy water: Mix hot water with wool wash or olive oil soap
  7. Wet fibers: Spray or pour water over fibers until saturated
  8. Gently rub: Use flat hands to rub gently 2-3 minutes
  9. Increase agitation: Rub more vigorously for 5-10 minutes
  10. Roll: Roll mat with felt inside, roll back and forth for 5-10 minutes
  11. Check: Unroll, check if fibers are felted (don't separate)
  12. Rinse: Rinse thoroughly in cool water to remove soap
  13. Squeeze: Gently squeeze out water
  14. Dry: Lay flat on towel to air dry, or machine dry if heat-set desired

Needle Felting

Dry Felting with Needles

Create shaped objects by stabbing wool with barbed needles, causing matting and compacting.

  • Needle Types: Special felting needles with tiny barbs (different gauges for different work)
  • Fiber: Use carded wool roving or pre-carded batts
  • Base: Work on foam pad or felting block (dense foam protects needles)
  • Technique: Repeatedly stab fibers with needle, turning as you work
  • Shaping: Build 3D objects by adding fiber and stabbing until firm
  • Needle Care: Needles are fragile - go slowly, avoid bent needles
  • Speed: Much faster than wet felting for small objects
  • Results: Creates firm, sculptural forms
  • Limitation: Cannot be used for fabric - too brittle

Nuno Felting

Felting Fiber to Fabric

Combines wet felting with pre-existing fabric (usually gauze or silk) to create lightweight, textured material.

  • Fabric Base: Use thin, loose-weave fabric (silk gauze, cotton voile, tulle)
  • Fiber Layer: Place thin layer of carded wool on top of fabric
  • Wetting: Wet with hot soapy water as in basic wet felting
  • Agitation: Rub and felt fibers into fabric base
  • Shrinkage: Fabric shrinks less than fiber, creating wrinkled textured surface
  • Results: Lightweight, durable, interesting surface texture
  • Advantage: Combines fabric structure with felted surface

Shrinkage Calculations

Predicting Final Felt Size

  • Average Shrinkage: 25-40% depending on agitation and fiber type
  • Light Felting: ~25% shrinkage
  • Moderate Felting: ~30% shrinkage
  • Heavy Felting: ~35-40% shrinkage
  • Calculation: Final size = Initial size Ã— (100% - Shrinkage %)
  • Example: 24-inch square starts with 25% shrinkage: 24 Ã— 0.75 = 18 inches

  • Testing: Always felt test sample first to determine actual shrinkage

Felted Garment Construction

Making Felted Garments

  • Method 1: Knit Then Felt: Knit large loose sweater, then full-wash to felt
  • Method 2: Felt Then Cut: Felt a large sheet, cut and sew pieces into garment
  • Patterns: Account for shrinkage when sizing - knit or cut 20-40% larger than finished size
  • Seaming: If felting after knitting, seams felt together. Cut method requires hand-sewn seams
  • Edges: Felted edges finish cleanly without binding
  • Durability: Felted garments are very durable and insulating
  • Care: Can be washed in cool water, may continue slight felting

8. Rope & Cordage Making

Fiber Selection for Cordage

Properties for Strong Rope

  • Length: Long staple fibers create stronger rope (less joints)
  • Strength: Individual fiber strength matters, but twist creates strength
  • Elasticity: Some elasticity prevents brittleness
  • Moisture Resistance: Important for outdoor/marine use
  • Best Plant Fibers: Hemp (strongest), Flax, Nettle, Jute
  • Best Animal Fibers: Wool, sinew, rawhide
  • Preparation: Process through retting/scutching to separate fibers

Three-Strand Rope Laying

Making Rope by Hand

Simple method to create rope from prepared fibers.

  • Fiber Preparation: Start with long parallel fibers (hackled fiber is best)
  • Create Yarns: Twist 3 bundles of fibers together (Z-twist direction)
  • Three Cords: Each of 3 main strands is composed of many twisted fibers
  • Ply Together: Twist 3 yarn-twisted cords together in opposite direction (S-twist)
  • Tension: Keep even tension on all three strands while twisting
  • Reverse Twist: Opposite direction twist in plying prevents rope unraveling
  • Strength: Twisted rope is many times stronger than parallel fibers
  • Thickness: Use more fiber bundles for thicker rope

8. Leather & Hide Working

Making Moccasins from Leather

Traditional Moccasin Construction

Moccasins provide comfortable, durable footwear using minimal materials. A basic one-piece moccasin uses a single piece of soft leather (deer hide or leather), sewn with minimal waste.

Materials

  • Hide: Soft brain-tanned leather (deer, elk, moose) or commercial garment leather (1.5-2.5 mm thick)
  • Thread: Strong waxed sinew or strong thread (upholstery thread, waxed linen)
  • Needle: Heavy leather needle (large eye, strong shaft)
  • Sole material: Heavier leather (3-5 mm) or rawhide for sole (optional)
  • Insole: Dried grass, bark cloth, or additional leather for interior cushioning

Pattern and Sizing

  • Foot tracing: Trace foot on paper, adding 1/2 inch all around for wearing ease and construction seams
  • Heel extension: Add 2 inches beyond heel for heel cup formation
  • Pattern shape: Roughly oval, wider at ball of foot, tapered at heel
  • Sizing check: Cut pattern from paper first, test fit before cutting leather

Construction Steps

  1. Cut leather: Using pattern, cut moccasin upper from soft leather. Include seam allowance (1/2 inch) on all edges
  2. Soften leather if needed: Wet with cool water, work leather supple by hand. Leather should be flexible but not limp.
  3. Form heel cup: Fold heel section upward and inward, creating cup shape. Hold or temporarily stitch.
  4. Sew heel seam: Using waxed sinew and running stitch, sew heel seam with stitches 1/4 inch apart. Begin at top heel, sew toward sole.
  5. Form forefoot: Fold leather up at toe area, creating toe pocket. Gather excess leather at toe.
  6. Sew toe closure: Sew toe seam, gathering excess leather neatly. Stitches should be small and tight.
  7. Side seams: Sew sides of moccasin from ankle opening to toe, using whip stitch (thread passes over edge) for strong seam
  8. Sole attachment (optional): If adding separate sole, stitch with heavy stitching all around edge
  9. Finishing: Trim excess leather, sand or smooth edges, test fit on foot
  10. Break-in: Wear moccasins to mold to foot shape. Leather stretches slightly with use.

    • Materials: Beeswax (natural wax from honeycomb), cotton or linen fabric
    • Process: Melt beeswax gently over low heat. Dip fabric or brush warm beeswax onto fabric. Allow to coolâ€”wax penetrates fiber and creates water-resistant surface.

    • Oils: Linseed oil (fast-drying), tung oil (moisture-resistant), walnut oil
    • Process: Mix oil with natural resin (pine tar, rosin) in ratio 2:1 oil to resin. Heat gently to combine. Apply warm to fabric or leather.
    • Fabric suitable: Cotton, linen, burlap (coarser fabrics absorb oil better)
    • Durability: 1-2 years with exposure, ages to darker color naturally

    Plant-Based Resin Waterproofing

    • Pine tar: Boil pine wood chips/sap, collect tar. Mix with grease (tallow or plant oil) and apply.

    Repair Techniques

    Darning Details

    Darning repairs holes in knitted garments by creating a patch of new weave.

    • Pattern: Run horizontal threads back and forth across hole, creating warp. Then weave vertical threads (weft) over/under horizontal threads.
    • Thread selection: Match original yarn weight and fiber content for invisible repair
    • Sizing: Darn slightly beyond hole edges (1/2 inch minimum) to anchor repair securely
    • Holes in socks: Most common darning application. Repeat darning extends sock life by years.

    Patching Woven Fabric

    • AppliquÃ© patch: Cut patch from matching fabric, 1-2 inches larger than hole in all directions. Whip stitch edges with matching thread, or use fabric glue + topstitching.
    • Backing patch: For seams or edges, apply patch to inside/underside, hidden from view
    • Stitching: Use small, tight stitches (1/8 inch apart) for durability. Multiple passes strengthen repair.
    • Worn areas: Reinforce elbows, knees before holes appear, extending garment life significantly

    Stitching Basics for Repairs

    • Whip stitch: Thread passes over edge of fabric, pulling closed. Used for edge-to-edge seams (bindings, patches)
    • Running stitch: Needle passes in/out of fabric at regular intervals. Basic, strong stitch for general seaming
    • Back stitch: Needle goes forward then back, creating double-layered line. Strongest hand stitch.
    • Blanket stitch: Stitch along edge with thread looping over needle. Prevents fraying, decorative finish.
    • Button sewing: Four-hole button: pass thread through alternating hole pairs, creating X pattern. Shank button: wrap thread around button stalk to create space for fabric thickness.

7. Raw Fiber Processing

Cotton Processing

Growing & Harvesting Cotton

  • Plant Growth: Cotton grows as shrub 3-6 feet tall. Prefers warm, well-drained soil with full sun. Growing season 5-7 months depending on climate.
  • Flowering: Flowers appear 60-70 days after planting. Blooms open cream/pink colored, fade to red after pollination.
  • Boll Development: After flower drops, green boll (seed pod) develops over 30-40 days, filled with developing fibers.
  • Fiber Maturation: Fibers mature as boll grows. Once mature, boll dries and splits open naturally.
  • Harvest Timing: Pick when bolls burst open and white fluffy fiber is fully visible and dry. Wet bolls contain seeds too tightly to process easily.
  • Hand Harvesting: Pick fibers from boll carefully without catching brown boll material (which contaminates batch). Store picked cotton in clean bags away from dirt.

Ginning: Separating Seed from Lint

  • Purpose: Remove seeds from raw cotton fiber (lint). Seeds will break needles and damage spindles if not removed.
  • Hand Ginning: Pass cotton between two dowels or wooden rollers. Roll toward you slowly, twisting cotton. Seeds fall away, lint wraps around roller.
  • Roller Gin Construction: Two wooden rollers (4-6 inches diameter, 12-18 inches long) mounted parallel in wooden frame. Powered by crank handle or treadle. Small gap (1/8 inch) between rollers controls fiber feed rate.
  • Feeding: Load small handful of seeded cotton into hopper above rollers. Rollers pull fiber between them. Seeds cannot pass gap, fall to ground. Ginned fiber exits on opposite side.
  • Safety: Never feed fiber with hands or hair near rollers. Use stick to push fiber if needed. Stop immediately if fiber jams.
  • Cleaning: After ginning, gently pick out any remaining seed fragments by hand or with large tweezers.

Carding Cotton with Hand Cards

  • Purpose: Align fibers parallel and remove remaining debris. Carded fiber is ready for spinning.
  • Hand Cards: Two rectangular blocks (4x7 inches typical) with short, densely-packed metal wires on surface. Wires bent at angle to comb fiber.
  • Basic Process: Lay small handful of cotton on left card. Brush right card across left card, combing fibers. Fibers transfer to right card, aligned with wire direction.
  • Multiple Passes: After first pass, transfer fiber back to left card, repeat. 3-5 passes typically needed for well-carded fiber.
  • Removing Neps: As you card, small tangles (neps) become visible. Remove by hand or repeat carding until neps disappear.
  • Final Preparation: On final pass, form fiber into loose roving shape. Fiber should be smooth, aligned, with consistent density.

Fiber Staple Length & Quality

  • Short-Staple Cotton: 0.75-1.1 inches long. Shorter fibers create weaker, less lustrous yarn. Easier for beginners. Better for bulky, rustic yarns.
  • Long-Staple Cotton: 1.1-1.6 inches long (e.g., Pima, Egyptian). Creates strong, lustrous, fine yarn. More difficult to spin but superior fabric quality.
  • Staple Length Matters: Longer staple = easier spinning, thinner yarn possible, stronger final yarn. Short staple requires thicker yarn, weaker at thin counts.
  • Observing Staple: When carded, fibers should mostly align length-wise. Measure longest visible fibers for rough staple estimate.

Flax â†’ Linen Processing

Growing & Harvesting Flax

  • Plant Growth: Flax grows 2-4 feet tall with single stem, minimal branching. Prefers cool, moist climate. Growing season 80-100 days.
  • Flowering: Small blue flowers bloom, lasting one day. Appearance indicates plant is mature.
  • Seed Development: After flowers drop, seed pods develop. Pods yellow when mature, rattle when shaken.
  • Harvesting Timing: Harvest for linen fiber when seed pods are yellow but not brown. This timing gives best fiber without excessive seed development.
  • Pulling (not Cutting): Grasp plant near soil, pull entire plant including roots from ground. Whole plant contains longest fibers. Cutting loses root-length fibers.
  • Bundle & Dry: Tie harvested plants in bundles. Lay on ground in thin layer or hang to dry 2-3 weeks. Fibers separate easier when dry.

Retting: Breaking Down Plant Structure

  • Purpose: Bacteria dissolve pectin holding fiber bundles to woody core (retted plant). Pectin removal allows fiber separation.
  • Water Retting (Faster): Submerge dry plant bundles in still water (pond, tank, barrel) at 70-75Â°F. Duration: 5-14 days depending on temperature. Higher temp = faster retting. Daily or every-other-day agitation prevents bottom plants from decomposing.
  • Water Retting Procedure: Weight down plants with boards to keep submerged. Check for retting completion by testing fiber separation. Bent stem should break easily, fibers separate with gentle pulling.
  • Dew Retting (Slower, Traditional): Lay dried plant bundles on ground in direct sun/rain. Moisture from dew, rain, and ground bacteria cause slow retting. Duration: 2-6 weeks depending on humidity and rain. Slower but less smell and labor.
  • Retting Completion Signs: Outer bark separates easily, woody center becomes soft/spongy, fibers pull away with gentle pressure. Over-retting weakens fibersâ€”yellow color indicates too long.
  • Stopping Retting: Once complete, rinse plants thoroughly in clean water, dry completely (2-3 weeks). Drying must be thorough or mold develops.

Breaking & Scutching Flax

  • Breaking: Crush woody core (shives) to break apart from fiber. Fiber bundle still holds shives together at this stage.
  • Breaking Tool: Simple flax break is two wooden bars hinged together. Retted flax placed between bars, pressed, twisted, pulled. Repeated pressure crushes shives. Hand-operated or foot-treadle powered.
  • Breaking Progression: One pass crushes obvious shives. Bend broken flax back and forth 5-10 times to separate shives from fibers. Check for remaining woody pieces.
  • Scutching: Removes crushed shive pieces clinging to fibers. Scutching tool is wooden blade (11-15 inches long, 2-3 inches wide, 1/4 inch thick) attached to wooden handle.
  • Scutching Process: Lay small bundle of broken flax across bench edge. Strike gently with scutching blade, rotating bundle. Shive pieces fly away, fibers remain. Dust and debris fall. Repeat until fibers appear clean, shiny.
  • Tools Needed: Flax break (crusher), scutching blade, wooden bench or board, smooth work surface.

Hackling: Aligning Fibers

  • Purpose: Comb scutched fiber to align long fibers parallel, remove short fibers and remaining debris. Hackled fiber is ready for spinning into fine linen yarn.
  • Hackle Tool: Board with many closely-spaced iron pins (1/8 to 1/16 inch spacing) protruding upward 1-2 inches. Fibers combed through pins, becoming aligned.
  • Hackle Sequence: Start with coarse hackle (larger pin spacing). Bundle of scutched fiber, thrust through pin bed 10-15 times, rotating. Fibers align, tangles separate.
  • Finer Hackles: Move to progressively finer hackles. Fine hackle (small spacing) gives ultimate alignment. Long fibers remain, short fibers and neps pulled free as waste (called "tow").
  • Alignment Result: Hackled fiber appears smooth, lustrous, with all fibers parallel. Ready for spinning into thin yarn.
  • Tow Usage: Short fibers and waste (tow) can be carded together, spun into coarser yarn, or used for paper/insulation.

Hemp Processing

Growing & Properties

  • Plant Structure: Hemp grows similar to flax but taller (4-10 feet), thicker stems, coarser fibers. Growing season 80-120 days.
  • Fiber Characteristics: Stronger, coarser than flax. Less lustrous, more uniform color (grayish). Fibers thicker diameter, lower dye absorption.
  • Rope vs. Fabric Grade: Rope-grade hemp grown for maximum length/strength, harvested late. Fabric-grade hemp harvested earlier, producing finer, shorter fibers suitable for cloth.
  • Harvesting: Pull entire plants (like flax). Dry thoroughly before retting.

Retting & Breaking Hemp

  • Water Retting: Similar to flax but longer duration. 2-3 weeks typical in water 70Â°F. Coarse stems require extended retting time for complete breakdown.
  • Dew Retting: 3-8 weeks typical. Hemp exposed to weather, bacteria break down slowly. Frequent turning recommended.
  • Breaking: Hemp requires more aggressive breaking than flax due to thicker stems. Multiple passes through flax break may be needed. Some breaking by hand/stone also helps.
  • Scutching: Scutching hemp removes shives like flax. May require multiple passes and heavier striking to remove all woody material.
  • Fiber Separation: After scutching, hemp still has some core material. Hand carding through coarse hackle, then fine hackle, produces usable fiber for spinning.

Wool Processing

Shearing & Fleece Preparation

  • Shearing Timing: Spring shearing (March-April in Northern Hemisphere) optimal. Wool grows throughout winter, longest at spring shearing. Shear once yearly, typically 6-12 pounds per sheep.
  • Shearing Safety: Professional shearing takes 5-10 minutes per sheep. Avoid nicking skin. Hand shears or electric clippers both acceptable for small flocks.
  • Fleece Collection: Immediately after shearing, place entire fleece on clean surface. Remove any visible debris, straw, dirt.
  • Skirting: Remove poor quality wool from edges. Belly wool (from underside), leg wool (coarser, shorter), and stained areas removed. Keep only best fiber from back, sides, neck.
  • Storage: Clean fleece stored in breathable bags (mesh, burlap), cool dry place. Moth-proofing essentialâ€”cedar, lavender, or mothballs recommended.

Washing & Scouring Wool

  • Purpose: Remove lanolin (natural sheep oil), dirt, and debris without damaging fiber structure.
  • Water Temperature Critical: Use hot water (140-160Â°F) for lanolin removal, but do NOT change temperature suddenly. Thermal shock felts wool. Maintain consistent temperature throughout wash.
  • Soap (Never Boiling): Hot water + mild soap (wool wash, dish soap, or castile). Boiling destroys fiber! Gently submerge fleece, let sit 15-30 minutes. Lanolin floats to surface, skim off.
  • Multiple Rinses: Remove soap with hot water rinses (same temperature as wash water). Rinse until water runs clearâ€”usually 3-5 rinses needed.
  • Drainage & Drying: After final rinse, gently press out water (do not wring). Spread on clean towels or drying rack indoors. Air dry 3-7 days depending on humidity. Stir occasionally for even drying.
  • Final Cleanliness: Well-washed wool feels soft, no oily residue, fibers separate easily.

Picking, Carding & Combing Wool

  • Picking: Gently pull fleece apart by hand into separate locks. Remove remaining matted areas, straw, debris by hand. Gentle handling prevents felting.
  • Carding: Hand cards or drum carder align fibers for woolen-style yarn (softer, airier, less dense). Fibers lay in all directions after carding, creating loft.
  • Combing (Worsted): Metal combs align all fibers parallel, same direction. Longer, finer fibers selected, short fibers combed out as waste. Worsted yarn (smooth, strong, lustrous) results.
  • Staple Length: Woolen wool typically 2-4 inches. Worsted-grade wool 3-5 inches (longer staple needed for combing).
  • Roving Production: Carded or combed fibers twisted lightly into loose rope form. Roving is ready for spinning into yarn.

Silk Culture & Processing

Sericulture: Silkworm Farming

  • Mulberry Trees: Silkworms (Bombyx mori) feed exclusively on white mulberry leaves. 1 pound of silk requires ~25 pounds fresh leaves. Trees planted in organized rows, pruned to promote leaf growth.
  • Lifecycle: Eggs hatch in spring. Tiny worms feed 24 hours daily for 4-5 weeks, growing 10,000x their initial weight. Worms stop eating when mature, becoming translucent white/yellowish.
  • Spinning Stage: Mature worm climbs to twigs/frame edges, secretes silk from two glands. Forms cocoon in 2-3 days, completely encasing itself.
  • Cocoon Structure: Single continuous filament 600-900 yards long, glued together with sericin (gum). Entire cocoon one unbroken fiberâ€”no knots or joins.
  • Timing: 8-12 days after cocoon formation, pupate inside completes. If allowed to continue, moth chews hole in cocoon, breaking fiber. Prevent by killing pupae before emergence.

Cocoon Processing & Reeling

  • Killing Pupae (Stifling): Expose cocoons to steam or dry heat (140-160Â°F for 30-60 minutes). Kills developing moth inside. Timing criticalâ€”too early damages fiber, too late moth weakens cocoon.
  • Softening: Place killed cocoons in hot water (160-170Â°F) for 15-30 minutes. Hot water softens sericin glue, making filament unwinding easier.
  • Finding Filament End: Rub cocoon gently underwater to separate outer fibers. Find end of main filament (appears as loose strand). This is unwinding point.
  • Reeling: As water heats cocoon, unwind filament by rolling cocoon slowly against smooth surface or pulling with gentle rotation. Filament gradually emerges as cocoon unwinds, yielding 600-900 yards raw silk.
  • Multiple Cocoons per Thread: Single filament too thin for practical use. Combine 4-8 filaments together by turning slightly as you reel, creating unified raw silk thread.
  • Degumming: Boil raw silk in soapy water to remove remaining sericin. Fibers lose slight stiffness, reveal natural luster and strength.

Throwing & Yields

  • Throwing: Twist multiple raw silk filaments together to create strong, balanced yarn. Increase twist for greater strength. Single-ply, 2-ply, or 3-ply common.
  • Yield Calculation: Approximately 1000-1200 cocoons yield 1 pound of raw silk. One cocoon = 1-1.5 grams raw silk. Significant weight loss occurs from water retting and sericin removal.
  • Fabric Grade: Highest quality silk uses largest, healthiest cocoons from pure-line breeding. Less selective rearing produces shorter filaments, lower yields.

8. Natural Dyeing Deep Dive

Mordanting Fundamentals

Understanding Mordants

  • Definition: Mordants are metal salts that bind dye molecules to fiber, creating permanent color. Without mordant, many natural dyes wash out quickly.
  • Chemistry: Mordant forms bridge between fiber protein and dye molecule. Metal ion sits between fiber and dye, creating stable color complex.
  • Alum (Potassium Aluminum Sulfate): Most common mordant. Brightens colors, essential for yellows, reds, oranges. Typical use: 8-20% weight of fiber.
  • Iron (Ferrous Sulfate): Saddens/darkens colors, shifts hues. Red becomes purple. Yellow becomes olive/brown. Small amount only (0.5-2%) or fabric becomes stiff.
  • Copper (Copper Sulfate): Produces greens, enhances blue-greens. Toxicâ€”handle carefully. Typical use: 1-4% weight of fiber.
  • Tin (Stannous Chloride): Brightens colors dramatically, intensifies reds. High cost, small amounts needed (0.5-1%). Creates stiff, brittle fiber if overused.
  • Tannin (Tannic Acid or Oak Galls): Prepares fiber for mordants, improves dye uptake, darkens some colors. Often used before alum.

Mordant Bath Procedure

  • Water Preparation: Use soft water if possible. Hard water minerals can interfere with mordant bonding. 1 gallon water per 1 pound fiber typical ratio.
  • Temperature Progression: Start at room temperature. Slowly heat to 160-180Â°F over 30-45 minutes. High temperature helps fiber absorb mordant.
  • Soaking Fiber: Wet fiber thoroughly before adding to bath. Dry fiber absorbs unevenly, causing mottled color. Soak 15-30 minutes before heating begins.
  • Bath Duration: Simmer at 160-180Â°F for 45-60 minutes. Fiber absorbs mordant gradually. Keep temperature constant.
  • Cooling & Storage: Cool gradually to room temperature while fiber remains in bath. If using water-bath method, can store mordanted fiber in bath overnight or several days.
  • Drying (Optional): Can dry mordanted fiber or dye while still damp. Drying allows storage but may reduce dye uptake slightly.

  • Brazilwood (Caesalpinia echinata)

    • Source: Wood chips or whole fallen fruit (Osage orange tree)

  • Iron Mordant + Tannin Recipe:

  • Step 2: After tannin dyeing, dip into iron water bath (0.5-1% ferrous sulfate). Chemical reaction between tannin and iron creates black color.
  • Step 3: Oxidization in air darkens color further over 24 hours.
  • Result: True black color, excellent fastness (one of most permanent dye combinations)
  • Adjustment: Light iron exposure = dark brown, medium exposure = dark gray-black, heavy exposure = jet black

  • Elderberry (Sambucus)

    • Characteristic: Purple to blue-purple. Moderate fastness.

  • Logwood (Haematoxylum campechianum)
    • Source: Chips of logwood heartwood (tropical tree, commercial dye)
    • Characteristic: Purple to violet. Excellent lightfastness and wash fastness.
    • Mordant: Alum + cream of tartar (purple), iron (dark purple/black)
    • Historic: One of most important dyewoods in history

Natural Dye Color Recipe Quick Reference

Indigo Vat Dyeing

Indigo Chemistry & Vat Construction

  • Problem: Indigo is insoluble in water. Cannot dye directly from bath like other dyes. Must be chemically reduced (made soluble) in special vat.
  • Fermentation Vat (Historical): Urine provides natural reducing agent. Mix indigo with stale urine (1-2 weeks old), add water, ferment 1-2 weeks. Indigo dissolves, bath becomes "yellow" color. Actually reduced indigo in solution.
  • Modern Chemical Vat (Sodium Hydrosulfite): Add sodium hydrosulfite powder to indigo + water bath. Chemical reaction reduces indigo, making it soluble. Faster than fermentation, more controllable.
  • Lye Vat (Traditional Alternative): Combine indigo with lye solution + bran flour. Bran ferments, creates reducing environment. Traditional in some cultures.
  • Water Temperature: Keep vat 130-160Â°F. Cooler temps slow reduction, higher temps can oxidize indigo. Consistent temperature critical.

Indigo Vat Procedure & Signs

  • Vat Setup: Mix indigo powder (0.5-1% weight of fiber) with small amount of hot water, form paste. Add to large pot of hot water (1-2 gallons per pound fiber).
  • Adding Reducing Agent: Sodium hydrosulfite: sprinkle powder into vat while stirring. Watch for color change from blue to yellow-green, then to clear yellow layer on top of blue sludge at bottom.
  • Active Vat Signs:
    • Clear yellow or yellow-green liquid on top (reduced indigo in solution)
    • Blue sludge settles at bottom (unreacted indigo)
    • Skin forms on surface (indigo re-oxidizing at air contact)â€”skim off before dyeing
    • pH paper shows alkaline (8-11 range)â€”acidic vat not active
  • Dyeing Process: Gently lower wet fiber into yellow layer, avoid disturbing blue sludge. Keep fiber submerged 10-15 minutes. Remove slowly, allow excess liquid to drain back.
  • Oxidation & Darkening: Expose fiber to air 15-30 minutes. Yellow indigo on fiber oxidizes to blue color. First dip = pale blue. Repeat dipping 5-10x for navy blue.
  • Multiple Dips: Between dips, gently stir vat (don't aerate), ensure surface skin is skimmed off. Yellow vat confirms still active.

9. Knitting & Crochet: Patterns & Projects

Gauge & Sizing Fundamentals

Understanding Gauge

  • Definition: Gauge = number of stitches per inch (or stitches per centimeter) in finished knitted or crocheted fabric.
  • Why Critical: Gauge determines finished garment size. If your gauge is off, garment will be wrong size.
  • Reading Gauge: Pattern specifies "18 stitches and 24 rows = 4 inches in stockinette stitch on US 6 needles." This is gauge.
  • Gauge Swatch: Knit sample (minimum 6x6 inches) in pattern stitch using specified needles and yarn. Measure stitches across 4 inches, count rows in 4 inches vertically.
  • Adjusting Needles: If swatch is too loose (fewer stitches per inch), use smaller needles. If too tight, use larger needles. Retest.
  • Fiber Matters: Same stitch on different yarns = different gauge. Yarn weight, fiber content, thickness all affect gauge.

Sizing Garments by Gauge

  • Example: Pattern calls for 20 stitches cast-on. Desired finished width is 30 inches. Gauge is 6 stitches per inch (from swatch).
  • Calculation: 30 inches Ã— 6 stitches/inch = 180 stitches to cast on (if pattern calls 20, multiply 20 Ã— 9 = 180)
  • Adjusting Pattern Size: Multiply all stitch counts by scaling factor. Length (rows) also scales by same factor.
  • Small Changes: If off by 1-2 stitches per inch, finished garment off by ~1-2 inches in width. Plan accordingly.

Knitting Pattern Fundamentals

Stockinette Stitch Basics

  • Pattern: Knit all stitches on right-side rows. Purl all stitches on wrong-side rows (or in circular, knit every round).
  • Appearance: Smooth V-pattern on front, bumpy nubs on back. Most common stitch for garments.
  • Properties: Drapes well, shows stitch definition clearly, prone to rolling at edges (use ribbing or garter stitch border)

Ribbing (1x1 and 2x2)

  • 1x1 Ribbing: Alternate knit and purl across row. Vertical lines of knit/purl columns. Tight, elastic (great for cuffs).
  • 2x2 Ribbing: Alternate 2 knits and 2 purls. Wider ribs, less elastic than 1x1, more elegant appearance.
  • Uses: Sleeve cuffs, waistbands, hat cuffs. Elasticity hugs body without stitching.

Garter Stitch

  • Pattern: Knit every row (or alternate knit and purl if working flat). Purl every round in circular.
  • Appearance: Horizontal ridges, bumpy texture, reversible (looks same on both sides).
  • Properties: Thick, warm, doesn't curl at edges. Slower to knit than stockinette (more stitches per row). Heavy fabric.
  • Uses: Scarves, blankets, afghans. Good for beginnersâ€”visible errors less obvious.

Seed Stitch

  • Pattern: Row 1: K1, P1 across. Row 2: P1, K1 across. Opposite of previous row each time.
  • Appearance: Bumpy, textured, reversible. No curl at edges.
  • Properties: Denser than stockinette, warm, elegant but slow to knit.
  • Uses: Sweaters, blankets, formal garments where sophisticated texture desired.

Sock Knitting: Construction Details

Cuff-Down Sock Method

  • Cast-On: 60-80 stitches on circular needles (DPN or magic loop). Ribbing (K2P2) for 1-2 inches creates elastic cuff.
  • Leg: Change to stockinette stitch. Knit straight for 6-8 inches or desired leg length.
  • Heel Flap: Work short rows on half the stitches (heel flap is stockinette + slipped stitches on edges). 12-16 rows typical. Slipped stitches create chain edge.
  • Heel Turn: Decrease stitches using short rows, turning fabric to create triangular shape. Turns 1/3 of heel stitches.
  • Gusset: Pick up stitches along heel flap edge, work decreases to match leg width. Creates shaping around heel without bunching.
  • Foot: Stockinette straight for 2-3 inches or desired foot length.
  • Toe: Decrease stitches using K2tog/SSK. Rows progress 5-stitch repeats, then 3-stitches, then tight decreases until 8 stitches remain. Bind off by threading yarn through remaining stitches, pulling tight.

Mitten Construction

  • Cuff: Ribbing (K2P2) for 1-2 inches, 40-60 stitches depending on hand size.
  • Hand: Stockinette stitch straight for 2-3 inches (covers back of hand).
  • Thumb Gusset: Increase 1 stitch per side every other round for 10-12 rounds. Creates diamond-shaped opening for thumb.
  • Dividing: Place gusset stitches on scrap yarn (will be picked up later for thumb). Continue hand portion on remaining stitches.
  • Hand Continuation: Knit hand portion 2-3 inches past gusset (covers top of hand to wrist).
  • Top Closure: Decrease same as sock toe until 8 stitches remain, close opening.
  • Thumb: Pick up gusset stitches, add stitches around opening edges. Knit straight for 1-2 inches or desired thumb length. Decrease and close.

Hat Construction

Basic Knit Hat Pattern

  • Cast-On: 80-120 stitches on circular needles (depending on head size, gauge). Measure head circumference Ã— 0.9 for stretchy fit.
  • Cuff: K2P2 ribbing for 1-2 inches. Elastic cuff ensures hat stays on.
  • Body: Change to stockinette stitch. Knit straight for 6-8 inches, or desired depth (usually 2-3 inches above ear level).
  • Crown Decrease: Decrease stitches in scheduled pattern. Rows 1-2: K2tog every 10 stitches (total stitches decrease 1/10). Skip 2 rows.
  • Second Decrease: Every other round: K2tog every 5 stitches. Stitches decrease to half.
  • Final Decrease: Every round: K2tog until 8 stitches remain. Tight decreases create pointed crown.
  • Finishing: Break yarn, thread through tapestry needle, pull through last 8 stitches, pull tight. Weave in end.

Needle & Hook Making

Making Needles from Natural Materials

  • Wood Needles: Dowels (wood rods from hardware store) filed/shaped to point. 0.5-inch diameter dowel sharpened to needle point takes ~30 minutes with file and sandpaper.
  • Bone Needles: Traditionally made from animal bone. Harder than wood, very smooth surface, don't wear hands during long knitting. Bone from poultry bones can be carved and sharpened.
  • Metal Wire Needles: Steel wire (coat hanger or welding rod) bent into needle shape. One end sharpened to point, other end wrapped with thread to form knob preventing stitches falling off.
  • Size Standardization: Diameter determines needle size. US 1 = 2.25mm, US 10 = 6mm, US 15 = 10mm (approximate). File wood/bone to desired diameter, test with gauge swatch.

10. Hatmaking & Headwear

Felt Hat Construction

Wool Felting Process

  • Felting Mechanism: Sheep wool fibers have microscopic scales. Hot water + friction + soap causes scales to lock together, creating inseparable mat.
  • Preparation: Card raw wool into thin sheets (roving works also). Layers of wool create bulk.
  • Wet Felting: Heat water to 140-160Â°F (hot but not boiling). Immerse wool layers, add soap (wool wash, dish soap). Gently agitate/rub wool. Scales lock together, wool shrinks and hardens.
  • Agitation Method: Squeeze soapy water through wool repeatedly. Rub gently but firmly against texture (burlap, bamboo mat). Increase friction graduallyâ€”too much too soon tears fabric.
  • Checking Progress: Lift corner of felt, see if fibers hold together. Initially separates easily, gradually becomes inseparable mat. 10-30 minutes typical.
  • Rinsing: Once felted, rinse repeatedly in warm then cool water to remove all soap. Cold rinse stops felting process.
  • Drying & Shrinkage: Squeeze out excess water gently. Air dry on flat surface or blocking form. Felt shrinks 20-40% during felting/dryingâ€”account for when sizing.

Blocking Felt Hat on Form

  • Hat Block: Wooden or plastic form shaped like human head. Sizes available for all head sizes (S/M/L/XL).
  • Shaping While Wet: Wet felted hat is pliable. Stretch wet hat over block while still warm. Pin edges to block, adjust shape.
  • Drying on Block: Leave hat on block until completely dry (12-24 hours). Maintains shape set by block.
  • Crown & Brim: Block determines depth of crown (taller = deeper hat), brim width (narrow to wide).
  • Finishing Details: Once blocked and dry, add ribbon band, feather, or embellishment to brim edge or crown.

Straw Hat Construction

Split Straw Braiding

  • Material: Wheat straw or rye straw, split lengthwise into thin strips (1/8 inch or smaller).
  • Braiding: Take 3-5 strips, braid tightly maintaining uniform width. Braid creates long rope.
  • Length: Braid several meters long. Braid can be stored for later hat creation.
  • Drying: Finished braid dried completely. Damp straw works more easily but must dry before forming hat.

Spiral Sewing & Blocking

  • Shaping Base: Coil braid into flat circle (hat base). Start center, spiral outward. Sew braid to itself using needle and thread or small stitches.
  • Building Up Sides: Continue spiral, sewing each row to previous row, gradually building height to create hat crown shape.
  • Adding Brim: Once crown is complete, continue spiral outward creating brim (2-4 inches wide).
  • Blocking Shape: Wet finished hat slightly, place on blocking form. Let dry 12-24 hours to set shape.
  • Finishing: Trim brim edge, add ribbon or bias binding to edge for neatness. Decorate with feathers, beads, or flowers.

Knit Caps & Hoods

Simple Knit Cap Pattern

  • Gauge & Sizing: Measure head circumference. Typical cap 80-120 stitches cast on (adjust for gauge).
  • Cuff: 1-2 inches ribbing (K2P2) creates stretch.
  • Body: Stockinette or pattern stitch, 6-8 inches straight.
  • Crown Decrease: K2tog every 10 stitches, skip 2 rounds. Then K2tog every 5 stitches every round. Final K2tog until 8 remain.
  • Variations: Slouchy cap (longer body 10-12 inches, slower decrease = looser crown). Beanie (shorter body 5-6 inches, faster decrease).

Sun Bonnets & Hoods

  • Sun Bonnet: Knit or sew from fabric. Design includes brim extending around face for sun protection, ties under chin.
  • Pattern: Front section (face opening area), back section (covers back of head/neck), brim (extends forward). Can be knit in pieces and seamed, or knit in rounds.
  • Hood: Knit tube covering entire head. Typically ribbed cuff at neck opening, stockinette tube upward. Opening at face is wide enough to see.
  • Fasteners: Both use ties, buttons, or drawstrings for securing under chin.

11. Lace & Decorative Techniques

Bobbin Lace Basics

Equipment & Setup

  • Lace Pillow: Firm, dense pillow (straw-filled traditionally) curved on sides. Supports thread tension during work.
  • Bobbins: Small wooden spools holding thread, weighted to provide thread tension. Threads pass through bobbins.
  • Pins: T-pins or special lace pins inserted into pillow. Pins define thread path and create lace pattern.
  • Pricking Pattern: Template showing pin positions. Pins inserted at marked positions, creating guides for thread crossing.
  • Basic Setup: Pillow placed on table/lap, pattern pinned to pillow surface. Bobbins hung from pins, threads cross under-over-under creating lace openings.

Basic Bobbin Lace Stitches

  • Cross and Twist: Two threads from adjacent bobbins cross each other, twist around each other. Basic stitch creating fabric.
  • Pin Stitch: Threads wrap around pin, creating loop at pin position. Pins define lace pattern geometry.
  • Whole Stitch: Twist, cross, twist, cross sequence. Creates dense fabric area.
  • Half Stitch: Simplified pattern creating lighter, more open fabric.
  • Cloth Stitch: Dense stitch creating solid fabric areas within openwork lace design.

Tatting

Shuttle Tatting Method

  • Equipment: Shuttle (boat-shaped bobbin) holding thread, ball of thread, needle.
  • Knot Creation: Hold thread in left hand, make loop. Shuttle thread passes through loop, creating doubled knot (called "double stitch").
  • Ring Formation: Series of doubled knots along thread, knotted ring created. Release loop to tighten knots into ring.
  • Picots (Decorative Loops): Thread loops extend from ring between knots, creating picot (small ornamental loop).
  • Chain Linking: Between rings, chain stitches create connecting threads. Rings linked into patterns.
  • Complexity: Advanced tatting creates intricate geometric patterns, edgings, full doilies.

Embroidery: Basic Stitches

Essential Hand Embroidery Stitches

  • Running Stitch: Needle passes in and out of fabric at regular intervals (1/8 inch typical). Creates basic line. Quick but visible thread.
  • Backstitch: Needle goes forward, then backward, creating overlapping stitches. Strong, fine line, looks like machine stitching when tight.
  • Satin Stitch: Parallel stitches fill shape completely. Stitches lie flat side-by-side creating solid, glossy area. Requires even thread tension.
  • Chain Stitch: Loop of thread held down by needle entering same point. Creates chain-like line. Good for filling areas quickly.
  • French Knot: Thread wrapped around needle, needle pushed through fabric near original entry point, creating knot on surface. Decorative raised knots for flowers, texture.
  • Split Stitch: Backstitch variant where needle splits previous thread, creating solid line with blended appearance.
  • Cross Stitch: X-shaped stitch filling counted squares in fabric. Geometric patterns created by crossing stitches in grid pattern.
  • Long & Short Stitch: Alternating long/short stitches in same area, creating shaded or graduated color effect (blending between colors).

Embroidery Design & Planning

  • Pattern Transfer: Draw design on paper, transfer to fabric using carbon paper, tracing wheel, or light box.
  • Fabric Choice: Even-weave fabric (linen) ideal for precision. Smooth cotton also works. Avoid stretchy knits.
  • Thread Selection: Embroidery floss (6-stranded, separable for thickness control), perle cotton, or wool. Thread weight matches stitch size.
  • Hoop Use: Fabric stretched in wooden or plastic hoop keeps stitches even, prevents puckering.
  • Color Planning: Sketch design with colors beforehand. Consider thread blending for shading effects.

12. Advanced Fiber Preparation & Processing

Tapa (Bark Cloth) Production

Paper Mulberry Processing

Tapa is traditional non-woven fabric made by pounding plant bark. Paper mulberry (Broussonetia papyrifera) is the premier source. Process yields fabric without spinning or weaving.

  • Bark Harvesting: Cut young branches (1-2 cm diameter). Best during growing season when bark separates easily.
  • Soaking: Soak bark in fresh water 1-2 hours to soften and separate outer bark from inner bast fiber layer.
  • Outer Bark Removal: Scrape away rough outer layer with bone or stone tool. Reveal clean bast layer.
  • Beating Process: Place soaked inner bark on smooth log or board. Beat with wooden mallet or grooved beating tool.
  • Beating Technique: Use overlapping strokes, gradually expanding fibers horizontally. Bark spreads and interlocks as fibers break down and compress.
  • Sizing & Starch Glue: After initial beating, brush fibers with plant starch paste (rice, arrowroot, or cassava starch) to bond fibers and add stiffness.
  • Final Beating: Complete final beating after starch application. Fibers meld into unified fabric sheet.
  • Drying: Lay finished tapa in sun to dry completely (1-2 days). Result: thin, leather-like fabric 1-2 mm thick.

Yield: One branch yields roughly 30-50 cm long fabric strip. Multiple strips can be felted together using vegetable paste for larger sheets.

Carding Techniques & Equipment

Teasel Head Carding

Teasel (Dipsacus fullonum) seed pods have thousands of tiny, flexible hooks ideal for carding fibers. Traditional carding tool predating wire carders.

  • Teasel Head Preparation: Dry teasel pods completely. Attach to wooden blocks (one teasel per 15 cm block) securely with twine or leather strips.
  • Design: Create two carding blocks: one stationary (laid on lap), one held in hand. Alternately brush fibers between blocks.
  • Fiber Feed: Lay raw fibers on stationary block. Brush handheld block across repeatedly until fibers align.
  • Teasel Advantages: Gentler than wire (doesn't break short fibers), excellent for delicate or short-staple fibers (cashmere, alpaca), produces soft prepared fiber.
  • Lifespan: Teasel heads last 200+ uses before hooks dull. Drying and storing carefully extends usable life.

Spinning Wheel Construction

Great Wheel (Walking Wheel)

Large diameter wheel (60-90 cm), hand-driven. Spinner walks back and forth while wheel turns. Ideal for long-staple fibers.

  • Wheel Construction: Rim diameter critical for gear ratio and fiber speed. Larger wheel = finer twist. Build from segmented wooden rim with wood or metal spokes.
  • Axle: Heavy wooden dowel (2-3 cm diameter) through center. Must rotate smoothly; consider bone or stone bearings for low friction.
  • Drive Ratio: Single whorl on axle (no gears); wheel directly drives spindle via leather cord. Spindle rotates 4-6x per wheel revolution.
  • Spindle: 20-25 cm long, tapered at both ends. Made from hardwood or bone. Flyer assembly holds fiber and guides twist.
  • Fiber Feeding: Hold drafted fiber in hand while walking, wheel twist inserts. Walking speed controls yarn thickness.
  • Advantage: Simple mechanics, produces thick, even yarn, ideal for woolen spinning. Disadvantage: requires space to walk, not portable.

Saxony Treadle Wheel

Smaller wheel (30-45 cm), foot-driven via treadle. Spinner sits; hands remain free for drafting. Modern design, more versatile than great wheel.

  • Treadle Mechanism: Foot pedal connected to crankshaft, converting up-down motion to rotational motion. Requires smooth, balanced crank design to prevent binding.
  • Wheel Drive: Leather cord connects wheel rim to pulley system with multiple whorls (different diameters). Change cord position between whorls to adjust twist ratio.
  • Twist Ratios: Typical Saxony provides 4:1, 6:1, 8:1, 10:1 ratios depending on whorl selection. Faster ratios for finer yarns.
  • Flyer Assembly: Rotates independently from spindle. Fiber passes through flyer hooks, twists inserted, then wound on bobbin.
  • Tension: Brake band on wheel adjusts takeup speed. Higher tension = tighter twist.
  • Construction: More complex than great wheel; requires well-fitted bearings, balanced crankshaft, and smooth treadle action.

Spinning Efficiency Comparison

Great wheel: 2-3 oz/hour (thick yarn). Saxony treadle: 1-2 oz/hour (fine yarn control). Choice depends on desired yarn properties and available time/space.

Loom Types & Tension Systems

Backstrap Loom (Portable Body Tension)

Ancient portable loom. Tension provided by weaver's body, one end of warp anchored to fixed point, other to leather strap around weaver's waist.

  • Warp Setup: Warp attached to top bar (fixed point or beam) and bottom bar (attached to backstrap). Weaver's body weight provides tension.
  • Tension Adjustment: Lean forward to increase tension, backward to decrease. Instant tension control while weaving.
  • Shed Creation: Use heddle rod (lifts alternating warp yarns) and sword (passes between raised/lowered warps) to form shed for weft insertion.
  • Weaving Width: Limited to weaver's arm span (~60 cm maximum practical width).
  • Portability: Completely portable; can be set up anywhere with anchor point (tree, post, etc.). Ideal for nomadic cultures.
  • Weave Types: Capable of plain weave, twill, brocade. Complex weaves limited by manual shed manipulation.
  • Yarn Speed: ~5-8 cm cloth produced per hour by experienced weaver.

Floor Loom (4-Harness with Heddles)

Stationary loom using harnesses and treadles. Each harness controls one group of warp yarns. Treadle activation lifts/lowers harnesses for shed creation.

  • Harness Design: 4 harnesses typical. Each harness is frame holding 100+ heddle eyes. Heddles (loops of string or wood) pass through warp yarns.
  • Heddle Spacing: Determine by dent density (see below). Space heddles evenly on harness.
  • Treadle Arrangement: 4+ treadles below loom, each connected to harnesses via tie-up rods. Different treadle combinations create different sheds.
  • Shed Complexity: 4 harnesses allow 16 possible treadle combinations = 16 different weave patterns (plain, twill, herringbone, etc.).
  • Warp Tension: Beam mechanism with screw or ratchet maintains consistent tension throughout weaving. Critical for even cloth.
  • Reed Dent: Metal reed (dent = space between metal wires) controls cloth density. See detailed section below.
  • Efficiency: ~30-50 cm cloth per hour by experienced weaver, creating complex patterns continuously.
  • Construction: Requires precise joinery, smooth bearings, and stable frame. Best built from hardwood.

13. Critical Fiber Processing Details

Fiber Retting Times & Temperature Sensitivity

Retting is water-based process that decomposes pectin binding fiber to stalk. Temperature dramatically affects speed.

  • Flax Retting: 5-10 days in cool water (15-20Â°C). Warm water (20-25Â°C) reduces to 3-5 days. Cold water (below 10Â°C) requires 2-3 weeks. Anaerobic retting (stagnant water with bacteria) faster than aerobic.
  • Hemp Retting: 3 days in warm water (20-25Â°C). Cold water extends to 5-7 days. Less temperature-sensitive than flax.
  • Nettle Retting: 4-6 days typical. Requires running water or daily water changes (stagnant water causes fiber damage).
  • Testing Readiness: Bend retting stalk sharply. If outer bark separates cleanly and fibers pull free, retting complete.
  • Over-Retting: Extends beyond optimal point; fibers become weak and brittle. Reduce yield and quality. Hard to judge in cold conditions.
  • Under-Retting: Fiber doesn't separate completely; remaining pectin must be beaten out (more labor). Possible but inefficient.

Wool Scouring Temperature Control

Wool contains lanolin (natural oil) and suint (salt/sweat). Scouring removes these. Temperature control is critical.

  • Optimal Temperature: 50-55Â°C (120-130Â°F). Hot enough to dissolve lanolin, cool enough to prevent felting.
  • Too Hot: Above 60Â°C, wool felts involuntarily (scales open, fibers lock together). Felted wool cannot be spun or dyed effectively.
  • Too Cold: Below 40Â°C, lanolin doesn't dissolve; grease remains in fiber.
  • Detergent: Use mild soap (saponin from soapwort plant, or ash-based lye at very dilute strength). Never use strong alkali (causes felting).
  • Multiple Rinses: Change rinse water 3-4 times. Use water at same temperature as scouring (temperature shock causes felting). Squeeze gently; never agitate.
  • Drying: Lay on flat screen in shade. Air dry completely (2-3 days). Never heat-dry (risk of felting).
  • Yield: Clean wool is 20-30% lighter than raw (mostly grease and dirt removed). Plan accordingly for projects.

Selvage Techniques & Cloth Edge Management

Selvage (finished edge of woven cloth) prevents unraveling and gives cloth professional appearance. Multiple techniques depending on loom type.

  • Twisted Selvage: Outer warp yarns twisted 2-3 times around weft as it passes. Creates reinforced, finished edge. Used on backstrap looms.
  • Double Warp Selvage: Use 2-4 thicker yarns at cloth edges. Provides natural reinforcement. Weft catches on double yarn, creating tight edge.
  • Selvedge Bar: On floor looms, metal wire rod guides weft around outer warps. Prevents weft thread from sliding off edge.
  • Beating Technique: Strike outermost weft closer to cloth (2 extra beats) to compress edge and prevent loosening.
  • Preventative: Ensure weft tension consistent (especially at edges). Uneven tension causes wavy selvage.
  • Repair: If selvage begins unraveling post-weaving, tack with needle and thread (ladder-stitch catches in weft, securing unraveled warp).

Reed Dent Selection for Cloth Density

Reed dent (space between wires in metal reed) directly determines cloth density (ends per inch, EPI). Selecting correct dent critical for desired fabric.

  • Dent Definition: Dent measured in dents-per-inch (DPI). Common: 8, 10, 12, 15, 20 DPI. Each dent holds single warp yarn (or 2 yarns for finer cloth).
  • Density Calculations: If using 12 DPI reed with 1 yarn per dent = 12 EPI. Using 2 yarns per dent = 24 EPI.
  • Fine Cloth: 20+ EPI requires 20 DPI reed or finer. Fine linen or cotton.
  • Medium Cloth: 12-16 EPI typical for wool, medium linen. Use 12-16 DPI reed.
  • Coarse Cloth: 8-10 EPI for heavy woolen cloth. Use 8-10 DPI reed.
  • Non-Existent Reeds: If precise dent unavailable, use thicker reed and space yarns with fishing line or plant twine between warp groups. Less effective but functional.
  • Weft Density: Picks per inch (PPI) controlled by beating tension. Higher beats = denser weave. Aim for balanced weave (EPI â‰ˆ PPI) for stable cloth.

Cloth Shrinkage Calculations & Pre-Weaving Planning

Finished cloth shrinks due to fiber relaxation, felting, and processing. Must anticipate shrinkage when planning project dimensions.

  • Typical Shrinkage Rates: Wool: 15-25% (variable by scouring method and fiber crimp). Linen: 5-8%. Cotton: 3-5%. Blends shrink according to fiber ratio.
  • Width Shrinkage: Usually 10-20% in width (depends on edge tension during weaving and finishing).
  • Length Shrinkage: 10-15% in length (from warp relaxation and finishing).
  • Shrinkage Causes: Fiber crimp relaxes (especially wool). Fibers shift in weave structure as tensions release. Fulling/finishing processes compress cloth.
  • Example Calculation: Weaving 100 cm length cloth from wool. Expect 12-15% shrinkage = finished length 85-88 cm. Plan initial weaving ~15% longer than desired final size.
  • Width Example: Want final width 50 cm. Expect 15% width shrinkage. Weave for 59 cm width.
  • Testing: Weave test swatch (30x30 cm). Wash and dry. Measure final size. Calculate actual shrinkage percentage for your specific fiber, spin, and finishing method.
  • Correction: If past weaving, can partially compensate with blocking (stretching cloth while damp) or fulling (opposite direction to relax). Imperfect but helpful.

Pro Tip: Add 20% to both length and width during weaving planning. Better to have slightly larger cloth than to fall short of project requirements.

Warp Tension Maintenance During Weaving

Consistent warp tension essential for even cloth and prevents yarn breakage. Tension naturally decreases as cloth is woven. Active management required.

  • Initial Tension: Tight enough to keep warp straight (no bowing), loose enough to prevent excessive strain. Test: pluck warp like guitar string; should vibrate with clear tone.
  • Progressive Winding: As cloth develops, wind finished cloth onto cloth beam while releasing new warp from warp beam. Most looms have mechanical advantage (screw/ratchet).
  • Winding Frequency: After every 5-10 cm of weaving, advance cloth. Prevents excess buildup.
  • Tension Loss Signals: Warp goes slack (loses tone when plucked). Cloth develops horizontal ridges/slack areas. Weft tension becomes inconsistent.
  • Correction: Tighten warp beam (screw turn or ratchet advance). Small turns; multiple small adjustments better than one large correction.
  • Thread Breakage: Over-tension causes warp yarn breakage. Under-tension causes dropped stitches and wavy edges. Balance critical.
  • Weather Factors: Humidity changes affect fiber tension. Humid days: fibers swell, tension naturally increases. Dry days: tension naturally decreases (requires more tightening).
