Sterilization Principles

Sterility Definition

Complete absence of viable microorganisms (bacteria, spores, viruses, fungi). Sterilization differs from disinfection (reduces organisms but not to zero). In medical practice: anything entering body or breaking skin barrier must be sterile. Equipment reusability increases cost-effectiveness in survival settings; proper sterilization between patients critical.

Microbial Load and Resistance

Bacterial spores (Bacillus, Clostridium) most resistant to sterilization; viruses and vegetative bacteria easier to kill. Process efficacy measured by Sterility Assurance Level (SAL): probability of single microorganism surviving. Medical devices typically require SAL of 10^-6 (1 in 1 million chance).

Sterilization Effectiveness Factors

  • Temperature: Higher = faster sterilization (exponential relationship)
  • Time: Longer exposure compensates for lower temperature
  • Moisture: Essential for steam sterilization; absent for dry heat
  • Penetration: Must reach all surfaces; wrapped instruments require adequate steam/gas penetration
  • Bioburden: Pre-cleaning removes organic material that shields organisms

Steam Autoclaving

Autoclave Principles

Combines heat, moisture, and pressure to denature proteins (kill organisms). Saturated steam at 121°C (250°F) under 15 psi pressure penetrates materials effectively. Pressure itself not killing factor; pressure allows higher temperatures in smaller space. Portable pressure cookers can function as field autoclaves.

Autoclave Cycle

  1. Evacuation: removes air (steam penetration requires air absence)
  2. Heating: steam enters chamber, temperature rises to 121°C
  3. Sterilization: hold at 121°C, 15 psi for prescribed time (typically 15-30 min depending on load)
  4. Exhaust: steam vents, chamber depressurizes
  5. Drying: residual moisture evaporates from load

Field Expedient Autoclave

Pressure cooker (typically 15 psi, reaches 121°C) functions as autoclave. Procedure: add 2-3 cups water to pot, place steamer basket, arrange instruments on basket (single layer, don't pack), cover but don't seal lid (need steam escape). Bring to pressure, maintain 15-30 minutes depending on load. Cool naturally before opening (sudden pressure drop risks steam burns). Works for wrapped/unwrapped instruments.

Boiling Method

Boiling Technique (Field Standard)

Place instruments in pot, cover with water, bring to rolling boil. Maintain vigorous boil for minimum 20 minutes (sea level). Time increases with altitude (add 10 minutes per 3,000 feet elevation). Cover pot to prevent recontamination. Remove with sterile forceps, place on sterile surface to cool. Does NOT sterilize bacterial spores but kills vegetative bacteria, viruses, most fungi.

Advantages and Limitations

Advantages: Simple, requires minimal equipment (pot, heat source, water), minimal cost, effective for most pathogens, can reprocess multiple times. Limitations: Does NOT kill spores (C. difficile, B. anthracis), limited temperature (<100°C at sea level), longer time needed, rusts some instruments if not dried immediately.

Post-Boiling Care

Remove instruments with sterile forceps (never bare hands). Place on clean, dry surface (cloth, paper) to dry. Once cool, store in clean container. If not used immediately, risk of recontamination; ideally use within hours. Re-boil if instruments stored >24 hours or if stored in contaminated environment.

Dry Heat Sterilization

Dry Heat Oven Method

Place instruments in dry oven at 160-180°C (320-356°F) for 45-120 minutes depending on temperature (lower temperature requires longer time). Works through protein denaturation and oxidative effects. Good for: instruments that rust with moisture, oils, powders. Penetration slower than steam; items must be individually wrapped or in open arrangement (not packed).

Flaming Method (Field Expedient)

For small instruments (forceps, scissors, needle holders): hold over flame briefly until red-hot. Kills organisms through combustion heat. Rapid, requires no equipment except heat source. Limitations: only works for metal items (heat capacity allows rapid cooling), damages some materials, risk of burns to operator.

Incineration

Burning contaminated materials (dressings, cultures) at high temperature (>800°C) achieves sterilization. Requires incinerator or very hot fire. For survival: use for non-recoverable contaminated materials (cultures, heavily infected dressings). Drawback: loss of reusable items, environmental concerns, limited access to sufficiently hot fire.

Chemical Sterilization

Glutaraldehyde (High-Level Disinfection/Sterilization)

2-3.5% solution achieves sterilization at 20-30°C for 6-10 hours (unstandardized as sterilant; more reliable for high-level disinfection in 20-45 minutes). Kills bacteria, viruses, fungi, spores. Used for: endoscopes, catheters, other heat-sensitive equipment. Requires: proper ventilation (toxic vapor), appropriate personal protective equipment, contact time adherence.

Ethylene Oxide Gas (Industrial)

Sterilant for heat-sensitive items; standard for single-use medical device manufacturing. Requires specialized equipment, extended aeration time (gas extremely toxic), not practical for field/survival use. Cost-prohibitive for small operations.

Peracetic Acid

Rapidly sterilizes at room temperature (efficient for high-level disinfection/low-temperature sterilization). Less toxic than glutaraldehyde but still requires ventilation. Decomposes to acetic acid (less toxic). Used for: medical device sterilization, pre-sterilized item re-exposure prevention. Limited availability in survival settings.

Bleach Solution for Disinfection (NOT Sterilization)

1:10 dilution of bleach (0.5% hypochlorite) = high-level disinfection. Does NOT achieve true sterilization but kills most pathogens including spores (slower). Contact time 10+ minutes. Primary use: environmental surface disinfection, equipment pre-cleaning. Not reliable as sole sterilization method.

Ultraviolet Sterilization

UV Light Mechanism

Ultraviolet light (254nm wavelength) damages microbial DNA, preventing replication. Effective against: bacteria, viruses, fungi. Not effective against spores (outer layer shields DNA). Limited to surface sterilization (no penetration into objects). Time required: 5-10 minutes for exposed surfaces at typical UV cabinet intensity.

UV Cabinet Limitations

  • Requires electricity (unavailable in many survival scenarios)
  • Objects must be directly exposed (cavities/indentations not sterilized)
  • Only surface effect; wrapped items not sterilized
  • UVA/UVB less effective than UVC; only UVC reliable
  • Bulbs degrade over time (output decreases)

Field Expedient UV Sterilization

Sunlight contains UV but at lower intensity and less UVC than sterilization bulbs. Extended direct sunlight (4+ hours) provides some disinfection effect but not reliable sterilization. Not recommended as primary sterilization method in survival scenario; better to use boiling or autoclaving.

Field Expedient Sterilization

Priority-Based Selection

First choice: Boiling (simple, effective, minimum requirements). For instruments/supplies entering wounds, sterile procedures. Second choice: Pressure cooker as autoclave (better sterilization if available). For critical procedures, known spore-forming organisms. Third choice: Flaming (only for metal items). Quick sterilization before procedure.

Pre-Sterilization Cleaning (Critical)

Organic material (blood, tissue, soil) protects organisms and reduces sterilization efficacy. Procedure: 1) Rinse under running water (remove gross contamination), 2) Scrub with brush and soap/detergent, 3) Rinse thoroughly until no visible debris, 4) Dry or proceed directly to sterilization. Proper cleaning reduces microbial load 99-99.9%; enables lower-intensity sterilization to achieve SAL.

Field Expedient Sterilizer Setup

Boiling pot: stainless steel preferred (less rust), large enough for instruments. Steamer basket or improvised platform keeps instruments off pot bottom. Cover: lid reduces steam loss, prevents recontamination. Sterile storage: clean cloth or paper, container protected from dust/moisture. Forceps or tongs for instrument handling (prevent hand contact).

Verification of Sterilization

Ideal: chemical indicators (color change at specific temperature, confirms adequate time/temperature). Field: observe rolling boil for prescribed duration, assumes adequate sterilization if boiled appropriately. Document sterilization (date, time, method) for equipment tracking. Test instruments occasionally by allowing to cool, then wrapping in sterile cloth; if recontaminated during storage, requires re-sterilization.

Critical Sterilization Safety

  • Steam burns = serious risk during autoclave/boiling sterilization. Never open pressurized autoclave immediately; allow depressurization. Use forceps/tongs, never bare hands to remove items from boiling water.
  • Instruments not fully dried before use = recontamination risk. Ensure complete drying before storage or use, especially for instruments stored <24 hours.
  • Inadequate sterilization time/temperature = failure to eliminate pathogens. Document sterilization parameters; don't estimate or shorten times.
  • Cross-contamination during removal = sterilization wasted. Use sterile forceps, place only on sterile surfaces. If sterile item touches non-sterile surface, re-sterilize.
  • Spore-forming pathogens (C. difficile, B. anthracis) = boiling insufficient. Use autoclave (if available) or accept risk of incomplete sterilization in survival scenario.

Common Sterilization Errors

  • Insufficient drying after sterilization: Moisture promotes recontamination and corrosion. Ensure complete drying before storage.
  • Packed instruments: Prevents steam/heat penetration; arrange in single layer for adequate exposure.
  • Recontamination during handling: Touching sterile items with bare hands, placing on non-sterile surfaces. Defeats entire sterilization process.
  • Shortened sterilization time: Assuming vigorous boil = sterilization without timing. Use timer; maintain prescribed time for reliability.
  • Ignoring altitude: Boiling temperature decreases with altitude; must add time for proper sterilization at elevation.
