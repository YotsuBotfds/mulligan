Survival Priorities: The Rule of 3s

The human body can survive with specific timeframes. Prioritize in this order:

The Three Rules Explained:

  • 3 minutes without air: Your brain suffers permanent damage after 4-6 minutes without oxygen. Rescue from drowning or suffocation is the absolute first priority.
  • 3 hours without shelter (extreme weather): Hypothermia or heat exhaustion in harsh conditions kills faster than you'd expect. In extreme cold, death can occur in under 3 hours.
  • 3 days without water: Dehydration impairs judgment, causes organ failure, and leads to death rapidly. Safe water is your third priority.
  • 3 weeks without food: The body can survive on stored energy. Hunger is the lowest priority if water is available.
Remember: These timelines vary with age, fitness, climate, and clothing. Someone in cold weather may succumb to hypothermia in 1-2 hours. A hydrated person in mild weather might last 4-5 days without water.

Psychological Survival: Mastering the Mental Game

More people fail to survive due to panic and poor decision-making than physical limitations. Your mind is your most powerful tool.

The STOP Method: Overcome Panic Instantly

STOP when crisis hits: [S] STOP - Pause. Do not act on impulse. [T] THINK - Breathe. Assess the actual situation. [O] OBSERVE - What resources do you have? What are the real threats? [P] PLAN - Create a simple, achievable action plan.

Dealing with Shock

  • Shock is the body's shutdown response to trauma. Victims may feel numb or detached.
  • Treatment: Lie down, elevate legs, keep warm, drink water if conscious. Talk reassuringly.
  • After shock passes, exhaustion and despair often follow. This is normal.

Maintaining Morale & Will to Survive

  • Create routines: Morning tasks, meal preparation, camp maintenance give structure and purpose.
  • Set small, achievable goals: "Today I'll collect water and build a better shelter" rather than "I need to find rescue."
  • Maintain hygiene: Washing, grooming, and keeping your shelter clean boost mental health dramatically.
  • Keep moving: Staying active prevents despair. Idle time invites panic.
  • Think rationally about odds: People ARE found. Rescue happens. Focus on what you control.

Leadership in Crisis

  • If others depend on you, project calm confidence even if you're terrified internally.
  • Assign tasks to others‚Äîthis provides purpose and keeps minds occupied.
  • Make decisions, communicate them clearly, and don't constantly second-guess yourself.
  • Acknowledge fears without dwelling on them: "Yes, this is dangerous. Here's what we'll do."
Critical: Panic is contagious. One person spiraling can break the group. The person who stays calm often becomes the de facto leader.

Shelter Building: Protection from the Elements

Shelter is your first physical priority in harsh weather. A poor shelter can turn survivable conditions into lethal ones.

Shelter Site Selection Criteria

  • Avoid low-lying areas (cold air sinks, water collects)
  • Avoid ridgelines and open areas (exposed to wind)
  • Look for moderate slopes with natural windbreaks (rock formations, dense trees)
  • Proximity to water, but not immediately next to it (insects, flooding risk)
  • Natural materials nearby for construction
  • Check for dead branches overhead ("widowmakers") before committing

Debris Hut (Best Emergency Shelter)

  • Find a sturdy branch as ridgepole or use Y-shaped trees
  • Lean ribs against the ridgepole at 45-degree angle
  • Layer leaves, bark, and branches over framework
  • Build thick: 1-2 feet of debris for insulation
  • Small entrance only‚Äîpreserves heat
  • Line interior with dry leaves for insulation from ground
  • Construction time: 1-2 hours for experienced builder

Lean-To Shelter

  • Fastest shelter to build (30-45 minutes)
  • Best when you need a fire reflector for warmth
  • Simply lean materials against a back support at 45¬∞ angle
  • Front must be open to benefit from fire heat

A-Frame Shelter

  • Excellent all-around shelter (1-2 hours to build)
  • Good insulation in moderate conditions
  • Door at one end, openable at other for ventilation
  • Can be made waterproof with proper layering

Snow Shelter (Quinzhee)

  • Pile snow (3-4 feet high), let it sit 1-2 hours to harden
  • Hollow out interior, leaving thick walls (12-18 inches)
  • Create entrance tunnel below sleeping area to trap heat
  • Poke hole in roof for ventilation (prevents CO‚ÇÇ buildup and condensation)
  • Sleeping platform on raised area inside
  • Time: 2-3 hours, but lasts entire winter if maintained

Tarp Configurations

  • A-Frame: Simple ridge line‚Äîminimal wind exposure
  • Lean-to: Maximum heat reflection from fire
  • Tube tent: Lay tarp on ridgepole, wrap around rope
  • Diamond: Stake corners low, creates peak‚Äîgood ventilation

Long-Term vs. Short-Term Shelter Site Selection

Short-Term Shelter (Emergency, 1-3 days): Speed and protection take priority. Seek natural features: caves, rock overhangs, dense brush. Build minimal structure. Focus on immediate threats (weather, visibility for rescue). Can occupy less-ideal locations temporarily.

Long-Term Settlement (Weeks to months): Site becomes critical. Consider:

  • Water source nearby but not immediately adjacent (prevents insect breeding, flooding)
  • Elevation with good drainage‚Äîavoid low points where cold air and water collect
  • Exposure to sunrise (morale, vitamin D) but afternoon shade
  • Access to dead wood for fuel without excessive harvesting
  • Defensible terrain if security concerns exist
  • Distance from large animal trails (you don't want to occupy predator territories)
  • Proximity to food sources: nut trees, berry bushes, hunting grounds, fishing spots
  • Stable ground that won't erode or become swampy with seasonal changes
  • Natural windbreak but not complete enclosure (stagnant air promotes disease)

Making Containers from Natural Materials

Bark Containers

  • Birch bark peels naturally in flexible sheets
  • Shape into cone or box by folding and securing with thin cordage or wooden pins
  • Create overlapping seams to shed water
  • Waterproofs naturally and lasts months to years
  • Ideal for carrying, storage, and cooking (can hold hot water for food warming)
  • Cedar and spruce bark also work but less flexible than birch

Clay Vessels

  • Find clay deposits near streams or excavations
  • Hand-shape into bowl, pot, or flat pan (no pottery wheel needed)
  • Smooth with water and stones
  • Dry slowly for 3-7 days in shade (prevents cracking)
  • Harden in fire: low heat (200¬∞F) for 2-3 hours, building to higher temps
  • Can be used for cooking, water storage, and transport once fired

Wooden Containers

  • Carve from soft wood using fire and stone tools
  • Create hollows by charring interior and scraping with stone
  • Can be sealed with plant resin or clay coating for water-tightness
  • More durable than bark, less fragile than clay

Water Carrying & Transport

  • Bladders: Animal stomachs/intestines tied at ends; flexible, lightweight, carry 2-5 gallons
  • Bark containers: Boil water then cool before storing; can insulate with wet cloth wrapping
  • Clay pots: Sealed with wax or pitch; keeps water cool through evaporation
  • Bamboo sections: Hollow naturally; seal ends with clay or leafy plugs
  • Transport yoke: Pole balanced across shoulders with containers on each end; distributes weight for long-distance carrying
  • Travois/sledge: Drag containers behind instead of carrying overhead (less fatigue for long distances)

Group Survival Dynamics

When multiple people survive together, psychological and social factors become as critical as physical tools.

Leadership & Decision-Making

  • One clear decision-maker prevents "survival by committee" paralysis
  • Leader must be calm, decisive, and fair‚Äînot necessarily loudest or physically strongest
  • Assign specific responsibilities to each person: water procurement, fire maintenance, shelter improvement, food gathering
  • Rotate duties to prevent resentment and keep all skills sharp in group
  • Acknowledge legitimate grievances but maintain decisiveness in priorities

Morale Management

  • Shared meals (however minimal) build cohesion‚Äîmake it a ritual, not just refueling
  • Celebrate small wins: successful fire, clean water, improved shelter
  • Allocate time for rest and conversation, not constant labor
  • Include physically weaker members in meaningful tasks (children, elderly, injured) to prevent despair
  • Address conflicts promptly‚Äîresentment spreads quickly in survival situations

Conflict Resolution

  • Establish basic group rules early: theft, violence, abandonment of duties have consequences
  • Allow disputes to be voiced but make final decisions quickly
  • Physical punishment for survival offenses is counterproductive‚Äîisolation or duty reduction work better
  • Know when to separate people temporarily to cool tensions

Day Signaling vs. Night Signaling

Daytime Signaling (Visibility Focus)

  • Smoke signals: Add green branches or damp material to fire. White smoke visible miles away. Pulsing smoke (cover/uncover with cloth) creates coded message.
  • Ground-to-air signals: Large V, X, or SOS made from rocks, logs, or cleared ground. Minimum 100 feet to be visible from aircraft.
  • Mirror flashing: Any reflective surface angled to sun. Visible 10+ miles. Three short flashes = SOS.
  • Bright cloth: Wave colored material (red, orange, yellow most visible). Tie to high point.
  • Advantage: Multiple simultaneous methods possible. Rescue aircraft actively searching during daylight.

Nighttime Signaling (Fire Focus)

  • Bright fire arrangement: Three fires in triangle (universal distress symbol). Keep dry materials nearby to ignite all three simultaneously when aircraft heard.
  • Strobe effect: Cover and uncover fire with cloth in coded pattern (three short, three long, three short = SOS).
  • Flares or bright accelerant: If available, burn bright chemicals (magnesium, sulfur) visible great distances.
  • Whistle signal: Three short blasts = distress. Can be heard further than voice. Audible in silence of night.
  • Challenge: Fewer rescue activities at night in most regions. Night signals intended for closer rescuers or to extend distress period into darkness.

Strategic Signaling Approach

  • Prepare signals in advance‚Äîdon't scramble when aircraft appears
  • Maintain adequate fuel supply for signal fires (separate from cooking fires)
  • Combine methods: smoke by day, fire by night, always have mirror and whistle ready
  • Mark location clearly with ground signals‚Äîrescuers need to land/approach
  • If no rescue within 3-7 days, shift mindset from "rescue is coming soon" to "I need to survive long-term here"

Fire Starting: Creating and Maintaining Flame

Fire provides warmth, water purification, food cooking, psychological comfort, and signaling capability. Master multiple ignition methods.

The Friction Method: Bow Drill

Friction Fire Components:

  • Bow: Flexible branch bent with cord/paracord
  • Shaft (spindle): Straight dry wood, 8-10 inches long
  • Fireboard: Flat dry wood with notch on edge
  • Bearing block: Holds shaft pressure from above
  • Tinder: Dry grass, bark, cloth, char cloth

Friction Fire Technique:

  1. Create dust‚Äîrapid reciprocal motion with full body weight on bearing block
  2. Dust will darken and smoke‚Äîthis is critical
  3. When dust turns brown/black and smoke increases, lift board immediately
  4. Glowing coal should be in dust on the board
  5. Transfer coal to tinder bundle with leaves/bark
  6. Blow gently to ignite‚Äînever exhale hard or you'll extinguish it

Hand Drill Method (Harder)

  • Same principle as bow drill but using hands only
  • Rapid downward spinning motion on fireboard
  • Harder, faster, more physically demanding
  • Best for survival practice only‚Äîbow drill is superior

Fire Plow

  • Harder wood dragged rapidly on softer wood
  • Creates pressure and friction heat
  • Less reliable than drill methods but simpler to set up

Flint & Steel

  • High-carbon steel struck against flint creates sparks
  • Sparks ignite char cloth (pre-charred cloth)
  • Transfer flame to tinder bundle
  • Char cloth making: 100% cotton soaked in potassium nitrate (saltpeter) or wood ash, then slow-baked in oven until dark

Magnifying Lens Fire

  • Requires bright sunlight
  • Focus lens on tinder bundle until smoke appears
  • Blow gently to encourage flame
  • Works with glasses, water droplet on leaf, or ice lens carved from ice

Fire Lay Types (After Ignition)

Teepee Fire

Best for starting fire. Kindling leans together. Good oxygen flow.

Log Cabin Fire

Excellent burn. Logs alternate directions. Burns hot and long.

Star Fire

Fuel-efficient. Push logs in as needed. Great for long-term fire.

Tinder/Kindling/Fuel Hierarchy

  • Tinder: Ignites from spark/flame immediately. Dry grass, bark, char cloth, cattail down, thistle down, fungus
  • Kindling: Pencil-thin to thumb-thick sticks. Catches from tinder flame, provides heat for fuel
  • Fuel: Wrist-thick or larger logs. Maintains fire for hours
Dry wood collection: Dead standing trees (snags) provide the driest fuel. Dead branches at height are drier than ground wood. Split wood reveals dry interior even in wet conditions.

Water Finding & Emergency Purification

Clean water is critical. Dehydration impairs judgment, physical performance, and survival instinct. Death can occur in 3 days without water in hot climates.

Finding Water

  • Vegetation: Lush green plants indicate moisture underground. Follow their locations.
  • Terrain clues: Water flows downhill. Follow valleys and draw lines at dawn/dusk when animals drink.
  • Animal signs: Bird droppings, animal tracks all converge at water sources. Follow them.
  • Morning dew: Cloth dragged through grass collects dew. Wring into mouth or container.
  • Solar still: Dig pit, place leafy vegetation inside, cover with plastic/clear material with stone in center to drip condensation into collection cup.
  • Transpiration bag: Tie plastic around leafy branch. Sun causes leaf water evaporation, which condenses inside bag.

Emergency Purification Without Equipment

1. Boiling (Most Reliable)

  • Rolling boil for 1 minute kills most pathogens
  • At high altitudes, boil 3 minutes
  • Does NOT remove chemical contaminants, only biological pathogens
  • Uses significant fuel‚Äîonly purify water you'll drink

2. Solar Disinfection (SODIS)

  • Fill clear plastic bottle with water
  • Expose to direct sunlight for 6-8 hours (or 2 days if cloudy)
  • UV radiation kills most bacteria, viruses, protozoa
  • Free, requires only sun and clear container

3. Filtration (Multiple Stages)

  • Stage 1: Large cloth filters out sediment and debris
  • Stage 2: Sand and gravel layer (improves clarity)
  • Stage 3: Activated charcoal (boil wood until blackened, crush to powder)
  • Stack layers in cloth cone or container with drainage hole
  • Does NOT remove viruses‚Äîmust be combined with boiling

4. Bleach Method (If Available)

  • Unscented household bleach (5-6% sodium hypochlorite)
  • Clear water: 2 drops per liter, let sit 30 minutes
  • Cloudy water: 4 drops per liter, let sit 30 minutes
  • Should smell faintly of bleach when ready

Collection and Storage

  • Collect from flowing water when possible (less stagnant)
  • Store in covered container away from direct sunlight (bacteria breeds in light)
  • Rotate stored water regularly
  • Boiled water should be cooled before drinking (prevents shock to system)
Never drink: Stagnant water, salt water, or water from obviously polluted sources (dead animals, human waste). Risk of giardia, cryptosporidium, and dysentery outweighs temporary thirst relief.

Signaling for Rescue: Making Yourself Findable

Even the best survival situation ends poorly if rescuers can't find you. Establish multiple signaling methods immediately.

Signal Fires (The Rule of Three)

  • Three fires arranged in triangle pattern is universal distress signal
  • Space fires 100 feet apart in triangle formation
  • Keep dry materials nearby to ignite fires quickly
  • Add green branches to create smoke during day
  • Use dry wood to create flame visible at night
  • Keep fires small until you hear/see aircraft, then ignite all three simultaneously

Mirror Signaling (Heliograph)

  • Any reflective surface (metal, mirror, shiny watch, clear ice)
  • Angle to reflect sunlight toward aircraft or distant rescuers
  • Visible 10+ miles away on clear day
  • Create flashing pattern: three short, three long, three short (SOS)

Ground-to-Air Signals

  • V: Need assistance/help
  • X: Need medical assistance
  • ‚Üí: Going this direction
  • SOS: International distress signal
  • Make signals large and visible from altitude (100+ feet across)
  • Use contrasting materials: dark rocks on light ground, cleared patches on vegetation

Whistle Signaling

  • Three 2-second blasts = distress signal (universal)
  • Carry whistle in survival kit‚Äîlouder and longer range than voice
  • Audible 1-2 miles in open terrain
  • Conserve voice energy; whistle doesn't tire you
  • Two short bursts = "I'm here" acknowledgment
Priority order: 1) Stay put (harder to find moving target), 2) Make fires, 3) Arrange ground signals, 4) Use whistle, 5) Keep mirror ready.

Emergency First Aid Priorities

Medical problems can become life-threatening quickly without proper response. Follow the ABC protocol.

ABCs of Survival Medicine

A - AIRWAY

  • Clear mouth of debris, vomit, tongue
  • Open mouth, tilt head back slightly
  • Remove choking objects carefully
  • If unconscious, put in recovery position

B - BREATHING

  • Check if chest rises
  • Listen for breath sounds
  • Feel for air at nose/mouth
  • If not breathing, begin rescue breathing (CPR)

C - CIRCULATION

  • Check for pulse (neck, wrist)
  • Look for signs of bleeding
  • Check skin color (pale = shock)
  • If no pulse, begin chest compressions

Recovery Position (For Unconscious, Breathing Victim)

Recovery Position Steps:

  1. Place victim on their side
  2. Tilt head back to keep airway open
  3. Bent leg prevents rolling forward
  4. Extended arm on ground stabilizes body
  5. This position prevents choking if vomiting occurs

Stopping Severe Bleeding

  • Direct pressure: Press wound with cloth, don't look underneath for 10+ minutes
  • Elevation: Raise bleeding limb above heart to reduce blood pressure at wound
  • Pressure points: Artery pressure between wound and heart reduces bleeding (inner arm, neck, groin)
  • Tourniquet: Last resort for life-threatening limb bleeding. Tie 2-3 inches above wound. Mark with time. Can cause tissue death if left on >2 hours.
  • Hemostatic agents: Crushed spider webs, sphagnum moss, charcoal ash promote clotting

Treating Shock

  • Lay victim down, elevate legs 12 inches
  • Keep warm with blankets/clothing
  • Reassure victim calmly‚Äîpanic worsens shock
  • Do NOT give food or water (can cause vomiting)
  • Loosen tight clothing
  • Monitor breathing and pulse regularly

Splinting Fractures

  • Immobilize broken bone to prevent further injury
  • Use natural materials: branches, bark, cloth strips
  • Support above and below fracture
  • Ensure splint isn't too tight (check circulation beyond splint)
  • Apply cold (snow, ice) if available to reduce swelling
  • Elevate limb to reduce swelling

Burn Treatment

  • Cool burn with water/snow immediately (stops heat damage)
  • Remove jewelry/constricting items
  • Cover with clean cloth to prevent infection
  • Do NOT apply ice directly (causes further damage)
  • Do NOT use ice water (can cause hypothermia)
  • Elevate burned area

Edible Plants: Quick ID & Universal Edibility Test

Plant knowledge takes years to master. When uncertain, use the Universal Edibility Test before consuming unknown plants.

Universal Edibility Test (12-24 Hour Process)

  1. Separate plant: Test each part separately (leaves, roots, stems, seeds)
  2. Skin test: Crush plant, rub on inner wrist. Wait 15 minutes for rash/burn
  3. Lip test: Place crushed plant on lips. Wait 3 minutes for burning/numbness
  4. Swallow test: Place small amount in mouth (don't swallow). Hold 3 minutes, check for burning/numbness. Spit out.
  5. Stomach test: If no reaction, swallow small amount. Wait 15 minutes for nausea/cramps
  6. Full portion: If no reaction, eat larger amount. Wait 5 hours observing for poisoning signs
  7. Regular consumption: Only eat after successful test. Some plants cause cumulative poisoning

Dangerous Look-Alikes (NEVER EAT)

  • Hemlock vs. Wild Carrot: Hemlock has purple splotches on stem, hairless. Wild carrot has hairy stem. Hemlock is deadly poison.
  • Death Camas vs. Wild Onion: Death camas has no onion smell. True onions smell strongly of garlic/onion when crushed.
  • False Hellebore vs. Wild Leek: False hellebore has no smell. Wild leeks smell distinctly onionlike.
  • Pokeweed berries: Young shoots edible (boil 3 times, change water each time) but berries and roots are deadly poison

Safe Plant Families (Generally)

  • Onion/Garlic family: All edible if they smell like onion/garlic when crushed. Never eat without smell test.
  • Mint family: Square stems, aromatic leaves. All members edible. Includes peppermint, spearmint, wild bergamot
  • Carrot family (Umbelliferae): DANGEROUS‚Äîincludes both edible (carrot, parsnip, parsley) and deadly (hemlock). Never eat without positive ID
  • Brassica family: Cabbage-like plants. Includes wild mustard, kale, broccoli relatives. All edible if it looks like cabbage
  • Rose family: Leaves often have serrated edges. Most shoots, seeds, roots edible. Includes berries
  • Legume family: Pea-like plants with bean pods. Most edible but some seeds/beans require cooking

Common Edible Wild Plants

  • Cattails: Entire plant edible‚Äîroots (starchy), shoots (eat raw), pollen (substitute for flour)
  • Dandelions: Leaves (bitter greens), roots (roasted coffee substitute), flowers (salad)
  • Clover: Flowers and leaves edible raw or cooked
  • Plantain: Young leaves edible raw, older leaves cooked. High nutrition.
  • Chickweed: Tender leaves, raw or cooked. High in vitamins
  • Purslane: Entire plant edible, high in omega-3 fatty acids
  • Wild berries: Only eat if you can identify species. Rule: solid colored berries safer than speckled
Poisonous plants signs: Milky sap, umbrella-shaped flower clusters with purple stems, white sap, strong musty smell, three leaves in triangular pattern, shiny berries, resemblance to hemlock.

Nutritional Priority When Foraging

  1. Calorie-dense: Nuts, seeds, roots, legumes
  2. Vitamins: Leafy greens, berries, shoots
  3. Protein: Legumes, nuts, wild grains
  4. Fats: Nuts, seeds, animal fats (if hunting)

Basic Tool Making: Creating Survival Equipment

Superior tools multiply your survival capabilities. Ancient humans survived with stone tools‚Äîyou can with primitive materials.

Stone Knapping (Making Stone Tools)

Hand Axe (Basic Chopping Tool)

  • Select stone with sharp edge (flint, obsidian, even broken glass)
  • Strike at acute angle with another hard stone
  • Flake breaks away leaving sharp edge
  • Repeat on multiple edges to create sharp cutting tool
  • Wrap base with cloth/leather to prevent cutting hand

Scrapers (For Hide/Wood Processing)

  • Create flat edge by striking stone to form 90-degree edge
  • Useful for scraping hide, wood working, butchering
  • Lighter than axe, more controlled

Bone Tools

  • Bone is harder than wood, holds sharp edge well
  • Fracture bone to create sharp points for fish hooks, needles, spears
  • Carve bone with stone tool to create fish hooks with barbs
  • Lash to wooden handles with plant fiber cordage
  • Bone needles for sewing clothes/leather

Fire-Hardened Spears

  • Select straight branch 6-8 feet long, 1 inch diameter
  • Sharpen point with stone tool into cone shape
  • Harden point by holding near (not in) flame until wood darkens
  • Harden wood makes point stronger and less likely to splinter
  • Attach stone or bone point by splitting wood and wedging in piece

Cordage from Plant Fibers

Bark Cordage

  • Remove bark from birch, willow, cedar in strips
  • Soak to soften, then split into thin strands
  • Twist strands together (2-3 strands for strength)
  • Creates rope for shelter, fishing, traps

Grass/Fiber Cordage

  • Collect long grasses, plant fibers (iris leaves, yucca)
  • Dry slightly (not brittle)
  • Twist 2-3 strands together with thumb and finger
  • Add new fibers by overlapping ends as old ones end
  • Stronger when wet, so wet before final use

Basic Traps (Figure-4 Deadfall)

Figure-4 Trigger Mechanism (Most Reliable)

  1. Vertical stick: Held upright under the weight
  2. Horizontal stick: Bent arm supporting the weight, catches vertical stick at angle
  3. Trigger stick: Extends from the joint, slightly upward so slight touch drops system
  4. When animal touches trigger, all pieces fall and heavy log crushes prey

Other Simple Traps

  • Snare: Loop of cordage attached to bent sapling. Animal enters loop, sapling snaps up and hangs animal by neck.
  • Pit trap: Deep hole with sharpened stakes pointing inward. Animal falls in, spikes impale it.
  • Fish trap: Funnel-shaped cage of sticks. Fish enter through small opening, can't escape.

Navigation Without Equipment: Finding Direction

Maps and compasses fail. Understanding natural navigation ensures you can always find direction.

Using the Sun (Shadow Stick Method)

Steps:

  1. Place straight stick vertically in ground
  2. Mark where shadow tip touches ground
  3. Wait 15-20 minutes
  4. Mark new shadow tip position
  5. Line between marks runs East-West (first mark = West)
  6. Perpendicular line runs North-South

Star Navigation (Night)

Northern Hemisphere: Polaris (North Star)

  • Find Big Dipper constellation (part of Ursa Major)
  • The two stars forming the "cup" point to Polaris
  • Distance to Polaris = approximately 5x the distance between pointer stars
  • Polaris marks true North
  • Height of Polaris above horizon = your latitude in degrees

Southern Hemisphere: Southern Cross (Crux)

  • Locate Southern Cross (4 bright stars in cross pattern)
  • Extend the long axis 4-5 times its length
  • This point marks South Celestial Pole
  • Drop perpendicular line to horizon = South direction

Moss Myth vs. Reality

  • Myth: Moss grows only on north side of trees
  • Reality: Moss grows where it's damp and shaded. Could be any side. Not reliable.
  • Better clue: Tree bark is thicker on north side (less sun damage), lichen grows more on north side
  • Still not 100% reliable‚Äîuse sun/stars instead

Wind Patterns (General)

  • In temperate zones, prevailing winds blow West to East
  • Observe which direction clouds consistently move
  • Not precise for navigation but useful for general orientation
  • Storm systems rotate‚Äîcan indicate direction of travel

Water Navigation

  • Water always flows downhill (toward lower elevation)
  • Following water eventually leads to civilization
  • Rivers confluences can be used to determine direction (smaller tributaries feed larger rivers going downhill)
  • Stay near water when lost (reliable resource, natural pathways)

Radiation & Nuclear Fallout Basics

In a nuclear event, understanding radiation behavior, shelter effectiveness, and decontamination procedures can mean the difference between survival and fatal exposure. This knowledge could save lives.

What is Radiation?

Radiation is energy released by unstable atoms. Three primary types reach Earth after nuclear detonation:

  • Alpha Particles: Heavy, positively charged. Stopped by paper or skin. Dangerous only if inhaled or ingested. Range: inches in air.
  • Beta Particles: Electrons traveling at high speed. Penetrate paper and light clothing. Stopped by plastic or thin metal. Range: feet in air. Can burn skin (beta burn).
  • Gamma Rays: High-energy light waves. Most penetrating. Require dense shielding (lead, concrete, earth). Range: miles. Primary acute hazard.

Fallout: What It Is and How It Travels

Fallout is radioactive dust and debris ejected into the atmosphere during nuclear detonation. The fireball superheats air, creating an updraft that carries particles high into the atmosphere. Larger particles fall nearby (local fallout, first 24 hours). Smaller particles circle Earth, falling gradually (global fallout, weeks to months).

Wind direction is critical: Fallout travels downwind. A 10-mph wind carries local fallout approximately 100+ miles in 24 hours. Anticipate fallout patterns based on wind forecasts.

Half-Life and Decay (The 7-10 Rule)

Radiation decreases predictably. For every 7x increase in time, radiation drops to 1/10 of its original intensity. This rule is approximately accurate for the first 2 weeks:

  • Hour 1 after detonation = baseline (100%)
  • Hour 7 = 10% of baseline
  • Hour 49 (7 √ó 7) = 1% of baseline
  • Hour 343 (7 √ó 7 √ó 7) ‚âà 2 weeks = 0.1% of baseline

Critical point: After 2 weeks, radiation levels drop to approximately 1% of initial exposure. This is why sheltering for 2 weeks is protective. 4 weeks (28 days) is much safer, reducing exposure to 0.1%.

Sheltering: Distance, Shielding, and Time

The three protective factors work together:

  • Distance: Radiation spreads; doubling distance reduces exposure to 1/4. Stay indoors initially.
  • Shielding: Dense materials (concrete, earth, lead) block gamma rays. Thickness matters exponentially.
  • Time: Sheltering indoors for 24-48 hours after fallout deposition dramatically reduces exposure as radiation decays.

Decontamination: Remove, Don't Absorb

Contamination means radioactive particles on your body or clothing. Decontamination removes these particles physically.

  1. Remove outer clothing: Take off outer layer (coat, shoes, outer pants). Bag separately. Removes 80-90% of contamination.
  2. Wash skin: Gentle soap and water. Wash all exposed areas (face, hands, neck, hair). Avoid scrubbing (drives particles deeper). Change water frequently.
  3. Wash hair thoroughly: Radioactive dust clings to hair. Shampoo multiple times.
  4. Clean under fingernails and between toes. Particles hide in crevices.
  5. Blow nose gently. Clear nasal passages of particles.
  6. Don't eat contaminated food or water. Internal exposure is most dangerous. Boil water if contamination possible. Discard outer layer of produce.

When Is It Safe to Go Outside?

Safety depends on radiation levels and deposition intensity. General guidelines:

  • First 24-48 hours: Stay indoors with all doors/windows sealed. This is most critical period. 80% of acute exposure occurs in first day if outside.
  • After 2 weeks: Radiation drops to ~1% of initial levels. Risk of acute radiation sickness (from single exposure) decreases dramatically. Areas can be entered briefly for essential supplies.
  • After 4 weeks: Radiation at 0.1% of initial. Much safer for longer outdoor exposure. Remaining risk is primarily long-term (cancer, genetic effects over years).
  • Monitoring: Ideally, use radiation detector (Geiger counter or dosimeter) to measure actual levels. Without instruments, follow fallout forecast guidance.

Improvised Dosimeter (Detecting Radiation Presence)

Without professional equipment, an improvised dosimeter can indicate presence of radiation:

  • Electroscope Method: Aluminum foil wrapped around charged object. Radiation ionizes air, discharging the foil. Foil collapse indicates radiation present.
  • Film Dosimeter (Original): Unexposed photographic film becomes fogged by radiation. Darker film = higher exposure. Keep film sealed until needed; expose in suspect area; develop to assess radiation presence.
  • Limitations: These methods detect presence but don't precisely measure dose. Useful for determining if area is radiating or if contamination exists.

Long-Term Health Effects and Protection

Immediate acute radiation sickness occurs above 100 rad. Lower doses cause long-term effects:

  • Cancer risk: Increases with dose, especially if exposed as child. Thyroid cancer risk highest from radioactive iodine.
  • Genetic effects: Radiation can damage reproductive cells. Risk increases with dose.
  • Cataracts: Lenticular opacity from radiation exposure (threshold ~200 rad).
  • Reducing risk: Limit exposure as much as possible. Potassium iodide (KI) protects thyroid if taken before or immediately after exposure (blocks iodine uptake).
Critical: Fallout shelters save lives. In nuclear scenario, sheltering for 2 weeks indoors dramatically reduces radiation exposure and risk. Having a prepared shelter location (basement, interior room, below-ground) is one of the most valuable survival preparations.

Cold Weather Survival

Cold kills faster than most realize. Hypothermia can develop in minutes. Understanding stages, treatment, and prevention is essential in temperate and arctic climates.

Hypothermia Stages and Recognition

Mild 95-90¬∞F (35-32¬∞C) Shivering (violent), numbness, confusion, difficulty speaking, poor judgment Get dry, insulate, warm core with hot beverages, move to shelter. Alert person remains able to help with self-rescue.

Severe Below 82¬∞F (below 28¬∞C) Unconsciousness, weak/no pulse, minimal breathing, body appears dead (pale, stiff) Medical emergency. "Not dead until warm and dead" - continue CPR even if unresponsive. Gentle rewarming. Avoid movement.

Hypothermia Treatment: Get Dry, Insulate, Warm Core

  1. Stop heat loss: Remove wet clothing. Get into dry environment/shelter. Wind is killer in cold - block it.
  2. Insulate from ground: Ground draws body heat. Use leaves, pine needles, anything to separate from cold earth.
  3. Warm core first: Extremities are sacrificial in survival. Focus on keeping core (chest, abdomen) warm. Warm beverages (never alcohol) help. Hot water bottles wrapped in cloth against core.
  4. Avoid rapid rewarming: Fast rewarming can cause afterdrop (core temperature drops further). Gradual rewarming is safer.
  5. Rest and monitoring: Hypothermia victim needs rest. Monitor vitals. Heart can be irritable in moderate hypothermia; rough handling risks cardiac arrhythmia.

Frostbite: Recognition and Treatment

Recognition: White or waxy skin (early), blistering and darkening (late stage), pain followed by numbness.

Prevention: Dry gloves/socks, keep extremities moving, never ignore numbness (sign of freezing tissue), limit exposure.

Treatment:

  • Rewarm in 98-102¬∞F (37-39¬∞C) water. Immerse for 15-30 minutes. Water should feel warm to elbow, not hot (hot water damages already-injured tissue).
  • Thaw completely. Refreezing frozen tissue is worse than staying frozen until definitive rewarming available.
  • Do NOT rub affected area. Do NOT use direct heat (fire). Do NOT massage.
  • After thawing: blisters may form (normal). Cover with loose bandage. Avoid infection. Antibiotics if available.
  • Severe frostbite (black, mummified appearance) requires surgical evaluation. Don't force thawing if medical care nearby (tissue may be salvageable with specialized care).

Snow Shelter Construction

Quinzhee (Snow Shelter)

  • Location: Avoid slopes (avalanche risk), seek sheltered areas where snow accumulates.
  • Pile snow: Shovel or gather snow into mound 3-4 feet high, 6-8 feet diameter.
  • Let set: Leave for 1-2 hours. Wind and time consolidate snow. Walls should be firm enough to carve.
  • Hollow interior: Dig from one side, leaving walls 12-18 inches thick. Entrance tunnel dug lower than sleeping platform (cold sinks).
  • Vent hole: Poke hole in roof (prevents CO‚ÇÇ buildup, reduces condensation). Block with cloth if wind enters.
  • Sleeping platform: Higher platform inside (cold air settles below). Line with insulation (leaves, branches, sleeping pad).
  • Insulation value: Snow provides surprising insulation (R-value ~1 per inch of depth). 12-18" walls provide significant protection from wind and extreme cold.

Snow Cave

  • Use natural feature (bank, drift) to minimize digging.
  • Carve out sleeping area above entrance level.
  • Vent hole for air circulation.
  • Faster to construct than quinzhee but less insulation.

Fire in Cold Weather

  • Platform of green logs: Wet wood pulled from ground (or green branches) create foundation. Prevents fire from melting into ground and sinking.
  • Reflector wall: Stones, logs, or snow bank behind fire reflects heat back toward shelter. Significantly increases effectiveness.
  • Windbreak: Position shelter to block wind. Wind dissipates heat. Protected area is warmer.
  • Fuel preparation: Cold makes kindling harder to ignite. Pre-split dry wood, small feather sticks burn better in cold.
  • Fire layout: Star pattern (logs radiating from center) allows gradual pushing inward as ends burn. Conserves fuel.

Clothing System: Layering for Cold

Base Layer (Moisture Wicking): Wool or synthetic (never cotton). Removes sweat from skin. Wet clothing is lethal in cold. Change if damp.

Mid Layer (Insulation): Wool, down, or synthetic insulation. Traps air. Provides warmth even if damp (advantage of wool). Multiple thin layers better than one thick (allows venting if overheated during activity).

Outer Layer (Wind/Water Protection): Windproof, water-resistant material. Reduces wind penetration and protects from precipitation.

Extremities: Hat critical (40% heat loss through head). Gloves, warm socks (change if wet). Mittens warmer than gloves (allows fingers to touch). Scarf protects neck and face.

Winter Water: Melting Snow Safely

  • Never eat snow: Melting snow in stomach requires body heat. Hypothermia risk increases. Melt first, then drink.
  • Melting method: Use fire/heat source. Add small amounts of snow to water and heat gradually. Snow insulates itself; too much snow prevents melting.
  • Water purity: Fresh snow is cleaner than old snow. Surface snow has more contaminants than subsurface. Boil if contamination suspected.
  • Insulated containers: Keep water from refreezing. Wrap in insulation or keep near body warmth.
Cold Survival Priority: Shelter > Fire > Water > Food. In extreme cold, hypothermia kills in hours. Get out of wind, dry, and warm immediately.

First 72 Hours: Hour-by-Hour Action Plan

This timeline provides the exact priorities for your first three days of survival.

HOUR 1-3: Immediate Survival

Priorities (in order):
  • Assess immediate threats (fire, water, collapsing structures, hostile persons)
  • Perform STOP method: Stop, Think, Observe, Plan
  • Check for injuries‚Äîtreat life-threatening wounds (severe bleeding, airway compromise)
  • Locate emergency supplies/equipment in immediate vicinity
  • Determine location: Are you lost? Do you have shelter nearby?
Actions to take:
  • Establish whether you're staying put or moving
  • If injured/exhausted: find immediate shelter (even temporary)
  • Begin basic fire (if temperature dropping)
  • Signal if near potential rescuers (fire, whistle, mirror)
  • Secure all valuable items from loss

HOUR 4-8: Shelter & Safety

Primary focus: Build proper shelter
  • Select shelter location (avoid hazards, near resources)
  • Gather materials: branches, leaves, bark, moss
  • Build debris hut or lean-to (target completion in 4-6 hours)
  • Gather firewood for night (prioritize‚Äînightfall adds psychological stress)
  • Organize shelter: insulation materials, bedding, fire placement
Secondary tasks:
  • If water available nearby: determine purification method (boiling vs. SODIS)
  • Establish fire lay type based on resources (teepee vs. log cabin)
  • Mark camp location with signal indicators visible from air
  • Create tool setup: establish a "workbench" area outside shelter

HOUR 8-12: Water & Fire

Critical task: Secure water supply
  • Locate water source: vegetation indicators, terrain, animal signs
  • Collect water (boil if time permits, or begin SODIS bottle exposure)
  • If no water found: Set up morning dew collection or solar still
  • Drink small amounts frequently (prevents dehydration shock)
  • Ration: you need minimum 1 liter per day in moderate climate
Fire maintenance:
  • Keep fire burning through night (warmth, morale, security)
  • Maintain adequate fuel supply nearby shelter
  • Sleep rotation in groups: one person tends fire while others rest
  • Smoke keeps insects away‚Äîadvantage of larger fire

DAY 1 (24 HOURS): Consolidation

Major focus: Improve shelter & tools
  • Reinforce shelter walls with additional leaves/bark layers
  • Create bedding platform (keeps body off cold ground)
  • Make simple tools: hand axe, scraper (improves work efficiency)
  • Expand water storage and purification
  • Begin food search if not injured/exhausted
Psychological priorities:
  • Create camp routines: morning water collection, midday tool work, evening fire tending
  • Maintain hygiene: wash, groom (massive morale boost)
  • Reassess group status‚Äîtalk with other survivors, acknowledge fears
  • Plan Day 2 goals (prevents despair)

DAY 2 (48 HOURS): Expansion

Shelter: Make permanent improvements
  • Build second entrance for wind/fire control if needed
  • Improve insulation: add grass layer to floor, seal gaps
  • Create hanging storage for food (keeps animals out)
  • Establish latrine area far from camp (sanitation = disease prevention)
Food gathering begins (if water/shelter secured)
  • Search for edible plants: berries, roots, leafy greens
  • Attempt fishing with improvised traps/hooks (low effort, can be left unattended)
  • Set Figure-4 deadfall traps for small game
  • Collect eggs if bird nests accessible
  • Remember: food is priority #4 (after air, shelter, water)
Signaling enhancement:
  • Arrange ground-to-air signal (V, X, or SOS) near camp
  • Use rocks/cleared logs to make 100+ ft letters visible from air
  • Create signal fire arrangement (triangle of three fire spots)
  • Keep whistle and mirror in constant possession

DAY 3 (72 HOURS): Optimization

Shelter: Weatherproofing
  • Add water-shedding design (sloped roof)
  • Create doorway protection (wind blocks, insulating layers)
  • Test shelter in rain if possible (patch leaks immediately)
  • Consider day/night temperature differences‚Äîadjust for upcoming weather
Food sustainability
  • Establish hunting/trapping schedule (morning/evening activity cycles)
  • Smoke/dry any game killed (extends storage time)
  • Create map of plant food sources nearby (expand daily foraging range)
  • Attempt new recipes: boiled plant roots, roasted seeds
Long-term planning
  • Plan for water storage/purification system (for dry seasons)
  • Identify nearby resources: flint for tools, medicinal plants
  • Create backup food cache away from main camp (security)
  • Plan for seasonal changes: shelter weatherproofing, fuel needs
  • If no rescue yet: Accept this may be long-term‚Äîshift mindset to sustainability
Critical Day 3 assessment: If no rescue by Hour 72, assume long-term survival. Shift focus from "rescue will find me soon" to "I will survive here indefinitely." This psychological shift prevents despair and maintains will to continue improving conditions.
