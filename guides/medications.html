Antibiotic Stewardship

Use narrowest spectrum effective for identified pathogen. Do NOT use antibiotics for viral infections (ineffective, promotes resistance). Complete full course even if symptoms improve (prevents relapse, resistance). Combine antibiotics for severe infections (broader coverage). Monitor for allergic reactions, side effects. Reduce dose for renal failure if available to assess function.

Acetaminophen

Dose: 325-650mg every 4-6 hours, max 3-4g daily. For fever/mild pain. Does NOT reduce inflammation. Lower GI upset risk than NSAIDs. Overdose = liver failure; strict adherence to dosing critical. Many combination products contain acetaminophen; check to avoid overdose.

Opioid Analgesics

Codeine: Mild pain, 15-60mg every 4-6 hours. Often combined with acetaminophen. Morphine: Moderate-severe pain, 5-10mg IM/SC or 10-15mg oral every 3-4 hours. Monitor respiration (respiratory depression risk). Use cautiously: addiction risk, constipation, respiratory depression, orthostatic hypotension. Keep naloxone available (opioid reversal agent).

Dosing and Administration

Routes of Administration

  • Oral (PO): Most common, requires swallowing/GI function. Absorption variable (food may affect).
  • Intramuscular (IM): Faster than PO, predictable absorption. Site: outer deltoid arm, vastus lateralis thigh. Draw with one needle, inject with fresh needle (prevent tissue damage).
  • Intravenous (IV): Fastest, predictable levels, but requires sterile access. Risk of infection, phlebitis. Dilute medications per protocol.
  • Subcutaneous (SC): Slower than IM, used for insulin, epinephrine, some antibiotics. Limit volume (1-2mL).

Adult Weight-Based Dosing

Standard reference: 70kg adult. Adjust for: significant weight difference (obese, cachetic), renal function (decrease if impaired), hepatic function (decrease if cirrhosis). Pediatric dosing: often weight-based (mg/kg); see pediatric section.

Concentration/Volume Calculations

Formula: Desired dose = (Concentration × Volume) / Total mL. Example: Amoxicillin 500mg powder + 5mL sterile water = 250mg/mL concentration. To give 500mg: (250mg/mL × X mL) = 500mg; X = 2mL. Double-check calculations (especially with unfamiliar concentrations).

Antibiotic-Drug Interactions

Fluoroquinolones: Reduce absorption of antacids, iron, zinc; space 2+ hours apart. Macrolides: Inhibit CYP3A4 (increases levels of many drugs). Tetracyclines: Chelate with calcium/iron/magnesium; avoid dairy, antacids. Metronidazole: Disulfiram-like reaction with alcohol (nausea, vomiting); avoid alcohol.

Storage and Shelf Life

Temperature Stability

Room temperature (68-77°F/20-25°C): most medications. Refrigerated (35-46°F/2-8°C): biologics (vaccines), some liquids. Avoid: freezing, direct sunlight, high humidity. In survival: store in cool, dry location. No refrigeration = reduced stability; priority: rotate stock, use oldest first.

Medication Degradation Factors

  • Heat accelerates breakdown (rule of thumb: stability halves per 10°C increase)
  • Humidity causes tablets/capsules to absorb water, promote decomposition
  • Light (especially for certain drugs) causes degradation
  • Oxygen exposure (oxidation) can affect efficacy
  • Physical damage to packaging allows contamination

Estimated Shelf Life (Room Temperature)

Medication ClassTypical Shelf LifeNotes
Tablets/capsules (sealed)3-5 yearsLonger if stored properly; after expiration, gradual potency loss
Liquids (suspensions)1-2 yearsShorter; powder reconstituted IM for shorter (7-14 days typically)
Injectables2-5 yearsVaries; heat-sensitive
Topical creams/ointments1-3 yearsSeparate container reduces contamination, extends life

Survival Storage Strategy

Label all containers with drug name, concentration, expiration date. Store desiccant packets (salt, rice) to reduce humidity. Rotate stock (FIFO: first in, first out). In post-collapse: assume manufacturers can't restock; use expired medications cautiously (lost potency likely, but not necessarily harmful; better than nothing for many). Maintain cool environment: underground storage, caves provide stable temperature.

Pediatric Dosing

Weight-Based Dosing

Most pediatric medications: mg/kg × weight (kg). Example: Amoxicillin 25mg/kg: child 20kg = 500mg. Need to know: child's weight (most accurate), age (approximation if weight unavailable). Dosing frequency often same as adults; shorter interval may be necessary (faster metabolism).

Pediatric Considerations

  • Liquid formulations preferred (difficulty swallowing tablets)
  • Taste: bitter medicines; mix with small amount food if possible
  • Rapid metabolism: may need higher mg/kg than adults
  • Kidney/liver immaturity in infants: longer dosing intervals, lower doses
  • Risk of medication errors higher with pediatric calculations; double-check all dosing

Critical Medication Safety

  • Allergy history = critical before administering ANY medication. Ask about allergies to: penicillin, NSAIDs, local anesthetics, other drugs. Document clearly.
  • Overdose risk = NSAIDs, acetaminophen, opioids. Know maximum daily doses; check combination products for duplicates.
  • Drug-drug interactions = potential for serious harm. If multiple medications, check compatibility; simplify regimen if possible.
  • Medication administration errors = verify 5 rights: right patient, right drug, right dose, right route, right time. Double-check before administering.
  • Unknown provenance = avoid medications from untrusted sources (contaminated, mislabeled). If forced to use, verify appearance, smell, texture before administering.

Common Medication Errors

  • Expired medication administration: Potency may be reduced; use fresh if available, but expired is better than nothing for serious conditions
  • Incomplete antibiotic course: Stopping early promotes resistance; complete full prescribed course
  • Drug interaction oversight: Check for interactions before administering new medication to patient on other drugs
  • Dosing calculation errors: High-risk for pediatric patients; double-check weight-based calculations
  • Wrong route administration: IV medication given IM or vice versa; verify route before administration
