From Stone Age to Information Age

A complete civilization-building sequence. Inspired by Dr. Stone's roadmap to civilization.

What to build. In what order. What depends on what.

Welcome to the Compendium Roadmap

This page is the master guide. It shows you the complete sequence of technologies humanity needs to redevelop, from the first day to modern times. Each technology is connected to the others‚Äîsome require previous discoveries, some enable the next generation.

The core principle: You cannot build a steam engine without metallurgy. You cannot have metallurgy without charcoal. You cannot smelt metal without pottery (for crucibles). Every step matters.

Use this roadmap to navigate the offline compendium. Each technology links to detailed guides. Follow the dependencies. Build in order.

Era 0: Immediate Survival

Day 1 ‚Äì 30

The first month. You have nothing but knowledge and your hands. Your goal: stay alive. No tools. No shelter. Just knowledge.

Core Technologies:
‚Ä¢ Fire (ignition, maintenance, heat control)
‚Ä¢ Shelter (natural caves, lean-tos, thermal insulation)
‚Ä¢ Water (sourcing, purification, collection)
‚Ä¢ Friction tools (digging sticks, pressure tools)
‚Ä¢ Basic traps and snares (twine from plant fiber)
‚Ä¢ Emergency food (edible plants, insects, basic hunting)
What You Need: Nothing. Only knowledge of survival techniques. This is where the compendium begins.
‚Üí Survival Basics

Critical success factor: Fire. Everything else depends on it. You must master fire in the first 3 days or you will not survive to Era 1.

Era 3: Bronze Age

Year 2 ‚Äì 5

The first true metallurgical age. Copper and tin create bronze‚Äîstronger than stone, more valuable than gold.

Core Technologies:
‚Ä¢ Charcoal kilns (controlled high-temperature burns)
‚Ä¢ Ore identification (malachite, copper, tin sources)
‚Ä¢ Copper smelting (ore + charcoal + heat)
‚Ä¢ Tin smelting (cassiterite reduction)
‚Ä¢ Bronze alloys (90% copper, 10% tin ratio)
‚Ä¢ Metal casting (molds, pouring, cooling)
‚Ä¢ Smithing basics (shaping hot metal)
Critical Dependency:
Pottery (crucibles) + Charcoal + Ore + Heat = Metal

More detailed: You need pottery crucibles to hold molten metal. You need charcoal hot enough to smelt (1100¬∞C+). You need ore deposits. You need a furnace that reaches and maintains this temperature.

Era 4: Iron Age

Year 3 ‚Äì 7

Bronze is good. Iron is everywhere. The challenge: iron requires hotter furnaces and better technology to work with it.

Core Technologies:
‚Ä¢ High-temperature furnaces (bloomery, shaft furnace)
‚Ä¢ Bellows (forced air, temperature control)
‚Ä¢ Iron smelting (ore + charcoal, 1200¬∞C+)
‚Ä¢ Steel production (iron + carbon control)
‚Ä¢ Blacksmithing (hammering, quenching, tempering)
‚Ä¢ Advanced tools (axes, plows, chisels)
‚Ä¢ Tool standardization
Prerequisite: Bronze Age technology. You need bronze tools to build iron furnaces. You need blacksmiths who understand metallurgy from working with bronze.

Temperature barrier: Iron smelting requires 1200¬∞C+. This requires: good furnace design, bellows to force air, and sustained heat. A bloomery furnace is the practical approach.

Iron Age Progression

‚Üí Advanced Metalworking ‚Üí Bloomery Furnace ‚Üí Blacksmithing
Historical note: Iron has higher melting point than copper (1538¬∞C vs 1085¬∞C), but lower melting point than smelting temperature. This creates iron "blooms"‚Äîspongy iron that must be hammered repeatedly to remove slag. Labor-intensive but possible.

‚ö° Critical Dependency Chains

These are the five most important sequences in the roadmap. They unlock other technologies and must be completed in order. Failure or delay in any one cascades through the entire civilization.

1. The Foundation Chain: Fire ‚Üí Metal ‚Üí Tools

FIRE (Day 1) ‚Üí Charcoal (Month 1) ‚Üí Pottery Kilns (Month 3) ‚Üí Smelting (Year 2) ‚Üí Bronze (Year 3) ‚Üí Iron (Year 4) ‚Üí Steel (Year 5) ‚Üí Everything else

Explanation: Fire is the foundation of all technology. It creates charcoal for fuel. Charcoal fires pottery, which makes crucibles for smelting. Smelting creates metals. Metals create tools. Tools build everything else. This sequence cannot be skipped or rushed.

2. The Knowledge Chain: Clay ‚Üí Paper ‚Üí Printing ‚Üí Literacy ‚Üí Innovation

Clay (Month 1) ‚Üí Pottery (Month 3) ‚Üí Paper (Year 8) ‚Üí Printing Press (Year 10) ‚Üí Literacy spreads (Year 12) ‚Üí Scientific revolution (Year 15)

Explanation: Pottery teaches you how to work with clay and fire. Paper production follows the same principles. The printing press multiplies knowledge. Literacy accelerates innovation exponentially. Without this chain, progress stalls after Year 10.

3. The Medicine Chain: Glass ‚Üí Lenses ‚Üí Microscope ‚Üí Germ Theory ‚Üí Medicine

Sand (available) ‚Üí Glass furnace (Year 8) ‚Üí Lens grinding (Year 10) ‚Üí Microscope assembly (Year 12) ‚Üí Bacteria observation (Year 14) ‚Üí Germ theory (Year 16) ‚Üí Antiseptics, antibiotics (Year 20+)

Explanation: Glass is common but requires high temperature furnaces. Lenses require precision glass grinding. Microscopes require mathematics and optics. Once you observe bacteria, germ theory follows naturally. This unlocks medicine, reducing mortality by 60%. This is the single biggest quality-of-life improvement possible.

4. The Power Chain: Magnets + Copper ‚Üí Generator ‚Üí Electricity ‚Üí Everything Modern

Copper wire (Year 5) + Magnets (Year 4, from iron) ‚Üí Generator (Year 30) ‚Üí Electricity distribution (Year 35) ‚Üí Motors (Year 38) ‚Üí Electronics (Year 45) ‚Üí Computing (Year 60)

Explanation: Electricity is the universal power carrier. One generator can replace 100 workers. It enables radio, motors, refrigeration, and eventually computing. This chain takes longest but has highest payoff. Modern civilization is impossible without this.

5. The Extraction Chain: Mining ‚Üí Refining ‚Üí Industrial Scale Production

Gunpowder (Year 12) ‚Üí Mining at scale (Year 15) ‚Üí Ore refining (Year 18) ‚Üí Industrial production (Year 25) ‚Üí Railroad transport (Year 30) ‚Üí Global supply chains (Year 40)

Explanation: Gunpowder enables precision mining. Mining at scale extracts ore faster. Refining converts raw ore to usable metal. Industrial production means you can make 1000 items per day instead of 1 per day. This drives everything from Year 15 onward.

Population Milestones

Each technology milestone unlocks the ability to support more people. Here's what you can achieve at each population level:

10 People

Year 0: Immediate survival. One person gathering, one hunting, one making fire. Everyone else on rotating shifts. No specialization.

Skills needed: Fire, water, shelter, basic hunting.

50 People

Year 1-2: Stone Age villages. Specialization begins: 10% crafters, 20% hunters, 70% gathering/farming. pottery makers emerge.

Skills needed: Tool crafting, pottery, basic agriculture.

200 People

Year 2-4: Agricultural settlements. 30% farmers, 20% crafters, 10% hunters. First specialists: potters, weavers, metalworkers learning bronze.

Skills needed: Organized farming, metallurgy basics.

1,000 People

Year 4-8: Bronze Age city-state. 50% agriculture, 30% crafts, 10% defense. Specialized roles: smiths, priesthoods, merchants, soldiers.

Skills needed: Iron tools, complex organization, trade networks.

5,000 People

Year 8-15: Medieval town. 40% agriculture, 40% crafts/trade, 10% defense, 10% clergy/administration. Water mills emerge‚Äî30% less farm labor needed.

Skills needed: Water wheels, advanced construction, glass making.

50,000+ People

Year 20+: Industrial city. 10% agriculture, 60% manufacturing, 15% service/trade, 10% administration. Steam power: 1 factory worker = 50 farm workers.

Skills needed: Steam engines, steel production, electricity, printing.

Key insight: Population growth is logarithmic, not linear. You can feed 10x more people with 3x more technology. By Year 15, one settlement can feed more people than 100 stone-age villages. This is where civilization scales.

Climate-Specific Roadmaps

The master roadmap assumes temperate climates. But your location changes everything. Here are region-specific variants that account for local advantages, constraints, and seasonal rhythms. The core technology sequence stays the same‚Äîbut priorities shift based on what nature provides and what nature demands.

Tropical/Equatorial Climates

Defining traits: Year-round growing season, abundant water (but humidity kills stored goods), high disease burden (malaria, dengue, parasites), fast decomposition of materials, no heating needed.

Unique Advantages:
‚Ä¢ Continuous crop production (two growing seasons per year in many regions)
‚Ä¢ Bamboo as primary building/tool material (grows faster than wood, incredibly strong)
‚Ä¢ Natural rubber from rubber trees (available, not requiring synthesis)
‚Ä¢ Fish and aquatic protein year-round
‚Ä¢ Fruit and nut abundance (coconut, mango, etc.)
‚Ä¢ Natural fibers (jute, hemp, sisal)
Unique Challenges:
‚Ä¢ Disease pressure is extreme (malaria, dengue, parasites, fungi)
‚Ä¢ Monsoon seasons bring flooding, soil erosion, and agricultural disruption
‚Ä¢ Metal and tools rust/corrode rapidly in humidity
‚Ä¢ Food preservation is hardest here (fermentation and drying work, but mold is aggressive)
‚Ä¢ Leather rots; need alternatives for waterproofing
‚Ä¢ High crop failure risk if monsoon timing shifts
Priority Sequence for Tropical Settings:

1. Disease prevention (Year 0-5): Malaria netting and dengue management must be Priority #1. Build elevated housing to avoid mosquitoes. Start germ theory work earlier if possible.

2. Food preservation (Year 1-3): Master fermentation (salt pickling, koji, tempeh). Smoking works but is labor-intensive. Salt preservation is critical.

3. Monsoon agriculture (Year 2-5): Rice cultivation for flooded paddies. Develop terraced mountain farms to redirect water rather than fighting it.

4. Bamboo engineering (Year 3-8): Don't skip to iron tools immediately. Bamboo is better than iron for many tropical applications‚Äîit doesn't corrode, it's lighter, it's naturally available.

5. Waterproofing (Year 4-10): Natural latex from rubber trees, palm oil products, wax treatments. These are more reliable than imported materials.
Key insight: Tropical regions can skip some phases of the roadmap. You don't need extensive food storage (continuous harvest). You don't need heavy winter clothing (cooling matters more). You should accelerate germ theory and disease prevention. Expect to reach similar population levels 15-20% faster due to continuous agriculture, but disease mortality may offset this unless medicine advances quickly.

Temperate Climates (Europe, Eastern US, East Asia)

Defining traits: Four distinct seasons, moderate precipitation, rich forest biomes, soil recovery periods, winter dormancy forces planning.

Unique Advantages:
‚Ä¢ Abundant timber and building materials
‚Ä¢ Natural iron ore deposits (often in soil and shallow deposits)
‚Ä¢ Cold storage for winter (preservation without active technology)
‚Ä¢ Distinct seasons train planning and foresight
‚Ä¢ Animal furs naturally available (beaver, fox, rabbit)
‚Ä¢ Leather stays stable and ages well
‚Ä¢ Spring water sources are dependable
‚Ä¢ Low disease pressure (plague cycles, but manageable)
Unique Challenges:
‚Ä¢ Winter requires fuel (heating), food storage, and animal care
‚Ä¢ Growing season is only 5-7 months
‚Ä¢ Spring flooding can destroy crops and villages
‚Ä¢ Freezing cycles damage roads, buildings, and infrastructure
‚Ä¢ Shorter daylight in winter reduces work productivity
Priority Sequence for Temperate Settings:

1. Winter preparation (Year 0-2): Food storage, firewood cutting, shelter insulation are non-negotiable. Build root cellars for stable storage.

2. Four-field crop rotation (Year 2-4): Don't use three-field rotation‚Äîgo straight to four-field: grain, legumes, other crops, fallow. This works better in temperate climates and regenerates soil faster.

3. Animal husbandry for winter (Year 2-4): Cattle, sheep, pigs. They eat summer crops and stored hay. They provide winter protein.

4. Wool and leather industries (Year 3-7): Sheep for wool (winter clothing), cattle hides for leather (waterproofing, tools).

5. Root cellar technology (Year 2-3): Underground storage keeps food at constant cool temperature. This is more valuable than any technology in temperate zones.
Key insight: Temperate zones mature slower but more stably. You have time to think, plan, and iterate. The roadmap's timeline is calibrated for temperate zones‚Äîexpect to track it closely. Winter planning teaches systems thinking. By Year 15, temperate settlements often exceed tropical ones in output due to superior infrastructure and planning culture.

Arid/Desert Climates

Defining traits: Water is THE constraint, abundant solar energy, thermal variations (hot days, cold nights), minimal disease pressure, low vegetation but hardy crops survive.

Unique Advantages:
‚Ä¢ Abundant sun (unlimited fuel for heating/drying)
‚Ä¢ Minimal disease pressure (dry kills microbes)
‚Ä¢ Date palms provide food, fuel, and materials year-round
‚Ä¢ Natural salt available (evaporation ponds)
‚Ä¢ Adobe/rammed earth construction is ideal (thermally massive)
‚Ä¢ Stone abundant for building
‚Ä¢ Clear skies enable astronomy and timekeeping
‚Ä¢ Natural evaporative cooling works well
Unique Challenges:
‚Ä¢ Water is limiting‚Äîirrigation is essential
‚Ä¢ Aquifer depletion is a long-term risk
‚Ä¢ Dust storms damage crops and equipment
‚Ä¢ Soil salinity increases with irrigation (requires management)
‚Ä¢ Limited biological materials (fewer trees)
‚Ä¢ Extreme temperature swings stress materials
Priority Sequence for Desert Settings:

1. Irrigation technology (Year 0-3): Channels, dams, wells, qanat systems (underground tunnels). Without this, settlement is impossible. Prioritize water engineering over metallurgy if necessary.

2. Drought-resistant crops (Year 1-2): Sorghum, millet, barley, chickpeas‚Äînot wheat. These survive with half the water.

3. Date palm cultivation (Year 1-3): Date palms need minimal water once established. Provide calories, fiber, wood, and shade.

4. Adobe/rammed earth construction (Year 2-4): Thermal mass building keeps interior cool during hot days, warm during cold nights. Preferable to stone in deserts.

5. Salt preservation and mining (Year 3-5): Evaporation ponds produce salt. This preserves food and enables trade with other regions.
Key insight: Desert settlements are fragile if water fails. Technology advances slowly until irrigation stabilizes. But once stable, deserts can be incredibly productive (Nile valley, Middle East, North Africa). The roadmap timeline stretches: expect Year 3 technology by Year 5-6 if water is uncertain. Prioritize geological surveys to find aquifers.

Arctic/Subarctic Climates

Defining traits: Extremely short growing season (6-8 weeks), hunting/fishing primary food, permafrost and frozen ground, long winter darkness, human morale is critical.

Unique Advantages:
‚Ä¢ Freezing is free preservation (food frozen, stays good for months)
‚Ä¢ Animal abundance (whales, seals, fish, game)
‚Ä¢ Fur and blubber available (insulation and caloric density)
‚Ä¢ Minimal disease pressure (cold kills pathogens)
‚Ä¢ Mineral deposits often accessible (bedrock exposed)
‚Ä¢ Ice as construction material (igloos, ice architecture)
‚Ä¢ Natural ice roads in winter (transport is free)
Unique Challenges:
‚Ä¢ Agriculture is impossible (growing season too short)
‚Ä¢ Hunting provides 90%+ of calories (supply is variable)
‚Ä¢ Darkness for 3-6 months affects morale and productivity
‚Ä¢ Extreme cold breaks materials (wood becomes brittle)
‚Ä¢ Metal tools require special handling (warmth makes them brittle in cold)
‚Ä¢ Nutrition is harder (vitamin C from organ meats and pine needle tea)
‚Ä¢ Isolation is extreme (trade networks are seasonal)
Priority Sequence for Arctic Settings:

1. Hunting and fishing (Year 0-ongoing): This is not a Year 1 tech‚Äîit's baseline survival. Master whale and seal hunting first. Fish traps. Trap game.

2. Dog sled transport (Year 1-2): Dogs are crucial. They hunt, they travel, they provide warmth. Invest in breeding and training immediately.

3. Sod house construction (Year 1-2): Not metal or wood‚Äîsod and turf. Whale bones as frames. This is better insulation than any Arctic alternative.

4. Food preservation by freezing (Year 0-1): Trivial in Arctic. Use it. Build ice cellars. Store everything frozen.

5. Vitamin C sources (Year 0-1): Organ meats contain vitamin C (don't cook them to death). Pine needle tea is backup. Prevent scurvy obsessively.

6. Morale technology (Year 2-10): Art, music, storytelling, social gatherings. Long darkness breaks minds. Community is survival.
Key insight: Arctic settlements have fundamentally different constraints. Agriculture phase doesn't happen. Jump straight from hunting/gathering to permanent settlements (Year 2-3 in temperate = permanent settlement here from Year 1). Expect technology advancement to be slower overall, but hunting pressure drives innovation in weapons and boats faster. Population is lower but denser (concentrated hunting grounds). By Year 30, a single Arctic settlement may have fewer people than a temperate town, but also requires far fewer resources.

Coastal Regions

Defining traits: Fish and shellfish as primary protein, salt extraction effortless, maritime trade routes, storm and tsunami risk.

Unique Advantages:
‚Ä¢ Fish protein abundant and reliable (less variable than hunting)
‚Ä¢ Salt extraction trivial (evaporation ponds, easy trade item)
‚Ä¢ Boat-building becomes primary technology (wood, sealskin, reeds)
‚Ä¢ Tidal power potential (tidal mills work well)
‚Ä¢ Seaweed as food and fertilizer (iodine, nutrients)
‚Ä¢ Shellfish beds provide long-term protein security
‚Ä¢ Coral and shell provide lime for mortar (important for construction)
‚Ä¢ Natural harbors encourage trade
Unique Challenges:
‚Ä¢ Storms damage boats and settlements
‚Ä¢ Tsunamis and storm surge are unpredictable
‚Ä¢ Ocean storms trap ships (seasonal navigation only)
‚Ä¢ Overfishing depletes stocks if not managed
‚Ä¢ Salt and humidity corrode tools and buildings
‚Ä¢ Disease risks from crowded ports and trade
Priority Sequence for Coastal Settings:

1. Boat building (Year 0-2): Better boats ‚Üí better fishing ‚Üí better trade. This is your highway. Master wooden boat construction, then later sailing.

2. Fish preservation (Year 1-2): Salting and smoking. Dried fish trades globally and keeps for months.

3. Navigation (Year 2-10): Stars, currents, landmarks. Build coastal charts. Enable longer voyages and trade routes.

4. Storm-resistant architecture (Year 2-5): Elevated foundations, storm cellars, reinforced roofs. Normal buildings blow away in hurricanes.

5. Salt production (Year 1-3): Evaporation ponds or salt pans. Trade salt inland (it's valuable and stable).
Key insight: Coastal settlements can skip agriculture entirely and trade fish for grain. Population scales on trade volume, not local production. Boat technology becomes as important as metallurgy. By Year 10, a coastal settlement may be wealthier than an inland one of the same size due to trade. But vulnerability to storms means boom-bust cycles.

Mountain/Highland Regions

Defining traits: Altitude affects cooking and weather, isolation enforces self-sufficiency, water abundance from streams, terrace farming possible, mineral deposits nearby.

Unique Advantages:
‚Ä¢ Terraced agriculture supports population on steep slopes
‚Ä¢ Water from mountain streams (abundant, reliable, clean)
‚Ä¢ Hydropower potential (streams drive mills)
‚Ä¢ Mineral deposits often nearby (ore, stone, gems)
‚Ä¢ Timber abundant
‚Ä¢ Natural fortification (difficult to attack)
‚Ä¢ Animal husbandry (goats, sheep thrive on steep terrain)
‚Ä¢ Cool climate year-round (easier food preservation)
Unique Challenges:
‚Ä¢ Altitude affects boiling point (cooking takes longer, preservation is different)
‚Ä¢ Winter snow can isolate for months
‚Ä¢ Terrain makes transport hard (no roads for carts)
‚Ä¢ Avalanches and landslides risk
‚Ä¢ Soil erosion on slopes
‚Ä¢ Settlement is spread out (hard to form large cities)
‚Ä¢ Trade with lowlands requires passes (seasonal)
Priority Sequence for Mountain Settings:

1. Terracing (Year 0-3): Not optional. Build terraces immediately. They're labor-intensive but they're your foundation for agriculture.

2. Livestock for mountain (Year 1-3): Goats and sheep. Cattle work on lower slopes. These animals thrive where crops struggle.

3. Water engineering (Year 1-4): Channels to divert streams to terraces. Hydropower mills (Year 4-6).

4. Winter survival (Year 2-4): Food storage, hay storage, animal shelter. Mountain winters are harsh.

5. Mining (Year 3-8): Locate and mine iron, copper, tin. Mountains often have deposits. This enables metallurgy faster than lowlands.
Key insight: Mountain settlements are inherently small and isolated. But they're also naturally defended and resource-rich. Expect slower technology advance (isolation), but also expect better-quality products (time, attention) and specialized crafts. By Year 20, a mountain settlement may be poorer than lowland equivalents but more self-sufficient and more technically skilled.

Seasonal Priority Calendar

Don't treat every day the same. Nature has rhythms. Work with them, not against them. Here's what you should focus on each season, universally applicable across climates (with local adjustments).

Seasonal Work Wheel (Temperate & Tropical)

Spring (Vernal Equinox ‚Üí Summer Solstice):
‚Ä¢ Planting season‚Äîall hands to fields
‚Ä¢ Repair tools before hard work begins
‚Ä¢ Animals birth (calves, lambs, piglets)‚Äîprotect them
‚Ä¢ Repair buildings and structures (easier before summer heat)
‚Ä¢ Build/improve irrigation before growing season
‚Ä¢ Gather spring plants (wild greens, herbs)
Summer (Summer Solstice ‚Üí Autumnal Equinox):
‚Ä¢ Maintaining crops‚Äîconstant weeding and care
‚Ä¢ Early harvests (berries, early vegetables)
‚Ä¢ Construction of buildings/mills (long daylight hours)
‚Ä¢ Preserving early harvest (drying, fermenting, smoking)
‚Ä¢ Animal care (shearing sheep, managing herds)
‚Ä¢ Long-term projects (mining, road building, infrastructure)

‚Ä¢ Main harvest‚Äîeverything must come in
‚Ä¢ Intensive food preservation (massive effort)
‚Ä¢ Firewood cutting and stacking
‚Ä¢ Winterizing buildings (insulation, repairs)
‚Ä¢ Preparing animals for winter (feeding, shelter)
‚Ä¢ Root cellar stocking

‚Ä¢ Tool making and maintenance (crafts indoors)
‚Ä¢ Textile work (spinning, weaving, sewing)
‚Ä¢ Education and knowledge work (reading, apprenticeships)

‚Ä¢ Storytelling and community building
Optimization insight: High-intensity labor (harvest, construction) happens when your environment supports it. Low-intensity skilled labor (crafting, design) happens during constraint periods (winter, wet seasons). This is how pre-industrial societies scaled. They weren't inefficient‚Äîthey optimized for human capacity and environmental rhythm.

If you're in the Southern Hemisphere, the roadmap still applies. But seasonal timing flips. Here are the key adjustments.

Seasonal Flip:
‚Ä¢ Spring happens in September (not March)
‚Ä¢ Summer is December-February (not June-August)
‚Ä¢ Autumn is March-May
‚Ä¢ Winter is June-August (COLD if you're south of 40¬∞S)
‚Ä¢ This affects all planting/harvest schedules. Update your calendar.
Navigation Changes:
‚Ä¢ Polaris is invisible. You cannot navigate by the North Star.
‚Ä¢ Southern Cross (Crux) is your guide. Four bright stars form a cross. The long axis points toward the South Celestial Pole.
‚Ä¢ Altitude of Southern Cross tells you latitude. Use it like Polaris in the North.
‚Ä¢ Trade winds blow east-to-west differently (east coast vs. west coast have opposite patterns)
Agricultural Differences:
‚Ä¢ Winter wheat planted in April/May (before Southern winter)
‚Ä¢ Corn/maize planted in September/October (Southern spring)
‚Ä¢ Cooler growing season than Northern Hemisphere equivalents
‚Ä¢ If you're below 40¬∞S, growing season is similar to temperate zones but shorter
‚Ä¢ If you're above 40¬∞S, you have tropical/subtropical growing conditions year-round
Adjustment timeline: Take the main roadmap. Shift all dates by 6 months. Everything else (technology sequence, dependencies, population milestones) is identical. The only real difference is that you cannot use Polaris for navigation‚Äîuse the Southern Cross instead.

First Year Timeline by Climate

Year 1 is critical. You establish patterns that echo for decades. Here's a detailed month-by-month breakdown for temperate and tropical climates. Use this as a template for your climate.

Temperate Climate (Europe, Eastern North America, East Asia) First Year

Month 1 (Spring, March-April in Northern Hemisphere):
‚Ä¢ Days are getting longer. Plant early crops (peas, beans, radishes).
‚Ä¢ Build/repair tools and shelter before the rush.
‚Ä¢ Scout for good farmland‚Äîhigh ground, water access, south-facing slopes.
‚Ä¢ Master fire maintenance (keep hearth alive during damp season).

‚Ä¢ Gather spring plants (nettle, sorrel, wild onions).

‚Ä¢ Herd any domesticated animals into fenced areas.
‚Ä¢ Create 4-5 stone tools variants (blades, scrapers, axes).

‚Ä¢ Weed fields constantly (this month is pure labor).
‚Ä¢ Fire pottery kiln multiple times. Accept failures. You're learning.

‚Ä¢ Harvesting early crops (late peas, early beans, greens).

‚Ä¢ Grain crops ripen. Prepare harvest tools.

‚Ä¢ MAIN HARVEST. Everything comes in now.

‚Ä¢ Winter preparation: insulate shelter, patch roof, stock food.

‚Ä¢ Weaving from prepared fiber (slow work, no rush).

This is not luxury‚Äîit's survival. If you miss the harvest window, people starve. If you don't prepare shelter, people freeze. The entire year is a sprint toward November.

Tropical Climate (Southeast Asia, Congo Basin, Amazon) First Year

Month 1-3 (Dry Season, varies by region):
‚Ä¢ Plant crops: rice in paddies, root vegetables (yam, taro, cassava).
‚Ä¢ Build shelter elevated above ground (mosquito prevention and monsoon prep).
‚Ä¢ Scout water sources and irrigation sites.
‚Ä¢ Fish heavily (dry season = good catches). Smoke and salt heavily.
‚Ä¢ Gather fruit (coconut, mango, papaya depending on season).
‚Ä¢ Experiment with pottery‚Äîhumidity is lower, kiln fires better.
Month 4-6 (Monsoon/Early Wet Season):
‚Ä¢ Monsoon rains arrive‚Äîflooding is normal.
‚Ä¢ Rice paddies flood intentionally. Manage water levels.
‚Ä¢ Build raised platforms and elevated storage (flood preparation).
‚Ä¢ Create bamboo structures (faster to harvest than wood).
‚Ä¢ Preserve food aggressively against mold/rot.
‚Ä¢ Hunt before wet season makes travel hard.
‚Ä¢ Maintain pottery production despite humidity.
Month 7-9 (Full Wet Season/Height of Monsoon):
‚Ä¢ Wet season farming: weed paddies, protect crops from rot.
‚Ä¢ Fish from flooded areas (abundance).
‚Ä¢ Fermentation work: miso, tempeh, kimchi (wet season preservation).
‚Ä¢ Textile work indoors (wet is better for fiber).
‚Ä¢ Build/improve structure (repairs from last monsoon).
‚Ä¢ Disease pressure is highest‚Äîmanage malaria prevention (elevation, mosquito netting).
Month 10 (Monsoon Tail/Early Dry):
‚Ä¢ Rains slow down. Early harvest of first rice crop possible.
‚Ä¢ Begin drying and sun-preservation of stored food.
‚Ä¢ Plant second crop in paddies (double-cropping is possible).
‚Ä¢ Repair structures damaged in monsoon.
‚Ä¢ Gather wild fruit (mango peak, coconut harvest).
Month 11-12 (Dry Season Return):
‚Ä¢ Harvest second crop and store heavily.
‚Ä¢ Gather dried goods before next monsoon.
‚Ä¢ Preserve food: smoking, salting, drying (take advantage of low humidity).
‚Ä¢ Hunt in dry season (easier movement).
‚Ä¢ Plan irrigation for next dry season planting.
‚Ä¢ Tool making during dry weather.
Tropical Year 1 Summary: By month 12, you have: two harvests if lucky, continuous fishing, preserved foods (fermented and dried), mosquito-resistant shelter, bamboo structures, pottery production established. You don't face winter starvation risk, but you face rot and disease risk. Preservation is more about fighting mold than fighting cold. Year 2 is about doubling crops and establishing year-round fishing/gathering.
