Fiber Sources & Identification

Cordage quality depends on fiber source. Different plants and animals provide fibers with varying properties: strength, flexibility, weather resistance, and durability.

Plant Fibers:

FlaxLong staple, smooth, lustrousVery HighRope, cloth, fishing line
HempCoarse, weather-resistant, strongHighMarine rope, binding

Palm fibersStiff, fibrous, weather-resistantMediumRope, matting, baskets

Animal Fibers:

  • Hair (horse, cow, deer): Coarse but strong; excellent for rough cordage and brushes
  • Intestine: Natural tubes useful for containers; can be twisted into cordage
  • Sinew (tendon): Extremely strong; primary cordage for bows and fishing line

Fiber Extraction Techniques

Water Retting (Softening Plant Fibers):

  1. Harvest plant stems in morning (before heat reduces sap)
  2. Bundle stems and submerge in pond/stream water
  3. Soak 1-2 weeks (until fibers separate easily from woody core)
  4. Remove from water; spread on ground for drying (2-3 days)
  5. Break dried stems to expose fibers
  6. Beat softly with wooden mallet to separate fibers
  7. Comb with crude comb (remove short fibers, wood fragments)

Dry Retting (Alternative):

Bundle plants; expose to sun/rain for 2-3 weeks. Weather breaks down pectin naturally. Slower than water retting but requires less water.

Bark Processing:

  1. Harvest bark from softwood tree (birch, willow, cedar) in spring (sap facilitates peeling)
  2. Score bark vertically; peel away from trunk
  3. Separate inner bark from outer bark (inner bark contains fibers)
  4. Soak inner bark 1-2 days; beat to separate fibers
  5. Dry and prepare for spinning

Sinew Extraction:

  1. Remove large tendons from animal legs/spine immediately after slaughter
  2. Dry sinew fully (2-4 weeks) before processing
  3. Beat dried sinew to separate into fibers
  4. Soak briefly to restore flexibility
  5. Twist fibers together to create cordage

Fiber Preparation & Drying

Cleaning:

Remove plant matter, dirt, and debris from extracted fibers. Soak in water, then beat gently with wooden mallet. Repeat until water runs clear. Dry thoroughly before spinning (moisture prevents twisting).

Sorting by Length:

Long fibers (combed out) are "line fibers"—suitable for strong cordage. Short fibers remaining are "tow"—used for coarser cordage or padding. Separate by carding (combing action) with two wire brushes.

Storage:

Keep dried fibers in cool, dry location protected from insects. Bundle fibers in handfuls; wrap loosely to prevent mold. Use within 1-2 years of drying (fibers deteriorate gradually).

Spinning Techniques

Simple Hand Spinning (No Equipment):

  1. Roll prepared fibers between palms, twisting as you roll
  2. As yarn builds, extend hands apart to create length
  3. Fibers naturally align and bind together during twisting
  4. Build yarn gradually (0.5-1 meter per minute pace)
  5. Weak spots appear as necks in the yarn; add extra twist when necks appear

Drop Spindle Spinning:

Spindle (wooden shaft with whorl for momentum) hangs by thread while twisting fibers. Preparation: loop fibers into hanging thread. Work: hold fibers at top of spindle; allow spindle weight to twist fibers while gradually extending arm to create yarn. Efficiency: 2-4 meters per minute. Superior to hand spinning for consistent results.

Spinning Wheel:

Mechanical device creating continuous twist. Treadle-powered wheel ideal for survival situations (no electricity). Consistency: much faster and more uniform than spindle (10-20 meters per minute possible). Requires wheel construction or salvage, but investment pays dividends in production speed.

Yarn Properties:

  • Ply: Twist angle affects strength. Tight twist = stronger but stiffer; loose twist = more elastic but weaker
  • Weight: Thinner yarn requires more twist; thicker yarn requires less twist
  • Smoothness: Consistent twist produces uniform appearance; variable twist looks coarse

Twisting & Cabling

Two-Ply Twist:

  1. Spin two separate yarns
  2. Hold ends together
  3. Twist both yarns together in opposite direction to their individual twist
  4. Result: balanced yarn with doubled strength

Three-Ply or Higher:

Twist multiple yarns together for even greater strength. Four-ply typical for rope-weight cordage. Twist direction: alternate direction from previous ply for mechanical binding.

Cabling (Creating Large Rope):

  1. Prepare multiple 3-4 ply cords
  2. Bundle 2-4 cords together
  3. Twist bundle in opposite direction to cord twist
  4. Creates rope with excellent strength and flexibility
  5. Typical rope: 3 cords x 4-ply each = very strong cordage

Rope Making Methods

Three-Strand Rope (Most Common):

  1. Prepare 3 equal-length cords (minimum 1.5m for functional rope 50cm long)
  2. Bundle cords together at one end; tie temporary knot
  3. Separate into 3 strands; hold with one hand
  4. Twist strands in opposite direction while walking backward with other hand
  5. Each step increases rope length and tightness
  6. Complete approximately 100 twists per meter of rope
  7. Secure end with binding (prevents unraveling)

Four-Strand Rope:

Superior strength with same diameter compared to three-strand. Construction identical except 4 strands instead of 3. Slightly slower to braid but more durable result.

Testing Rope Quality:

Bend rope into circle—should return to straight form. Rope that remains bent lacks adequate twist. Pull rope with force—should not slip or unravel. Properly made rope withstands 80-90% of component fiber strength.

Tip: Create trial ropes from readily available materials (cattail fluff, dried grass) before using valuable fibers. Technique improves significantly over 10-20 practice ropes.

Net Construction

Simple Hand-Tied Net (Fishing or Trapping):

  1. Prepare cordage: 6-8 meters length, 3-5mm diameter
  2. Create frame: bend cordage into rectangle or triangle
  3. Tie cross-strings at 10-15cm intervals creating grid
  4. Tie diagonal strings creating diamond-shaped mesh (typical fish net opening: 2-3cm)
  5. Secure all intersections with square knots
  6. Complete net: 1-2 square meters size, 2-3 hours labor

Knot Mesh Method:

Alternative: create mesh by tying individual knots rather than weaving. Knots ensure mesh openings remain uniform. Typical knot: square knot between adjacent lines. More secure but slower than woven mesh.

Weighted Edges (Fish Nets):

Attach weights (stones wrapped in cordage, or bone/clay weights) to bottom edge. Weights keep net bottom submerged while floats (wood or cork) keep top above water. Fish entering net push it down, creating collapse that traps fish.

Fishing Line & Specialized Cordage

Fine Fishing Line from Sinew:

  1. Prepare sinew fibers (very thin, 1-2mm)
  2. Twist 3-4 fibers together lightly
  3. Result: extremely strong line (sufficient to land fish 2-5kg)
  4. Knot strength: use clinch knot or improved clinch knot
  5. Diameter: 1-2mm; invisible underwater

Hair/Intestine Fishing Line:

Horse hair bundled and twisted creates surprisingly strong fishing line (2-3kg test strength). Intestine (gut) from fish or animal dried and twisted creates very fine, strong line. Both materials less visible underwater than plant fiber cordage.

Specialized Knots for Fishing:

  • Palomar knot: Strong, simple, reliable for attaching lure/hook
  • Clinch knot: Excellent strength; requires 5-7 wraps
  • Surgeon's knot: Joins two lines with strength

Common Mistakes & Solutions

Mistake 1: Inadequate Fiber Preparation

Error: Spinning fibers that still contain woody material or dirt. Results: weak cordage that breaks during use, poor twist consistency.

Solution: Thoroughly clean and comb fibers before spinning. Remove short fibers and fragments. Allow proper drying (moisture content 10-15% optimal).

Mistake 2: Inconsistent Twist

Error: Varying twist rate while spinning. Some sections over-twisted, others under-twisted. Results: weak cordage that breaks at under-twisted sections.

Solution: Establish consistent pace and hand positions. Practice spinning on simple materials first. Rhythm is more important than speed.

Mistake 3: Insufficient Final Twist in Rope

Error: Twisting strands together only 50-70 times per meter. Rope lacks sufficient tightness to bind fibers. Results: fibers slip under load.

Solution: Twist at least 80-100 times per meter of rope. Rope should appear very tight and dense. Test-load rope to verify strength before trusting with critical tasks.

Mistake 4: Poor Knot Selection in Net

Error: Using simple overhand knots in fishing nets. Knots slip under load as fish struggles. Results: net unravels during use.

Solution: Use square knots, improved clinch knots, or other reliable knots. Test knot strength by pulling hard before completing entire net.
