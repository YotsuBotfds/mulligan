Pigment Chemistry and Sources

Pigments are insoluble powders that provide color. Unlike dyes (soluble), pigments remain suspended in medium (oil, water, gum). High-quality pigments are: (1) finely divided particles, (2) consistent color, (3) non-toxic, (4) resistant to light/heat/chemicals, (5) opaque (covers background).

Carbon Black Production

Carbon black (pure amorphous carbon) is most reliable, permanent black pigment available. Produced via incomplete combustion of organic materials.

Lamp Black Method

  1. Source: Oil, tallow, or pine resin burn in oxygen-limited environment
  2. Setup: Suspend flat dish/lamp above shallow tray. Lamp holds burning material (oil-soaked cloth, candle, pine splints)
  3. Collection: Carbon particles (soot) rise from flame, cool in air, settle in tray as fine black powder
  4. Duration: Run lamp continuously 4-8 hours to accumulate useful quantity (100-200g)
  5. Yield: ~10-20% of burned fuel becomes carbon black; remainder escapes as CO₂/smoke

Furnace Black Method (Larger Scale)

  1. Setup: Small furnace or kiln, draft control to restrict oxygen
  2. Fuel: Wood, coal dust, or oil spray into hot zone (700-1000°C)
  3. Combustion: Incomplete oxidation: 2C + O₂ → 2CO (incomplete), CO oxidizes slowly in cooler air: 2CO + O₂ → 2CO₂. Intermediate zone contains fine carbon particles
  4. Collection: Cyclone separator or settling chamber collects black powder. Gas exhaust through chimney
  5. Yield: 30-50% of input carbon becomes usable pigment

Properties

Carbon black particles 10-500nm diameter (extremely fine). High oil absorption capacity (used as extender in paints). Does not fade or change color. Water-resistant. Cost: essentially free if produced on-site; price historically determined by fuel cost, not material rarity.

Iron Gall Ink (Traditional)

Iron gall ink (iron gallate) was standard writing ink for 1000+ years. Still superior to modern inks for permanence on parchment/paper.

Chemistry

Tannic acid (from oak galls) + iron(II) sulfate → dark blue-black ferrous gallate complex. Reaction: Fe²⁺ + tannic acid → Fe-tannate (blue-black complex). On paper, iron oxidizes to Fe³⁺ (black ferric gallate), even more permanent.

Recipe (Traditional)

  1. Materials: Oak galls (wasp-induced growths on oak trees), iron(II) sulfate (ferrous sulfate, "copperas"), gum arabic (binder)
  2. Preparation: Crush 15g oak galls to fine powder. Dissolve 7g ferrous sulfate in 100mL water. Gum arabic 2g dissolved in 10mL warm water
  3. Mixing: Combine galls suspension + ferrous sulfate solution, allow reaction 24-48 hours (color darkens to blue-black). Filter through cloth to remove gall particles. Add gum solution to increase viscosity
  4. Result: ~100mL black ink, permanent to light and water after drying on paper

Historical Variations

Medieval recipes added: (1) vitriol (sulfuric acid, increased permanence), (2) vinegar (slowed oxidation, improved shelf life), (3) alcohol (preservation, improved flow). These variations improved ink stability but similar base chemistry.

Walnut Ink (Excellent Option)

Walnut hulls contain high tannin content (similar to oak galls). Produces permanent brown ink:

  1. Collect walnut hulls (outer husks), dry completely
  2. Ferment in water 6 months in sealed container (develops dark brown color from oxidation of tannins)
  3. Filter, add ferrous sulfate (5% by volume) to darken/stabilize
  4. Add gum arabic for viscosity
  5. Result: permanent brown-black ink, water-resistant, archival quality

Pigment Grinding and Preparation

Pigment particle size critically affects color intensity and application properties. Finer grinding = stronger color, better application.

Hand Grinding Method

  1. Materials: Mortar and pestle (ceramic, not wood which absorbs pigment). Pigment powder (50-200g typical batch size)
  2. Technique: Add pigment to mortar. Use pestle with circular/grinding motion, pressure maintained throughout. Grind 30-60 minutes for fine powder
  3. Duration: Longer grinding = finer particles = more saturated color. Can continue 2-4 hours for very fine powder
  4. Transfer: Once finished, transfer to container. Pigment is now ready for mixing with binder

Mill Grinding (Larger Scale)

Ball mill: ceramic cylinder filled partially with pigment and ceramic/steel balls. Rotating cylinder causes balls to tumble, grinding pigment to extremely fine particles (1-10 microns). Run time: 8-24 hours produces very fine, uniform pigment. Cost: building/operating mill higher than hand method, worthwhile for production scale.

Particle Sizing

Crude assessment: mix tiny amount pigment with water, observe color: (1) coarse (>10 microns) = pale/light color when diluted, (2) medium (1-10 microns) = moderate color, (3) fine (<1 micron) = intense, saturated color. Fine grinding preferred for inks (less pigment needed).

Gum Arabic Solution (Standard)

  1. Dissolve 5-10g gum arabic powder in 100mL water
  2. Heat gently (not boiling) until dissolved, 30-60 minutes
  3. Cool completely, filter through cloth to remove particles
  4. Use at 5-10% concentration in final ink (dilute with water as needed)

Gum arabic dries slowly (hours to days) but produces crisp, waterproof writing on paper. High permanence - gum does not degrade over centuries.

Linseed Oil Ink (Printing)

  1. Heat linseed oil to 120-150°C (careful - oil flammable). Goal: partial oxidation (drying oil formation)
  2. Cool to room temperature
  3. Mix with pigment (30% pigment, 70% oil by weight)
  4. Grind thoroughly in mortar to uniform paste

Dries by oxidation (weeks) and becomes hard film. Superior for printing (adheres well to metal/stone rollers). Toxic fumes during heating - work outdoors.

Pen and Printing Applications

Writing Ink (Gum-Based)

Mix carbon black or iron gall ink powder with gum arabic solution at 1-3% pigment concentration. Strain through fine cloth. Consistency should be thin (flows through pen) but dark (gives rich color). Test on scrap paper - if flows too slow, dilute with water; if too pale, add more pigment.

Calligraphic Ink

Same as above but with special attention to flow properties. Egg glair (egg white) historically used in medieval manuscripts (Cennino Cennini's 14th-century recipe) because it prevented feathering on parchment (ink bleeding into fibers). Recipe: mix 1 egg white with 1g gum arabic, 1g sugar, 5g carbon black. Age 24 hours before use.

Printing Ink (Linseed Oil-Based)

Linseed oil + pigment mixture applied to woodblock/metal plate via rollers, paper pressed on inked surface. Requires proper ink consistency (thick paste), proper paper pressure (uniform contact), and patience (multi-day drying). Traditional woodblock printing method.

Stamp Ink (Quick-Drying)

Problem: gum-based inks dry too slowly for stamping. Solution: use alcohol-soluble shellac or acetone-based solvents. Recipe: dissolve 5g shellac flakes in 50mL alcohol, add carbon black (2-5%) and gum (1-2%) as thickener. Dries in 1-2 minutes on paper.

Permanence and Archival Properties

Recording critical information requires permanence. Some inks fade or degrade within years; others last centuries.

Permanence Factors

Pigment: Carbon black = excellent (permanent). Iron gall = excellent (oxidizes to stable compound). Plant dyes = fair to good (some fade in light). Synthetic dyes = mixed (fugitive colors worst).

Binder: Gum arabic = excellent (stable centuries). Linseed oil = excellent (hardens, resists water). Gelatin = good (slight yellowing over 100+ years). Hide glue = good (stiff, inflexible but permanent).

Paper/substrate: Acid-free paper = essential for permanence. Acidic paper (most modern paper) causes ink to fade and paper to yellow/become brittle over 50-100 years. Historical rag paper (cotton/linen fiber) = excellent permanence if kept dry.

Archival-Quality Ink

Iron gall ink on acid-free rag paper = 500+ year permanence (proven by medieval manuscripts still readable). Carbon black + gum arabic on acid-free paper = similar permanence. Plant dyes fade faster (100-300 year range depending on plant/conditions). Avoid all synthetic dyes if permanence critical (fade in 20-50 years even on archival paper).

Storage and Preservation

Ink Storage

  • Containers: Glass bottles preferred (stains plastic, especially gum-based inks). Ceramic also acceptable
  • Sealing: Tight-sealing caps prevent evaporation. Gum inks dry thick if exposed to air - seal immediately after use
  • Temperature: 10-25°C optimal. Avoid freezing (gum separates) and heat >30°C (promotes degradation)
  • Light: Keep away from direct sunlight (some plant dye inks fade from UV). Opaque containers preferred

Shelf life: gum-based inks 3-5 years if stored properly (gum hardens over time, losing fluidity). Oil-based inks years to decades (oxidation eventually hardens them completely). Iron gall ink improves with age (becomes darker as iron oxidizes) - historical practice was aging inks before use.

Written Documents Storage

  • Environment: 15-25°C, 30-50% humidity. Fluctuating temperature/humidity causes paper expansion/contraction, stress to ink/fiber
  • Light: Keep out of direct sunlight (fades inks, yellows paper). Low-level ambient light acceptable
  • Acid: Store in acid-free environment (acid-free folders/boxes). Lignin in wood pulp paper and cardboard is acidic - avoid direct contact
  • Pests: Insects/fungi damage paper and inks. Keep dry, inspect regularly

Properly stored archival documents last centuries. Iron gall ink documents from 1400s-1700s still perfectly readable. Acid-free paper expected to last 300-500 years minimum.
