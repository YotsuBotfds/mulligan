Livestock Selection

Choosing the right animals for your homestead depends on available land, water, climate, and your primary goals (meat, dairy, fiber, labor, or manure production). Each animal has specific requirements and advantages.

Poultry Management

Chicken Breeds: Eggs vs. Meat

Egg Layers: Leghorn (white, 300+ eggs/year), Rhode Island Red (brown, 250-300 eggs), Sussex (spotted, good dual-purpose), Andalusian (blue-green eggs).

Meat Breeds: Cornish Cross (fast growth, 8-10 weeks to butcher), Brahma (large, heritage), Cochin (broody, good mothers).

Dual-Purpose: Wyandotte, Orpington, Plymouth Rock (reliable layers, decent meat quality).

Coop Design

A well-designed coop provides protection from predators, adequate ventilation, roosting space, nesting boxes, and run access for foraging.

Feeding & Watering

  • Layer Feed: 16-18% protein (commercial pellets or grain mix) - 0.25 lbs/bird/day
  • Supplemental Grains: Cracked corn, oats, barley (10-15% of diet)
  • Kitchen Scraps: Vegetable trimmings, fruit, cooked grains (10-20% of diet)
  • Free Range: 2-3 hours daily provides insects, greens, grit (reduces feed costs 20-30%)
  • Water: 0.25-0.5 lbs per bird daily; change daily to prevent disease

Egg Collection & Storage

Collect eggs daily to prevent brooding and reduce breakage. Store in cool (55-65¬∞F), humid location. Don't wash eggs if they'll be stored long-term (protective bloom is removed). Shelf life: 3-4 weeks unwashed, 2-3 weeks washed refrigerated.

Brooding & Hatching

Broody Hen Method: Hens naturally incubate 10-12 eggs for 21 days. Provide quiet, safe nest. Humidity 40-50%. Turn eggs daily (hen does automatically).
Artificial Incubation: Incubator maintains 99.5¬∞F (forced air) or 101-102¬∞F (still air). Turn eggs 5-7 times daily for 18 days. Stop turning last 3 days (lockdown). Humidity: 40-50% days 1-18, 75% days 19-21.

Butchering Basics

  1. Select bird at 12-16 weeks old for meat breeds (roasting) or spent layers (stewing)
  2. Withhold feed 8-12 hours before processing to clean intestines
  3. Bleed-out: Stun with blow to head; sever carotid arteries. Allow 1-2 minutes full drain
  4. Scald: 140-142¬∞F for 30 seconds (removes feathers easier)
  5. Pluck: Remove feathers quickly while warm
  6. Eviscerate: Remove organs cleanly; save liver, heart, gizzard if not damaged
  7. Cool: Chill in ice bath immediately. Rest 12-24 hours before cooking

Goat Husbandry

Dairy vs. Meat Breeds

Dairy: Alpine (high production, 6 lbs milk/day), Saanen (white, mild milk), Nubian (high butterfat, 4 lbs/day), LaMancha (distinctive ear placement, 6 lbs/day).

Meat: Boer (muscular build, 4-6 month butcher), Spanish (hardy, forage efficient), Fainting goats (Myotonic, unique traits).

Housing & Fencing

Goats are expert escapists. Provide secure fencing (minimum 4.5-5 feet tall), shelter from wind and precipitation (3-4 sq ft per goat), and adequate space (200-300 sq ft per animal).

Goat Fact: A bored goat will climb on everything, escape through impossible gaps, and generally cause chaos. Provide enrichment: varied terrain, climbing structures, and foraging opportunities.

Feeding

  • Browse (Preferred): Trees, shrubs, brush (goats love these!). Browse is nutritionally superior to grass
  • Hay: 1.5-2 lbs per 100 lbs body weight daily (quality varies; legume hay preferred)
  • Grain: 0.5-1 lb per 100 lbs body weight (dairy goats need more)
  • Minerals: Loose mineral mix (not salt blocks) - critical for reproduction and health

Milking Schedule & Technique

Dairy goats are milked 1-2 times daily (twice daily produces more milk). Establish consistent schedule. Milk after kids are weaned (8-10 weeks).

  1. Restrain goat on milk stand (feeds goat as reward)
  2. Clean udder with warm water, dry thoroughly
  3. Pre-dip teat with iodine or sanitizing solution
  4. Milk by hand or machine (5-10 minutes)
  5. Post-dip teat immediately after milking
  6. Cool milk to 50¬∞F or below within 1 hour
  7. Store in food-grade containers; consume within 10 days or freeze

Breeding & Kidding

Breeding Season: Fall (September-November in Northern Hemisphere). Gestation: 145-155 days (typically ~150). Does reach puberty at 4-6 months; better to breed at 7-8 months of age (breeding at 150 lbs). Bucks are seasonal breeders.

Kidding (Birthing): Spring delivery (January-March). Most deliveries happen within 30 minutes. Single kids vs. multiple kids is breed/doe dependent. Kids stand and nurse within 1-2 hours. Leave dam and kids together 24-48 hours minimum.

Cattle Management

Dairy vs. Beef Basics

Dairy: Holstein (black/white, 60+ lbs milk/day), Jersey (small, rich milk), Guernsey (golden milk), Brown Swiss (early maturity). 305-day lactation cycle.

Beef: Angus (marbled meat), Hereford (hardy, feed efficient), Brahman (heat tolerant, large frame).

Pasture Requirements & Rotational Grazing

Space: Minimum 2 acres per head (rough guideline; varies by forage quality, season, rainfall). Good pasture provides 80-90% of cow's forage needs in season.

Rotational Grazing: Move cattle to fresh paddocks every 3-7 days to prevent overgrazing, reduce parasites, and improve pasture health. Rest paddocks 30-60 days before regrazing.

Milking (Dairy Cattle)

  • Establish strict 2x/day milking schedule (12 hours apart)
  • Milk using machine or by hand (hand-milking 4-8 lbs/day is possible but labor-intensive)
  • Pre-dip teats; strip foremilk to check for abnormalities
  • Post-dip teats with iodine solution
  • Cool milk to 40¬∞F immediately; store in stainless containers
  • Test milk composition: fat %, protein %, somatic cell count

Calving & Care

Heifers first calve at 24-30 months. Gestation: 283 days. Watch for signs of labor: relaxed ligaments around tail, udder filling, mucus discharge. Most births occur 30-60 minutes from first labor signs. Calf should stand within 1 hour and nurse within 2-3 hours.

Colostrum (first milk) is critical - provides antibodies and energy. Calf needs 4 quarts (first 24 hours), 2 quarts twice daily for 3-4 days.

Draft Oxen

Oxen (castrated males) are superior to horses for plowing and heavy hauling in rough terrain. Bovine strength exceeds horse pound-for-pound. More forgiving than horses, long working life (20+ years). Require less feed than horses. Slower than horses (2-3 mph vs. 6 mph), but exceptional pulling power.

Pig Farming

Pen Design

Pigs require 50 sq ft indoor space per animal, 400+ sq ft outdoor pen if available. Strong fencing (pigs can push/root under), shelter from extremes, wallows for thermoregulation. Deep bedding (straw, leaves) for rooting and insulation.

Feeding (Omnivore Diet)

Pigs are omnivores and excellent recyclers. Feed requires 3-4% body weight daily.

  • Commercial Feed: Grower (16% protein) or finisher (14% protein) pellets
  • Kitchen Waste: Vegetable scraps, grain, dairy, cooked meat (never raw meat)
  • Forage: Pigs root for tubers, grubs, roots - provide paddock access
  • Seasonal Bounty: Acorns, chestnuts, windfall fruit, garden waste
  • Water: Constant access to clean water (1-3 gallons daily)

Breeding Cycle

Gestation: 3 months, 3 weeks, 3 days (114 days exactly - hence the saying). Farrowing: 10-12 piglets average. Sows farrow twice yearly.

Puberty: Gilts (young females) reach breeding age at 5-6 months. Boars at 6-7 months. Breed when gilts reach 250-300 lbs for best results.

Farrowing (Birthing)

Provide quiet farrowing house with bedded nest area. Piglets arrive over 2-4 hours. Most arrive head-first in amniotic sac. Piglets nurse within 30 minutes of birth. Colostrum critical (2-3 ounces per piglet within 4 hours).

Piglet Mortality Prevention: Maintain warmth (85-90¬∞F for newborn piglets with heat lamp), ensure colostrum intake, prevent crushing (piglets huddle under heat, away from sow's body weight), monitor for weak piglets, practice early intervention with bottle feeding if needed.

Processing/Butchering

Pigs are typically butchered at 250-280 lbs (6-7 months old). Stunning (electrical or percussion), exsanguination (bleeding), scalding (140¬∞F, 1-2 minutes), scraping hair, evisceration, and cutting into primals. Yields ~65-75% usable meat. Fat renders well for lard. Bones make excellent stock. Hide/skin has uses in tanning.

Sheep Husbandry

Wool vs. Meat Breeds

Wool: Merino (fine fiber, 12-16 microns), Corriedale (medium wool, dual-purpose), Romney (longer staple, wet-resistant), Icelandic (double coated, heritage).

Meat: Dorper (meat quality, minimal wool), Hampshire (muscular, fast growth), Southdown (fine meat, small frame).

Dual-Purpose: Corriedale, Polypay, Targhee (wool + meat + milk potential).

Shearing Schedule

Sheep are shorn annually (spring before warm season), typically March-May in Northern Hemisphere. Unshorn sheep suffer in heat, develop parasites in wet conditions, and reduce productivity. Shearing day is labor-intensive but essential. Average fleece weight: 8-15 lbs (varies by breed and condition).

Lambing Season

Breeding: Fall (September-November); gestation 145-147 days. Lambs born spring (late winter/early spring). Single lambs typical in young ewes; twins/triplets in mature ewes.

Lambing: Most ewes deliver unassisted over 30-60 minutes. Assist if presentation abnormal (breech, leg back). Lamb stands within 1 hour, nurses within 2-3 hours. Colostrum critical (first milk). Lambs weaned at 8-12 weeks.

Pasture Management

Sheep require 0.5-1 acre per head (varies by forage quality). Rotational grazing prevents parasites. Sheep excel at brush/forage management (convert vegetation to meat/wool). Move flock every 5-10 days to fresh pasture. Rest paddocks 30+ days.

Foot Rot Prevention

Foot Rot: Bacterial infection affecting hoof and skin. Causes lameness. Prevention: good drainage (avoid swampy pasture), regular foot inspection, footbaths with copper sulfate or formalin, trim feet regularly.

Treatment: Isolate affected sheep, trim hoof to remove infected horn, treat with antibiotic foot dip or spray. Cull chronically infected sheep. Prevents spread through flock.

Beekeeping

Langstroth Hive Design

Bee Biology & Life Cycle

  • Queen: Lays eggs; colony backbone. Lives 3-5 years. Lays ~2000 eggs/day during active season
  • Worker (Female): Sterile females perform all tasks: nursing, cleaning, guarding, foraging. Live 6 weeks (summer) or 4-6 months (winter)
  • Drone (Male): Mate with queen. 20-40 per hive. Expendable; evicted in fall to conserve resources
  • Brood Cycle: Egg ‚Üí larva (5 days) ‚Üí pupa (12 days) = 21 days to worker bee, 24 days to drone

Swarming

Swarming is hive reproduction. When overcrowded or resources abundant, bees rear new queens and half the colony (15,000-20,000 bees) leaves with old queen. Swarm clusters on branch; can be captured in box. Prevents natural swarming through: regular inspections, replacing old combs, ensuring adequate space, culling drone comb.

Honey Harvesting

Extract honey when super is 75%+ capped. Use bee escape (one-way door) to clear super 24 hours before harvest. Uncap cells with hot knife, extract using centrifuge, strain, and bottle. Honey crystallizes over time (normal) - reheat gently to liquify. 1-2 supers per hive yields 30-60 lbs honey/year (varies by region and weather).

Wax Processing

Render old brood comb and cappings into beeswax. Melt in double boiler, strain through cloth, cool in water bath. Beeswax uses: candles, cosmetics, wood finish, food wrapping. 1 pound beeswax per 10 pounds honey (approximate ratio).

Varroa Mites

Varroa mites parasitize bee brood and adults. High infestations cause Colony Collapse Disorder (CCD). Treatment: powdered sugar dusting, hive access strips, alcohol wash for monitoring, or miticide treatments. Check mite load: alcohol wash (sample 300 bees) - more than 3 mites per 100 bees warrants treatment.

Draft Animals: Horses, Mules, Oxen

Harnessing Overview

Proper harnessing is essential for both animal welfare and work efficiency. Well-fitted harness distributes load safely and allows maximum power transmission.

Horses for Draft

Large draft breeds (2000+ lbs): Percheron, Shire, Belgian, Clydesdale. Excel at steady power but slow (2-4 mph). Capable of 1500-2000 lbs pull in plow or 3000+ lbs haul. Calm temperament, long working life (20+ years). Require grain supplement beyond pasture, shoeing every 6-8 weeks.

Mules

Hybrid (donkey sire, horse dam). Stronger than horses proportionally, more sure-footed, hardier. Live longer (30+ years). Superior mountain/rough terrain workers. More independent (stubborn) temperament. Require less feed than horses of equivalent size.

Oxen

Castrated male cattle. Superior pulling power in rough conditions, patient, long-lived, forgiving. Slower than horses. Exceptional for stump pulling, forest work, plowing. Modern resurgence due to low feed requirements and sustainability.

Plowing with Draft Animals

A yoke or harness attaches animal to plow. Adjustable depth (usually 6-8 inches for most soils). Work in straight lines for best results. Walk behind plow, guide with handles. Speed varies: oxen 2-3 mph, horses 4-5 mph. One animal plows ~1 acre/day (8 hours). Maintain animal's condition with adequate rest and water.

Hoof Care

Clean hooves daily (remove stones, mud). Trim feet every 6-8 weeks to prevent overgrowth and soreness. Shoes protect working animals: nail shoes on, not glued. Examine hooves frequently for cracks, thrush (fungal infection), or abscess signs.

Feeding Requirements

  • Pasture: Good quality pasture provides primary nutrition (1.5-2% body weight daily)
  • Hay: Winter or when pasture unavailable (1.5-2.5% body weight)
  • Grain: Heavy work requires supplementation (0.5-1 lb per 100 lbs body weight per hour of work)
  • Water: 5-10 gallons daily minimum (more when working)

Training Basics

  1. Start young (2-3 years old for horses, slightly younger for oxen)
  2. Halter-break: lead on rope with clear commands
  3. Plow training: use trained animal as example; work in pairs when possible
  4. Commands: standardized calls (Gee=right, Haw=left, Whoa=stop)
  5. Consistency: use same person initially; establish trust
  6. Patience: takes weeks to months; never rush progress

Working Dogs

Dogs have worked alongside humans for 15,000+ years. In post-collapse scenarios, dogs provide guarding, herding, hunting, and draft capabilities. Different breeds excel at different tasks. Selection, training, and care of working dogs ensures reliable farm/homestead partners.

Guard Dogs

Purpose: Protect property and livestock from predators and intruders. Alert residents to danger.

Suitable Breeds

  • Livestock guardian breeds: Great Pyrenees, Anatolian Shepherd, Tibetan Mastiff, Maremma. Large, fearless, independent. Bred to live with livestock and defend them from predators (wolves, bears). Excellent at this task.
  • Personal protection: German Shepherd, Doberman, Rottweiler. More handler-bonded than livestock guardians. Aggressive if provoked but controllable by owner. Good for home/property defense.
  • Multi-purpose farm guard: Mixed breeds often superior to purebreds. Local farm dogs have proven ability to handle local predators and climate.

Training Basics

  • Socialization: Expose to people/animals as puppy (8-16 weeks). Aggressive dogs that haven't socialized are dangerous and uncontrollable.
  • Territory marking: Dogs naturally patrol and mark territory with urine. Allow dog to roam fenced area unsupervised 2-3 hours daily. Presence and scent deter intruders.
  • Bonding with livestock: Introduce dog to farm animals (cattle, sheep, goats) as young dog. Dog accepts animals as "herd" to protect rather than prey.
  • Alarm barking: Encourage barking at strangers/unusual sounds. Guard dog should alert; excessive barking can be trained down. Consistent response to "quiet" command prevents noise issues.
  • Handler control: Guard dog must respond to owner commands ("come," "stay," "stop"). Daily training 15-30 minutes prevents aggression spiraling out of control.

Herding Dogs

Purpose: Move, control, and manage livestock (cattle, sheep, goats). Reduce labor needed for livestock management.

Suitable Breeds

  • Cattle herding: Border Collie, Australian Cattle Dog, Heeler. Intense focus, quick reflexes. Nip heels to move cattle (instinctive behavior).
  • Sheep herding: Border Collie, Australian Shepherd, Old English Sheepdog. Responsive to subtle commands. Gentle control‚Äîsheep easily injured by overly aggressive herding.
  • Goat herding: Similar to sheep breeds. Spanish dogs (Mast√≠n, Perro de Presa) also effective.

Training: Basic Commands

  • "Come": Return to handler immediately. Taught with rewards (food, praise). Essential for safety and control.
  • "Lie down": Dog drops to ground and stays until released. Stops herding action. Critical for preventing injuries to livestock.
  • "Away": Move outward, circling livestock clockwise (or counter-clockwise). Different handler signals for different directions.
  • Whistle commands: Sharp whistle carries farther than voice in field. Different tones = different commands (one blast = stop, repeated = come, sharp = change direction).
  • Duration: Herding dogs naturally want to work. Training occurs 20-30 minutes daily on small stock. As dog understands commands, can work larger herds independently.

Hunting Dogs

Purpose: Locate, pursue, and retrieve game. Dramatically increase hunting success and range.

Hunting Styles & Breeds

  • Tracking (scent hounds): Bloodhound, Beagle. Follow scent trail to locate wounded or hidden prey. Excellent for large game (deer, boar).
  • Flushing (bird dogs): Spaniel, Pointer, Setter. Locate birds in cover and flush them for hunter's shot. Sprint-and-stop hunting style.
  • Retrieving: Labrador, Golden Retriever. Chase down shot prey and retrieve to handler (essential for water fowl, game birds).
  • Catch dogs: Pit bull, Boxer. Chase and hold prey (boar, large game) until hunter arrives. Dangerous work; not ideal for inexperienced handlers.

Training Hunting Dogs

  • Early exposure: Introduce puppies to hunting scenarios (gunfire sounds, tracking scents, water) before formal training (8-12 weeks).
  • Prey drive: Encourage natural instinct to chase, track, retrieve. Use toys, games, live prey exposure (under supervision).
  • "Steady" command: Dog waits calmly for signal before pursuing prey. Prevents premature flushing; allows hunter to be ready.
  • "Retrieve" training: Teach dog to bring prey/objects to handler. Essential for success; ineffective hunter without retrieval.
  • Field work: Gradually increase hunting difficulty. Start with simple scenarios; progress to complex terrain, multiple prey, difficult conditions.

Sled and Draft Dogs

Purpose: Pull sleds or carts, especially in snow/ice. Enable transportation in winter conditions without horses.

Suitable Breeds

  • Sled dogs: Siberian Husky, Alaskan Malamute, Samoyed. Bred for cold, snow, and sustained pulling. Thick coat, high endurance.
  • Draft dogs: Saint Bernard, Newfoundland. Larger, slower but stronger pull. Better for cart work than high-speed sled racing.

Harness Fitting and Team Management

  • Harness design: X-back or sled dog harness distributes load across shoulders and chest (not neck). Proper fit prevents injury and maximizes power transmission.
  • Measurements: Girth around chest, length from shoulder to hip, neck circumference. Harness too loose slips; too tight restricts breathing.
  • Team formation: Pair dogs with compatible temperaments and similar strength. Lead dogs (smartest, most responsive) positioned front. Wheel dogs (strongest) at rear near sled.
  • Commands: "Gee" = right turn, "Haw" = left turn, "Whoa" = stop, "On-By" = pass another team. Consistent verbal/whistle commands critical.
  • Training: Start young dogs (6-12 months) with experienced team dog. Begin in pairs, gradually add to larger teams. Multi-year progression to reliable team.
  • Breaks: Even strong sled dogs tire. Stop every 45-60 minutes to rest, hydrate, cool (especially important in warm weather).

Feeding Working Dogs

Caloric needs higher than pet dogs: Working dogs require additional energy for labor.

  • Baseline (pet dog): 1-2% body weight daily in food energy.
  • Working dog: 2-4% body weight daily depending on work intensity. Herding dog = 2-2.5%; hunting dog = 2.5-3%; sled dog = 3-4%.
  • Composition: Higher protein (25-30%) to maintain muscle. Fat for energy (15-20%). Balance with whole grains.
  • Scraps & organ meat: Dogs thrive on animal byproducts (liver, heart, kidney, organs). More efficient than whole muscle meat. Boil with vegetables/grains for complete meal.
  • Bone broth: Simmer bones 12-24 hours. Creates rich broth; joint health benefits (collagen, minerals). Give 1-2 cups weekly.
  • Greens: Cook vegetable scraps (non-toxic: carrots, squash, green beans). Raw is acceptable if fresh. Aids digestion.
  • Frequency: Working dogs eat 1-2 times daily depending on work schedule. Split feeding (2x daily) is easier on digestion during heavy work.

Basic Veterinary Care for Working Dogs

Wound Care

  • Caused by: Animal bites (livestock, predators, other dogs), barbed wire, puncture wounds during herding/hunting.
  • Assessment: Clean wound with water. Examine depth‚Äîpuncture wounds easily become infected (bacteria-filled). Bleeding wounds need pressure to stop flow.
  • Treatment: Cleanse with saline/clean water. Remove hair around wound (easier to keep clean). Apply antibiotic ointment if available. Cover if in dirty environment (loose bandage; allow air circulation).
  • Infection signs: Swelling, discharge (pus), foul smell, heat around wound, fever. Herbal treatment (plantain, turmeric) if antibiotics unavailable. Systemic infection (fever, lethargy) requires serious intervention.

Parasite Control

  • Internal parasites: Worms (roundworms, tapeworms, hookworms). Signs: diarrhea, weight loss, distended belly, lethargy. Treat with dewormer (fenbendazole, pyrantel) every 6-8 weeks during active work season.
  • External parasites: Fleas, ticks, mites. Cause itching, skin infections, anemia (if heavy infestation). Remove ticks manually. Treat with herbal options (diatomaceous earth, neem oil) or commercial flea treatment.
  • Prevention: Regular grooming (removes ticks/fleas early). Keep bedding clean and dry. Avoid contaminated water sources when possible.

Distemper and Disease Signs

  • Canine distemper: Viral infection. Signs: fever, lethargy, discharge from nose/eyes, cough, diarrhea, neurological signs (seizures). Fatal if untreated. Vaccination in healthy dogs; isolation if infection suspected.
  • Parvovirus: Severe gastrointestinal infection, especially young dogs. Bloody diarrhea, vomiting, dehydration. Supportive care (fluids, rest, bland food) critical. High mortality if untreated.
  • Rabies: Fatal once symptoms appear. Prevention: vaccination if available. Suspect rabies in any dog with behavior changes, excessive salivation, aggression, or paralysis. Euthanasia often only option.
  • Common cold/respiratory: Cough, nasal discharge, mild fever. Usually self-limiting. Keep dog warm, rested; provide supportive care. Antibiotics if secondary bacterial infection develops.

Breeding Working Dogs

  • Selection criteria: Choose dogs that excel at their task AND have good temperament. Aggression/unpredictability is dangerous trait (cull from breeding).
  • Genetic testing: If possible, avoid hereditary problems (hip dysplasia, eye disease). Mixed breeding with unrelated strong dogs reduces genetic issues.
  • Age/maturity: Females should be 18+ months old before first breeding. Males 12+ months. Too-young breeding stresses young dogs.
  • Performance records: Document dog's working ability, temperament, offspring performance. This enables selection for best traits.
  • Culling weak performers: Dogs that fail at their task or show dangerous traits should not breed. Focus resources on proven working animals.

Veterinary First Aid

Wound Care

  1. Clean: Rinse with clean water or saline solution. Remove debris, dead tissue
  2. Disinfect: Apply iodine-based or chlorhexidine solution (avoid hydrogen peroxide on deep wounds)
  3. Dry: Pat dry with clean cloth
  4. Treat: Apply antibiotic ointment or wound powder
  5. Bandage: Wrap if needed to prevent contamination (remove bandage in 24-48 hours to allow air flow)
  6. Monitor: Watch for swelling, heat, discharge indicating infection
Serious Wounds: Gaping wounds, puncture wounds, or those affecting tendons require veterinary care. Stitch large wounds within 12 hours for best healing.

Bloat Treatment

Bloat (ruminal tympany) occurs when ruminants cannot expel gas from stomach. Signs: distended left side, kicking at belly, distress, rapid breathing.

Emergency Treatment: Stomach tube (passed through mouth) relieves gas pressure. Slow passage - insert gently, evacuate gas gradually. If tubing unavailable, trocar (14-16 gauge needle) through left side flank punctures stomach in emergency only.

Prevention: Avoid sudden grain changes. Don't let hungry animals loose on fresh legume pasture. Provide hay first. Limit grain intake (never >1.5% body weight daily).

Hoof Trimming

Regular hoof care prevents lameness, infection, and deformity. Trim every 6-8 weeks (varies by growth rate and work).

  1. Restrain animal appropriately (stocks, halter, or lie down)
  2. Clean hoof thoroughly with hoof pick
  3. Trim sole dead tissue with hoof knife or rasp
  4. Level frog (V-shaped tissue) - remove diseased sections
  5. Balance hoof: trim so sole is level and frog is protected
  6. Leave adequate sole (hard) to protect foot

Setting Simple Fractures

Important: Complex fractures, leg fractures in large animals, or spinal injuries typically require euthanasia. Only simple, clean fractures are amenable to home treatment.

Leg Fractures (Small Animals): Clean and disinfect wound. Immobilize leg with splint (wood, PVC, or padded cardboard) extending above and below joint. Wrap firmly but not so tight as to cut off circulation. Monitor for swelling. Antibiotics for open fractures.

Recovery: 4-8 weeks depending on fracture type and location. Keep animal confined and calm.

Isolation of Sick Animals

  • Monitor closely for disease progression or improvement
  • Consult veterinarian for serious conditions

Basic Birthing Assistance

Normal Presentation: Front feet first, nose between feet (head down). Most animals deliver without intervention in 30-60 minutes.

Malpresentation Signs: No progress after 1 hour, excessive straining, protruding bladder, foul odor from birth canal.

  • Head Back: Head bent to side. Cup head in hand, gently flex neck forward. May need to reposition (call vet if stuck)

Breeding & Genetics Basics

Line Breeding vs. Crossbreeding

Line Breeding: Breeding related animals (closely but not directly) to concentrate desirable traits. Risk: inbreeding depression (reduced vigor, birth defects, disease susceptibility) if taken too far. Used to establish breed characteristics. Coefficient of inbreeding should stay under 10% ideally.

Crossbreeding: Breeding two different breeds or purebred lines. First generation (F1) shows hybrid vigor: stronger, faster growth, disease resistance. Examples: Angus √ó Hereford for beef cattle, Spanish goat √ó dairy goat for hardiness + production.

Selecting for Traits

  • Phenotype: What you see/measure (size, color, production level). Use phenotype for selection decisions
  • Genotype: Genetic makeup underlying phenotype. Can't see directly; must infer from relatives and offspring
  • Heritability: Proportion of variation due to genetics (vs. environment). High heritability traits: size, breed color. Low heritability: survival, disease resistance
  • Selection Intensity: How different selected animals are from flock average. Greater difference = faster genetic change
  • Selection Criteria: Choose few important traits (too many reduces progress). Examples: egg production and feed conversion in chickens; milk volume and butterfat in dairy goats

Breeding Records

Keep detailed records to track performance and make informed decisions:

  • Individual animal ID (ear tag, photo, tattoo)
  • Birth date, sire and dam
  • Production data (eggs, milk yield, weight gain)
  • Health history (diseases, veterinary treatments)
  • Reproduction data (breeding dates, conception rate, number born)
  • Progeny performance (offspring results)

Inbreeding Depression

When related animals breed, homozygosity increases (less genetic diversity). Negative effects: reduced size, lower fertility, increased disease, shortened lifespan, abnormalities. Manage by:

  • Limiting coefficient of inbreeding to <5% per generation
  • Rotating sires; don't use same male for many generations
  • Introducing new genetics (outcross) periodically
  • Culling animals carrying lethal recessive genes

Hybrid Vigor

F1 hybrids (first cross) show 5-15% improvement in traits like:

  • Growth rate
  • Feed efficiency
  • Disease resistance
  • Reproductive output
  • Longevity

Limitation: Vigor decreases in F2 and beyond (back to parental average). Most profitable to breed two purebred lines and use only F1 offspring (commercial strategy).

Pasture & Feed Management

Rotational Grazing System

Rotational Grazing Benefits

  • Prevents overgrazing; allows pasture recovery
  • Reduces parasite burden (animals avoid own feces)
  • Improves forage quality and plant diversity
  • Better manure distribution (reduces patchy fertilization)
  • Extends grazing season (more even growth across paddocks)
  • Increases stocking density (more animals per acre sustainably)

Hay Making

Timing: Cut when legumes are in bloom, grasses at boot stage (before seed set). Best quality from spring growth.

  1. Mow when moisture is lowest (morning dew dry, cool weather best)
  2. Tedding (turning): spread hay to dry, 1-2 days depending on weather
  3. Windrow: rake into rows for easier collection
  4. Baling: bale when moisture is 15-20% (too wet = mold, too dry = brittle)
  5. Storage: protect from rain/moisture; store under cover if possible

Quality Assessment: Leafy hay (more leaves = higher protein), green color (not brown/gray), fresh smell (not moldy), no dust (indicates quality loss).

Silage Basics

Silage is fermented forage, higher in moisture than hay (40-60%). Chop green forage, pack tightly in silo/pit/bag, seal to exclude air. Fermentation preserves feed and improves digestibility. Ready after 3-4 weeks fermentation.

Advantages: Harvest earlier (better quality), works in wet weather, nutrient retention higher. Disadvantage: Requires proper sealing; spoilage if exposed to air.

Grain Storage

Store grains in cool, dry location. Moisture below 13% prevents mold. Metal bins or wooden boxes with tight lids keep out rodents and moisture. Check regularly for signs of weevils or spoilage (off smell, discoloration). Food-grade oils mixed in at low levels deter rodents.

Calculating Feed Needs Per Animal Per Season

Example: One 500-lb sheep for winter (120 days):

  • Daily hay requirement: 2% body weight = 10 lbs/day
  • Grain supplement: 0.5 lbs/day (variable, depends on production level)
  • Total daily dry matter: 10.5 lbs
  • 120-day winter needs: 10.5 √ó 120 = 1,260 lbs hay + 60 lbs grain

Adjust based on: Body condition (poor = more feed needed), production (lactating/pregnant = more), weather (cold = more), forage quality (poor = more needed).

Processing Animal Products

Butchering Basics

Hanging and Aging: Carcass is hung for 7-14 days at 35-40¬∞F (cold room or cooler). Aging allows enzymes to break down connective tissue, tenderizing meat. Don't skip this step - improves meat quality significantly.

Meat Cuts (Beef Example)

Primal Cuts:

  • Chuck: Shoulder; ground or braising cuts (tougher, good for stew)
  • Rib: Premium steaks (rib eye, prime rib); expensive, tender
  • Loin: Highest quality steaks (T-bone, porterhouse, tenderloin)
  • Round: Leg; lean, tougher cuts; ground meat or roast
  • Brisket: Chest/breast; smoking/braising cuts (long slow cook)
  • Plate & Flank: Thin cuts; marinating improves tenderness

Rendering Fat (Making Lard)

Rendering Pig Fat to Lard

Process:

  1. Cut fresh fat into small cubes (1-2 inches)
  2. Heat in heavy pot on LOW heat (overcooking burns lard, turns it brown and bitter)
  3. Stir occasionally as fat melts. Temperature should stay below 250¬∞F
  4. Continue until solids (cracklings) sink and fat is clear (2-4 hours)
  5. Strain through cheesecloth into clean jars
  6. Cool at room temperature, then refrigerate
  7. Lard keeps 6+ months refrigerated, much longer frozen
  8. Cracklings (solids) are edible; use in baked goods or render further for high-quality lard

Making Sausage

Ratio: 75% lean meat, 25% fat (pork shoulder, beef chuck with pork fat added). Grind meat and fat together through fine die.

Seasoning: Salt (2%), pepper, herbs (garlic, thyme, sage). Chill everything before mixing. Mix well until sticky (proteins develop, bind ingredients). Stuff into casings using stuffer or horn.

Types: Fresh sausage (refrigerate, cook within 2-3 days), smoked sausage (hot-smoke or cold-smoke), cured sausage (dry cure, age weeks to months).

Tanning Hides (Cross-Reference to Textiles)

See Textiles & Natural Fibers section for detailed hide tanning methods. Brief overview: raw hide is salted/dried, then processed through liming (removal of hair), deliming, tanning (vegetable tannins, brain tanning, or chemical), and finishing. Results in supple leather.

Wool Processing (Cross-Reference to Textiles)

See Textiles & Natural Fibers section for detailed wool processing. Overview: fleece is skirted (remove dirty edges), washed (remove lanolin), carded (align fibers), spun into yarn. Can be dyed with natural dyes before or after spinning.

Offal & Organ Meat

Don't waste organ meat! High in nutrients.

  • Liver: Rich in iron, vitamin A; best fresh (saut√©, pan-fry, or grind for p√¢t√©)
  • Heart: Lean, flavorful; braise or grind
  • Kidneys: Distinctive flavor; braise or slow-cook
  • Tongue: Tender when cooked low-and-slow; excellent sliced
  • Bones: Make stock (collagen, minerals); simmer 12-24 hours

Cleanliness is Critical: Sanitize all equipment before and after use. Keep meat cold (below 40¬∞F). Work in clean environment. Avoid cross-contamination (separate cutting boards for meat). Cook to safe internal temperatures: 160¬∞F ground meat, 165¬∞F poultry, 145¬∞F beef/pork whole cuts.

Cattle Management

Breed Selection & Purpose

Beef Breeds:

  • Angus (Black Angus, Red Angus): Natural polled (no horns), muscular, excellent meat quality, adapt well to pasture and grass-fed systems. Black Angus is premium beef breed.
  • Hereford: Red body with white face; hardy, efficient feed converters, thrive on pasture. Excellent foragers in marginal land.
  • Charolais: Light-colored; fast-growing, lean meat, large frame. Good for crossbreeding programs.

Dairy Breeds:

  • Holstein: Black and white; highest milk volume (6-9 gallons/day), but lower butterfat. Dominant in commercial dairy.
  • Jersey: Small, light-brown; high butterfat (5.5%), golden-colored milk. Rich milk for cheese/butter. Smaller feed requirement (~3-4 gallons/day typical).
  • Guernsey: Fawn and white; high butterfat, golden milk. Better foragers than Jerseys.

Dual-Purpose Breeds:

  • Dexter: Smallest breed (700-900 lbs); red/black; meat and milk, perfect for homestead. Slow-growing, long lifespan (15+ years), low feed cost. Milk yield 2-4 gallons/day.
  • Red Poll: Red without horns; good meat and milk, hardy, thrifty foragers. Moderate milk yield (3-4 gallons/day).
  • Milking Devon: Reddish-brown; smaller frame, good milk (4-5 gallons/day) and lean meat.

Nutrition & Feeding

Daily Dry Matter Intake: Cattle consume 2-3% of body weight daily as dry matter. A 1,000 lb cow eats 20-30 lbs of dry matter per day (roughly 40-60 lbs fresh grass or hay with moisture).

Pasture vs. Stored Feeds:

  • Pasture: Best nutrition spring-fall; 2+ acres per cow minimum depending on climate and species mix. Rotate paddocks every 3-7 days to prevent parasite buildup and allow recovery. High-quality pasture reduces supplementation needs.
  • Hay: Winter feed; legume hay (alfalfa, clover) is richer than grass hay (timothy, orchard grass, brome). Good hay should be green, aromatic, fine stems. Poor-quality hay causes digestive upset and malnutrition. Dairy cows need legume hay; beef cattle can thrive on grass hay.
  • Silage: Fermented forage (corn silage, haylage); higher moisture, requires proper storage in silo or bags. More digestible than hay, higher energy. Transition cattle gradually to silage to avoid digestive upset.

Mineral & Salt: Cattle need free-choice salt block or loose salt (~1-2 oz/day). Macro minerals: calcium:phosphorus ratio ideally 2:1. Calcium from legume hay; phosphorus from grains. Micro minerals critical: copper, zinc, selenium, cobalt, iodine. Deficiency causes poor hoof quality, weak immunity, reproductive problems. Use mineral supplement or loose mineral mix (free-choice).

Grain/Concentrate: Lactating dairy cows need 1-2 lbs grain per gallon milk. Beef cattle fattening need 1.5-2% of body weight in grain daily (finishing ration). Introduce grain slowly to avoid rumen acidosis. Whole grains, processed grains (cracked, rolled), and protein supplements (soybean meal, distillers grain) are options.

Reproduction & Calving

Breeding Cycle: Cows cycle every 21 days; gestation is 283 days (~9.5 months). Heifers (first-time mothers) should be bred at 14-16 months old (calve at 24-26 months). Age at first calving affects lifetime productivity; too young causes dystocia (difficult birth).

Signs of Impending Labor:

  • Udder swelling and tightness 2-4 weeks before
  • Vulva relaxation and swelling 24 hours before
  • Tail head ligaments loosen; becomes soft/movable
  • Wax beads appear on teats 12-48 hours before (colostrum drying on teat ends)
  • Restlessness, isolation from herd, mucus discharge from vulva
  • Contractions visible: flanks sink, sides "hug" (1-2 hours before active pushing)

Normal Calving: Most cows calve unassisted if healthy. Active labor: cow lies down, strains, front feet and nose visible (anterior presentation). Second stage (pushing) takes 30 min‚Äì2 hours. Calf born, umbilical cord breaks; cow licks calf clean, stimulating breathing. Placenta passes within 3-8 hours (retained placenta if longer = infection risk).

Assisted Birth: If no progress in 2 hours of active labor, presentation is wrong (breech, sideways), or calf is too large, ASSIST. Wash perineal area; use clean chains or straps on calf's front legs at fetlock; pull at angle downward then toward cow's hocks in synchronized rhythm with her contractions. Excessive force causes trauma; call veterinarian if calf doesn't deliver with gentle traction in 30 minutes.

Colostrum (First Milk): CRITICAL in first 6 hours of life. Colostrum contains antibodies (IgG), growth factors, energy. Without colostrum, calf dies or becomes chronically ill. Calf must absorb colostrum within 6-12 hours (intestinal permeability closes after). Bottle-feed if cow won't nurse: 4-5 gallons colostrum, divided into 2-3 feedings in first 12 hours.

Milking Management (Dairy Cows)

Milking Frequency: Twice daily (12-hour intervals) is standard for dairy cows. More frequent milking (3x/day) increases yield 10-15% but increases labor. Cows accustomed to routine: same time daily or they withhold milk.

Let-Down Reflex: Oxytocin hormone triggered by stimulation (washing, suckling, machine). Reflex lasts 5-10 minutes. Wash udder/teats with warm water to stimulate; wait 30 seconds, then attach milker. Calm environment maximizes let-down; stress/fear inhibits it.

Teat Health & Mastitis Prevention:

  • Clean udder/teats before each milking (warm water, clean towel or spray).
  • Strip test (first 2-3 streams from each teat into a cup) checks for clots/discoloration indicating mastitis.
  • Dip teats in iodine solution after milking (kills bacteria on teat skin).
  • Dry teats thoroughly after milking to prevent bacteria colonization.
  • Maintain milking equipment sanitation: hot water rinse immediately after use, disassemble and scrub, sterilize daily.
  • Regular milking cluster removal (avoid over-milking) prevents teat damage.
  • Monitor for mastitis signs: heat/swelling of udder, hard quarters, discolored/lumpy milk. Treat promptly with antibiotics or natural remedies (see disease section).

Milk Yield by Breed: Jersey 3-5 gallons/day; Guernsey 4-6 gallons/day; Holstein 6-9+ gallons/day (high production is 10-12 gallons/day); Dexter 2-4 gallons/day; dual-purpose breeds 3-5 gallons/day. Peak yield occurs 6-8 weeks post-calving; declines gradually. Lactation cycle: ~305 days; then 60-day dry period (rest before next calf) to allow mammary tissue recovery and colostrum accumulation.

Surgical & Management Procedures

Castration: Critical for steers; prevents aggressive behavior, unwanted breeding, improves meat quality. Methods:

  • Banding: Elastrator band around scrotum (below testicles); cuts off blood supply. Testicles atrophy in 2-3 weeks and drop off. Painless initially; discomfort when band tightens (2-4 days). Cheapest method; no infection risk if hygienic.
  • Burdizzo (Bloodless Castrator): Crushes spermatic cords without cutting skin. Apply above each testicle for 10 seconds; blood vessels crush but skin intact. No infection risk, less bleeding than surgical. Requires proper technique.
  • Surgical Castration: Scalpel incision over testicle; testicle exteriorized and ligated or tied off; incision closed. Bleeding risk, infection risk, but complete removal. Done by vets; most common in older animals.
  • Timing: 2-4 weeks old (banding), 2-6 weeks old (Burdizzo), any age (surgical). Younger = faster healing, less stress.

Dehorning: Remove horns by 3-4 weeks old (hot iron cautery) or use caustic paste on horn buds. Older cattle require surgical dehorning (scoop dehorner with anesthetic). Horned cattle need wider space, are dangerous to handlers and other animals. Modern dairy breeding favors naturally polled breeds.

Ear Tagging & ID: Mandatory for tracking, health records, meat traceability. Tag in ear with unique number. Some jurisdictions require USDA eartags (for cattle movement). Record tag number with cow name/birthdate.

Sheep & Goat Husbandry

Sheep Breed Selection

Wool Breeds:

  • Merino: Fine wool (16-24 microns); 12-15 lbs fleece/year. Hardy, great mothers, efficient on poor pasture. Wool in high demand for premium textiles.
  • Corriedale: Dual-purpose (wool + meat); medium wool (24-30 microns), 10-12 lbs/year. Larger frame than Merino.

Meat Breeds:

  • Suffolk: Black head/legs, white body; fast-growing, muscular, excellent meat. Most common meat breed in North America.
  • Dorper: Brown/white; meat quality excellent, short glossy coat requires less maintenance. Hardy, good foragers. Heat-tolerant; excellent in southern climates.
  • Hampshire: Dark brown/black; large frame, rapid growth, high meat yield.

Hair Sheep (Primitive Breeds‚ÄîNo Shearing Needed):

  • Katahdin: American breed; hair coat, excellent mothers, high fertility, good foragers. No shearing needed; hair naturally sheds or can be plucked.
  • St. Croix: Caribbean heritage; fine hair, hardy, parasite resistance, maternal instinct strong. Excellent dual-purpose.

Goat Breed Selection

Dairy Breeds:

  • Nubian: Large ears, dished face, high butterfat (4-5%). Milk yield 2-4 gallons/day. Excellent for cheese, friendly temperament.
  • Saanen: White, refined; high milk yield (3-5 gallons/day), lower butterfat (3%). Best milk volume breed. Sensitive to cold.
  • LaMancha: Distinctive tiny ears; medium milk yield (2-3 gallons/day), good butterfat (3.8-4%). Friendly, long lactation. Excellent mothers.
  • Alpine: Medium-large; high yield (3-5 gallons/day), good butterfat. Hardy, active.

Meat Breeds:

  • Boer: South African origin; muscular, fast-growing, excellent meat quality. Less milk than dairy breeds but good mothers.
  • Spanish: Hardier than Boer, great foragers, lean meat. Thrive in harsh environments.

Fiber Breeds:

  • Angora: Produce mohair; soft, lustrous fiber (longer staple than sheep wool). Annual shearing yields 10-12 lbs mohair per goat.
  • Cashmere: Fine downy undercoat; harvested by combing (not shearing). 4-8 oz of raw fiber/year per goat; extremely valuable.

Lambing & Kidding

Breeding & Gestation: Ewes and does cycle seasonally (fall-winter for most breeds); gestation 150 days (~5 months). Multiples common: ewes average 1.5-2 lambs/year; does average 2-3 kids/year. Early breeding (lamb/kid at 4-5 months) extends reproductive life.

Jug Pens (Bonding Pens): Small pen (8x8 ft) for dam + offspring immediately after birth. Keeps family unit together, prevents lamb/kid from getting separated in large flock. After 3-7 days (bonding complete, dam/baby recognize each other), move to larger paddock. Prevents mis-mothering and orphaning.

Weak Lambs/Kids‚ÄîTube Feeding: If lamb/kid doesn't nurse within 2 hours or appears weak, assist with bottle feeding. Use clean feeding tube (not forcing milk into lungs). Warm colostrum or milk replacer to 104¬∞F. Feed 2-3 oz per feeding, 4-6 times daily initially. Esophageal feeder (tube passes through mouth, down esophagus to stomach) safer than bottle if inexperienced. Transition to bottle over days, then to creep-feeding (solid food available ad-lib).

Shearing & Fleece Handling

Timing: Spring (pre-lambing) is ideal; removes wool before heavy lactation (easier on dam, reduces heat stress). Some shear in fall if second cut is planned.

Methods:

  • Hand Shearing (blade shears): Slow but gentle; preserves fleece quality if done carefully. One person shears 4-6 sheep/day. Blades must be sharp.
  • Machine Shearing: Fast (1-2 min/sheep); electric or gasoline-powered. Requires skill to avoid cutting skin. Professional shearers charge $5-15 per head. One good shearer does 80-120 head/day.

Fleece Handling: Roll fleece (cut-side out) immediately after shearing to keep fibers aligned. Skirt (remove dirty/short fibers from edges). Store in clean, dry location. Avoid compression during storage (degrades fiber). Sell raw fleece to mills or processors, or clean/card/spin yourself for hand knitting/weaving.

Parasite Management (The #1 Killer)

Internal Parasites (Gastrointestinal Worms): Cause of 50%+ sheep/goat mortality/poor production. Symptoms: anemia (pale mucous membranes), diarrhea, weight loss, lethargy, swollen belly (bottle jaw). Prevention:

  • FAMACHA Scoring: Assess eyelid color (conjunctiva). Red = no anemia. Pink = mild. Pale pink = moderate. White = severe anemia (indicating high worm load). Score 3-4 times/year. Only treat animals scoring 4+ (white/very pale). Reduces unnecessary deworming, slows anthelmintic resistance.
  • Targeted Deworming: Treat only high-parasitized individuals, not whole herd. Reduces drug resistance. Use dewormers rotated annually (avermectins, benzimidazoles, levamisoles).
  • Rotational Grazing: Move animals to fresh pasture every 7-14 days. Larvae crawl up grass; moving herd before peak larvae exposure prevents reinfection. Rest pasture 4+ weeks between animal grazing.
  • Multi-Species Grazing: Rotate cattle then sheep then goats on same pasture. Different parasites; breaks lifecycle. Cattle eat grass parasites never shed spores for sheep.

External Parasites (Lice, Mites, Ticks): Mange, scab (contagious ecthyma in goats). Treat with pour-on insecticides or dips. Isolate affected animals to prevent spread.

Fencing

Sheep: 48-inch woven wire (no bottom gaps‚Äîlambs squeeze through). Bottom 6 inches should be closer spacing to prevent lamb escape. Electric fencing for rotational paddocks. Predator protection essential: guardian dogs, night shelter.

Goats (Notorious Escape Artists): 4.5-5 ft minimum height fencing. Woven wire or high-tensile electric preferred. Goats jump; climb; test every fence. Corner reinforcement critical. Goats also lean on/climb fencing; sturdy posts 4-6 ft apart essential. Electric fencing works if properly trained (touch fence as kid, learn respect). Top line 48", bottom line 12" high effective for most goats.

Swine Production

Breed Selection

Modern/Commercial Breeds:

  • Yorkshire (Large White): White, long body; bacon type (lean, long). Excellent mothers. Fast-growing; 250 lbs at 5.5 months.
  • Duroc: Red/reddish-brown; hardy, fast-growing, excellent mothers. Meat quality slightly fattier than Yorkshire (good for flavor). Feed efficient.

Heritage/Specialty Breeds:

  • Berkshire: Black with white points; excellent pork quality (marbling), slow-growing (250 lbs at 6-7 months), strong flavor prized by chefs. Expensive breeding stock.
  • Gloucestershire Old Spots: White with colored spots; excellent foragers, good meat quality, great mothers. Thrive on pasture.
  • Kunekune: Small (300-400 lbs at maturity), docile, easy to handle, efficient feed converters. Perfect for homestead. Excellent pork quality (marbling).

Housing & Environment

Shade is Critical: Pigs lack functional sweat glands; sunburn and heat stress kill pigs above 85-90¬∞F. Provide solid shade structures or wallow (shallow muddy area). If muddy area unavailable, wet wallows (spray system) critical in summer.

Space Requirements: Minimum 8 sq ft/pig indoor (more humane: 12-15 sq ft). Pigs in groups less than 8 sq ft fight/stress. Outdoor space: 500-1000 sq ft per pig on pasture (rotated). Overcrowding causes tail-biting, disease outbreaks.

Fencing: Strong hog panels (16 gauge minimum) or electric fencing. Pigs are strong; weak fencing = escape. Bury fence 12 inches or bend outward to prevent rooting under. Electric fence must be charged; pigs respect only live fence.

Shelter: 3-sided hut minimum per group of 4-6 pigs, bedded with straw/wood shavings. Winter shelter insulated. Summer shelter elevated for air flow. Feeders and water stations away from sleeping area (pigs naturally defecate away from bed).

Feeding & Nutrition

Omnivorous Diet: Pigs eat nearly anything: kitchen scraps, vegetable waste, grains, root vegetables, forage, pasture. No raw meat (trichinosis risk). No raw potatoes (solanine toxin). Green potatoes, moldy feed prohibited.

Feed Conversion Efficiency: Pigs convert 3.5 lbs feed to 1 lb gain (excellent compared to cattle at 7-8:1). Reduces cost per pound gain. Finishing pigs (150-250 lbs) need high-grain diet; grower pigs (80-150 lbs) moderate grain; piglets and sows thrive on forage + supplement.

Grain Ration: Corn 60-70%, soybean meal or fishmeal 10-15%, minerals/vitamins 2-3%, rest roughage or forage. Pigs on pasture need less grain (25-50% grain, rest pasture/forage). Finishing ration (last 4-6 weeks): high grain (80%+) to speed weight gain and marble meat.

Water: Critical; 2-3 gallons/pig/day minimum. Automatic waterers (nipple drinkers) prevent waste. Check daily in heat (pigs dehydrate quickly).

Breeding & Farrowing

Gestation: "3 months, 3 weeks, 3 days" = 114 days. Sows cycle every 21 days. Breed at 6-8 months old (first heat); farrow at 12-14 months (ideal size for first litter). Average litter: 8-14 piglets (Kunekune smaller litters, 4-8).

Farrowing Setup: Farrowing crate (metal enclosure, sow lies down without crushing piglets) or farrowing pen with guards (rails preventing sow from crushing piglets in corners). Piglets must be able to move freely. Heat lamp for piglets (90-95¬∞F), separate from sow area.

Piglet Survival: Iron injection (200 mg iron dextran IM) at 3 days old critical (prevent anemia); OR provide access to clean soil (naturally iron-rich, pigs root and consume). Without iron, piglets become pale, weak, die at 2-3 weeks.

Nursing: Sows nurse piglets 4-8 weeks (earlier weaning at 3-4 weeks increases productivity). Piglets eating solid food at 1-2 weeks (creep feeder). Colostrum essential first 24 hours; weak piglets benefit from tube feeding colostrum immediately after birth.

Butchering & Processing

Timing: Kunekune 6-8 months (300-400 lbs); heritage breeds 6-7 months (250 lbs); commercial breeds 5-5.5 months (220-250 lbs). Carcass yield ~70% (lard, organs removed).

Scalding & Scraping vs. Skinning:

  • Scalding/Scraping (Traditional): Hot water 145-150¬∞F for 30-60 seconds loosens hair. Scrape with scalper or blade to remove hair (easier after scalding). Retains skin; higher meat quality. Labor-intensive.
  • Skinning (Fast): Remove skin with knife; faster than scraping. Exposed meat dries out if not processed immediately; meat doesn't keep as well.

Rabbit Production

Breed Selection

Meat Standard:

  • New Zealand White/Red: 10-12 lbs; fast-growing (8-10 weeks to fryer size 5 lbs). Excellent feed conversion (2.5-3:1). Does breed easily year-round; high prolificacy (8-10 kits per litter).
  • Californian: Similar to NZ in performance; white with colored points; 10-11 lbs.
  • Champagne d'Argent: Silver-gray; excellent meat quality; slower growth than NZ.

  • Rex: Soft, plush coat (fur industry standard); medium-large (10 lbs); slower growth. Beautiful fur but meat production secondary.

Large Breed:

  • Flemish Giant: 13-15 lbs; slow-growing (12+ weeks to 5 lbs). Meat yield excellent but growth rate slower than commercial breeds. Expensive to feed. Good pets/show animals.

Housing & Environment

Cage Specifications: Wire-bottom cage (good drainage; 1/4" wire prevents foot problems) minimum 2.5 sq ft per rabbit (18x24"). Larger is better; does nest in corner. Nesting box 12x6" for pregnant does.

Ventilation & Location: Elevated cages (off ground) for air circulation. Protect from rain/direct sun. Ideal temperature 55-70¬∞F. CRITICAL: Rabbits die rapidly above 90¬∞F (no sweat glands). Shade, ventilation, or cooling system essential in hot climates.

Hutches (Outdoor): Solid-sided, partially enclosed for predator/weather protection. Ventilation essential; wire-front. Nest boxes for breeding does. Predator-proof (raccoons, foxes, hawks).

Breeding & Production

Breeding Age: Does first breed at 4-5 months; bucks at 5-6 months. Does breed year-round (no estrous cycle/heat; induced ovulation by mating). Gestation 31 days.

Nest Box Preparation: Place nest box in doe's cage 3 days before due date. Line with hay, straw, fur (doe pulls fur from chest/sides). Doe kindles (gives birth) usually early morning in nest box (8-10 kits typical).

Kit Care: Does nurse 1-2x daily. Kits' eyes open at 10 days; eat solid food at 3 weeks. Weaning at 4-5 weeks. Separate bucks from does post-weaning to prevent fighting. Young rabbits (fryers) raised communally until processing.

Feeding

Hay: Timothy, orchard grass, or other grass hay unlimited. Alfalfa limited (too rich; causes urinary calculi in bucks). Good hay is foundation; pelleted diet supplement not replacement.

Pellets: 1 oz per pound body weight daily. Quality rabbit pellets 18%+ protein for growth; 16-17% for adults. Hay must always be available (prevents digestive upsets from pelleted diet alone).

Vegetables: Carrots, lettuce, kale, parsley (small amounts, 2-3x per week). Fresh water critical; automatic waterers or bottles daily fresh fill.

Efficiency & Production

Extraordinary Efficiency: 6 productive does (birth -> culling) produce 200+ lbs meat/year with minimal space (6 cages = 15 sq ft). Feed conversion 3.5:1 (better than poultry). Each litter (8-10 kits) raised to fryer size (5 lbs) = 40-50 lbs meat per doe per year. Low startup cost; no infrastructure needed beyond cages.

Poultry In-Depth

Chicken Breed Categories

Egg Layers (High Production):

  • Leghorn: Small, active; world record egg layer (364+ eggs/year possible). White, brown, or buff varieties. Feed efficient; minimal meat quality. Flighty temperament.
  • Australorp: Black, large breed; 250-300 eggs/year. Good meat size. Broody (will incubate eggs naturally). Australian origin; hardy.
  • Sussex: Brown/tan with black stripes; 250+ eggs/year. Good dual-purpose bird. Friendly.

Meat Breeds (Broilers):

  • Cornish Cross: Specifically bred for meat (not layer). Reaches 5-6 lbs at 8 weeks (fastest growth). Poor parents (low egg production). Cannot breed naturally (body shape). Prone to leg/heart problems. Standard commercial broiler.
  • Cornish (Heritage): Slow-growing heritage version (16-20 weeks to fryer size); excellent meat quality; can breed naturally; long lifespan.

Dual-Purpose (Eggs + Meat):

  • Rhode Island Red: Red/brown; 200-250 eggs/year; 7-8 lb roosters. Hardy, broody, excellent mothers. Historic American breed.
  • Wyandotte: Silver-laced or golden; 200-240 eggs/year; good meat size. Cold-hardy, broody, excellent mothers.
  • Barred Rock (Plymouth Rock): Black and white striped; 200-250 eggs/year; large meat birds. Broody, friendly temperament.
  • Orpington: Black, buff, blue varieties; 180-220 eggs/year; 10+ lb roosters. Excellent broody mothers; calm, friendly. Excellent for small holdings.

Housing & Space

Indoor Space: 4 sq ft per bird minimum (overcrowding causes pecking, disease). Better: 6-8 sq ft per bird for health. Roosting bars 2-4 ft high (hens roost naturally; prevents ground diseases). Nest boxes 1 per 4 hens (12x12x12" minimum). Litter floor (wood shavings, straw) for dust bathing and insulation.

Outdoor Run: 10 sq ft per bird minimum. Grass/vegetation preferred (foraging reduces feed cost 20-30%). Shade structures protect from predators/sun.

Ventilation Over Insulation: Moisture/ammonia buildup kills chickens (respiratory disease). Ventilation (windows, vents) essential year-round; better to have cold/drafty house than warm/humid. Moisture at roof line indicates poor ventilation.

Egg Production

Light Requirements: 14+ hours of light daily for egg production. Winter decline is natural (short days). Artificial light (timer, 60-watt bulb in coop) extends laying season. Layer feed 16-18% protein supports production.

Production Curve: Pullets (young hens) start laying at 18-24 weeks (breed-dependent). Peak production (80-90%): weeks 25-60 of life. Decline in production year 2 (30-50% fewer eggs). Most commercial systems cull hens after year 1; homesteads keep hens 2-3 years (lower production acceptable for pest control, fertilizer, forage). Hens live 5-8 years in backyard systems.

Molting: Forced/natural molt (fall) = feather regrowth, rest cycle. Lasts 2-3 months; egg production stops. Molt triggered by decreasing daylight/nutrition stress. Proper nutrition (protein) speeds feather regrowth.

Brooding Chicks

Temperature Management: Start 95¬∞F week 1; decrease 5¬∞F per week until feathered (week 5-6, ambient temperature). Chicks pile under heat lamp if cold (and smother); scatter if too hot. Manual brooding: heat lamp, thermometer, daily monitoring. Broody hen: safer, easier (hen regulates temperature, teaches socialization), takes 3 weeks.

Equipment: Brooder (large box/container with heat lamp), feeders/waterers, bedding (wood shavings NOT cedar/pine initially‚Äîtoxic). Chick starter feed 24% protein (medicated or unmedicated). Electrolytes in water first 48 hours prevent stress dehydration.

Butchering Poultry

Timing: Cornish Cross 8 weeks (5-6 lbs); heritage breeds 16-20 weeks (4-5 lbs); layer culls any age (tougher meat). Ducks butcher at 7-8 weeks (5-6 lbs); geese 12-16 weeks (10-12 lbs).

Scalding & Plucking: Hot water 145-150¬∞F for 30-60 seconds loosens feathers (heating speeds plucking). Mechanical plucker (drum with rubber fingers) or hand-plucking. Eviscerate (remove organs via belly incision or through vent). Ice-chill immediately.

Aging: Hang carcass 2-4 hours before processing (muscle relaxation, easier butchering). Chill 24 hours before freezing (improves tenderness).

Ducks & Geese

Ducks: Hardier than chickens; excellent foragers (aquatic insects, slugs, snails). Duck eggs richer than chicken (more yolk, more fat) ‚Äî excellent for baking; richer flavor. Pekin (white) standard meat breed; Khaki Campbell (layer). Require water source (pond, kiddie pool) for health. Quieter than chickens/geese. 7-8 week butcher = 5-6 lbs. Eggs 300/year (Khaki Campbell).

Geese: Guard animals (loud, protective); excellent foragers on pasture (eat more grass than any poultry). Need water (breeding behavior, feather maintenance). Loud hissing deters predators. Eggs 30-40/year. 12-16 week butcher = 10-12 lbs. Slow-growing but long-lived (15-20 years). Meat darker, richer than chicken.

Turkeys

Broad-Breasted vs. Heritage: Broad-breasted (Broad-Breasted Bronze, White) grow fast, large; reach 25-30 lbs in 16 weeks. Cannot breed naturally (body shape); require artificial insemination (expensive). Short lifespan. Heritage breeds (Narragansett, Standard Bronze) breed naturally, live longer, more disease-resistant; slower growth (20 weeks to 16 lbs), excellent flavor.

Disease Susceptibility: Turkeys more prone to disease than chickens (blackhead, respiratory illness). Require cleanliness, separate housing if possible. Poults (young turkeys) brooded similarly to chicks but need higher protein (28%) and vitamin E. Higher mortality in poults than chicks.

Guard & Working Dogs

Livestock Guardian Dogs (LGDs)

Purpose & Behavior: Guard livestock 24/7 against predators (coyotes, wild dogs, bears, eagles). Unlike herding dogs, LGDs work independently; bonded to livestock from puppyhood. They live WITH the flock/herd (not house pets). Territorial; deter predators by presence, barking, and aggressive defense if needed.

Breeds:

  • Great Pyrenees: Large (100-160 lbs), white, thick double-coat. Calm, independent, excellent mothers. Tolerant of flock. Cold-hardy. Bark frequently.
  • Anatolian Shepherd: Large (80-150 lbs), fawn/tan/brindle. Intense, fierce protector. More aggressive than Pyr; requires firm hand. Excellent in harsh conditions.
  • Maremma: Italian breed; white, 65-100 lbs. Less common but excellent. Calm, protective without aggression toward flock.
  • Kangal: Turkish breed; large, powerful, intense protector. Requires experienced handler.

Training (Start Early): Puppies introduced to livestock at 8 weeks. Spend 24/7 with flock/herd for 6-12 months during formative years. Bond strengthens; dog considers livestock "family." Never penned away from stock during training phase. Some aggression toward strange dogs/predators is natural; refine but don't eliminate. Neutering/spaying reduces aggression (recommended). Training by example (older LGD) or direct instruction (consistent handler); slow, patient process.

Feed & Care: Guardian dogs consume ~2% body weight daily (large dogs eat 3-5 lbs/day). Lower-cost diet acceptable (quality less critical than herding dogs). Working condition without obesity (maintains endurance, protects joints). Annual health check, vaccinations, parasite control. Hip dysplasia common (screen breeding animals).

Herding Dogs

Purpose: Move/control livestock on command. Work at handler's direction (unlike LGDs). Intense, high-energy; require mental stimulation and training. Best for active handlers managing large flocks.

Breeds:

  • Border Collie: Small-medium (30-55 lbs), high intelligence, intense focus ("eye"‚Äîstare at sheep). Best herding dog. Require 2+ hours exercise/training daily. Can become neurotic without work.
  • Australian Shepherd: Medium (40-65 lbs), energetic, intelligent. Good herders; more versatile than Border Collie. Excellent family dogs with work.
  • Old English Sheepdog: Large, gentler herding; historical "drover" dog. Good for calm movement of livestock.

Training Basics: Start at 6-12 weeks old. Basic commands: "come-bye" (move around left side of animals), "away" (right side), "lie down" (stop/crouch), "walk up" (move forward to animals). Repetition, consistency, positive reinforcement (treats, praise). Professional training accelerates but foundation work by handler creates best bond.

Pest Control Dogs

Terriers (Rat Control): Jack Russell, Rat Terrier, Wire Fox Terrier bred historically for hunting rodents. Fast, tenacious; 10-20 lbs. Free-range in barn/property; kill rats/mice opportunistically. Alert temperament; excellent farm companions.

Curs & Hunting Dogs (Hog Control): Mixed-breed dogs, often larger (40-80 lbs), used for hunting feral hogs and other game. Regional/traditional breeds. Require hunting experience/authority; not typical farm dogs.

General Dog Care

Nutrition: 2% body weight daily (working dogs may need more if exerting heavily). Quality matters; balanced protein, fat, carbohydrate. Avoid grain-free fads. Free-choice water always available.

Reproduction: Breeding dogs screened for hereditary conditions (hip dysplasia, eye disease). Breeding at 2+ years old (after reaching full maturity). Gestation 63 days; litters 4-8 puppies typical. Nursing dam needs 1.5-2x normal calories. Weaning at 4-6 weeks.

Common Ailments:

  • Hip Dysplasia: Genetic joint disorder; painful, progressive. Screen breeding animals (X-rays, OFA certification). Maintain lean body weight, avoid jumping.
  • Skin Issues: Mites, lice, fungal infections (mange). Treat with topical/oral parasiticides. Improve hygiene/nutrition.
  • GI Upset: Diarrhea from diet change, parasites, infection. Feed bland diet (rice + plain chicken), probiotics. Prevent via clean water, quality feed.

Rabies Awareness: Rabies fatal once symptomatic; always lethal. Vaccinate all dogs against rabies (legal requirement most jurisdictions). If bitten by unknown animal, seek immediate medical attention (post-exposure prophylaxis possible only if given before symptoms). Wildlife with odd behavior (staggering, aggression, excessive salivation) likely rabid; avoid, report to authorities.
