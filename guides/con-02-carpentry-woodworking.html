Tool Selection & Making

In survival situations, you must make tools from natural materials. Understanding both commercially available and improvised tools ensures you can work in any scenario.

Essential Tools & Alternatives:

  • Saw: Commercial hand saw (critical) or improvised bone/stone saw. Bone saws effective on softwood but require 20% more effort.
  • Axe: Hafted stone head or bone wedge. Requires well-hardened handle. Modern axes superior for speed, but stone tools sufficient.
  • Chisel: Sharpened bone, stone edge, or hardened wood wedge. Stone chisels chip easily; bone chisels superior.
  • Hammer: Rock with flat face, wood mallet, or bone hammer. Effectiveness depends on striking surface density.
  • Plane: Sharpened bone blade set in wooden frame. Slower than metal planes but produces smooth surfaces.
  • Clamps: Rope tension clamps or wooden screw clamps. Rope clamps sufficient for most applications.

Making a Stone Adze (Multi-Purpose Carving Tool):

  1. Select dense stone (flint, basalt) 8-10cm diameter
  2. Strike to create sharp edge perpendicular to long axis
  3. Create hafting notch by careful edge chipping
  4. Lash to hardwood handle with cordage
  5. Test edge sharpness before heavy use
  6. Resharpen by careful stone-on-stone edge work
Tip: Bone tools consistently outperform stone for precision work. If you have access to large animal bones, dedicate time to making bone tools—they pay dividends over months of construction work.

Wood Selection & Processing

Wood quality determines structural integrity and construction ease. Understanding wood properties and processing methods is fundamental to carpentry success.

Wood Selection by Application:

OakVery HighStructural, PostsStrong, rot-resistant, difficult to cut
MapleVery HighFloors, ToolsHard, dense, excellent wear
AshHighTool HandlesFlexible, shock-absorbing
PineLowGeneral BuildingEasy to cut, lightweight, needs drying
BirchHighBentwork, FurnitureFlexible when steamed, rot-prone
SpruceLowFramingStraight grain, lightweight, weak in tension

Lumber Processing:

Tree felling: Cut at base at 45-degree angle (easier than horizontal cut). Let tree drop naturally; do not try to control fall without experience. Allow felled log 2-4 weeks for bark to loosen.

Bark removal: Use wooden or bone wedge to split bark from log, or soak in water 1-2 weeks to allow easy removal. Begin at butt end where bark naturally separates.

Log splitting: Use wedges and maul or axe. Split along grain. Split diameter determines usable pieces: 50cm log yields 4-6 planks; 30cm log yields 2-3 planks. Waste is 15-20%.

Sawing planks: Use pit saw (two-person) or hand saw. For critical structural members, saw along grain to create quartersawn planks (more stable, less warping). Standard sawing acceptable for general purposes.

Drying: Stack cut lumber with 2cm spacing, protected from weather. Softwood dries in 6-12 weeks; hardwood requires 6-18 months. Air-dry lumber has 12-15% moisture content (acceptable for structures).

Safety Warning: Green (wet) wood is 50% heavier than dry. Do not underestimate weight of freshly cut logs. Use proper technique and leverage; do not rely on strength alone.

Mortise & Tenon Joinery

Mortise and tenon joints are strongest traditional wood-to-wood connections. Widely used in furniture, doors, windows, and frames.

Basic Mortise & Tenon Design:

Mortise: Rectangular hole in receiving wood. Standard dimensions: depth 2/3 of wood thickness; width 1/3 of wood width; length 2.5-3 times width.

Tenon: Projecting piece from joining wood, fitting into mortise. Must be slightly smaller than mortise (0.5mm tolerance) for tight fit when glued.

Cutting Mortise by Hand:

  1. Mark mortise layout on wood using knife—cuts fibers cleanly
  2. Create small pilot hole at mortise center using bone drill or stone chisel
  3. Use sharp chisel to remove wood in layers (5-10mm depth per pass)
  4. Work from both sides toward center to avoid tearout at far side
  5. Cut perpendicular to wood surface; do not angle chisel
  6. Check fit with tenon test piece; adjust as needed
  7. Final fit should require light hammer taps to seat fully

Cutting Tenon by Hand:

  1. Mark tenon profile on four sides using knife and square
  2. Use saw to cut long-grain sides (create vertical walls of tenon)
  3. Use chisel to shape cross-grain shoulders where tenon meets body wood
  4. Test fit in mortise regularly; remove small amounts of material
  5. Final dimension: tenon fully enters mortise with light resistance

Layout Precision:

Mortise and tenon success requires accurate layout. Use sharpened bone or hardwood marking gauge. Mark mortise and tenon dimensions on all pieces before cutting any. Double-check all dimensions before committing cuts.

Tip: Create a test joint first using scrap wood identical to finished piece. This reveals any technique issues and tool problems before irreplaceable material is ruined.

Dovetail Joints

Dovetail joints provide superior strength and mechanical interlocking. Traditionally used in drawers and boxes where pulling stress is high.

Dovetail Types:

Through dovetails: Tails (protruding pieces) visible on both sides. Strongest but requires precise fit. Typical angle: 8-degree slope (1:6 ratio in softwood, 1:8 in hardwood).

Half-blind dovetails: Tails hidden from one side. Used where appearance matters (drawer fronts). Requires careful layout.

Box joints: Simplified version using rectangular protrusions instead of angled. Faster to cut, adequate strength for light boxes.

Layout for Through Dovetails:

  1. Measure and mark baselines on all pieces (typically 2-3mm in from edge)
  2. Divide width into equal sections (typical: 3-5 tail sections per side)
  3. Draw angled lines at 8-degree slope from baselines to represent tails
  4. Mark pin areas (receiving wood) by transferring tail pattern
  5. Label all pieces and mark grain direction clearly

Cutting Through Dovetails:

  1. Clamp wood vertically in vise with grain vertical
  2. Use hand saw to cut tail edges at 8-degree angle
  3. Cut with saw leading on waste side—do not touch scribed line
  4. Chop out waste between tails using sharp chisel
  5. Test fit with pin board (receiving board)
  6. Mark pin positions by placing tail board on top and scribing with knife
  7. Cut pins identically to tails
  8. Final assembly: tap together with mallet; glue if permanent

Dovetail Spacing:

Minimum tail width: 1.5cm for softwood, 1cm for hardwood (avoids splits). Spacing affects appearance and strength: widely spaced tails (5-8cm) appear coarse but are strongest; closely spaced tails (1-2cm) appear refined but require precision.

Timber Framing Techniques

Timber framing creates strong structures using massive timber beams joined with traditional joinery. Foundational for buildings that last centuries.

Structural Design Principles:

  • Load paths: Design structures so loads travel directly through beams to ground. Avoid complex stress patterns.
  • Beam sizing: Rule of thumb: depth in inches should equal span in feet divided by 20 (beam 20 feet span requires 12-inch depth minimum).
  • Joint strength: Joints must be stronger than adjoining beams (typically 90% of beam strength).
  • Redundancy: Design so single joint failure does not collapse structure.

Common Timber Frame Joints:

Post-to-beam: Mortise and tenon where beam sits in post mortise. Post absorbs bearing load directly. Requires 25% post diameter depth mortise.

Beam-to-beam: Joinery where beams meet at angles. Commonly: half-lap joints, mortise-tenon combinations, or scarfs (overlapping angled joints).

Connection hardware: Use wooden pegs (10-12mm diameter hardwood) to lock joints, preventing movement from wood shrinkage or seasonal expansion.

Raising Timber Frame Structure:

  1. Assemble frame on ground with all joints fitted dry
  2. Test all connections; adjust before final assembly
  3. Disassemble completely
  4. Reassemble at height using gin pole (mechanical advantage lever) or block and tackle system
  5. Insert pegs into holes during assembly to lock frame
  6. Check for square using diagonal measurements
  7. Brace frame temporarily until roof attaches

Furniture Construction

Building sturdy furniture ensures comfort in survival situations. Functional designs are far superior to decorative styles in survival contexts.

Essential Survival Furniture:

Sleeping Platform (Bed):

Elevated 60cm off ground using timber frame and rope webbing or wooden slats. Dimensions: 2m long, 1.2m wide minimum. Frame uses 5cm diameter posts, 10cm cross members. Rope webbing (2cm natural fiber rope) stretched across frame at 15cm spacing provides flexible support. Alternative: split log slats laid 5cm apart.

Storage Box:

Constructed from planks joined with dovetails or mortise-tenon. Dimensions: 60cm x 40cm x 40cm. Hinged lid with stay bar prevents opening too far. Interior shelf divides space for organization. Bottom board sits on batten supports (prevents moisture absorption through ground contact).

Table:

Simple design: four square-section legs (6cm x 6cm), cross-braced at 40cm height for strength. Top made from wide planks (glued and doweled together, or mortised into legs). Height: 75-80cm. Bracing provides torsion strength.

Chair:

Practical design: vertical back support (1.2m), angled seat frame (15-degree slope back), four legs with cross-bracing. Seat surface made from rope webbing (comfortable and durable). Minimum height: 45cm seat to floor.

Wood Movement Compensation:

Wood shrinks and swells with moisture changes. Account for movement in wide boards: allow 5mm per 30cm width for seasonal movement. Use floating panels (not fully glued) and slots for screw holes (not centered in hole—allows movement).

Making Hand Tools

In extended survival situations, you must fabricate tools. Understanding tool principles allows improvisation from available materials.

Making a Wooden Plane:

  1. Select hardwood block (oak, maple) 25cm long, 8cm wide, 5cm tall
  2. Cut blade slot perpendicular to length (40mm deep)
  3. Create wedge to lock blade in place
  4. Sharpen bone or stone blade to 25-30 degree angle
  5. Flatten sole of plane to accept wood surfaces evenly
  6. Test: plane should remove thin shavings (1mm or less) in one direction

Making a Saw from Bone:

  1. Use large animal rib bone (60cm or longer)
  2. Create triangular cross-section by splitting or grinding
  3. Sharpen edge to 30-degree angle using fine stone
  4. Create handle from antler or hardwood at one end
  5. Cut teeth into edge using small chisel (2-3mm spacing)
  6. Use with two-handed pulling motion; effective on softwood

Making Wooden Clamps:

  1. Use hardwood block (10cm x 5cm x 2.5cm) as base
  2. Drill hole through width for wooden screw (5cm diameter)
  3. Create wooden screw by tapering hardwood dowel and cutting grooves (4-6 grooves)
  4. Mount screw vertically; tighten by rotating screw through wood nut
  5. Movable jaw allows variable clamping width
  6. Sufficient for holding wood during joinery work

Common Mistakes & Solutions

Mistake 1: Insufficient Tool Sharpness

Error: Attempting woodworking with dull tools. Results: forced cuts, tool breakage, wood tearout, poor-fitting joints, extreme fatigue.

Solution: Dedicate 30% of woodworking time to tool maintenance. Sharpen chisels, saws, and planes weekly. Dull tools require 3-5x more effort than sharp ones.

Mistake 2: Ignoring Grain Direction

Error: Planing or chiseling against grain direction. Results: deep tearout, rough surface, tool chattering, unsafe tool control.

Solution: Always identify grain direction (usually visible in wood end grain). Plane downslope of grain direction. Work from high grain to low grain.

Mistake 3: Over-Tightening Joints

Error: Driving mortise-tenon joints too tightly during test-fit, jamming them. Results: unable to disassemble, wood splits during removal, joint damaged.

Solution: Test-fit joints without force. Mark fitting difficulty. Disassemble immediately. Do not fully tap joints until final assembly with glue.

Mistake 4: Ignoring Wood Movement

Error: Building furniture with wood glued across grain or fully constrained. Wood shrinks seasonally, causing splits, warping, or failed joints.

Solution: Allow wood movement in wide boards (floating panels, slotted screw holes). Glue only in grain direction. Accept seasonal dimension changes as normal.
