Body Condition Scoring

5-Point Scale (Most Common)

  • Score 1 (Poor): All ribs visible, spine sharp and prominent, no fat deposits
  • Score 2 (Thin): Ribs visible but not prominent, minimal fat, angular features
  • Score 3 (Ideal): Ribs easily felt but not visible, smooth body lines, adequate muscle
  • Score 4 (Fat): Ribs hard to feel, fatty deposits visible, slight sagging
  • Score 5 (Obese): Ribs not palpable, excessive fat, poor mobility

Illness Indicators

  • Fever: Temperature >102¬∞F (horse/dog); >103¬∞F (ruminants)
  • Dehydration: Skin tenting (pinch test >2 seconds return), dry mucous membranes, sunken eyes
  • Lethargy: Lack of appetite, depression, standing apart from herd/flock
  • Abnormal Discharge: Nasal, ocular, or genital discharge; foul-smelling urine/feces
  • Lameness: Weight shifting, reluctance to bear weight, swelling or heat in limbs
  • Bloating: Distended left flank (cattle/sheep), discomfort, gas smell
  • Diarrhea/Constipation: Abnormal consistency, frequency changes, dehydration risk

Common Diseases by Species

Cattle

Bloat (Frothy & Free Gas)

  • Cause: Excess gas accumulation in rumen; legume-heavy diet (alfalfa, clover)
  • Signs: Left side distension, grunting, drooling, difficulty breathing, collapse if untreated
  • Treatment: See Emergency Procedures for trocar technique; oral oils (mineral oil 1-2 liters), massage rumen, limit fresh legumes

Milk Fever (Hypocalcemia)

  • Cause: Calcium depletion after calving, common in high-production cows
  • Signs: Weakness, trembling, inability to stand, low temp, pale mucous membranes
  • Prevention: Limit high-calcium diet 2 weeks pre-calving; increase roughage post-calving
  • Treatment: Increase calcium/phosphorus supplementation; oral calcium gluconate (500g mixed in water/gruel)

Mastitis

  • Cause: Bacterial infection of udder tissue; poor hygiene, rough milking, injury
  • Signs: Swollen, hot, hard quarters; discolored milk (flakes, water, blood); fever; reduced milk
  • Treatment: Frequent milking (relieve pressure), warm compresses, systemic antibiotics (penicillin IV); discard milk

Pinkeye (Infectious Keratitis)

  • Cause: Moraxella bovis bacteria; spread by flies, dust
  • Signs: Eye discharge, conjunctivitis, cloudiness, squinting, tearing
  • Treatment: Clean eyes 2-3x daily with saline solution; tetracycline ointment; antifly measures; dark shelter reduces discomfort

Foot Rot

  • Cause: Anaerobic bacteria (Fusobacterium); wet/muddy conditions
  • Signs: Severe lameness, hoof tissue necrosis, foul odor, swollen interdigital space
  • Treatment: Trim diseased tissue aggressively; foot bath in 5% CuSO‚ÇÑ solution (or 10% zinc sulfate) for 5-10 min daily; dry housing; foot bandaging

Horses

Colic

  • Cause: Digestive upset; dietary changes, dehydration, impaction, gas distension, strangulation
  • Signs: Lying down/rolling, sweating, elevated HR, lack of appetite, reduced feces
  • Treatment: Walking to prevent rolling injury; mineral oil via nasogastric tube (1-2L); electrolyte water; NSAIDs for pain

Laminitis

  • Cause: Inflammation of hoof laminae; high grain diet, excessive carbs, sepsis, obesity
  • Signs: Heat in hooves, digital pulse palpable, reluctance to move, rocked-back stance
  • Treatment: Cold water soaks (20+ min, 3-4x daily); NSAIDs; restrict grain, increase forage; trim toe to reduce tension

Thrush

  • Cause: Anaerobic bacteria in frog; wet, muddy environment
  • Signs: Foul-smelling black discharge from frog; lameness if deep
  • Treatment: Trim away dead tissue; apply dilute bleach/copper sulfate daily; improve drainage; dry bedding

Strangles (Equine Distemper)

  • Cause: Streptococcus equi bacteria; highly contagious
  • Signs: Fever, nasal discharge, swollen lymph nodes (mandibular, pharyngeal), abscesses
  • Treatment: Supportive care; isolate; warm compresses on swollen nodes to promote drainage; penicillin early

Goats & Sheep

Internal Parasites

  • Cause: GI worms (gastrointestinal roundworms most common)
  • Signs: Diarrhea, weight loss, rough coat, anemia (pale mucous membranes), FAMACHA score 4-5
  • Treatment: Fecal egg count (0-100 normal); dewormer rotation; FAMACHA scoring; high-quality forage improves immunity

Bloat

  • Cause: Frothy bloat from legume consumption; gas bloat from obstruction
  • Signs: Distended left side, restlessness, grinding teeth, labored breathing
  • Treatment: Oral vegetable oil (200-300ml); gentle percussion/massage; trocar if severe; prevent with proper diet transitions

Enterotoxemia (Clostridium perfringens)

  • Cause: Bacterial toxin; high-carb diet, stress, poor immunity
  • Signs: Acute diarrhea (often bloody), abdominal pain, rapid progression to death
  • Prevention: Vaccination (CD&T vaccine); avoid sudden diet changes; good sanitation
  • Treatment: Limited; supportive fluids, electrolytes; NSAIDs

Foot Rot

  • Cause: Anaerobic bacteria in moist hoof environment
  • Signs: Lameness, hoof tissue breakdown, foul odor
  • Treatment: Trim affected tissue; CuSO‚ÇÑ or zinc sulfate foot baths daily; dry bedding; isolation from flock

Pigs

Erysipelas

  • Cause: Erysipelothrix rhusiopathiae bacteria
  • Signs: Diamond-shaped skin lesions, fever, lameness, swollen joints
  • Treatment: Penicillin early; reduce stress; improve sanitation

Mange

  • Cause: Mite infestation (sarcoptic most severe)
  • Signs: Intense itching, hair loss, thick crusty skin, often ears/legs first
  • Treatment: Ivermectin (0.3mg/kg); lime sulfur dips; clean housing; treat all animals in contact

Poultry

Marek's Disease

  • Cause: Herpesvirus; incurable, highly contagious
  • Signs: Progressive paralysis, nerve damage, tumors, pale combs
  • Prevention: Vaccination at hatch; culling infected birds; biosecurity

Coccidiosis

  • Cause: Coccidia protozoan in intestines; damp bedding risk factor
  • Signs: Bloody diarrhea, lethargy, ruffled feathers, dehydration
  • Treatment: Amprolium (coecstat) in water; dry bedding; isolation

Egg Binding

  • Cause: Large or malformed eggs; insufficient calcium; young/old hens
  • Signs: Straining, lethargy, dragging rear, failure to lay
  • Treatment: Warm baths (20 min); calcium supplements; gentle abdominal massage; increase water; reduce production pressure

Bumblefoot (Staphylococcal Infection)

  • Cause: Bacterial infection of foot pads; rough perches, injury
  • Signs: Swollen foot pad, scab, pus, lameness
  • Treatment: Clean wound; remove pus; topical antibiotic; soft bedding; smooth perches

Dogs

Parvovirus

  • Cause: Canine parvovirus (CPV); highly contagious via feces
  • Signs: Vomiting, bloody diarrhea, lethargy, fever, rapid dehydration
  • Prevention: Vaccination series; disinfection (bleach 1:10); quarantine
  • Treatment: Supportive fluids, electrolytes; bland diet if recovered

Distemper (CDV)

  • Cause: Canine distemper virus; highly contagious airborne
  • Signs: Fever, nasal/ocular discharge, respiratory signs, neurological (seizures, myoclonus)
  • Prevention: Vaccination; isolation
  • Treatment: Supportive care; antiseizure agents; isolation; poor prognosis

Mange

  • Cause: Sarcoptic or demodectic mites
  • Signs: Hair loss, itching, crusty skin, especially ears/legs
  • Treatment: Ivermectin (0.2-0.3 mg/kg weekly); lime-sulfur dips; selenium shampoos

Internal Worms

  • Cause: Roundworms (ascarids), hookworms, tapeworms
  • Signs: Diarrhea, weight loss, rough coat, pale mucous membranes, pot belly in puppies
  • Treatment: Dewormer rotation; monthly prevention; fecal exams; good sanitation

Parasite Control

Fecal Egg Count (FEC) Technique

  1. Collect 5g fresh feces from rectum
  2. Mix with 45ml water or salt solution (1:9 ratio)
  3. Filter through cheesecloth; place under microscope (10x magnification)
  4. Count eggs in field; multiply by 50 = eggs per gram (EPG)
  5. Interpretation: <100 EPG = low, 100-500 = moderate, >500 = high infection

Rotational Grazing Strategy

  • Principle: Move animals to clean pasture before parasite reinfection (14-21 days)
  • Timing: Leave pasture 3-4 weeks for larvae die-off; shorter rotation for high-risk animals
  • Species Rotation: Rotate cattle ‚Üí sheep ‚Üí cattle; breaks many parasites' life cycles
  • Benefits: Reduces chemical dewormer use, improves pasture quality, lower infection pressure

Natural Dewormers

Herbal Preparations

  • Diatomaceous Earth (food-grade only): 1-2% of daily feed for 30 days; mild dewormer for GI parasites
  • Garlic: 10-20g fresh garlic per animal daily; mild anthelmintic, immune boost
  • Pumpkin Seeds: 15-30g ground seeds daily; traditional antihelmintic, especially for tapeworms
  • Wormwood (Artemisia annua): Decoction 50ml 2x daily for sheep/goats; potent antimalarial herbs
  • Black Walnut Hull: Tincture 5-10ml daily (ruminants); strong anthelmintic, avoid overdose

Chemical Dewormer Rotation

Alternate drug classes to prevent resistance: Benzimidazoles (fenbendazole) ‚Üí Macrocyclics (ivermectin) ‚Üí Imidazothiazoles (levamisole).

  • Treat at seasonal peaks (spring/fall) or based on FEC results
  • Include all animals in group treatment to reduce reinfection
  • Allow 10-14 day rest between different drug classes

Wound Care

Basic Wound Cleaning Protocol

  1. Restraint: Secure animal safely; provide pain relief if severe
  2. Clipping: Clip hair 2 inches around wound for visibility and hygiene
  3. Rinsing: Flush with clean water or saline (1 tsp salt/500ml water); remove debris
  4. Disinfection: Mild povidone-iodine scrub (10% solution); avoid harsh betadine on deep wounds
  5. Drying: Pat with clean cloth; assess wound depth and contamination
  6. Bandaging: Sterile gauze, antibiotic ointment, cohesive wrap; change daily initially

Suturing Large Animals (Simple Wounds)

Caution: Only for clean, fresh wounds (<12 hours). Deep/contaminated wounds should NOT be closed without professional help.
  • Materials: Sterile sutures (size 0-2), needle holder, scissors, gloves
  • Technique: Simple interrupted stitches, spacing 0.5 inches apart; knot on side; tension even
  • Wound Depth: Suture deep layers first (subcutaneous), then skin; avoid dead space
  • Removal: 14-21 days depending on location; keep dry and bandaged

Fly Strike Prevention & Treatment

  • Prevention: Remove soiled wool/hair; ear tags; fly repellent; good sanitation; maintain BCS
  • Treatment: Inspect daily; clip affected area; flush maggots; apply insecticide (permethrin-based); systemic ivermectin
  • Danger Signs: Foul odor, tissue invasion, fever = emergency; isolate from herd

Proud Flesh Management

Overgranulation tissue that prevents healing; common after major wounds.

  • Prevention: Aggressive cleaning, regular bandage changes, pressure wrapping
  • Treatment: Topical copper sulfate solution (dilute to 3-5%) applied 2-3x weekly; mechanical debridement; skin grafting if severe

Poultice Recipe for Abscesses

  • 1 part drawing salve or magnesium sulfate (Epsom salt)
  • 2 parts warm water
  • Add flour/oatmeal to create paste consistency
  • Apply to abscess, wrap loosely; change 2x daily until drainage
  • Once draining, switch to antibiotic ointment and dry bandaging

Birthing Assistance

Normal Presentation

  • Position: Calf lying on sternum (front-legs-first), forelimbs extended, head between legs
  • Progression: 1-4 hours for cattle; contractions every 2-5 minutes by active labor
  • Signs of Progress: Visible hooves/head at vulva, increasing straining, fluid discharge
  • When to Assist: No progress after 2 hours; excessive distress; 1 leg only; fluid without fetus

Common Malpresentations & Correction

Posterior Presentation (Breech)

  • Recognition: Rear legs/rump deliver first instead of head
  • Risk: Umbilical cord compression; must deliver quickly once rear emerges
  • Procedure: If only rear emerging, can allow delivery but pull hard with contractions; if front present too, convert by pushing calf back, rotating, repositioning front legs

Head Turned Back

  • Recognition: Only forelegs visible; head tucked to side
  • Correction: Push calf back into uterus (during rest between contractions); rotate calf to realign head with forelegs; reposition carefully

One Leg Retained

  • Recognition: One foreleg extended, one folded back
  • Correction: Push calf back; cup hand over folded leg joint; straighten leg at elbow/shoulder; maintain position; allow delivery

Manual Delivery Assistance

  1. Examination: Confirm normal presentation; check pelvis for blockage
  2. Hygiene: Scrub arms/hands; use obstetrical lube (soap/oil solution)
  3. Traction: Pull downward-backward during strains; use obstetrical chains/straps on legs if needed
  4. Timing: Pull WITH contractions only; rest between; excessive traction risks injury
  5. Assistance: Call for help if needed; two assistants can pull simultaneously on each foreleg

Retained Placenta

  • Normal: Placenta passes 4-12 hours post-delivery
  • Retention >24 hours: Infection risk (metritis/sepsis); requires intervention
  • Treatment: Oxytocin (20 units IM) to encourage expulsion; do NOT pull membrane (may tear); gentle traction after softening; inspect fully expelled membranes
  • Prevention: Good calcium/mineral status during pregnancy; clean calving area

Neonatal Care

  • Immediate: Clear nasal passages; dry calf; ensure breathing (stimulate if needed)
  • Colostrum: CRITICAL: Feed within 2 hours, 5-10% body weight; repeat at 12 and 24 hours
  • Navel Dipping: 7% iodine solution on umbilical stump; reduces infection risk
  • Temperature: Hypothermia common; keep dry, warm bedding, heat lamp if <95¬∞F
  • Bonding: Allow dam to lick/bond if possible ( 1+ hour)

Foot Rot Treatment Protocol

Key: Aggressive tissue removal is essential; systemic antibiotics support but do not cure alone.
  1. Restraint: Secure animal; local anesthesia optional (2% lidocaine)
  2. Tissue Removal: Carefully cut away ALL diseased (black/soft) tissue; expose healthy pink tissue
  3. CuSO‚ÇÑ Bath: 5% copper sulfate solution; soak affected hoof 5-10 min daily for 5-7 days
  4. Alternative: 10% zinc sulfate foot bath (gentler on tissue)
  5. Bandaging: Sterile wrap after bath; keep as dry as possible
  6. Housing: Dry straw/bedding; isolate if possible; improve drainage
  7. Duration: 2-3 weeks to full healing if caught early; chronic cases worse

Abscess Drainage

  • Recognition: Painful swelling in hoof; heat; lameness
  • Localization: Use hoof tester to find tender area
  • Drainage: Carefully pare away sole to locate pus pocket; lance if needed; allow drainage
  • Aftercare: Poultice with Epsom salt, warm packs 2-3 days; then dry bandaging
  • Recovery: Usually dramatic improvement after drainage

Hoof Anatomy Quick Reference

  • Sole: Hard outer surface; trim minimally (risk of abscess)
  • Frog: V-shaped shock-absorbing tissue; trim to remove dead/diseased portions only
  • Wall: Hard outer hoof; trimmed to 2.5-3 inches from sole in healthy hooves
  • White Line: Junction between wall and sole; carefully trim away dead tissue

Castration & Dehorning

Castration Methods

Elastrator (Rubber Band) - Most Common

  • Timing: 1-4 weeks old for lambs/kids; <4 months for calves/piglets
  • Technique: Apply tight rubber band above testes; cuts off blood supply
  • Advantage: No tools needed; low infection risk; minimal bleeding
  • Timeline: Scrotum sloughs off in 2-4 weeks
  • Pain Management: NSAIDs or local anesthesia highly recommended

Surgical Castration

  • Requirements: Restraint, sharp scalpel/knife, good hygiene, antiseptic
  • Technique: Incise scrotum; exteriorize testicles; sever spermatic cord (tie or cauterize to prevent bleeding)
  • Aftercare: Keep wound clean; monitor for infection; NSAIDs for pain
  • Best Age: Young animals (younger = faster healing)

Burdizzo (Bloodless Castration)

  • Technique: Crushing spermatic cord with pliers-like tool; no incision
  • Advantage: No open wound; low infection risk
  • Disadvantage: Timing critical; older animals tolerate poorly
  • Best For: Young lambs/kids

Dehorning Methods & Timing

Disbudding Paste (Chemical) - Preventive

  • Timing: 1-4 weeks of age, before horns visible as buds
  • Product: Caustic potash or sodium hydroxide paste
  • Technique: Clip hair around bud; apply paste; cover with protective cap for 12-24 hours
  • Result: Scab forms; horn never develops
  • Precaution: Prevents permanent horn buds in 80-90% if done early

Hot Iron Dehorning

  • Timing: 2-12 weeks old optimal
  • Temperature: 400-500¬∞C iron
  • Technique: Apply to horn bud 5-10 seconds; burn must be complete around circumference
  • Aftercare: Watch for infection; may require topical antibiotics

Surgical Dehorning

  • Timing: Older animals (>1 year) if banding/paste failed
  • Technique: Local anesthesia; saw or dehorning shears to remove horn bud and tissue; cauterize bleeding; suture if needed
  • Risk: Higher infection risk; longer recovery

Pain Management & Aftercare

  • Analgesics: NSAIDs (meloxicam, phenylbutazone) for 3-7 days post-procedure
  • Local Anesthesia: Lidocaine 2% blocks if available; inject around surgical site
  • General Care: Keep area clean; monitor for infection; adequate nutrition aids healing
  • Fly Prevention: Important post-procedure to prevent fly strike on open wounds

Emergency Procedures

Bloat Emergency - Trocar Insertion (Cattle/Sheep)

Critical Steps

  1. Location: LEFT flank ONLY; behind last rib; approximately 1 fist-width down from top
  2. Preparation: Clip/clean area; restraint essential (can use rope halter)
  3. Insertion: Use 10-12 gauge needle or small trocar; push firmly through skin and muscle; listen for gas release
  4. Aftercare: Keep trocar sterile; leave 24-48 hours if needed; monitor for peritonitis (fever, lethargy)
  5. Prevention: Address cause (diet change, legume restriction); oral vegetable oil 500ml daily

Choking (Horses)

  • Signs: Nasal discharge, drooling, coughing, respiratory distress, panic
  • Immediate: Stay calm; reduce stress; remove food/water
  • Treatment: Nasogastric tube if trained (carefully advance); mild laxatives (mineral oil); patience (often resolves 12-48 hours)
  • Avoid: Do NOT push object down; may cause perforation

Prolapse (Vaginal, Rectal, Uterine)

Vaginal/Rectal Prolapse

  • Cause: Straining, pregnancy pressure, genetics
  • Emergency Signs: Exposed tissue, bleeding, swelling, severe pain
  • Immediate: Place animal in standing position; do not manipulate roughly
  • Treatment: Epidural anesthesia (if available); clean tissue gently; replace carefully; suture vulva partially to prevent re-prolapse (Caslick's suture)
  • Aftercare: Antibiotics; NSAIDs; monitor closely for infection/reoccurrence

Uterine Prolapse (Post-Calving Emergency)

  • Recognition: Large mass protruding from vulva post-delivery
  • URGENT: Treat within 2-3 hours or organ becomes unviable
  • Procedure: Epidural + systemic antibiotics; clean uterus gently; replace carefully working from apex outward; oxytocin to contract uterus; suture vulva partially
  • Survival: ~50% if treated quickly; worse if delayed/contaminated

Snake Bite (Livestock)

  • Venomous vs Dry Bite: Swelling at bite site = venomous; increased within 30 min
  • Immediate: Keep animal calm (stress worsens absorption); identify snake if possible
  • Treatment: Supportive care (fluids, electrolytes); NSAIDs for swelling; antihistamines if available; monitor airway (can swell and obstruct breathing)
  • Antivenom: Rarely available; expensive; regional snake-specific antivenom if accessible within 4-6 hours
  • Prognosis: 60-80% survive with supportive care alone if not severe/airway affected

Heat Stroke

  • Signs: Temp >104¬∞F, excess salivation, weakness, staggering, collapse
  • Immediate: Move to shade; apply cold water (especially ears, neck, legs); increase air circulation
  • Hydration: Offer small amounts of cool (not ice) water; electrolyte solution; IV fluids if severely dehydrated
  • Recovery: Monitor closely; permanent neurological damage if temp >106¬∞F

Pharmacy & Dosing

Common Antibiotics

Penicillin (Intramuscular or Intravenous)

  • Cattle/Horse: 10,000-20,000 units/kg once daily
  • Sheep/Goats: 10,000-20,000 units/kg once daily
  • Swine: 10,000-20,000 units/kg once daily
  • Dogs: 10,000-20,000 units/kg 2-4x daily
  • Uses: Bacterial infections, mastitis, wounds, respiratory disease

Tetracycline (Oral or Injectable)

  • Cattle/Horse: 10-20 mg/kg 2-3x daily
  • Sheep/Goats/Swine: 10-20 mg/kg 2-3x daily
  • Dogs: 10-20 mg/kg 3-4x daily
  • Uses: Respiratory, pinkeye, GI infections; also tick/rickettsial diseases

Ivermectin (Injectable or Oral)

  • Cattle: 200 mcg/kg IM, repeat in 7-10 days
  • Sheep/Goats: 200 mcg/kg IM (higher dose tolerance than cattle)
  • Swine: 300 mcg/kg IM
  • Dogs: 200 mcg/kg; caution in collies/sensitive breeds (CNS toxicity)
  • Uses: Internal parasites, external parasites (mites, lice), heartworm

Ampicillin (Oral or Injectable)

  • Dose: 10-20 mg/kg 2-4x daily (variable by route)
  • Uses: Gram-positive/negative bacteria; respiratory, GI, udder infections

Pain Management

NSAIDs (Non-Steroidal Anti-Inflammatory Drugs)

  • Phenylbutazone (Horse/Dog): 2-4 mg/kg 2-3x daily for 5-7 days
  • Meloxicam (Cattle/Small Ruminants): 0.5 mg/kg once daily
  • Flunixin Meglumine (Multi-species): 1-2 mg/kg 2-3x daily

Natural Pain Relief

  • Willow Bark Tea: 1-2g dried bark brewed in water; mild analgesic for chronic pain
  • Turmeric (Curcumin): 0.5-1g daily; anti-inflammatory; use in feed
  • Ginger: Fresh 10-20g or tea; anti-inflammatory

Oral Electrolyte Recipe

  • 1 liter clean water
  • 6 teaspoons sugar (or 40g dextrose)
  • 1 teaspoon salt (sodium chloride)
  • 1/2 teaspoon potassium chloride (or small amount of coconut water)
  • Optional: 1/4 tsp baking soda (sodium bicarbonate) for severe acidosis

Dosing: Offer ad libitum; dehydrated animals: 5-10 liters daily until rehydrated (skin tenting <2 sec)

Ivermectin (Macrocyclic) 200 mcg/kg 200 mcg/kg 300 mcg/kg
Levamisole (Imidazothiazole) 7.5 mg/kg 7.5 mg/kg 10 mg/kg

Herbal Remedies

  • Garlic (Antibacterial): 10-20g fresh daily per large animal; immune support
  • Oregano Oil (Antifungal/Antimicrobial): 1-2ml daily in feed; respiratory/GI support
  • Thyme (Expectorant): Infusion 50-100ml daily; cough support
  • Plantain (Wound Healing): Fresh leaves applied topically; internal tea for pneumonia support
  • Echinacea (Immune Boost): Tincture 5-10ml daily; respiratory support during early illness

Preventive Medicine

Vaccination Protocols by Species

Cattle

  • Core: Bovine Viral Diarrhea (BVD), Infectious Bovine Rhinotracheitis (IBR), Parainfluenza, BRSV
  • Schedule: Primary at 4-6 months; booster 3-4 weeks later; annual thereafter
  • Optional: Leptospirosis, Brucellosis (per region), Clostridium (tetanus, blackleg)

Sheep/Goats

  • Core: CD&T (Clostridium C&D, tetanus, enterotoxemia)
  • Schedule: Primary at 4-6 weeks; booster 3-4 weeks; annual boosters
  • Optional: Caseous lymphadenitis, pneumonia vaccines

Horses

  • Core: Tetanus, Equine Encephalomyelitis (East/West), Influenza, Rhinopneumonitis
  • Schedule: Primary series, then annual boosters
  • Optional: Rabies, Potomac Horse Fever, Strangles

Poultry

  • Core: Marek's disease (hatch vaccination)
  • Optional: Newcastle disease, bronchitis (if available, limited farm setting)

Dogs

  • Core: Rabies (required by law), DHLPP (Distemper, Hepatitis, Leptospirosis, Parvo, Parainfluenza)
  • Schedule: Primary at 6-8 weeks; boosters every 3-4 weeks until 16 weeks; rabies at 12-16 weeks; annual DHLPP, triennial rabies

Quarantine & Biosecurity

  • New Animals: Isolate 2-4 weeks; observe for signs of illness
  • Separate Feeding: Use dedicated feeders/waterers for new animals
  • Handler Hygiene: Change clothes/boots between groups; wash hands frequently
  • Facility Sanitation: Regular cleaning of high-touch surfaces; disinfect birthing areas
  • Feed Storage: Protect from wildlife; avoid contamination vectors
  • Disease Reporting: Know local disease reporting requirements (e.g., tuberculosis, brucellosis)

Record Keeping System

Maintain detailed records for traceability and pattern recognition:

  • Individual ID: Ear tag, neck band, or distinctive marking
  • Birth/Age: Date of birth; acquisition date
  • Medical History: Illness, treatment, dates, drugs used, dosages
  • Reproductive History: Breeding dates, calving/lambing dates, offspring outcomes
  • Vaccinations: Type, date, expiration, next booster due
  • Production: Milk yield, weight gain, egg production (if applicable)
  • Deaths: Date, age, suspected cause (aids herd health analysis)

Seasonal Health Checks

Spring

  • Body condition assessment; adjust feed if poor
  • Parasite monitoring (fecal egg counts); strategic deworming
  • Hoof trimming; prepare for pasture season
  • Vaccination boosters for breeding season

Summer

  • Fly strike prevention; monitor for parasites
  • Heat stress monitoring; shade/water access
  • Pasture rotation for parasite management

Fall

  • Pre-breeding examinations; reproductive soundness
  • Vaccination updates for upcoming winter
  • Body condition; prepare for winter feeding

Winter

  • Monitor for respiratory disease; ventilation checks
  • Nutritional status; avoid deficiency disease
  • Shelter inspection; prevent cold stress
