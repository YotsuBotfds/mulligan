Bloomery Overview and Yields

Bloomery is ancient smelting technology (2000+ years) still effective for small-scale iron production. Ore and charcoal loaded into pit furnace, heated to 1100-1350°C by air blast. Iron cannot fully melt at this temperature (melting point 1538°C) but becomes plastic/spongy, allowing slag to drain away. Final product: 15-30kg iron bloom per furnace run, requiring 3-4 days of initial ore/charcoal consumption and hammering consolidation.

Advantages: simple construction (clay/stone available most locations), fuel-efficient relative to output, produces wrought iron (pure, malleable, weldable), scalable from household backyard to village operation. Disadvantages: slow (8-12 hours active smelting per run), labor-intensive (constant bellows operation required), low reduction efficiency (20-30% ore converts to usable iron), requires extensive post-smelting hammering.

Material Selection and Preparation

Bloomery construction requires heat-resistant materials capable of withstanding 1100-1350°C for extended periods.

Furnace Pit Lining

Clay: Primary material. Needs high refractory clay (kaolin, fireclay) if available, or local clay with >20% silica. Test: form ball, heat to bright red (800°C), should not slump. Common clay acceptable but erodes faster (replaced annually). Best clay: from pottery makers, fire clay bricks if obtainable.

Stone: Use in outer shell for structural strength. Granite, basalt, or river stone (not limestone - decomposes at 900°C and destroys furnace). Stack 20-30cm thick around clay lining. Stones should be fist-sized or larger for efficient packing.

Clay preparation: Dig clay (if needed), dry completely (1-2 weeks in sun), crush to powder, wet to workable putty consistency (40-50% water by weight). Mix sand 1:4 ratio (1 clay : 4 sand by volume) to reduce cracking during drying. Tread or knead 30-60 minutes to homogenize. Result: tempered clay ready for molding.

Bellows Construction

Air pump required: Simple box bellows: wooden frame 0.5x0.5m, depth 0.2m, leather bag (or heavy burlap) seal, handle for pumping. One-way valves (simple leather flaps) allow air in on upstroke, discharge on downstroke. Rate: 10-20 strokes/minute produces 0.5-1 m³/minute air flow at 20-30 kPa pressure - adequate for bloomery. Replaces every 2-3 years (leather deteriorates).

Air pipe: Clay or metal tube 5-10cm diameter, leads from bellows to furnace pit, ends inside pit 10-15cm above bloom consolidation level. Pipe must withstand 1100°C without cracking - higher-quality refractory clay used only for pipe interior 5cm section inside furnace; remainder can be standard clay.

Optimal Dimensions

Size balances efficiency with constructability and operator capability.

Step-by-Step Construction

Phase 1: Pit Excavation (Days 1-2)

  1. Mark circular area 1.2m diameter
  2. Excavate pit 1.0m deep with vertical walls
  3. Flatten bottom, compact soil
  4. Dig three drain holes at pit bottom (5-8cm diameter) sloped slightly outward - drains slag after cooling

Phase 2: Stone Foundation (Day 2-3)

  1. Line pit bottom with large stones (fist-sized, ~10cm layer) - base
  2. Begin stacking stone walls up pit sides, using clay mortar between stones (same clay as furnace lining)
  3. Stack vertically as high as ground level (~1m), compact stones with clay mortar

Phase 3: Clay Lining (Day 3-4)

  1. Apply 10-15cm clay lining to inside of stone pit - press firmly to eliminate gaps
  2. Smooth interior to eliminate cracks and voids (cracks allow gas bypass, reducing efficiency)
  3. Allow to dry 1-2 days (cover with straw to prevent fast drying which causes cracking)
  4. Install air pipe: push clay tube through wall 20-30cm above pit bottom, angled slightly downward from outside
  5. Seal around pipe with clay mortar, compact thoroughly

Phase 4: Dome Construction (Days 4-5)

  1. Create dome frame: dome shape can be built free-form (easier) or using clay brick arch form
  2. Free-form: hand-stack clay "bricks" (20x10x5cm size, sun-dried or fired if available) in domed pattern, starting at rim and angling inward/upward at 10° tilt per layer
  3. Dome height: 0.6-0.8m above rim. Crown opens 0.3m diameter hole at top for flame exhaust
  4. Smooth interior clay joints, eliminate voids
  5. Cure 2-3 days slow dry (cover partially with straw to prevent cracking)

Phase 5: Surroundings Setup (Day 5)

  1. Build bellows system adjacent to furnace pit (see section below)
  2. Connect bellows to air pipe with wooden/clay tube
  3. Build ore/charcoal storage area 5m away (prevents flying sparks/ash from entering materials)
  4. Construct work area with anvil, tongs, hammer (needed for bloom consolidation)

Phase 6: Curing (Days 5-7)

Allow furnace to cure fully at room temperature for 2-3 days before first firing. Slow heating in first run: keep fire at 600-700°C for first 2 hours, gradually increase to full operating temperature. Rapid heating causes cracks/breaches.

Bellows System

Simple Wooden Box Bellows

Dimensions: 0.5m x 0.5m x 0.2m deep. Construction: wooden frame (pine or hardwood beams 5x5cm), two leather bags (or canvas for less-durable substitute) - one on each side of center baffle. Operation: push down on top, air drawn through lower leather flap valve into chamber; pull up, upper flap opens and air discharged through discharge tube into furnace pipe.

Each stroke: ~10-15 liters air. Steady strokes at 15 per minute = 150-225 liters/minute = 0.25-0.375 m³/minute adequate flow for bloomery.

Bellows Maintenance

Leather seals deteriorate 18-24 months of regular use. Signs of failure: pressure drops (bellows less responsive), air leaks visible/audible around seams. Replacement: soak old leather in water (1 day) to soften, remove, measure, cut replacement leather from large animal hide (cow), reinstall with copper rivets or stitching. Cost: minimal if hides available locally.

Alternative: Foot Bellows

If labor-intensive hand bellows infeasible: foot-operated pump using two foot pedals operates two chambers alternately. Operator stands on pedals rhythmically, hands free for other tasks. Less familiar but effective - used in traditional Asian smelting.

Charging and Operation Sequence

Charge Calculation

For 1m³ furnace pit (nominal capacity):

  • Roasted iron ore: 100-120kg (hematite Fe₂O₃ preferred)
  • Charcoal: 30-40kg (finely broken, 5-10cm pieces)
  • Clay/limestone flux (optional): 5-10kg (aids slag fusion)

Total volume: about 150-160L packed loosely, fits pit comfortably.

Charging Procedure (Day of Smelting)

  1. Preparation: Assemble all materials nearby. Charcoal should be dry (store under cover). Ore should be dry and roasted (800°C for 2 hours reduces some moisture, improves reactivity)
  2. Base layer: Place 10cm charcoal at pit bottom, spread evenly
  3. Alternate layers: Add 15cm ore layer, then 10cm charcoal, then ore, repeat until pit nearly full (10-15cm headspace at top)
  4. Start fire: Light top charcoal layer with kindling (dry wood), allow flame 30 minutes to establish
  5. Begin air blast: Start gentle bellows operation at 5-10 strokes/minute (slow, warming furnace gradually). Increase to 15 strokes/minute after 30 minutes when flame established
  6. Add ore: As materials burn down, periodically add new ore/charcoal layers (every 30-60 minutes) maintaining furnace fullness. Typical run: 4-6 ore additions over 8-hour period

Operational Duration

Continuous operation 8-12 hours typical. Constant bellows operation required. Requires 2-3 person team: (1) bellows operator (most physically demanding), (2) charcoal feeder/temperature monitor, (3) general labor/preparation.

Temperature Management

Maintaining 1100-1350°C optimal zone critical. Temperature control by adjusting air blast rate and fuel addition rate.

Temperature Indication (No Pyrometer)

Observe color through furnace pit opening/viewing port:

ColorTemperature (°C)StateAction
Dark red700-900Warming phaseIncrease bellows, add fuel
Bright cherry red900-1100Reduction zoneIncrease bellows slowly
Orange-red1100-1300Optimal zoneMaintain bellows, monitor
Yellow-orange1300-1400Too hotReduce bellows, check for escape
White (very rare)>1400Critical riskStop operation, inspect damage

Observe flame color at furnace top: bright yellow/orange flame = good combustion and likely good temperature. Dark/red flame = lower temperature. Flame should roar when bellows operated (sound of vigorous oxidation).

Heat Loss Prevention

Draft control: cover furnace top opening with straw/clay board to reduce heat loss, leave 10-15cm opening for flame exhaust. Excessive draft (windy location) cools furnace - build windbreak 2-3m away upwind. Cold air from bellows intake can cool furnace if located too close - locate bellows 2-3m away, insulate air pipe with straw wrapping to preheat air.

Bloom Extraction and Consolidation

After 8-12 hour smelting run, furnace must cool or bloom extracted while hot.

Hot Extraction (Traditional)

  1. Stop bellows: Cease air blast, allow furnace to rest 10-30 minutes
  2. Prepare tools: Have large flat prying bar (1m long steel rod or wooden beam), tongs, wooden sledge/hammer ready
  3. Drain slag: Use long rod to punch through one of the bottom drain holes, breaking the solidified slag plug. Molten slag flows out - be alert for danger (flows away but very hot, 1000°C+)
  4. Extract bloom: Using long prying bar and tongs, carefully work under the bloom mass. Try to lift entire mass in one piece - very heavy (50-100kg) and very hot (800-900°C). Place on flat stone or steel plate outdoors
  5. Cool briefly: Allow 2-5 minutes exposure to air to form initial oxide skin (prevents oxidation penetration), then douse with water if too hot to work

Consolidation Hammering

Extracted bloom is spongy (porous, contains trapped slag and gas). Hammering while hot squeezes out slag, consolidates metal:

  1. Reheat if cooled: If bloom cooled below red heat (<700°C), reheat in forge fire to bright cherry-red
  2. Strike: Place bloom on anvil (or large flat stone), strike with sledgehammer (4-6kg mass, 2-3 person operation: one operator hammer, one holding tongs repositioning bloom, one operating bellows for reheating). Strikes should be powerful, full-swing (not tapping)
  3. Pattern: Strike center, turn 90°, strike again, rotate, repeat in spiral pattern covering entire bloom
  4. Reheating: After 10-15 strikes, bloom cools below optimal working temperature (dark red), return to forge, reheat 15-30 minutes to bright cherry-red, resume hammering
  5. Duration: Total hammering 2-4 hours (repeated heating/hammering cycles). Continue until slag ceases to expel (observe no more slag particles flying from impacts) and bloom achieves dense appearance

Final product: 20-30kg usable wrought iron from ~100kg ore charge (20-30% efficiency). Metal is relatively pure iron, soft and ductile, suitable for smithing into tools/weapons.

Yield Calculations

Predicting iron output from ore charge helps assess viability.

Ore Analysis

Iron ore composition varies widely:

Ore TypeIron Content %Typical Fe2O3 %Gangue % (waste)
Hematite (Fe₂O₃)70%100~20% SiO₂
Magnetite (Fe₃O₄)72%89~15% SiO₂
Limonite (Fe₂O₃·3H₂O)40-60%Varies~40% H₂O + SiO₂
Bog ore20-40%Varies~50% organic matter

Example: 100kg hematite (70% iron content) = 70kg pure iron. Bloomery efficiency ~25-30% = 17.5-21kg usable bloom extracted. After 10% hammering loss (slag expulsion) = 15-19kg final product.

Calculation: (Ore mass × iron content % × furnace efficiency) - hammering loss = final iron.

Energy Requirements

Charcoal consumption: 0.3-0.5kg charcoal per kg iron produced. 100kg ore charge requires 20-40kg charcoal, roughly 50-60 kg wood needed (wood carbonizes at ~20-25% efficiency). Heat value of charcoal ~29 MJ/kg; furnace captures ~20-25% as useful heat (rest lost as exhaust). Total energy: 1000-1500 MJ for 20kg iron = 50-75 MJ per kg iron - substantial energy input.

Maintenance and Reuse

Post-Run Inspection

After each smelting run, allow furnace to cool completely (24-48 hours). Inspect interior for:

  • Cracks in clay (common, resealable)
  • Breaches in wall (rare if constructed properly)
  • Slag adherence to walls (hardens as insulating layer, protects clay underneath)
  • Damage to dome (visible from interior by looking up through drain holes)

Crack Repair

If small cracks (<1cm) observed: seal with clay mortar (same mix as lining). Clean crack with tool, wet thoroughly, force mortar deep into crack, smooth flush with surrounding surface, allow 1-2 days drying. Furnace ready for next run.

Reuse Life

Clay bloomery furnaces typically last 5-10 years with good maintenance (annual repairs, proper curing between runs). Stone backing prevents rapid deterioration. When clay becomes severely eroded (>20% thickness loss), rebuild interior lining (2-3 days labor). Slag layer buildup actually helps - provides additional insulation and wear protection.

Seasonal Considerations

Bloomeries work year-round but require more fuel in winter (furnace loses more heat). Spring/summer optimal - better weather for extended operation, less heating burden. Cold weather operation possible with additional charcoal allowance (20-30% more fuel) and covering furnace top to reduce heat loss.

Safety Critical: Bloomery smelting is extremely hot work. Burns are common from splashing slag or contact with bloom. Charcoal monoxide exposure (from bellows operation) can cause headaches - ensure good ventilation. Never leave furnace unattended during operation - can runaway to dangerous temperatures. Have water supply nearby for emergencies. Iron oxide dust from ore can cause respiration issues with prolonged exposure - dust mask recommended during charging.
