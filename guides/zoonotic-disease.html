Rabies

Rabies Virus Transmission

Transmission: saliva (bite/scratch/mucous membrane contact). Animals: dogs (90%+ human deaths), bats, raccoons, skunks, foxes, coyotes. Incubation: typically 1-3 months but highly variable (weeks to years, longer incubation = slower progression). No treatment once symptomatic (nearly 100% fatal); prevention critical via post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP).

Clinical Presentation

Prodromal phase (2-10 days): Fever, malaise, headache, sensory changes at bite site (tingling, burning). Active disease phase: Encephalitis symptoms (agitation, aggression, hypersalivation, hydrophobia [fear of swallowing], hallucinations, delirium). OR paralytic rabies: descending paralysis from bite site. Terminal phase: Coma, death typically from respiratory failure.

Post-Exposure Prophylaxis (PEP)

Must start immediately after exposure (bite/scratch/mucous membrane contact with saliva from high-risk animal). Delays >24 hours reduce efficacy. Components: 1) Wound washing (vigorous with soap and water, removes virus, very important). 2) Rabies immunoglobulin (RIG) infiltrated around wound if available (20IU/kg). 3) Rabies vaccine: series injections (traditionally 5 injections; newer 4-dose schedules available if supplies limited).

Animal Assessment

If animal caught: quarantine for observation (10 days for dogs/cats). If animal shows no symptoms after 10 days: likely not rabid, but observation recommended for full incubation period. If animal shows symptoms/dies: must assume rabid and administer PEP. If animal cannot be captured: assume rabid and give PEP.

Leptospirosis

Transmission and Reservoir

Transmission: urine from infected animals (rats, mice, livestock) in water/wet environments. Contamination: entry through breaks in skin, mucous membranes, eyes. Common in tropical/subtropical areas, after flooding, in occupations with animal contact (farm workers, miners, veterinarians).

Clinical Presentation (Biphasic)

Leptospiremic phase (Days 1-7): Fever, headache, myalgia, chills. Immune phase (Days 8-30+): Fever defervesces, then meningitis symptoms (headache, stiff neck, altered mental status), liver injury (jaundice), kidney injury (oliguria), pulmonary hemorrhage. Severe form (Weil's disease): jaundice, renal failure, hemorrhagic manifestations, mortality 5-15%.

Diagnosis and Treatment

Diagnosis: serology (antibodies after 5-7 days), blood culture (first week), PCR if available. Treatment: doxycycline (100mg BID) if mild and early (within 5-7 days); levofloxacin (500mg daily) alternative. Severe: IV ceftriaxone or penicillin G. Supportive: fluid management (careful with renal failure), treating complications (kidney support if needed). Prognosis: mild cases resolve; severe cases have high mortality if untreated.

Prevention

Avoid contaminated water (especially floodwaters), maintain rodent control (reduce reservoir), wear protective footwear in high-risk areas, treat water (boiling, chlorination). In survival: avoid open water sources if rodent activity high, cover cuts before water exposure.

Brucellosis

Transmission

Transmission: contact with infected animals/products (unpasteurized dairy, undercooked meat, placental tissue from infected animals). Occupations: herders, slaughterhouse workers, veterinarians, farmers. Incubation: 1-8 weeks variable.

Clinical Features

Fever (often undulating—rises and falls cyclically), night sweats, fatigue, malaise, headache, myalgia. Chronic brucellosis: arthralgia (especially knees, hips, shoulders), depression, relapsing fever pattern. Diagnosis: serology, blood culture (can be slow-growing). Treatment: doxycycline (100mg BID x 6 weeks) PLUS rifampin (600mg daily x 6 weeks) for systemic infection. In acute: fluids, fever management, NSAIDs for pain.

Chronic Disease Management

Some patients develop chronic form (chronic fatigue, arthralgia, depression) lasting months to years. Requires prolonged antibiotic courses, supportive care, psychological support. Prognosis: generally good if treated early; chronic form more difficult.

Anthrax

Anthrax Routes and Presentations

Cutaneous (skin): Most common (95% of natural cases). Black necrotic eschar (hence "anthrax"), painless, surrounded by edema. Pulmonary (inhalation): Severe; initial flu-like symptoms progressing to respiratory distress, mediastinal widening, shock, high mortality (>50% untreated). Gastrointestinal: Diarrhea, abdominal pain, high mortality if untreated.

Treatment

Cutaneous: ciprofloxacin (500mg BID x 7-10 days) or doxycycline (100mg BID x 7-10 days). Pulmonary/systemic: IV antimicrobials (ciprofloxacin, doxycycline) + supportive care. Early treatment critical (cure rate >90% for cutaneous if treated promptly). Vaccination available (for high-risk persons); no vaccine for post-exposure usually (antibiotics sufficient for PEP).

Biosafety Concerns

Anthrax spores highly stable in environment; contaminated soil remains infectious for decades. In survival scenario with animal exposure: assume zoonotic source, treat with respect, proper decontamination/burial of animal remains. Natural anthrax transmission rare in developed countries; most concern post-collapse era from environmental spores.

Tick-Borne Zoonotic Diseases

Lyme Disease (Borrelia burgdorferi)

Transmission: Ixodes tick (small, difficult to detect). Symptoms: erythema migrans (expanding red rash from tick site, classic bullseye appearance), fever, arthralgia, Bell's palsy, carditis possible. Diagnosis: serology (late stage), culture (early, rare), PCR. Treatment: doxycycline (100mg BID x 14-21 days early stage) or amoxicillin if pregnant/pediatric. Late stage (arthritis, carditis): longer courses (28 days) or IV ceftriaxone if severe.

Rocky Mountain Spotted Fever (R. rickettsii)

Transmission: American dog tick or wood tick. Symptoms: sudden fever, headache (severe), myalgia, rash (initially macular, becomes petechial, classic on palms/soles). Mortality: 20% untreated. Diagnosis: serology, immunostain of rash. Treatment: doxycycline (100mg BID) IMMEDIATELY if suspected (do NOT wait for confirmation). Prognosis: excellent if treated early; mortality increases with delays.

Tick Fever (Q Fever)

Transmission: Coxiella burnetii (highly infectious, respiratory droplets or animal contact). Acute: fever, headache, fatigue, pneumonia. Chronic: endocarditis (in patients with valvular disease), high mortality if untreated. Diagnosis: serology, PCR. Treatment: doxycycline + fluoroquinolone (for chronic Q fever). Prevention: tick avoidance crucial (vaccine available for occupations at high-risk).

Tick Bite Prevention

Wear long sleeves/pants in brush, use insect repellent (DEET 20-30%), perform daily body checks (ticks often crawl for hours before attaching), remove ticks correctly: grasp close to skin with tweezers, pull straight out (don't twist/crush—risks releasing pathogen into bite).

Prevention Strategies

Animal Contact Precautions

  • Avoid contact with wild animals; use barriers (gloves, clothing)
  • When handling domestic animals: use care, watch for illness signs
  • Hunting/butchering: wear gloves, wash hands, cook meat thoroughly
  • Avoid bites/scratches; treat any wound immediately (wash, disinfect)
  • Educate children: no wild animal contact, report unusual animal behavior

Waterborne Transmission Prevention

Boil water from potentially contaminated sources (leptospirosis, giardia from wildlife), avoid swimming in flood-affected areas (leptospirosis risk), protect water supplies from animal contamination (cover well, fence off livestock), maintain sanitation distance from water (prevent animal waste runoff).

Meat and Dairy Safety

Cook meat thoroughly (internal temperature 165°F/74°C minimum), avoid unpasteurized dairy (brucellosis, tuberculosis), proper butchering hygiene (separate cutting surfaces, hand washing, no cross-contamination), store meat properly (cold chain maintained).

Post-Exposure Response Protocol

Immediate Actions After Exposure

  1. Wash wound aggressively with soap and water (removes surface pathogens)
  2. Determine exposure type: bite, scratch, mucous membrane exposure
  3. Identify animal (species, if possible capture for observation)
  4. Consider which zoonotic diseases relevant (geography, animal type, symptoms if any)
  5. Assess need for rabies PEP (high-risk animal, significant exposure)
  6. Assess need for other prophylaxis/treatment (depends on disease risk)

Rabies PEP Timeline

Administer as soon as possible after exposure. Efficacy drops significantly if delayed >24 hours. Beyond 10-14 days: questionable benefit (disease may already incubating). In survival scenario: treat immediately if animal bite/exposure (do not delay waiting for confirmation). Rabies PEP unlikely harmful if given unnecessarily; risk of NOT treating when indicated is 100% mortality.

Prophylaxis Decision Tree

High-risk animal bite (dog, bat, raccoon): Rabies PEP. Unknown animal/no animal available: Rabies PEP (err on side of caution). Animal observation possible: Keep animal under observation 10 days; if no symptoms, rabies unlikely (but some risk remains). Known healthy animal: Wounds still treated (standard precautions), rabies PEP not necessary if documented healthy.

Critical Zoonotic Disease Warnings

  • Rabies exposure = medical emergency. PEP must begin immediately; delay >24 hours significantly reduces efficacy. Once symptomatic, essentially 100% fatal—prevention is critical.
  • Anthrax inhalation = severe respiratory disease with high mortality. Early treatment (within days) critical; delay = likely death.
  • Rocky Mountain Spotted Fever = mortality 20% untreated. Do NOT wait for serological confirmation; treat immediately if clinical suspicion and risk area.
  • Anthrax bioterrorism concern = in post-collapse scenario, environmental anthrax spores could pose risk. Proper environmental decontamination essential.

Common Zoonotic Disease Errors

  • Delayed rabies PEP: Even small delays reduce efficacy; give immediately after bite.
  • Improper tick removal: Crushing tick spreads infection; remove with tweezers, don't squeeze.
  • Ignoring flu-like illness after animal exposure: Could indicate zoonotic disease; assume risk and monitor/treat.
  • Underestimating chronic disease: Some zoonotic infections cause chronic symptoms; long-term antibiotic courses may be needed.
  • Contact spread assumption: Most zoonotic diseases NOT person-to-person; isolation not needed (except plague, if pneumonic). Focus on contact precautions with infected animals/tissues.
