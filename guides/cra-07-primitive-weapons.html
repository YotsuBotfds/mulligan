Bow Construction

Bows are among oldest weapons; properly made bows kill large game. Single bow takes 20-40 hours to craft but lasts decades with care.

Wood Selection:

  • Yew: Superior elasticity; traditional bow wood. White sapwood bends easily; dark heartwood provides backing support. Limited availability in some regions.
  • Ash: Good flexibility; readily available in temperate forests. Less dense than yew but adequate for hunting bows.
  • Elm: Acceptable alternative; strong heartwood provides backing support.
  • Avoid: Pine, spruce (too flexible, won't store energy); oak (too brittle, shatters)

Stave Preparation:

  1. Select straight-grained branch 2-3 inches diameter, 5-6 feet long
  2. Split log (quarter or half—avoid pith which creates weakness)
  3. Allow drying 2-4 weeks (green wood becomes less brittle as water evaporates)
  4. Scrape/plane to remove bark and partial sapwood (exposes uniform surface)
  5. Shape to taper: thicker at center (handle), thinner at tips

Bow Profile Design:

Recurved bow (tips curve away from archer) stores more energy in shorter length. Straight bow is simpler to make. Longbow (6+ feet tall) efficient; shorter bow (3-4 feet) easier to handle in brush.

Bow Assembly:

  1. Create handle (grip) at center: 1.2 inches diameter, 4-5 inches long
  2. Taper limbs: reduce diameter from handle to tip (1/8 inch at tips)
  3. Test thickness by attempting to bend (should bend uniformly without cracking)
  4. Create nocks (grooves for string at tip ends)
  5. String with sinew, plant fiber, or manufactured cord (minimum 50 lb pull weight)
  6. Test draw: archer should draw smoothly 28 inches with good back muscles

Bow Draw Weight & Hunting Effectiveness:

Arrow Crafting

Quality arrows determine bow effectiveness. Poorly made arrows miss targets; well-made arrows fly true and penetrate deeply.

Shaft Material:

  • Straight wood: Hazel, birch, or ash. Requires selecting completely straight sections. Soak then straighten while drying (tedious but effective).
  • Bamboo: Natural straight grain; naturally light weight; strongest material available
  • Cane/reed: Adequate if arrow selected for straightness

Arrow Construction Sequence:

  1. Shaft selection: Choose straight material 5-6mm diameter, 28 inches long
  2. Fletching: Attach feathers (goose, turkey, or other large bird feathers) at rear. Typical: three fletches at 120-degree spacing, 2-3 inches long
  3. Binding: Wrap plant fiber or sinew around fletches to secure them (0.5 inches above and below attachment)
  4. Nock: Cut notch at rear (fits on bowstring). Typical notch: 1/2 inch deep, fits string snugly
  5. Broadhead attachment: Tie or glue stone/bone/metal point to front (see point-making guides below)
  6. Spine test: Arrow should deflect slightly under own weight when held horizontally

Arrow Points:

Bone points: Sharp, piercing well but brittle. Typical size: 1-2 inches long, small barbs for grip.

Flint/stone points: Extremely sharp but fragile. Smaller than bone; require fine knapping skill.

Metal points: If available, iron points vastly superior. Triple the penetration of bone at same draw weight.

Accuracy Practice:

Begin shooting at 10-15m range. Groups should tighten as archer develops muscle memory. Expect 10-30m effective hunting distance after 50+ hours practice. Competition archers achieve excellent accuracy; survival hunters need basic competence.

Atlatl (Spear-Thrower)

Atlatl extends arm length mechanically, increasing spear velocity 2-3x compared to hand throw. Effective hunting weapon for mid-size game.

Atlatl Construction:

  1. Shaft: Hardwood, 20-24 inches long, 1 inch diameter. Must be straight and balanced.
  2. Handle: Carved groove fitting archer's hand. Proper grip critical for control.
  3. Hook/socket: Rear end has hook or socket receiving spear butt. Hook design: 1-inch projection from shaft.
  4. Counterweight: Optional stone or bone weight 5-10 inches from hook (improves leverage and accuracy). Weight: 2-3 pounds typical.
  5. Strap: Leather or fiber strap connecting atlatl to archer's fingers (allows controlled release).

Spear Design (For Atlatl Use):

Shorter than hand-thrown spear (5-7 feet vs. 8-12 feet). Must balance on atlatl hook. Weight concentrated forward (heavy point). Rear butt fits hook precisely (sloppy fit reduces accuracy).

Throwing Technique:

Overhead motion similar to baseball pitch. Atlatl extends arm and stores energy in flex. Release occurs when arm extended forward; hook releases spear with high velocity. Effective distance: 40-80m for accuracy; kills at 30-50m.

Advantages vs. Bow:

  • Heavier projectile penetrates deeper (larger game capability)
  • Shorter learning curve (basic competence in 20-30 hours)
  • Less training required for consistent accuracy
  • Easier to manufacture (fewer precision requirements)

Sling Construction & Use

Sling propels projectile (stone or clay ball) with surprising velocity. Roman soldiers with slings were formidable soldiers. Concealable weapon requiring minimal materials.

Sling Construction:

  1. Pouch: Leather or woven fabric rectangle, 4x3 inches
  2. Cords: Two cords of equal length (24-30 inches), attached to pouch ends
  3. Release knot: One cord has knot tied at end (held by pinky/ring finger). Other cord has loop (held by thumb/forefinger).
  4. Ammunition: Lead balls (1-2 ounces) ideal; smooth stones adequate

Sling Operation:

  1. Place projectile in pouch
  2. Hold sling by both cords (knot in one hand, loop in other)
  3. Swing overhead in figure-eight motion (builds rotational speed)
  4. Complete 2-3 full rotations
  5. Release loop-end cord; knot remains held
  6. Projectile launches at 60-90 m/s velocity

Accuracy & Lethality:

Effective distance: 30-60m for accurate shooting (difficult to master). Head shot kills; body shots stun or injure. Skilled slingers (professional soldiers) achieved remarkable accuracy. Modern recreation slingers consistently hit targets at 40m.

Spear & Lance Making

Spears are most fundamental hunting weapons: simple, reliable, effective. Multiple points allow hunting with multiple spears without constant retrieval.

Hunting Spear (Hand-Thrown or Jabbing):

  1. Shaft: Straight wood 8-12 feet long, 1-1.5 inches diameter. Ash preferred (flexible, won't break).
  2. Balance: Point weight 1-2 ounces; total spear 3-5 pounds. Balance point 1/3 from point end (ensures point-forward flight).
  3. Point attachment: Bind bone/stone point with sinew using spiral binding (wraps prevent point rotation).
  4. Grip section: Leave 1-2 inches of shaft bare (hand contact area). Grip should be slightly thinner than shaft (easier to grasp).

Fishing Spear (Multi-Pronged):

2-4 bone/wood prongs spreading 4-6 inches wide at point. Prongs 6-8 inches long; slightly barbed to catch fish. Spring assembly holds prongs together until fish impact spreads them (holding fish on prongs). Effective to 2-3 meter depth in clear water.

War Lance (Mounted Use):

Long spear (10-12 feet) designed for mounted warriors. Heavy point (4-8 ounces) stored energy from horse momentum. Shaft tapered to control weight distribution. Point narrower than hunting spear (penetrates armor).

Mechanical Trap Systems

Traps allow hunting without constant presence. Effective for small-to-medium game. Design determines species caught.

Deadfall Trap:

  1. Framework: Heavy log, rock, or wooden structure (10-100 pounds depending on game)
  2. Trigger: Balanced stick holding deadfall suspended above game path
  3. Bait: Placed under deadfall, attached to trigger stick
  4. Operation: Animal pulls bait; trigger releases; deadfall falls killing animal instantly
  5. Setting: Requires careful balance; deadfall must fall cleanly without getting stuck

Snare Trap:

  1. Loop: Cordage formed into running loop (diameter matches target animal)
  2. Trigger: Hook or bent stick supporting loop, releasing when disturbed
  3. Anchor: Secured to fixed point (tree, stake) preventing escape
  4. Placement: Set where animals naturally travel (game trail)
  5. Effectiveness: Strangles or holds animal until trapper arrives

Pit Trap:

  1. Dig: Hole 2-3 meters deep in game trail
  2. Disguise: Cover with light branches and leaves (hides hole from sight)
  3. Stakes: Optional sharpened stakes at bottom (increases lethality)
  4. Effectiveness: Large animals (deer, boar) fall and are trapped. Small animals may escape.

Fish Trap (Weir):

Stone/wood structure in stream creating funnel leading into enclosure. Fish naturally swim upriver; funnel design prevents escape. Effective in streams with seasonal fish migrations.

Defensive Weapons

Shield Design:

Wooden frame (1.5-2m tall for body protection) covered with hide or woven material. Central boss (metal or wood) deflects blows. Weight: 4-8 pounds (requires strength for extended holding). Effective against primitive weapons (spears, arrows).

Club/Mace:

Hardwood shaft with heavy head (stone, bone, or metal). Simple and lethal. No maintenance required (unlike swords which need sharpening). Effective up to 3-4 meter range if mounted on horse.

Armor Concepts:

Leather armor (boiled/hardened) deflects slashing attacks. Multiple layers provide protection. Heavy armor (metal) is superior but requires metalworking capability (see forging guides). Helmet reduces head strike severity.

Safety Considerations

Primitive weapons are lethal. Accidents cause death or permanent injury. Practice safety protocols always.

Archery Safety:

  • Never nock arrow unless shooting
  • Always check target and beyond for people
  • Shoot only on designated range with clear 100m+ safety zone
  • Retrieve arrows only when no one shooting
  • Never draw bow at person unless defending life

Spear/Atlatl Safety:

  • Clear throwing area completely before using
  • Establish 100m+ radius safety zone
  • Warn people before throwing
  • Never retrieve while others might throw

Trap Safety:

  • Mark trap locations (prevents accidental triggering)
  • Check traps daily (prevents spoilage, unnecessary animal suffering)
  • Use minimum force necessary (kills instantly vs. slow death)
  • Remove unsuccessful traps to prevent non-target captures

Common Mistakes & Solutions

Mistake 1: Poorly Shaped Bow Limbs

Error: Bows with uneven limb thickness. Thicker limb stores more energy, bends less, resulting in off-target shots.

Solution: Test bend by hand before completing bow. Both limbs should bend identically under same force. Sand/scrape to equalize thickness.

Mistake 2: Improper Arrow Spine

Error: Arrows too stiff or too flexible for bow draw weight. Stiff arrows fly left; flexible arrows fly right.

Solution: Test arrows by bending by hand (should match bow stiffness feel). Adjust fletching balance or weight to correct flight.

Mistake 3: Weak Trap Construction

Error: Animal escapes trap due to loose binding or weak trigger mechanism. Wasted effort and animal suffering.

Solution: Test trigger before setting live bait. Use multiple binding/rope passes. Ensure deadfall/snare cannot be pushed open by animal thrashing.
