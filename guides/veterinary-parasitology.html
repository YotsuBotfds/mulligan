Internal Parasites (Helminths)

Gastrointestinal Nematodes (Roundworms)

Common species: Cooperia, Haemonchus, Oesophagostomum (livestock); Toxocara, Ancylostoma (dogs/cats). Transmission: fecal-oral (grazing contaminated pasture, coprophagy). Symptoms: diarrhea, anemia (especially Haemonchus—blood-feeding), weight loss, poor growth, pale mucous membranes. Diagnosis: fecal float/McMaster count (identifies eggs, estimates burden). Treatment: anthelmintics (deworming agents).

Tapeworms (Cestodes)

Transmission: intermediate hosts (fleas for pets; arthropods for livestock). Symptoms: variable (often asymptomatic in adults; more severe in young animals). Segments visible in stool (rice-grain appearance). Diagnosis: visual identification of segments, fecal exam. Treatment: praziquantel or similar tapeworm-specific anthelmintics.

Flukes (Trematodes)

Transmission: through water (metacercariae on plants). Common: liver flukes (Fasciola), lung flukes. Symptoms: anemia, weight loss, poor production, potentially fatal if heavy burden. Diagnosis: fecal exam (eggs), serology. Treatment: specific anthelmintics (triclabendazole for liver flukes). Prevention: eliminate snail hosts (if possible), prevent water contamination.

External Parasites

Ectoparasites (Lice, Mites, Fleas)

Lice: Blood-feeding or biting. Transmission: direct contact, fomites (shared equipment). Symptoms: itching, hair loss, scabbing, reduced performance. Mites: Cause mange/scabies. Highly contagious. Sarcoptes mites (burrow into skin—sarcoptic mange); Psoroptes/Chorioptes (surface—psoroptic/chorioptic mange). Symptoms: severe itching, hair loss, skin thickening/cracking, secondary infection. Fleas: Irritation, anemia (heavy infestation), tapeworm transmission (flea ingestion). Diagnosis: visual identification, scraping (mites), fecal exam for tapeworm transmission.

Tick Infestation

Ticks transmit disease (see zoonotic disease section) but also cause direct damage. Heavy tick burden: anemia, tick paralysis (rare, neurologic). Removal: grasp near mouthparts with tweezers, pull straight out. Do NOT crush (releases pathogens). Disinfect bite site. Tick control: pasture management (remove brush, tick habitat), rotating grazing, seasonal dipping if access to chemical control.

Fly Infestation

Botflies: Larval migration under skin, parasitic burden. Screwworms: Flesh-eating larvae in open wounds (catastrophic, high mortality if untreated). Stable flies: Blood-feeders, painful bites, reduced production. Treatment: fly control (elimination of breeding sites), wound dressing (prevent fly access to screwworm wounds), specific anthelmintics (ivermectin covers some internal parasites + some external parasites).

Protozoan Parasites

Coccidiosis

Transmission: fecal-oral (contaminated water/feed). Common in young animals (ruminants, poultry, rabbits). Symptoms: diarrhea (often bloody), anemia, dehydration, weight loss, potential death if severe/untreated. Diagnosis: fecal exam (oocysts). Treatment: coccidiostats (sulfonamides, amprolium if available) or toltrazuril. Prevention: sanitation, separate young animals (reduce transmission), rotation grazing, stress management (improves immunity).

Giardiasis

Transmission: cysts in water. Symptoms: diarrhea, weight loss, malabsorption. Common in young ruminants, less severe in adults. Diagnosis: fecal antigen, PCR. Treatment: metronidazole, albendazole. Prevention: clean water, sanitation.

Cryptosporidiosis

Transmission: oocysts in water/feces. Highly contagious. Symptoms: severe watery diarrhea, weight loss, dehydration. Severe in young/immunocompromised. Diagnosis: fecal exam (small oocysts, difficult to see), special staining. Treatment: supportive care, fluid/electrolyte replacement. Antiparasitic: paromomycin sometimes helps. Prevention: sanitation, isolation of affected animals.

Diagnosis Methods

Fecal Examination

Direct smear: Fresh feces on slide with saline, examine for motile parasites, larvae, oocysts. Flotation: Feces mixed with flotation solution (sugar, salt, other solutions); parasites float to surface, collected on coverslip. Most practical field method. Sedimentation: Heavy parasites sediment; used for some flukes/nematodes. McMaster count: Quantifies eggs per gram feces (indicates parasite burden). More labor-intensive, used for monitoring/research.

Visual Inspection

Gross observation of feces for: tapeworm segments, nematode worms (especially post-treatment). Visual identification of ectoparasites: lice, mites, ticks. Skin scrapings: for mite identification (material under microscope). Flea combing: collect fleas/flea dirt on paper, identify.

Clinical Signs and Herd History

Symptoms suggestive of parasites: diarrhea, weight loss, anemia (pale mucous membranes, pale conjunctiva), reduced production, poor growth in young animals. History: recent grazing contaminated pasture, introduction of new animals, seasonal occurrence. Parasite prevalence varies seasonally and geographically.

Dosing Considerations

Weight-based dosing critical: under-dosing = ineffective treatment + resistance development. Over-dosing = toxicity. Accurate weighing essential; estimate if scale unavailable (visual estimate less reliable). Fatting animals: calculate dose on current weight (may need repeat as weight increases). Young animals: metabolize drugs faster; may need higher mg/kg or more frequent dosing.

Treatment Timing and Monitoring

Treat: before grazing season (spring deworming), mid-season if burden high, strategically before winter if resources limited. Repeat: 2-3 weeks after initial treatment (targets emerging larvae). Monitor: fecal exam post-treatment (confirms efficacy), observe clinical improvement (weight gain, coat quality, reduced diarrhea). Failure to respond: suggests resistance (switch drug class) or incorrect diagnosis.

Prevention Strategies

Pasture Management

  • Rotation grazing: move animals before parasite larvae mature (species-dependent, typically 3-4 weeks minimum)
  • Alternate species: different species have different parasites; rotating pasture use reduces buildup
  • Spelling (resting) pastures: allows larvae to die (no host to infect); >2 months typical
  • Clean pastures for young animals: move young animals to uncontaminated pasture (low parasite risk)
  • Avoid wet areas: some parasites favor moisture (flukes need water, many nematodes survive better in wet conditions)

Sanitation

Separate water from feces: prevent water contamination (reduces GI parasite transmission). Clean feeders/waterers: remove accumulated feces. Isolate infected/treated animals: prevent cross-contamination. Proper waste disposal: prevent pasture contamination from manure piles.

Nutrition and Immunity

Good nutrition supports parasite resistance: adequate protein, minerals (copper, selenium, zinc), vitamins. Young animals: need nutrition support for immunity development. Older animals: may tolerate higher parasite loads if well-nourished. Stress management: stress reduces immunity (transport, weather changes, mixed herd groups); minimize stress during high-parasite season.

Selective Treatment

Not all animals need treatment: some resistance develops naturally. Treat high-risk groups: young animals, pregnant/lactating females, immunocompromised. Monitor non-treated animals: if disease develops, treat then. Reduces anthelmintic use, slows resistance development.

Species-Specific Considerations

Ruminants (Cattle, Sheep, Goats)

GI nematodes primary concern. Haemonchus (blood-feeder): anemia, weakness; requires frequent monitoring. Coccidiosis: primarily young animals (post-weaning age critical). Flukes: wet areas/snail habitats. Lice: common in housed animals (winter housing increases transmission). Ectoparasites: seasonal variation (higher spring/fall).

Poultry

Internal: large roundworms, tapeworms, cecal worms. Coccidiosis: devastating in young birds (chicks, poults). External: lice, mites (especially Northern fowl mite—anemia/death if heavy). Treatment: often added to water or feed (convenient for flock treatment). Sanitation critical: deep litter becomes contaminated.

Dogs and Cats

Roundworms: puppies/kittens commonly infected (maternal transmission possible). Hookworms: severe in young animals (anemia). Tapeworms: common, often from fleas. Heartworms: transmitted by mosquitoes (prevent with ivermectin if endemic). Fleas/ticks: constant risk (prevention essential = year-round in many climates). Intestinal protozoa: giardia common in young animals.

Critical Parasitology Safety

  • Untreated parasite burden = poor performance/weight loss/potential death. Treat appropriately; don't assume animals will outgrow parasites.
  • Anthelmintic resistance = emerging problem. Avoid repeated dosing of same drug class; rotate drug classes, use combination therapy if available.
  • Overdosing = toxicity. Accurate weight critical; some drugs have narrow safety margins (morantel, some antiparasitic combinations).
  • Zoonotic parasites = some animal parasites infect humans (toxoplasmosis from cats, some worms from contact). Use hygiene with infected animals.

Common Parasitology Errors

  • Under-dosing: Ineffective treatment + resistance development. Weigh accurately, calculate proper dose.
  • Repeated same-class dosing: Promotes resistance. Rotate drug classes annually.
  • Ignoring young animals: Assume all young animals need parasite management; fecal exam confirms need.
  • Over-treatment: Not all animals/situations need treatment; selective treatment reduces drug use, resistance risk.
  • Neglecting sanitation: Treatment without hygiene = reinfection. Combined approach essential.
