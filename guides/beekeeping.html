Why Keep Bees

Honey

Honey is one of nature's most remarkable foods and preservatives. A single colony can produce 30-100 pounds of surplus honey annually, depending on region and climate. Honey contains approximately 64 calories per tablespoon and consists primarily of simple sugars (fructose, glucose) that provide immediate energy.

Preservation Properties

Honey's low moisture content (17-20%) creates an environment hostile to bacterial growth. Its high osmotic pressure literally dehydrates microorganisms. This makes honey one of the only foods that never spoils‚Äîarchaeological honey from Egypt remains viable after 3,000+ years. For long-term storage, keep honey in sealed containers away from humidity. Honey may crystallize over time; gently warm to restore fluidity without damaging properties.

Medicinal Uses

  • Wound healing: Raw honey's antibacterial enzymes produce hydrogen peroxide, accelerating tissue repair
  • Cough suppressant: Studies show raw honey rivals over-the-counter cough syrups
  • Digestive support: Honey contains prebiotics that feed beneficial gut bacteria
  • Anti-inflammatory: Reduces inflammation in throat and respiratory tract
  • Energy source: The glucose hits bloodstream immediately; fructose sustains longer

Beeswax

Produced by worker bees to build comb, beeswax is a waxy secretion from glands on their abdomen. One pound of beeswax requires 8-10 pounds of honey to produce. Unlike honey, beeswax can be melted, molded, and re-used indefinitely without degrading.

Beeswax Applications

  • Candles: Burns slower and cleaner than paraffin, produces bright flame, smells pleasant
  • Waterproofing: Rub on fabric, leather, rope‚Äîrepels water and resists rot
  • Sealant: Seals jars, protects cut wood, creates weather-tight seals on containers
  • Furniture polish: Creates natural, food-safe finish on wood
  • Medicinal salves: Base for lip balm, healing creams, muscle rubs
  • Thread conditioning: Wax threads before sewing‚Äîreduces fraying, increases durability

Pollination Services

Even without harvest, a single colony provides tremendous value through pollination. Bees pollinate roughly 75% of global food crops. A healthy hive can visit 50,000-100,000 flowers in a single day. This translates directly to increased yields in nearby vegetable gardens, fruit trees, and crops. Many commercial agriculture operations keep bees specifically for pollination, often earning more from pollination contracts than honey sales.

Propolis

A sticky resin collected from tree buds, propolis serves as hive sealant and antibacterial agent. It contains potent compounds with antimicrobial, antifungal, and anti-inflammatory properties. Bees use it to seal cracks and prevent disease. Humans can harvest it by scraping propolis from the hive interior (wear gloves‚Äîit stains). Create a tincture by soaking in alcohol for 2-4 weeks, then straining. Use topically for wounds or internally in small doses for immune support.

Sustainability Note: One colony produces surplus honey/wax only after the bees have stored enough for their own survival. Always leave adequate stores for winter‚Äînever take all the honey or the colony will starve.

Bee Biology

Colony Structure

A honey bee colony is a superorganism‚Äînot merely a group of individual insects, but a collective entity with distributed cognition and remarkable communication.

The Queen

  • Only fertile female in the colony
  • Produces pheromones that regulate colony behavior and reproduction
  • Lays 1,500-2,000 eggs daily during peak season
  • Lives 2-5 years (compared to workers at 6 weeks)
  • Fed exclusively royal jelly throughout her life

Worker Bees

  • Sterile females performing all labor: nursing, building, foraging, defense
  • Live 6 weeks in summer, 4-6 months in winter
  • Progress through roles based on age: house bee (0-21 days), field bee (21-42 days)
  • Possess specialized structures: pollen baskets on hind legs, nectar stomach separate from digestive stomach
  • Perform approximately 12 jobs during their lifetime

Drone Bees

  • Males, produced from unfertilized eggs
  • Sole purpose: mate with virgin queens (rarely successful‚Äîonly 0.1-1% mate)
  • Larger than workers, cannot sting, cannot feed themselves
  • Expelled from hive in fall to conserve winter resources
  • Live 40-60 days

Annual Cycle

Winter (December-February): Cluster in tight ball around queen, vibrating to generate heat (maintain 92-95¬∞F in center). Consumption dramatically reduced; colony living off stored honey. No brood rearing. Workers live 4-6 months.

Early Spring (March-April): Queen begins laying, brood increases, population grows. Bees consume significant stores for energy to feed larvae. Critical period‚Äîinadequate stores = starvation. Assess hive weight.

Late Spring (May): Peak egg laying. Population explosion‚Äîhive grows from 10,000 to 40,000+ bees. Honey flow begins. Risk of swarming increases. Swarm cells visible when colony feels crowded.

Summer (June-August): Peak activity. Honey production. Multiple brood patterns. Continuous laying. Heat and humidity managed through fanning. Foragers work intensely. Drones present. Summer workers live only 5-6 weeks despite abundant food.

Early Fall (September): Brood rearing declines. Drones expelled. Colony shifts to preparation mode: reducing entrance, propolizing cracks, binding down equipment. Last generation of long-lived winter bees produced.

Late Fall (October-November): Brood nearly ceases. Males completely gone. Population stabilizes at 10,000-20,000 (minimum viable overwintering population). Final stores assessed. Insulation added. Entrance reduced.

Swarming Behavior

Swarming is the colony's reproductive strategy‚Äîwhen the hive feels overcrowded, it splits. A portion of the population (typically 30-50% plus the old queen) departs to establish a new colony. This is a critical window: swarms are docile (gorged with honey, seeking new home, not defensive) and vulnerable to capture.

Swarm signs: Multiple queen cells (large, peanut-shaped), reduced laying, bees clustering outside the hive, agitated behavior.

Prevention: Provide space (add supers before crowding), maintain young queens (old queens swarm more readily), manage hive configuration, split proactively.

Communication: The Waggle Dance

When a forager locates a nectar source, she returns to the hive and performs the waggle dance‚Äîa figure-eight pattern encoding direction and distance to the resource.

  • Direction: Angle relative to the sun (if 45¬∞ right of vertical, source is 45¬∞ right of sun)
  • Distance: Duration of waggle phase (longer = farther)
  • Quality: Vigor and duration of dance (rich source = longer dance)

Other bees observe the dance, memorize the pattern, and fly directly to the location‚Äîaccurate to within meters at distances exceeding 3 miles. This is abstract symbolic communication, arguably more sophisticated than most animal language.

Hive Construction

Top-Bar Hive (Simplest Design)

The top-bar hive is ideal for offline scenarios‚Äîrequires minimal tools, no precision lumber, and uses natural comb without frames. Bees build comb downward from wooden bars, mimicking natural tree cavity nesting. Construction is forgiving, and management is intuitive.

Advantages

  • Build with rough-sawn wood, no frame precision required
  • Lower cost than Langstroth setup
  • Bees construct comb naturally without commercial foundation
  • Easy hive inspection‚Äîlift bars individually
  • Reduced parasite pressure (bees manage comb ecology)
  • Works with captured swarms, no special equipment needed

Disadvantages

  • Honey extraction is crush-and-strain only (comb cannot be reused)
  • Slightly lower yields than Langstroth
  • Requires more frequent inspections to prevent burr comb

Langstroth Hive (Standard Commercial)

The Langstroth is the most widely used hive in commercial apiculture. It uses removable frames with foundation‚Äîbees build comb on the foundation, creating uniform, extractable combs. More complex to build but enables higher yields and easier management at scale.

Advantages

  • Frames are reusable year after year
  • Honey extraction with centrifugal force (extractor) = higher yields
  • Modular‚Äîadd/remove boxes for seasonal needs
  • Compatible with commercial equipment and knowledge
  • Easier to assess queen laying patterns

Disadvantages

  • Requires precision lumber and woodworking
  • Foundation purchase needed (beeswax or plastic)
  • Higher initial cost
  • More intrusive inspections‚Äîremove entire frames

Skep Hive (Historical Woven Straw)

The skep is a domed hive woven from straw or wicker, used for centuries. While illegal in some countries (deemed inferior for bee welfare), understanding skep construction is valuable for offline preparedness. Bees build comb freely inside, no frames.

Construction Notes

  • Dome shape from coiled straw rope, bound with ash splints or hazel twigs
  • Entrance hole at bottom, 1.5-2" diameter
  • Roof/cover: straw roof or wooden lid with thatch
  • Base board underneath or set on straw/wood block
  • Interior smoother, less sharp surfaces than exterior
  • Typical diameter 16-18 inches, height 14-16 inches

Extraction challenge: Killing the colony or cutting comb (destructive). Modern view sees this as animal cruelty; however, in true collapse scenarios, knowledge of extraction may be necessary. Entire comb harvested as crush-and-strain.

Log/Tree Hive (Most Primitive)

A hollowed log section closed at both ends, entrance hole drilled at bottom. Bees build comb freely. Simplest form of beekeeping‚Äîminimal intervention required. Excellent for establishing feral populations.

Log Hive Construction

  • Log selection: 10-15" diameter, 24-36" length, hollow or carefully hollowed (dull chisel)
  • Interior: Smooth walls, remove loose bark. Rough interior actually preferred‚Äîbees attach comb better
  • Entrance: 1.5" hole drilled at bottom
  • Top: Wooden cap or bark piece, sealed with clay or mud
  • Bottom: Solid wood base, slight slope for drainage
  • Mounting: 4-8 feet up on post or tree (protection from predators)
  • Ventilation: Allow slight air exchange; seal well to prevent moisture

Advantages: Extremely low cost, completely offline, minimal maintenance. Disadvantages: Honey extraction requires killing bees or cutting comb, difficult to assess health, swarming uncontrolled.

Getting Bees

Catching Swarms

A swarm is a free colony ready to establish itself. Swarms are docile, self-selected for desirable traits (successful foragers, good queens), and represent natural reproduction. Catching swarms is the cheapest, most rewarding method of acquiring bees.

Swarm Traps (Bait Hives)

  • Purpose: Attract homeless swarms during swarm season (May-June, sometimes July)
  • Design: Small box (5-10L) hung 12-25 feet high on pole or tree, entrance 1-1.5" diameter
  • Bait: Lemon grass oil (pheromone attractant) or used comb from previous bees
  • Interior: Rough wood, old comb, or straw for anchoring foundation
  • Placement: Edge of clearing, 100 yards from other hives, morning sun exposure
  • Timing: Set traps before peak swarm season; check daily during season

Swarm Capture Technique

  1. Observe swarm clustering (usually on branch, post, or structure)
  2. Wait 24 hours if possible‚Äîqueen joins cluster after 2-4 hours, colony becomes "committed"
  3. Lay sheet beneath branch, shake limb so swarm falls onto sheet
  4. Scoop bees into hive box or cage, set entrance toward cluster
  5. Bees follow pheromone of queen into box‚Äîself-loading process
  6. Wait 2-3 hours; if most bees in, cover and transport
  7. Install in prepared hive with comb/foundation and entrance reducer

Swarm Calling

In areas with many keepers, swarms often pass through. Local knowledge and communication increase success. Join local beekeeping associations; list yourself as available for swarm removal. Spread word among neighbors.

Splitting Existing Colonies

If you already have a hive or access to one, a split creates a new colony from resources of the old. Requires care to ensure both units survive.

Walk-Away Split Method

  1. In late spring when hive has excellent population and brood, prepare new hive box nearby
  2. Move original hive 4 feet to side
  3. Place new hive in original spot
  4. Transfer 4-5 combs with brood, pollen, nectar from original hive to new hive
  5. Include frame with eggs or young larva (for emergency queen cells)
  6. Return bees to new hive; field bees navigate to location, strengthen new colony
  7. Original hive at side gets all returning foragers daily‚Äîcolony develops new queen
  8. Provide both units adequate stores; monitor for starvation

Risk: One or both hives may fail if not enough bees or food. Best done in strong flow, when forage abundant.

Finding & Catching Wild Colonies

In areas with established feral populations, wild colonies can be located and relocated.

Scout & Locate

  • Look for bee traffic during bloom‚Äîwatch flowers, water sources
  • Follow bees in early morning or late afternoon (direct flight home)
  • Triangulate location; follow from multiple observation points
  • Likely homes: hollow trees, wall cavities, attics, rocky outcrops

Extraction & Relocation

  • Requires smoke, patience, protective gear
  • Extract comb with bees attached into boxes or cages
  • Locate and secure queen (critical‚Äîhive won't move without her)
  • Transport to new location; allow 24-48 hours orientation before opening
  • Success rate highly variable; strong reorientation risk
Ethics & Law: Confirm ownership of property before removing bees. Some jurisdictions require permits or prohibit relocation of wild bees. Document your actions. Offer compensation if on private land.

Seasonal Management

Spring (March-April)

First Inspection

  • On warm day (>55¬∞F), open hive carefully on windless afternoon
  • Assess live/dead status: lift hive‚Äîfeel weight (good sign if heavy)
  • Look for brood in multiple cells (indicates laying queen)
  • Check food stores‚Äîshould have 3-4 frames of honey minimum
  • Remove dead bees, clean entrance, check for mold or disease
  • Replace old/moldy comb

Feeding & Nutrition

If hive light on stores, feed to prevent starvation. Sugar syrup (1:1 or 2:1 sugar:water) stimulates brood rearing. Continue until natural nectar flow arrives (fruit blossoms).

Swarm Prevention

  • Checklist swarm signs: multiple queen cells, reduced laying, congestion
  • Provide space: add second box before crowding
  • Remove queen cells if prevention desired (or allow one to develop for controlled split)
  • Ensure young queen (2+ year old queens swarm more readily)

Summer (May-August)

Honey Supers

  • Add honey supers (lighter boxes) before main flow (local indicator: acacia blooms, wildflowers dense)
  • Place across top of brood chamber with excluder (optional but recommended‚Äîprevents queen in super)
  • Add second super if first becomes 2/3 full
  • Watch for filling speed; harvest partially if colony crowded

Monitoring & Maintenance

  • Weekly visual inspection: entrance activity, flight pattern, population presence
  • Monthly (or bi-weekly) detailed inspection if time allows
  • Ensure water source available (shallow dish, pebbles, or bee water feeder)
  • Manage grass/vegetation‚Äîmaintain clear area around hive
  • Monitor for obvious disease or aggressive behavior

Management Decisions

  • Allow swarms or conduct controlled split (depends on goals)
  • Manage drone comb if varroa mites present (remove drone brood comb to break mite cycle)
  • Assess nectar/pollen availability; supplemental feeding if drought

Fall (September-October)

Honey Harvest

  • Harvest supers when 80%+ capped (ready for extraction)
  • Use crush-and-strain method (no extractor needed‚Äîsee Honey Harvest section)
  • Remove supers before killing frost‚Äîbees need time to cluster

Winterization

  • Assess stores: Brood chamber must contain 40-60 pounds of honey (6-8 frames for top-bar, varies by hive type)
  • Reduce entrance: Install entrance reducer (1/4" hole) to minimize heat loss and prevent robbing
  • Add insulation: Wrap hive in tar paper or woodchip insulation; slope roof for drainage
  • Medication: Treat for varroa mites if population present (see Bee Health section)
  • Monitor brood: Confirm queen laid last autumn brood (long-lived winter bees essential)

Late Fall Feeding

If stores inadequate, feed heavy syrup (2:1 sugar:water) or fondant (powdered sugar + honey) to build winter stores. Bees must process and store before cluster-up. Feed in September-early October; stop before hard freeze.

Winter (November-February)

Monitoring

  • Minimal intervention‚Äîdisturbance breaks cluster and kills bees
  • Visual inspection only: check entrance for dead bees (sign of disease) or moisture
  • Weigh hive monthly (can lift back end‚Äîshould feel heavy) or estimate stores
  • Clear entrance snow/ice but don't disturb

Insulation & Ventilation Balance

  • Hive must retain heat (insulation) but manage moisture (ventilation)
  • Top entrance/vent reduces moisture accumulation
  • Sloped roof diverts moisture away from cluster
  • Adequate insulation on sides and roof

Emergency Feeding

If hive light on stores (can assess by weight), provide emergency food without opening hive. Methods: place candy board (fondant/sugar patty) on top bars, or medicated sugar water drip feeders accessible from outside.

Honey Harvest

When to Harvest

Honey is ready when capped (sealed with beeswax) and moisture content below 20%. Capped honey indicates bees believe it's shelf-stable and protected. Harvest too early and honey may ferment; too late and you'll lose the crop to early winter.

Readiness Assessment

  • Visual: At least 70-80% of comb surface capped with white wax
  • Timing: Late summer/early fall, before first hard frost
  • Weight: Frames with honey feel substantially heavier than empty frames
  • Smell: Ripe honey has fragrant, strong aroma
  • Taste: Take small sample on stick‚Äîshould be thick, not runny

Crush-and-Strain Method (No Extractor)

The most accessible harvest method, requiring only basic kitchen equipment. This method is perfect for offline scenarios and works excellently with top-bar hives (where comb isn't reused anyway).

Equipment Needed

  • Large pot or clean food-grade bucket (5+ gallon)
  • Colander or strainer (coarse mesh)
  • Cheesecloth or fine netting
  • Wooden spoon or paddle
  • Glass jars for finished honey
  • Clean cloths/towels

Process

  1. Remove supers: Smoke bees down; lift honey-filled frames
  2. Brush bees: Gently brush bees off frames with soft brush or handful of grass into hive
  3. Cut comb: Using warm knife, cut comb from frames (or crush comb with hands in bucket if frames are to be discarded)
  4. Crush: Break comb into small pieces in pot; mix with wooden spoon to release honey
  5. First straining: Pour through colander into clean bucket, allowing honey to drain (1-2 hours)
  6. Fine straining: Pour through cheesecloth into final jars (catches pollen, wax particles)
  7. Setting: Let honey settle in jars 24-48 hours; skim foam from surface if desired
  8. Storage: Seal jars; store in cool, dark place indefinitely

Honey Quality Considerations

  • Color: Varies from pale blonde to dark brown depending on floral sources; all are acceptable
  • Crystallization: Normal and natural; warm gently to re-liquefy without damaging enzymes
  • Flavor profiles: Early season = lighter, delicate; late season = darker, more robust
  • Raw vs. processed: Minimal straining preserves all enzymes and nutrients

Cutting Comb Honey

Comb honey (honeycomb still intact) is prized for texture and flavor. Bees invest significant effort building comb; selling comb honey commands premium prices.

  • Timing: Cut comb in mid-season when perfect cells forming (not late season)
  • Frame selection: Choose frames with all or nearly all capping
  • Cutting: Use heated knife (warm in hot water between cuts) to cut clean edges
  • Packaging: Place in clear plastic or wooden box; seal with tape
  • Storage: Keep cool and dry; comb may whiten (sugar crystals) over time‚Äînormal

Filtering & Storage

Honey's natural preservation requires minimal processing, but filtering removes unwanted particles while preserving nutritional value.

Proper Storage

  • Containers: Glass jars (preferred), food-grade plastic, or sealed ceramic
  • Environment: Cool, dark, dry location; avoid direct sun and heat
  • Humidity: Very low (<17%); excessive humidity may cause fermentation
  • Shelf life: Indefinite; archaeological honey remains viable after millennia
  • Crystallization: Expected and harmless; gently warm jar in hot water to liquify
  • Pollen settling: Pollen and fine particles settle to bottom; remix or consume as-is

Yield Estimates

A strong colony in good nectar flow produces 30-100+ pounds surplus honey annually (varies by region, weather, forage). Top-bar hives typically 20-50 pounds; Langstroth setup 50-100+ pounds. First-year package bees may produce little surplus as they build comb and population.

Beeswax Processing

Sources of Beeswax

Beeswax is harvested from cappings (wax covering honey), comb (after honey extraction), and burr comb (comb built between frames/boxes). Don't waste this precious material‚Äîone pound of rendered beeswax is worth significant money or trade value.

Collection Methods

  • Cappings: When uncapping honey for extraction, save the wax cappings
  • Old comb: Replace brood comb periodically (every 3-4 years); save discarded comb
  • Drone comb: Remove periodically for mite management; saves wax and helps colony health
  • Burr comb: Clean away between frames; save all

Solar Wax Melter (Simplest Method)

A solar melter requires only a box, glass, and sun. It gently melts beeswax without scorching or losing properties.

Construction

  • Build wooden box: 24" √ó 24" √ó 12" deep (or any size‚Äîlarger = slower but more capacity)
  • Interior line with plastic sheet (creates moisture barrier)
  • Slant top toward south; install glass sheet at 30-45¬∞ angle
  • Paint interior black to absorb heat
  • Place comb scraps on angled interior; honey drains down, wax collects below

Process

  1. Set up solar melter in full sun (south-facing exposure optimal)
  2. Fill with comb scraps and cappings
  3. Check daily; wax will melt gradually and separate from comb debris
  4. Once full of melted wax, move to shade to cool (wax hardens on bottom, debris filters through cloth)
  5. Scrape wax off bottom; store in cool place

Double-Boiler Rendering (Indoor Method)

More controlled than solar, allowing processing in any weather. Requires careful temperature control to avoid scorching.

Setup & Procedure

  1. Fill large pot with water; place smaller pot inside (double-boiler style)
  2. Heat water gently (don't boil‚Äî<180¬∞F ideal)
  3. Place comb scraps and cappings in top pot
  4. Wax melts gradually; stir occasionally with wooden spoon
  5. Monitor temperature: too hot = scorches wax (darkens, loses properties); too cool = slow melting
  6. Once fully melted, pour through fine cheesecloth into container of cold water
  7. Wax hardens into disk; remove from water, scrape off debris underside
  8. Re-melt if impure; strain again for cleaner product

Filtering & Purification

Multiple melts and strainings produce increasingly pure wax. First rendering typically dark (honeycomb debris, pollen); final product pale yellow to white depending on starting material and number of remelts.

  • First rendering: Dirty, dark wax‚Äîacceptable for candles, waterproofing
  • Second rendering: Lighter, cleaner‚Äîbetter for cosmetics, salves
  • Third+ rendering: Nearly white, very pure‚Äîpremium salves, lip balms

Molding Beeswax Candles

Dipped Candles (Traditional Method)

  1. Melt clean beeswax in double-boiler (170-190¬∞F)
  2. Prepare wick: twisted paper cord or cotton string (not synthetic)
  3. Dip wick into melted wax repeatedly (20-30 dips), allowing brief cooling between each
  4. Each dip adds thin coat; candle grows thicker with each pass
  5. Continue until desired diameter (match dipping time to size desired)
  6. Roll on flat surface if desired for smooth finish
  7. Allow final cooling; trim wick to 0.25-0.5"

Molded Candles

  1. Prepare mold: paper towel tube, tin can, or custom mold
  2. Suspend wick in center using wick sticker or clothespin across top
  3. Pour melted wax into mold (heat 185-195¬∞F)
  4. Allow cooling; wax shrinks‚Äîmay need second pouring to fill top layer
  5. Once fully cooled, remove from mold; trim wick

Beeswax Candle Properties

  • Burns cleaner than paraffin (no soot)
  • Releases faint honey scent naturally
  • Melting point ~145¬∞F (won't melt in normal room temperature)
  • Smokeless and longer-lasting than paraffin
  • Slower burn rate (10-15 hours per dipped candle)

Waterproofing & Sealant Applications

Fabric Waterproofing

  • Melt beeswax; brush or rub into fabric (canvas, linen, cotton)
  • Gently heat fabric (iron on low or sun-dry) to set wax
  • Creates water-resistant layer; allows fabric to breathe
  • Ideal for tarps, storage covers, clothing

Wood Sealing

  • Mix melted beeswax with few drops linseed oil
  • Apply with cloth or brush to wood surfaces
  • Polish with clean cloth after drying
  • Protects from moisture, enhances grain, feels pleasant

Rope & Cord Treatment

  • Pull natural fiber rope through warm melted beeswax
  • Excess wax makes rope water-resistant and longer-lasting
  • Prevents rot and fraying

Bee Health

Varroa Destructor Mites

The single greatest threat to managed honeybees. Varroa mites are parasites that feed on hemolymph (bee blood), weakening individual bees and transmitting viruses. An untreated mite population doubles every 4-6 weeks. High mite loads lead to colony collapse, typically in fall/winter.

Identification

  • Appearance: Reddish-brown, flat, oval mites (1-2mm), visible to naked eye on bees
  • Location: Found on bee bodies and in brood cells (especially drone cells)
  • Signs of infestation: Twisted wings (deformed wing virus), spotty brood, crawling bees

Monitoring Mite Load

  • Sugar roll: Place 300 bees in jar, add powdered sugar, roll jar gently‚Äîmites stick to sugar, fall into bottom. Count mites: <5 = acceptable, 5-10 = monitor, >10 = treat immediately
  • Sticky board: Place board under screen hive floor (bees above screen). Mites fall through. Count over 24 hours; <3/day acceptable, >10/day = serious infestation
  • Visual inspection: Open hive; look for mites on bees and in capped brood

Natural/Non-Chemical Treatments

Powdered Sugar Dusting - Safest method, no toxins, repeatable

  • Mix 1 pound powdered sugar with hive frames/bees (dust on bees during inspection)
  • Mites lose grip and fall; bees groom sugar off, resume normal behavior
  • Must repeat every 5-7 days for 4-6 weeks (during brood-free period, most effective)
  • Treatment rate: dust entire exposed brood frame area

Drone Comb Removal - Integrated pest management

  • Varroa prefer drone (male) brood 15:1 over worker brood
  • Encourage drone comb production by using larger-cell foundation or cutting comb to 6mm cells
  • When drone comb capped, cut out and remove (freeze or discard); new comb grows, mites concentrated in it
  • Repeat monthly during brood season‚Äîremoves 30-40% mite population

Screened Bottom Board - Passive control

  • Install screen at hive bottom (mites naturally fall and can't climb back)
  • Must be unobstructed; sticky board below catches fallen mites
  • Provides airflow (ventilation benefit) and passive mite reduction

Hive Replacement - Long-term strategy

  • Replace 1-2 combs annually (older comb contains more mites/pathogens)
  • Requeen every 1-2 years with young, vigorous queens
  • Maintain small-cell foundation (5.4mm vs. standard 5.8mm) if possible‚Äîbees cap brood faster, reducing mite reproduction

American Foulbrood (AFB)

Bacterial disease (Paenibacillus larvae) causing larvae death, severe for beekeeping. Spores extremely persistent‚Äîcan survive >40 years in honey.

Symptoms

  • Sunken, dark capped brood
  • Ropy slime strands when comb opened (characteristic "ropey" appearance)
  • Smell: sour, sickly-sweet odor
  • Scattered dead pupae, not mass brood death

Management

  • Confirm diagnosis: Only antibiotic (Terramycin/oxytetracycline) is effective treatment
  • If no chemicals available: Destroy affected combs, possibly entire hive (most reliable approach)
  • Prevention: Never transfer combs between hives without confirmation of health; practice sanitation
  • Legal note: Many regions require reporting and may mandate destruction of affected colonies

European Foulbrood (EFB)

Less severe than AFB; often resolves without treatment if hive strong. Caused by various Melissococcus bacteria.

Symptoms

  • Unsealed (uncapped) brood with dead larvae
  • Yellow-brown discoloration of larva
  • Mild sour smell (less intense than AFB)
  • Scattered pattern, not systematic

Treatment

  • Often self-limiting in strong colonies (bees uncap and remove diseased larvae)
  • Improve hive conditions: ensure adequate food, reduce crowding, maintain young queen
  • Remove affected combs if severe
  • Antibiotics (oxytetracycline) effective if chemical treatment available

Nosema

Fungal infection (Nosema ceranae) of gut lining. Weakens winter survival; adult bees become dysenteric. Diagnosis requires microscope.

  • Prevention: Maintain young, healthy queen; ensure adequate stores; good hive sanitation
  • Treatment: No cure; supportive care‚Äîensure warm shelter, adequate food, ventilation
  • Mitigation: Replace infected combs; disinfect equipment if moving between hives

Small Hive Beetles

A pest (Aethina tumida) in warm climates. Larvae tunnel through comb, contaminating honey with larvae and feces.

Control Methods

  • Maintain strong colonies: Strong hives suppress beetle populations
  • Remove and freeze combs: If beetles present, place comb in freezer (below 50¬∞F) overnight to kill larvae
  • Reduce hive volume: Remove empty comb, keeping hive space small‚Äîbees control beetles better in compact space
  • Entrance reducers: Restrict entrance to reduce beetle escape
  • Petroleum jelly/oil: Smear around hive entrance‚Äîbeetles get stuck trying to enter/exit

Products & Uses

Mead (Honey Wine)

Mead is fermented honey‚Äîthe oldest known alcoholic beverage (older than beer or wine). Honey fermented with water and wild or cultured yeast produces a distinctive, age-worthy drink.

Simple Mead Recipe

  • Ingredients: 3-5 pounds honey per gallon of water (adjusts final ABV); no other ingredients required (wild fermentation) or add yeast (ale, champagne, or mead yeast)
  • Preparation: Mix honey and water (warm gently to dissolve if needed); cool to room temperature
  • Fermentation: Add yeast (if using) or leave covered in cloth for wild fermentation. Ferment in glass carboy with airlock 2-4 months (until bubble rate slows dramatically)
  • Bottling: Siphon into bottles; cap and age 6+ months (improves significantly with age)
  • Final ABV: Typical range 10-16% (higher honey = higher alcohol)

Mead Variations

  • Melomel: Mead with fruit (berries, apples, stone fruit)
  • Metheglin: Mead with herbs/spices (ginger, clove, cinnamon‚Äîmedieval style)
  • Cyser: Mead with apple juice
  • Pyment: Mead with grape juice

Propolis Tincture

Propolis (bee glue) has powerful antimicrobial and wound-healing properties. A simple tincture extracts these compounds into alcohol.

Preparation

  1. Collect propolis by scraping hive interior (gloves recommended‚Äîstains permanently)
  2. Place in glass jar; cover with high-proof alcohol (80+ proof vodka minimum, 190-proof grain alcohol ideal)
  3. Seal and store in cool, dark place for 2-4 weeks
  4. Strain through cheesecloth; discard solids
  5. Store tincture in amber bottles away from light

Uses

  • Topical wound treatment: apply 1-2 drops to cuts, abrasions; repeat 2-3√ó daily
  • Sore throat: gargle with few drops in water (don't swallow)
  • Immune support: 5-10 drops daily in tea or water (internal use)
  • Antifungal: apply to fungal infections

Beeswax Candles

Described earlier under Beeswax Processing; note the premium properties: clean burn, pleasant scent, long-lasting.

Furniture Polish & Wood Care

Beeswax Wood Polish

  • Recipe: Mix 1 part melted beeswax with 2-3 parts linseed oil
  • Application: Rub onto wood with cloth; polish with clean cloth
  • Result: Natural sheen, food-safe, moisture-resistant

Leather Conditioner

  • Beeswax mixed with lanolin (or tallow) conditions leather
  • Apply sparingly to boots, saddles, belts
  • Waterproofs while maintaining flexibility

Thread Conditioning

A simple but valuable use: pull sewing thread through beeswax before stitching. The wax prevents fraying, increases thread strength, and makes hand-sewing smoother. Particularly valuable for repairs in field/survival situations.

Honey as Wound Dressing

Medical research has validated traditional use: honey (especially raw, with pollen intact) accelerates wound healing.

Mechanism

  • Osmotic effect: High sugar concentration draws moisture from wound, reducing bacterial proliferation
  • Hydrogen peroxide: Enzymes in honey (glucose oxidase) produce hydrogen peroxide, cleaning the wound
  • Anti-inflammatory: Reduces inflammation and promotes tissue regeneration
  • Antibacterial compounds: Methylglyoxal and other components kill bacteria directly

Application

  • Clean wound gently with water
  • Apply layer of raw honey directly to wound
  • Cover with clean bandage (honey is messy)
  • Change dressing and reapply honey daily until healed
  • Particularly effective for burns, cuts, abrasions, and slow-healing sores

Note: Do not use for deep infections or puncture wounds where infection risk is high‚Äîprofessional medical care needed in those cases.

Safety

Protective Equipment from Scratch

Veil (From Window Screen)

  • Materials: Window screen (metal preferred for durability), elastic or cord, brim material (hat or stiffener)
  • Construction: Sew screen to elastic band; attach band to stiff brim (coat hanger works); create drawstring at bottom to tighten around jacket collar
  • Design: Full-face veil extends 18-24" below hat; face stays 2-3" away from screen to prevent stinging
  • Benefit: Bees confuse screen as part of vegetation; won't sting through mesh

Gloves (Makeshift)

  • Commercial gloves: Leather work gloves or beekeeping gauntlets (preferred)
  • Emergency: Wrap hands in leather or fabric; secure at wrist with elastic or cord
  • Note: Gloves reduce dexterity; many experienced beekeepers go bare-handed using calmness and speed

Suit/Clothing

  • Light-colored clothing: Bees are less aggressive toward light colors
  • Long sleeves and pants: Tuck pants into socks or boots; cover all skin
  • Avoid dark/fuzzy materials: Resemble predators (bears)
  • Anchor clothing: Tighten cuffs and collar so bees can't crawl in

Smoker Construction

A smoker masks alarm pheromones, calming bees. Smoke makes bees think hive is in danger, triggering feeding rather than aggression.

DIY Smoker

  • Container: Metal can (3.5-4" diameter, 4-5" tall‚Äîfood can or paint can)
  • Draft tube: Metal pipe or drilled rod running through bottom (creates oxygen flow)
  • Bellows: Improvised from leather or fabric (tied to pipe or separate)
  • Fuel: Dried grass, straw, wood chips, cardboard‚Äîmaterial that smolders (not burns)
  • Construction: Drill holes around can bottom for air intake; pipe exits through top; grate inside holds fuel

Using Smoke Safely

  • Light smoker 5-10 minutes before work; let fuel catch
  • Use gentle puffs; excessive smoke stresses bees and degrades honey flavor
  • Work with bee flight direction (smoke bees in one direction, work from opposite side)
  • Smoke entrance before opening hive (prevents bees rushing out)
  • Keep smoker away from hive when not actively calming

Sting Treatment

Immediate Care

  • Remove stinger: Scrape stinger off with flat edge (fingernail, knife)‚Äîavoid pinching (squeezes toxin in)
  • Clean area: Wash with soap and water to prevent infection
  • Ice: Apply ice pack 15-20 minutes to reduce swelling and pain
  • Elevation: Raise affected limb above heart if possible
  • Alkaline treatment: Baking soda paste or honey application (honey's antibacterial also reduces reaction)

Pain Management

  • Over-the-counter antihistamine (if available) reduces itching/swelling
  • Topical hydrocortisone cream
  • Pain relief: acetaminophen or ibuprofen

Infection Prevention

  • Resist scratching (itching worse if scratched)
  • Watch for spreading redness, increasing swelling, warmth (signs of infection)
  • Apply honey topically (antibacterial) if infected or slow-healing

Anaphylaxis Warning

A small percentage of humans develop severe allergic reactions to bee venom. Anaphylaxis is life-threatening and requires immediate medical intervention.

Warning Signs

  • Rapid swelling beyond sting area (face, throat, lips)
  • Difficulty breathing or wheezing
  • Nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain
  • Dizziness or loss of consciousness
  • Rapid or irregular heartbeat

Response

  • If known allergic: Carry epinephrine auto-injector (EpiPen); use immediately if symptoms develop
  • Call emergency services: Do not delay seeking medical care
  • If unknown allergy: Stop beekeeping immediately if mild anaphylaxis signs (significant swelling beyond sting); avoid bee exposure
Critical: Anyone with known severe bee allergy should NOT keep bees. The risk of exposure during hive work is unacceptably high.

Troubleshooting

Absconding

Colony abandons hive completely‚Äîbees fly away, leaving eggs/brood to perish. Indicates severe problem or unsuitable hive conditions.

Common Causes

  • Insufficient food: Starvation fear triggers departure
  • Poor ventilation: Excessive moisture, mold, CO‚ÇÇ buildup
  • Queen failure: Dead or failing queen causes colony disintegration
  • Extreme pest pressure: High mite load, beetles, or wax moths
  • Hive damage: Broken comb, rodent damage, structural failure
  • Wrong hive location: Moved after bees oriented to original spot; they return to original location

Prevention & Recovery

  • Ensure adequate food stores before and during season
  • Maintain proper ventilation and moisture management
  • Monitor queen laying patterns; requeen if decline evident
  • Control pests aggressively
  • Never relocate established hives; if necessary, move >2 miles or less than 3 feet
  • If absconded, clean hive thoroughly, repair damage, and restock with new swarm/package

Aggressive Colonies

Some colonies develop aggressive temperament‚Äîsting readily, pursue, exhibit high-strung behavior. Partially genetic, partially environmental.

Causes & Solutions

  • Genetics: Some hives bred for aggression (or poor genetics). Solution: requeen with calm genetics
  • Older queen: 3+ year old queens produce aggressive daughters. Solution: replace with young queen annually
  • Excessive pest pressure: Defensive behavior increases with mites/disease. Solution: treat pests, improve health
  • Poor feed timing: Robbing and starvation stress increase aggression. Solution: manage feeding properly, ensure stores
  • Excessive handling: Repeated, rough inspections increase defensiveness. Solution: minimize hive visits, work gently

Immediate Management

  • Work in cool weather (morning, overcast) when bees less active
  • Use heavy smoke; bees stay docile when focusing on self-preservation
  • Minimize hive opening; work quickly and intentionally
  • Consider requeening if aggression severe and other factors ruled out
  • In extreme cases, destroy colony if danger to yourself or neighbors

Queen Failure

Queen stops laying or dies, colony cannot reproduce. Without queen, colony collapses within weeks (no brood replacement).

Symptoms

  • Absence of eggs/young brood (larvae still present if recent failure)
  • Worker bees laying eggs (appear scattered, multiple per cell, laid on sides)
  • Reduced population (no new brood, colony not replacing losses)
  • Nervous, erratic colony behavior

Recovery

  • Emergency queen cell: If hive has eggs/young larvae, bees will build emergency queen cells (rare in feral queens)
  • Requeen with purchased queen: Place new queen in cage with escort bees; introduce over 3-5 days (allows hive to accept new pheromone profile)
  • Combine with another hive: If queenless colony weak, merge with strong colony (newspaper method‚Äîseparate colonies by newspaper, gradually tear away so bees acclimate to each other)
  • Start over: If no replacement available, clean hive and restock with new swarm/package

Robbing Behavior

Foragers from one colony enter another and steal honey. Leads to fights, colony weakness, potential disease transmission.

Prevention

  • Reduce entrance: Use entrance reducers (limit bees able to defend)
  • Feed inside hive: Use internal feeders rather than outside feeders (prevent attracting robber bees)
  • Manage equal strength: Avoid weak hives near strong ones (screen weak hives or combine)
  • Minimize nectar shortage: Supplement during dearth (lack of natural forage)
  • Isolate hives: Space several feet apart; vary hive appearances (colors, configurations)

Intervention

  • If robbing observed, shut down entrance completely for 2-3 days (bees stop orienting to that location)
  • Then open entrance slowly with reducer
  • Move affected hive if possible (breaks foraging orientation)
  • Strengthen weak hive by giving brood frames from strong colony

Winter Survival Issues

Starvation

  • Prevention: Ensure 40-60 pounds honey in brood chamber before winter
  • Assessment: Weigh hive monthly or judge by feel (lift back edge‚Äîshould feel heavy)
  • Emergency feeding: Provide fondant or sugar patties if stores insufficient (cannot open hive mid-winter without breaking cluster and causing deaths)

Excessive Moisture

  • Cause: Bees exhale moisture when clustered; hive must have ventilation
  • Prevention: Upper entrance/vent allows moist air to escape
  • Sloped roof: Directs condensation away from cluster
  • Absorbent material: Cloth or burlap above cluster absorbs moisture before dripping back

Dysentery/Disease

  • Symptom: Brown staining on hive exterior, musty smell
  • Cause: Usually associated with poor sanitation, nosema, or inadequate cleansing flights
  • Prevention: Ensure dry hive, ventilation, regular comb replacement
  • Treatment: Supportive only; replace combs if available, ensure clean entrance
