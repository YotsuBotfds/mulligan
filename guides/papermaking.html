1. Papermaking from Plant Fibers

Paper production begins with the selection and preparation of plant fibers. Unlike modern mechanical pulping, traditional methods rely on natural fibers that possess sufficient length and strength to create durable paper. The quality of the final product depends directly on the source material and the care taken during processing.

Raw Materials

Several plant sources provide excellent fiber for papermaking:

  • Bark fibers: Willow, mulberry, and elm bark contain tough, long fibers ideal for handmade paper. The fibrous inner bark (phloem) is stripped and processed.
  • Cotton rags: Worn cotton clothing provides pure cellulose fibers. Historically the gold standard, as cotton fibers are long, strong, and pure.
  • Straw: Wheat, rye, and rice straw offer abundant cellulose, though shorter fibers require more beating to achieve strength.
  • Hemp: Cannabis sativa fiber is exceptionally strong and long, producing durable paper suited for documents.
  • Papyrus: The aquatic plant Cyperus papyrus produces pith that can be pressed directly (not true paper, but a writing surface).
  • Linen: Flax fiber from linen scraps is excellent, producing very strong paper.
  • Wood: Can be used but requires extensive cooking in alkali; short fibers need heavy beating.

Retting and Initial Processing

Before cooking, fibers must be partially separated and cleaned through retting ‚Äîa fermentation process that weakens the pectin binding fiber bundles together.

  • Soak raw plant material in water for 1-3 weeks
  • Microbes break down pectin and lignin
  • Rinse thoroughly to remove dissolved material
  • Spread to dry, or proceed directly to cooking

Cooking Fibers in Alkali

The cooking process dissolves lignin and remaining gums, leaving pure cellulose fibers. Traditional methods use wood ash lye (potassium hydroxide), a caustic alkali solution.

Wood Ash Lye Preparation

  • Collect hardwood ash (oak, birch, or other deciduous wood)
  • Mix ash with water in a large pot (approximately 1 part ash to 3 parts water)
  • Let settle for several hours
  • Carefully pour off the clear, brown liquid (this is lye)
  • Test pH: lye should feel slippery and turn brown paper dark

The Cooking Process

  • Fill a large pot with lye solution (depth 4-6 inches)
  • Heat to 70-80¬∞C (moderately hot)
  • Add prepared fibers, stirring occasionally
  • Cook for 2-4 hours depending on fiber type
  • Fibers should become pale and separate easily
  • Drain and rinse multiple times with fresh water until neutral pH
  • Store in damp condition if not proceeding immediately
Safety Note: Wood ash lye is caustic and will burn skin. Always wear gloves, protect eyes, and work in ventilated areas. Neutralize any spills with vinegar before cleanup.

Beating and Mashing Pulp

Once fibers are cooked and rinsed, they must be beaten to separate individual fibers and develop fibrillation‚Äîthe mechanical loosening of fiber walls. This creates hydrogen bonding when the sheet forms, giving paper strength.

  • Traditional beating: Pound fibers with a wooden mallet on a flat stone or wood surface for 30-60 minutes
  • Fermentation beating: Mix fibers with water and allow them to partially rot for 1-2 weeks, then pound (less labor-intensive)
  • Completion test: When pulp is pale, fluffy, and fibers are difficult to separate by hand, beating is complete
  • Consistency: Maintain 2-3% fiber concentration (very dilute) for optimal sheet formation

2. The Mould & Deckle

The mould and deckle are the fundamental tools of papermaking. Together, they form a frame that captures pulp fibers from water, allowing them to bond into a sheet. The mould is the permanent frame with screen, while the deckle is a removable top frame that prevents pulp from escaping.

Mould Construction

The mould is a wooden frame with a screen or mesh bottom that allows water to drain while fibers settle into a mat.

  • Frame material: Hardwood (oak, maple, or ash) 1-2 inches wide and 0.5-1 inch thick
  • Frame size: Typically 8x10 inches to 12x18 inches, depending on desired paper size
  • Screening: Traditionally laid wire (thin parallel wires with crossing weft wires) or woven mesh
    • Laid wire creates characteristic paper watermark lines
    • Woven mesh (like window screen) is easier to make and produces smooth paper
  • Attachment: Screen is nailed, stapled, or glued to frame with multiple attachment points
  • Surface treatment: Oil or seal wood to prevent warping and rotting

Deckle Construction

The deckle is simply a frame the same external dimensions as the mould, with no screen. It sits on top of the mould during papermaking to contain the pulp slurry.

  • Same frame dimensions as mould (internal opening matches mould's screen area)
  • No screen required; solid frame suffices
  • Should fit snugly on mould to prevent pulp leakage
  • Can be slightly thicker for durability
Mould and Deckle Components

Making Wire Screen

Creating laid wire is labor-intensive but produces authentic paper. Alternative approaches include:

  • Linen thread mesh: Weave linen thread on a simple frame loom to create screen; works well for casual papermaking
  • Window screen: Use modern fiberglass or aluminum screening if available; not traditional but functional
  • Plant fiber mesh: Thin strips of bamboo or wood can be lashed together at right angles to form a grid

Sizing Considerations

The mould size determines the maximum paper sheet size. Important dimensions:

  • Finished paper: Will be slightly smaller than the internal mould opening (approximately 0.25 inches shorter on each side)
  • Standard sizes: Foolscap (13x16"), Post (15x19"), Crown (15x20"), and larger formats possible with two-person operation
  • Frame depth: The deckle and mould combined should be 1-2 inches tall to contain pulp slurry
Maintenance: After each use, clean the screen by gentle brushing underwater to remove embedded fibers. Store in a dry location to prevent mold and warping.

3. Sheet Forming & Pressing

The formation of a paper sheet is the heart of the papermaking process. This involves suspending fibers in water, capturing them on the screen, and transforming them into a solid mat through couching and pressing.

Preparing the Vat

  • Fill a large tub or vat with water (depth 12-18 inches)
  • Add beaten pulp to achieve 1-2% concentration (very dilute; pulp should be barely visible in water)
  • Stir well to disperse fibers evenly throughout the vat
  • Allow fibers to settle for a few minutes if not using immediately
  • Before each sheet, stir gently to resuspend fibers evenly

Dipping the Mould

This is where the papermaker's skill is most evident. The goal is to create an even distribution of fibers with consistent thickness.

  1. Stack deckle on top of mould with tight fit
  2. Hold mould level (parallel to vat surface) with both hands
  3. Submerge the frame completely into the vat with a smooth, swift motion
  4. Pause briefly to allow fibers to settle on the screen (2-3 seconds)
  5. Withdraw the frame with one smooth motion, keeping it level and parallel to the water surface
  6. Allow excess water to drain through the screen for 2-3 seconds
  7. Once drainage slows, perform a gentle shake : rock the frame back-and-forth (not up-and-down) in a small motion; this helps fibers interlock and consolidate

Couching the Sheet

Couching (from French coucher, "to lay down") transfers the wet sheet from the mould to a felt. This is accomplished with a swift, confident motion.

  1. Have a damp felt (wool or linen cloth) ready on a flat surface
  2. Remove the deckle carefully from the mould
  3. With the mould still level, position it directly above the felt
  4. Press the mould screen-side-down onto the felt with firm, even pressure
  5. Rock slightly to ensure all fibers transfer
  6. Lift the mould straight up in one smooth motion
  7. The wet sheet should adhere to the felt
Sheet Formation Steps (Side View)

Post Formation (Layering)

After couching each sheet, it remains on the felt. Additional sheets are couched on top of it in alternating fashion. This stack is called a post .

  • Place a clean felt on top of the first couched sheet
  • Couch the next sheet onto the fresh felt
  • Continue alternating sheets and felts until the post is complete (typically 10-50 sheets depending on space and equipment)
  • Felts alternate with sheets, so a post of 20 sheets requires 21 felts

Pressing the Post

Water must be removed from the post to consolidate the sheets and improve bonding. This is achieved through pressure.

  • Hand pressing: Use a wooden board and mallet to strike the stack, distributing pressure evenly
  • Screw press: A simple wooden frame with a threaded screw applies steady downward pressure
  • Weight pressing: Place heavy stones or sandbags on top of the post; apply pressure for 2-4 hours
  • Multiple pressings: Press once, remove accumulated water, press again; repeat 3-4 times to remove maximum moisture

Drying the Paper

Once pressed, sheets must be dried. This final stage determines the paper's properties.

  • Air drying on felts: Hang or lay felts in a warm, dry location with good air circulation; takes 3-7 days
  • Drying on boards: Carefully separate damp sheets from felts and lay flat on wooden boards; dry in a warm room
  • Drying in sun: In warm climates, sun drying is fast but may cause uneven shrinkage and curling
  • Drying boards arrangement: Stack boards with spacers between them to allow air circulation
  • Humidity control: Too-humid conditions slow drying; too-dry conditions cause excessive shrinkage and cockling
Tip for Even Sheets: The thickness of formed paper depends on the amount of fiber suspended in the vat. Consistent sheet thickness requires maintaining even vat concentration‚Äîstir frequently and use consistent dipping motion and timing.

4. Sizing & Finishing

Raw paper fresh from the drying process is extremely absorbent‚Äîink applied to it would spread uncontrollably like water on a paper towel. Sizing refers to the addition of a material that reduces absorbency, allowing ink to sit on the surface and form sharp lines. This is essential for writing and printing.

Gelatin Sizing

Gelatin (derived from collagen in animal bones and hides) is the traditional sizing agent. It creates a hydrophobic layer on fiber surfaces without clogging the paper structure.

Gelatin Sizing Procedure

  • Prepare gelatin solution: Dissolve gelatin powder (1-2 grams per liter of water) in hot water; stir until completely dissolved
  • Cool to working temperature: Allow to cool to 40-50¬∞C; if too hot, it may damage paper
  • Dip or float sizing: Pass dried paper sheets through the gelatin bath, or float sheets on the surface for 10-30 seconds
  • Hang to dry: Suspend sized papers to dry completely before storage
  • Hardening (optional): For extra durability, soak in dilute alum solution (1-2% alum by weight) after gelatin drying

Starch Sizing

Starch provides a less robust sizing effect but is easier to produce and more economical than gelatin.

  • Preparation: Boil wheat, corn, or rice starch in water; use 0.5-2% concentration
  • Application: Dip sized dried paper through the starch solution, or brush on with a wide brush
  • Drying: Dry completely; starch-sized paper may remain slightly stiff

Burnishing and Calendering

These mechanical treatments improve surface smoothness, which enhances ink adhesion and produces sharper printed images.

  • Burnishing: Rub the paper surface with a smooth, hard tool (bone folder, smooth stone, or polished wood) to compress and flatten the surface
  • Calendering: Pass paper between two smooth, hard rollers under pressure; produces uniform smoothness
  • Effect: Smoother surfaces accept finer detail and sharper ink edges, though they lose some of the rustic character of rough-sized paper

Trimming and Finishing

  • Deckle edge removal: Use a sharp knife or guillotine to trim the rough edges created during sheet formation
  • Size standardization: Cut to standard page sizes (foolscap, crown, etc.) or custom dimensions
  • Stacking and pressing: Stack finished sheets under light weight to keep them flat
  • Storage: Keep in cool, dry conditions protected from light to preserve whiteness and prevent yellowing

5. Ink Making

Ink is the essential medium for transferring writing and printed images to paper. Traditional inks are created from pigments, binders, and liquids. The quality of the ink significantly affects both the clarity of the mark and the permanence of the document.

Carbon Black Ink

Carbon black (soot) is the most accessible and common ink pigment. It produces deep black marks and is relatively permanent.

Carbon Black Ink Recipe

  • Collect soot: Burn hardwood (oak or birch) in a lamp or candle; collect black soot on a ceramic surface held above the flame
  • Grind the pigment: Crush soot finely using a mortar and pestle to create a fine powder (take care not to inhale)
  • Prepare gum solution: Dissolve gum arabic powder (a tree resin) in water at 10-15% concentration; heat gently if needed
  • Mix ink: Combine ground carbon black with gum solution in ratios from 1:1 to 1:3 (soot to gum) depending on desired consistency
  • Grind to uniformity: Use a grinding stone or millstone to thoroughly blend pigment and binder until ink is smooth and uniform in color
  • Storage: Store in sealed containers; ink improves with age as pigments settle and integrate with binder

Iron Gall Ink

This ink was standard in medieval and Renaissance Europe. It forms a chemical bond with fibers, making it highly permanent and resistant to fading.

Iron Gall Ink Recipe (Historic)

  • Collect oak galls: Oak galls are growths on oak trees caused by wasps; dry and crush them to powder
  • Extract tannins: Soak crushed galls in water for several days; the liquid darkens as tannins leach out
  • Prepare ferrous sulfate solution: Dissolve iron sulfate (copperas, a mineral salt) in water
  • Mix inks: Combine gall extract with ferrous sulfate in roughly equal volumes; the mixture immediately turns blue-black
  • Add gum arabic: Stir in gum solution (5-10%) to improve flow and reduce bleeding on paper
  • Allow maturation: Age the ink for 1-2 weeks, stirring occasionally; it darkens further and becomes more stable
Iron Gall Ink Note: This ink is highly corrosive and will eat through metals; use only glass or ceramic containers and ceramic pens. Despite this drawback, documents written with iron gall ink last centuries with minimal fading, making it ideal for important records.

Plant-Based Inks

Various berries, nuts, and plant materials produce colored inks suitable for illustration and less formal writing:

  • Walnut ink: Boil green walnut hulls; the dark brown liquid can be used directly or thickened with gum arabic
  • Blackberry ink: Crush ripe berries, strain, and thicken with gum arabic; produces dark purple-brown
  • Indigo ink: Prepare indigo pigment and bind with gum arabic for blue ink
  • Woad ink: Similar to indigo; produces blue to blue-green depending on processing

Ink Quality Testing

  • Flow test: Dip a quill in ink and allow it to flow onto paper; ink should flow steadily without skipping
  • Drying test: Write a line and observe drying time; acceptable inks dry in 10-30 seconds on properly sized paper
  • Feathering test: Observe the ink line edge under magnification; should be sharp, not feathered or spread
  • Fade resistance: Expose sample text to sunlight for weeks; permanent inks show minimal fading
  • Water resistance: Drop water on dried ink; permanent inks should not smudge or run

Ink Consistency Adjustments

  • Too thick: Add small amounts of water; gum solution can also thin while improving flow
  • Too thin: Evaporate excess water by gentle heating, or add more pigment and binder
  • For brush use: Make thinner consistency (high water content) to flow smoothly
  • For pen use: Make thicker consistency (low water content) for controlled flow from nib

6. Quill & Reed Pens

Before the invention of metal nibs, scribes and writers used pens fashioned from natural materials. Quill pens from bird feathers remain superior for fine writing and calligraphy, while reed pens offer quick and economical alternatives.

Quill Pen Preparation

Goose quills are most common, though swan, crow, and other large bird feathers work similarly. The quill must be hardened and sharpened to create a functional nib.

  1. Select and clean the feather: Choose the 5th, 6th, or 7th primary flight feather; strip the barbs and wash thoroughly
  2. Harden the quill: Heat sand in a shallow pan to about 300¬∞F (150¬∞C); hold the quill barrel (hollow tube) in the sand for 30-60 seconds to harden; this makes it less prone to splitting
  3. Create the shaft: Cut away the barbs leaving 6-8 inches of usable writing shaft
  4. Scrape and shape: Using a sharp knife, scrape the outside of the barrel to taper it slightly and create a comfortable grip
  5. Cut the nib slit: Make a shallow vertical slit down the center of the quill tip, about 0.5 inches long; this slit allows ink flow
  6. Trim the nib: Cut away excess quill on each side of the slit, creating a flat writing edge at an angle (usually 45 degrees for general writing)
  7. Fine-tune the point: Use a fine knife to sharpen the corners of the nib to a desired sharpness
Quill Nib Cutting Angles and Details

Quill Maintenance

  • Quills dull with use and benefit from periodic resharpening
  • After extended use, carefully recut the nib to restore sharpness
  • A worn quill eventually becomes too short; a new one must be prepared
  • Store quills in a dry place; they can become brittle or soft if exposed to extreme humidity

Reed Pen Preparation

Reed pens are simpler to make and suitable for less formal writing. Bamboo stalks also work well.

  1. Select a reed: Choose dried bamboo or river reed, 0.5 inches in diameter
  2. Cut to length: Cut a 6-8 inch length
  3. Create the nib: Using a sharp knife, make an angled cut at one end at 45 degrees, removing 1-2 inches to create a writing point
  4. Cut the ink channel: Make a shallow vertical slit in the center of the nib, about 0.25 inches long
  5. Smooth the surface: Use fine sand or a stone to smooth the writing surface

Dip Pen Technique

  • Dip the nib into ink to a depth of about 0.5 inches‚Äîink will be drawn into the channel by capillary action
  • Allow excess ink to drip back into the well
  • Hold the pen at the correct angle (typically 45 degrees for quill pens) to the writing surface
  • Write with moderate pressure; the nib should glide across the paper without scraping
  • Redip as needed‚Äîa single dip may be sufficient for 20-50 words depending on pen size and writing pressure
  • Keep ink clean; remove particles that may cause skipping

7. Movable Type Printing

The invention of movable type, credited to Johann Gutenberg in 15th-century Germany, revolutionized information distribution. Individual letter-shaped pieces of metal, once cast, could be rearranged to print different texts. This section covers the process of creating type.

Overview of the Process

Creating movable type involves several stages: carving a letter punch, using it to create a matrix (mold), and casting individual type pieces from metal.

Carving the Punch

A punch is a hardened steel rod with a letter carved into its tip. Traditionally, this was cut by hand using fine engraving tools.

  • Material: High-carbon steel or hardened steel rod, 0.25-0.5 inches in diameter
  • Design: Letter design must be slightly larger than the final type size, as some shrinkage occurs during casting
  • Carving: Using fine chisels and burins (engraving tools), carve the letter in relief (raised) on the punch tip
  • Depth: The letter should be carved deep enough to create sufficient impression in the matrix (typically 0.1-0.2 inches deep)
  • Finishing: Polish the punch to remove tool marks; a smooth surface creates cleaner matrices
  • Hardening: Heat the punch steel to high temperature and quench in oil to achieve proper hardness for repeated use

Creating the Matrix

A matrix is a negative mold created by striking the punch into soft metal. This matrix is then used repeatedly to cast individual type pieces.

  • Matrix material: Copper or bronze (softer metals that accept punch impressions without shattering)
  • Preparation: Anneal (heat and cool) the copper to make it soft and workable
  • Striking: Place the copper blank on a lead block or wooden surface
  • Apply the punch: Strike the punch vertically into the copper with controlled hammer blows
  • Impression depth: The punch should penetrate 0.1-0.15 inches into the copper to create a proper mold
  • Multiple strikes: For larger matrices or cleaner impressions, strike multiple times from slightly different angles
  • Filing and fitting: File the matrix to remove burrs and ensure the surrounding metal is smooth and level

Casting Type

Once a matrix exists, individual type pieces (sorts) are cast by pouring molten metal into the matrix.

  • Metal alloy: Lead, tin, and antimony are mixed (typically 80% lead, 10% tin, 10% antimony) to create a metal with a low melting point (around 350¬∞C), easy castability, and hardness suitable for printing
  • Casting equipment: A mold, or type mold , holds the matrix and shapes the molten metal into the proper type body (tall enough for the platen and margins)
  • The type mold: Consists of two metal parts: one with the matrix cavity, another that shapes the sides and back
  • Casting process:
    1. Heat the type mold to 150-200¬∞C (warm enough to prevent metal solidification too quickly)
    2. Heat the metal alloy to 400-450¬∞C (just above its melting point)
    3. Pour the molten metal into the mold cavity containing the matrix
    4. Allow to cool for a few seconds
    5. Open the mold and eject the newly cast type
  • Finishing: Remove any metal burrs or excess using a file; the type is now ready for use
  • Quality control: Test-cast type should have:
    • Sharp, clear impression of the letter
    • Even height (approximately 1 inch for standard European type)
    • Smooth edges without casting flaws

The Printing Press Mechanism

A printing press transfers ink from composed type onto paper using controlled pressure. The Gutenberg press used a platen (flat pressure plate) driven by a large screw.

Platen Press Mechanism (Simplified)

Setting Type

Before printing, individual type pieces must be arranged to form words and pages. This is called composition or typesetting .

  • Type case: Type is organized in a wooden box with small compartments, each containing one letter or character
  • Composing stick: A shallow metal or wooden tray that holds a line of type as it is being set
  • Line length: Lines must be set to a consistent width; spaces of varying sizes (quads and spaces) are used to align the right edge
  • Assembly: Type pieces are selected letter-by-letter and placed in the composing stick from right to left (since the final image must be a mirror of the text)
  • Justification: Lines are made even on both sides by adjusting space widths between words
  • Transfer to forme: Once a page or section is composed, the type in the composing stick is carefully transferred to a larger frame called a forme
  • Locking the forme: Type and spacing material are held together tightly in the forme using a wooden or metal frame and wedges

Printing Process

  1. Ink the forme by rolling an ink-covered roller (ink ball) across the type surface
  2. Place a sheet of paper on the inked type
  3. Pull the lever to lower the platen, applying firm pressure
  4. Hold pressure for 2-5 seconds to allow ink transfer
  5. Raise the platen and remove the printed sheet
  6. The impression depth and pressure are critical: too little creates faint marks; too much damages type and tears paper

Ink Distribution and Registration

  • Registration: Proper alignment of multiple color inks (if printing in color) is essential; guides or marks on the press help ensure consistent positioning
  • Ink consistency: Ink must be properly thinned (not too thick) to roll smoothly across type without clumping
  • Roller pressure: The ink roller must apply even pressure across the type without pressing too hard, which would displace type pieces

8. Block Printing

Block printing, also known as woodcut or wood engraving, is a relief printing technique where an image is carved into a block of wood. The raised surfaces are inked and pressed against paper to create prints. This technique predates movable type in China and has been used for both artistic and textual reproduction.

Choosing and Preparing the Block

  • Wood type: Hardwoods (cherry, maple, apple, boxwood) are preferred for their fine grain and durability
  • Grain direction: Wood grain should be straight and fine; avoid knots and irregular grain
  • Block size: Dimensions depend on the desired print size; thickness should be at least 1 inch for adequate strength
  • Preparation: Plane the surface smooth; sand with fine sandstone or charcoal powder until perfectly flat and smooth
  • Coating: A thin coating of chalk or lime dust helps the design show up clearly during carving

Transferring the Design

  • Direct drawing: Draw the image freehand on the block with a sharp pencil or brush
  • Transfer paper: Create a paper version of the design (in reverse), then transfer it to the block surface using rubbing or a printing frame
  • Carbon paper method: Tape carbon paper to the block, then draw the design; the carbon transfers to the wood
  • Mirror reversal: Remember that the final print will be a mirror image of the carved block

Carving Tools and Techniques

Several specialized tools are used for woodcut carving:

  • Knife: A sharp knife with a straight blade for making outline cuts and removing large areas
  • Gouges: U-shaped or V-shaped cutting tools for creating lines and removing material
    • U-gouge: Produces broader lines and curves smoothly
    • V-gouge: Creates sharp, fine lines ideal for detail
  • Chisels: Flat-edged tools for removing larger areas of wood
  • Burins: Fine engraving tools for extremely detailed work

Carving Process

  1. Outline the design: Use the knife or V-gouge to cut a clean outline around the areas to be printed
  2. Remove background: Use larger gouges and chisels to carve away the wood surrounding the design; the background should be lower than the image areas
  3. Add detail: Use fine tools to carve internal lines and details within the image
  4. Smooth and refine: Remove any splinters or rough areas; the raised surfaces should be smooth for clean printing
  5. Test prints: Make test prints on paper to check the carving; areas intended to be white should be completely removed

Single-Color Block Printing

  • Inking: Roll or brush ink evenly across the raised surface of the carved block
  • Paper placement: Position paper carefully on the inked block, aligned as desired
  • Printing methods:
    • Hand pressure: Rub the back of the paper with a smooth tool (bone folder, wooden spoon) to transfer ink
    • Baren: A specialized tool with a smooth, rounded surface for rubbing
    • Screw press or weight: Apply controlled pressure for consistent results
  • Ink coverage: Ensure even ink distribution; uneven inking creates spotty prints

Multi-Color Block Printing

Creating color prints requires separate blocks for each color. This is more complex but allows for striking visual effects.

  • Separate blocks: Carve one block for each color, carefully planning which areas are assigned to which color
  • Registration marks: Use precise marks or guides to ensure blocks align perfectly; even small misalignment creates blurred or shifted colors
  • Block sequence: Print lighter colors first, darker colors last; this helps identify misalignment issues early
  • Ink mixing: Ensure inks are properly matched to the intended colors before printing
  • Example workflow: For a three-color print:
    1. Carve the yellow block
    2. Print yellow on all sheets
    3. Carve the red block
    4. Register sheets and print red
    5. Carve the blue block
    6. Register sheets and print blue

Block Printing on Textiles

The same carved blocks can be used to print on fabric using textile inks.

  • Textile inks: Use inks specifically formulated for fabric; these differ from paper inks in their binders and drying characteristics
  • Fabric preparation: Wash fabric to remove sizing; press smooth before printing
  • Printing: Use more forceful pressure than for paper to ensure good ink transfer through fabric fibers
  • Fixing color: After printing, heat-set the ink by steaming or ironing according to ink manufacturer's instructions

9. Bookbinding

Bookbinding is the craft of assembling individual paper leaves into a durable, functional volume. The method ensures that pages remain secure, lie flat when opened, and the binding itself lasts centuries. Traditional binding combines careful folding, sewing, and cover construction.

Page Folding: Folio, Quarto, Octavo

A large sheet of printed paper is folded multiple times to create smaller pages. The number of folds determines the page size and the number of pages in a single "gathering" or "signature."

  • Folio: One fold (the sheet is folded in half)
    • Creates 2 leaves (4 pages)
    • Large page size; economical for books requiring few pages
  • Quarto: Two folds (the sheet is folded in half, then in half again)
    • Creates 4 leaves (8 pages)
    • Medium page size; common for historical printed books
  • Octavo: Three folds (the sheet is folded in half three times)
    • Creates 8 leaves (16 pages)
    • Smaller page size; economical in materials, suitable for longer works

Signature Gathering and Collation

Each folded sheet is called a signature or gathering . Signatures are nested within each other (one inside another) and sewn together to form the text block.

  • Folding: Fold each sheet carefully along the grain direction (which runs vertically in most traditionally made paper)
  • Nesting: Place signatures inside one another in sequence (signature 1 inside signature 2, all nested inside signature 3, etc.)
  • Collation: Verify that signatures are in proper order and all pages are present
  • Number of signatures: A book of 100 pages (octavo) would require about 7 signatures (each containing 16 pages)

Sewing the Text Block

Signatures are sewn together to create a unified text block that will not fall apart even if the binding is damaged.

  • Sewing stations: Holes are punched or drilled in the fold of each signature, typically 4-5 holes per signature spaced evenly along the spine
  • Sewing thread: Traditionally linen thread; heavy-duty thread is essential for durability
  • Sewing method: The simplest method is the over-sew : thread passes through all holes in one signature, then continues to the next signature, linking them together
  • Sewing to cords: In finer bindings, thread loops around raised cords that run perpendicular to the spine; this creates the characteristic ridges seen on antique books and distributes stress across multiple anchor points
  • Knots and fastening: The thread is tied off at the end with a secure knot; excess thread is trimmed
Codex Binding Structure

Constructing the Covers

The covers protect the text block and provide the book's visual appearance and durability.

  • Cover boards: Heavy cardboard or thin wood boards form the structural foundation
    • Thickness: 0.125 to 0.25 inches
    • Material: Pasteboard (made from compressed paper fibers), chipboard, or thin plywood
  • Covering material: Cloth or leather covers the boards and connects them
    • Linen or cotton cloth is glued to the boards with a strong adhesive (paste or glue)
    • Leather (goat, calf, or sheepskin) provides superior durability and beauty but is more expensive and labor-intensive
  • Assembly: Two boards are positioned slightly apart (creating a hinge for the spine) and covered with a single piece of fabric; endpapers are glued to connect the covers to the text block
  • Spine construction: The spine connects the front and back covers, incorporating the sewn text block; it must be strong enough to support the weight of all pages during opening

Decoration and Finishing

  • Tooling: Apply gold leaf or color to the cover using heated metal stamps (dies); this creates decorative patterns and book titles
  • Embossing: Press leather or cloth with a die to create a raised image without color
  • Marbling: Create distinctive patterned paper for end pages by floating ink on water and transferring the pattern to paper
  • Lettering: Paint or emboss the book title and author on the spine and cover

Binding Styles

  • Quarter binding: Leather covers only the spine; cloth or paper covers the boards
  • Half binding: Leather covers the spine and most of the board corners
  • Full leather: The entire binding is covered in leather
  • Cloth binding: Durable and affordable; standard for most modern handbound books

10. Record Keeping & Archives

In a post-collapse or off-grid society, the preservation of knowledge through careful record-keeping and library organization is essential. A community's survival and advancement depend on maintaining and accessing information about agriculture, medicine, technology, history, and culture.

Writing Systems

The foundation of record-keeping is a clear, legible writing system understood by the community.

  • Alphabetic systems: Simpler to learn and use than logographic systems; suitable for most languages
  • Standardization: Establish consistent letterforms, spacing, and punctuation so records are uniformly legible
  • Shorthand: Develop abbreviated forms of common words and phrases to speed writing; this is especially useful for copying manuscripts
  • Documentation: Create a reference document showing all letterforms, numerals, and punctuation marks with clear examples

Establishing a Community Library

A library serves as the community's repository of knowledge and memory.

  • Location: Choose a dry, secure location protected from weather, pests, and accidental fire
  • Climate control: Maintain moderate, stable temperature and humidity (ideal: 60-70¬∞F, 35-55% relative humidity)
  • Security: Protect against theft and unauthorized modification of records
  • Organization: Arrange books by subject category, making them easy for community members to locate
  • Access policy: Establish clear rules for borrowing, reading, and copying records

Cataloging and Organization Systems

A well-organized library requires a system to locate works quickly.

  • Catalog cards: Create an index on cards listing each work by author, title, and subject with its location in the library
  • Shelf marks: Assign each book a shelf location mark (e.g., "Medical/Herbals/001"); write this on the spine and in the catalog
  • Accession numbers: Assign each volume a unique number in the order it enters the collection; this helps identify works in dispute
  • Subject classification: Organize books into broad categories:
    • Agriculture and food production
    • Medicine and healing
    • Crafts and manufacturing
    • History and culture
    • Philosophy and ethics
    • Mathematics and science
    • Administration and law
  • Cross-referencing: Create multiple catalog entries for works that span multiple subjects

Preservation Practices

Books and documents deteriorate over time from environmental stress, biological damage, and handling. Proactive preservation extends their lifespan.

  • Environmental control:
    • Stable temperature: Fluctuations cause paper expansion and contraction, leading to brittleness
    • Moderate humidity: Too-dry air causes paper to become brittle; too-humid air promotes mold and insects
    • Exclude light: Natural and artificial light fade inks and degrade paper; store valuable works in darkness
  • Pest control:
    • Inspect regularly for signs of insects (bookworms, silverfish) or rodents
    • Store in sealed containers when possible
    • Use natural pest deterrents (cedar, neem leaves, diatomaceous earth) rather than toxic chemicals that may damage paper
  • Physical handling:
    • Keep hands clean and dry before handling books
    • Support the spine when opening books
    • Use bookmarks; avoid folding page corners
    • Do not write in valuable original texts
  • Binding repair:
    • Loose pages can be resewn to the binding
    • Torn pages can be reinforced with Japanese tissue paper and reversible glue
    • Weak bindings can be strengthened by resewing

Fire Protection

Fire is the greatest threat to a library.

  • Prevention:
    • No open flames or smoking in the library
    • Electrical systems should be well-maintained and inspected regularly
    • Chimneys and heating systems must be regularly cleaned and inspected
  • Firefighting equipment:
    • Keep water (buckets, barrels) and sand readily available
    • Ensure quick access to library exits
    • Train staff in basic firefighting techniques
  • Backup copies:
    • The most important records should be copied by hand or other means
    • Store backup copies in a separate, secure location
    • Critical information (medicine, agriculture, law) should exist in at least two locations

Copying and Document Reproduction

Important documents must be copied to preserve knowledge in case the original is damaged or lost.

  • Hand copying: The most reliable method for long-term preservation; trained scribes copy important works by hand
  • Printing: Once important works are identified, they can be printed using movable type or block printing to create multiple copies
  • Selection criteria: Prioritize copying:
    • Medical knowledge (herbs, treatments, anatomy)
    • Agricultural information (crop varieties, seasons, soil management)
    • Technical skills (metalworking, construction, tool-making)
    • Community laws and customs
    • Mathematical and scientific knowledge

11. Alternative Writing Surfaces

While paper is the ideal writing surface for most purposes, other materials have been used historically and may be necessary in specific contexts. Each has distinct properties affecting durability, availability, and ease of writing.

Parchment and Vellum

These are processed animal hides that create a superior writing surface that can last centuries with proper care.

  • Material source: Sheepskin, goatskin, or calf skin
  • Preparation process:
    1. Soak fresh hides in water for several days to soften
    2. Place on a wooden frame and scrape away hair and flesh with a sharp blade
    3. Wash and dry in the sun on the frame (this stretches and tightens the skin)
    4. Smoke or lime may be used to adjust the color and durability
  • Properties:
    • Extremely durable; documents on parchment have survived 1,000+ years
    • Accepts ink readily, especially iron gall ink
    • Can be erasable (with a knife) if needed, allowing reuse (palimpsests)
    • Expensive and labor-intensive to produce
  • Best uses: Important records, religious texts, legal documents, important personal records

Clay Tablets

Clay tablets are one of the oldest writing surfaces, used in ancient Mesopotamia. They are extremely durable and require no special materials beyond clay.

  • Production: Form clay into flat rectangular tablets and smooth the surface
  • Writing: Use a stylus (pointed stick or reed) to press marks into the soft clay; cuneiform scripts were developed for this method
  • Drying: Allow tablets to dry in air or fire-harden them in an oven for maximum durability
  • Storage: Clay tablets are nearly indestructible and can be stored indefinitely
  • Limitations:
    • Heavy and fragile when thin
    • Writing system must be adapted to stylus marks (not suitable for all alphabets)
    • Correction requires careful scraping or starting over
    • Not ideal for continuous long-form text
  • Best uses: Record-keeping (inventory, ledgers), standardized information (recipes, formulas), permanent archival records

Birch Bark

In northern regions where birch trees are common, bark serves as a natural, sustainable writing surface.

  • Preparation:
    1. Carefully peel bark from birch logs in spring (when bark separates easily)
    2. Dry flat under light weight to prevent curling
    3. Flatten further by heating gently between boards
  • Writing: Ink or charcoal marks adhere well to the white inner surface
  • Durability: Birch bark survives centuries if kept dry; waterlogged examples have been found preserved in peat bogs
  • Properties: Lightweight, easy to produce, pleasant appearance, suitable for sketching and writing
  • Best uses: Letters, temporary records, artistic expression, documents for storage in dry locations

Wax Tablets

Wax tablets were used in ancient Rome and medieval Europe for temporary notes and practice writing. They are reusable, making them economical for frequent revision.

  • Construction:
    1. Create a shallow wooden frame
    2. Pour melted beeswax (or tallow or animal fat) to fill the frame
    3. Allow to cool and harden
    4. Optional: Cover with a thin layer of color (charcoal or plant dyes mixed with wax) to make marks more visible
  • Writing: Use a stylus with a pointed end for writing and a flat end for erasing
  • Erasing: Smooth the wax surface with the flat end of the stylus; heat gently to reset the surface
  • Best uses: Student practice, temporary notes, calculations, correspondence, business records
  • Durability: Low; wax is susceptible to damage from heat and pressure; intended for temporary use only

Slate and Stone

Stone provides the ultimate durability for critical records that must survive indefinitely.

  • Material selection: Fine-grained slate or limestone splits into flat sheets suitable for writing
  • Inscription:
    • Use a chisel and hammer to carve letters into the stone surface
    • For temporary marking, use chalk or charcoal (washes away)
  • Durability: Stone carvings survive thousands of years with no degradation; among the most permanent writing surfaces
  • Limitations:
    • Labor-intensive to inscribe; errors cannot be corrected
    • Heavy; suitable only for important records
    • Not suitable for extended text; best used for short inscriptions
  • Best uses: Community laws and decrees, historical records, names and dates of important events, monument inscriptions

12. Paper Sizing: Preventing Ink Bleeding

Sizing Chemistry & Purpose

Sizing prevents ink from bleeding/spreading into paper. Unsized paper absorbs liquid immediately; sized paper resists absorption, allowing ink to sit on surface longer, creating crisp marks.

  • Why Size: Blank unsized paper absorbs ink like blotting paper (feathered edges, pale color). Sizing creates barrier; ink sits on surface, creating sharp text/images.
  • Chemistry: Sizing agents are hydrophobic (water-repelling) compounds. Weak sizing: water still penetrates eventually. Full sizing: water/ink repelled indefinitely.
  • Traditional Sizing Methods: Gelatin sizing (from bone/collagen), starch sizing (from grain), alum-rosin sizing (medieval/modern).

Gelatin Sizing (From Bone Glue)

Gelatin is historically dominant sizing method. Creates excellent sizing, improves paper handling, adds slight gloss.

  • Gelatin Preparation: See Bone Glue section in Leather Tanning guide. Briefly: boil bones 3-6 hours, strain to gel. Gelatin cools solid; reheat to liquid for sizing.
  • Sizing Ratio: Weak sizing (brief soak): 1-2% gelatin by weight. Strong sizing (multiple dips): 5-10% gelatin.
  • Process:
    • Heat gelatin solution to 50-55¬∞C (not boiling).
    • Dip paper sheet, drain excess. Or: submerge 1-2 seconds, remove.
    • Hang to dry (4-6 hours). Paper curls slightly as it dries (normal).
    • Press between flat boards if flatness needed.
  • Testing: Drop water onto sized paper. Unsized: water soaks in immediately (dark spot). Sized: water beads, sits on surface 10+ seconds.
  • Multiple Dips: For stronger sizing, dip again after first coat dries. Each dip increases water resistance exponentially. 2 dips = moderate sizing. 3+ dips = very strong sizing.
  • Effect on Paper: Gelatin sizing makes paper slightly stiffer, less absorbent, gives subtle gloss. Paper stays flat longer after wetting (useful for calligraphy).

Starch Sizing (From Grain)

Starch sizing from wheat, rice, or corn. Cheaper than gelatin, more sustainable if grain available locally. Creates adequate sizing for most purposes.

  • Starch Extraction: Boil grain flour in water, strain through cloth. Liquid contains starch suspension.
  • Strength: Grain starch weaker than gelatin (requires more dips or higher concentration). Typically 5-10% starch.
  • Process:
    • Heat starch suspension to 50-60¬∞C (avoid boiling, starch breaks down).
    • Dip paper, drain. Multiple dips improve sizing.
    • Dry 4-8 hours. Starch-sized paper often requires pressing (stiffer than gelatin-sized).
  • Advantages: Renewable source, less labor-intensive than gelatin. Adequate for inks, watercolors.
  • Disadvantages: Weaker sizing, requires more dips, starch can support mold if stored damp (add antimicrobial like salt).

Alum-Rosin Sizing (Medieval)

Rosin (pine tree resin) + alum creates very strong sizing. Used historically in medieval manuscripts, creates durable, well-sized paper. Requires both materials (alum is trade good, rosin from pine/turpentine production).

  • Rosin Preparation: Rosin is byproduct of turpentine extraction or sold directly. Dissolve in weak lye (wood ash water) or alcohol for easier handling. Traditional: dissolve in hot alum solution.
  • Ratio: Alum 2-3%, rosin 1-2% by weight in water. Complex chemistry; traditional recipes vary.
  • Process:
    • Dissolve alum in hot water first.
    • Add dissolved rosin (stirred into alum solution).
    • Heat gently to 50-60¬∞C. Liquid turns cloudy (rosin particles suspended).
    • Dip paper briefly (1-2 seconds). Rosin coating adheres to fiber.
    • Dry 6-12 hours. Very stiff result initially; flexes after weeks of aging.
  • Testing: Very strong sizing; water beads extremely well, takes 30+ seconds to penetrate.
  • Effect: Creates hardest, most durable paper. Excellent for documents, manuscripts (longevity). Slightly yellows paper. Requires specific materials (alum, rosin).
