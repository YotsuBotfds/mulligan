Why Breed Plants

The Fundamental Purpose

Plant breeding is not merely seed saving‚Äîit is the deliberate manipulation of genetics to create crops better suited to your specific environment and needs. While saving seed maintains existing varieties, breeding actually improves them over generations. For a community rebuilding civilization, this difference is critical.

Benefits of Active Breeding Programs

  • Adaptation to Local Conditions: Select plants that thrive in your climate, soil, and water availability. A wheat variety suitable for your latitude and season performs better than generic commercial seed.
  • Disease Resistance: In a post-agricultural-input scenario, you cannot rely on fungicides or pesticides. Breeding disease-resistant varieties is essential. Genetic diversity within a crop provides insurance against pathogen outbreaks.
  • Extended Growing Season: Breed earlier-maturing varieties for short seasons. Later-maturing varieties for areas with longer growing periods. Critical for food security.
  • Increased Yield and Nutritional Quality: Selection for larger seeds, more productive plants, and better nutrition compounds over generations.
  • Storage and Processing Qualities: Breed for lower moisture content (easier storage), higher protein (legumes), better flavor (root crops, grains), or characteristics that improve fermentation, drying, or storage.
  • Genetic Insurance: Landrace varieties‚Äîlocally adapted, genetically diverse populations‚Äîcan withstand environmental shocks and disease pressure far better than pure lines.

Seed Saving vs. Breeding

Seed Saving: Collecting and storing seeds from plants in your garden. Maintains existing genetic traits. Prevents loss of heirloom varieties.

Plant Breeding: Deliberately selecting plants with desirable traits, crossing them, and evaluating offspring. Creates new varieties suited to your specific conditions. Requires multiple years and careful record-keeping.

Every thriving ancient civilization developed local crop varieties through selective breeding. This knowledge is as important as farming itself.

Basic Genetics for Farmers

Dominant and Recessive Traits

Each plant has two copies of most genes (except in specialized cases). One copy comes from each parent.

  • Dominant traits: Expressed even if only one copy is present. Notation: capital letter (A)
  • Recessive traits: Only expressed when two copies are present. Notation: lowercase letter (a)

Example: Seed color in beans. Brown (B) is dominant, white (b) is recessive.

  • BB = brown beans (homozygous dominant)
  • Bb = brown beans (heterozygous‚Äîlooks brown but carries white gene)
  • bb = white beans (homozygous recessive)

Polyploidy: Understanding Chromosome Sets

Most plants are diploid (2n)‚Äîthey have two sets of chromosomes. Some crops have more:

  • Diploid (2n): Tomatoes, beans, peas. Most "normal" plants.
  • Tetraploid (4n): Durum wheat (used for pasta). More vigorous, larger seeds.
  • Hexaploid (6n): Bread wheat. Three pairs of chromosome sets. This is why wheat is so adaptable.
  • Sterile hybrids: Triploid (3n) organisms often produce no viable seed‚Äîseedless grapes, watermelons.

Practical implication: Bread wheat (hexaploid) tolerates more genetic variation and environmental stress than diploid crops. Understanding your crop's ploidy level explains breeding challenges.

Understanding Pollination

Self-Pollinating vs. Cross-Pollinating

This is the most important distinction for seed purity and breeding.

Self-Pollinating Crops (Pollen fertilizes same flower or plant)

  • Tomato - Flowers are perfect (have both male and female parts), pollen drops within flower. Cross-pollination rare (<5%).
  • Pepper - Similar to tomato. Mostly self-pollinating.
  • Beans - Flowers close before anthers release pollen. Automatic self-pollination in bud.
  • Peas - Flowers enclosed, self-pollinate before opening.
  • Wheat, barley, oats - Grain flowers are closed, self-pollinate 99%+.
  • Rice - Self-pollinating.
  • Lettuce, spinach - Mostly self-pollinating (though some bolting plants cross-pollinate wind).

Advantage for breeding: Self-pollinating crops are easy to keep pure. A single plant is genetically isolated. You can grow many varieties side-by-side without cross-contamination.

Cross-Pollinating Crops (Pollen must come from different plant)

  • Corn - Male flowers (tassels) at top shed pollen. Female flowers (silks) at side cannot self-pollinate. Wind-pollinated.
  • Squash, pumpkin, melon, cucumber - Large insect-pollinated flowers. Require bees. Naturally cross-pollinate.
  • Brassicas (cabbage, broccoli, cauliflower, kale, Brussels sprouts) - Insect-pollinated. All varieties easily cross with each other (one variety per seed year in small plots).
  • Beets, chard - Wind-pollinated. Multiple varieties will cross.
  • Carrot, parsnip - Insect-pollinated. Biennial‚Äîmust overwinter to flower.
  • Alliums (onion, leek, garlic) - Wind or insect pollinated. Onions cross easily.

Challenge for breeding: Cross-pollinating crops easily hybridize. You must use isolation distances, physical barriers, or hand-pollination to maintain purity.

Isolation by Time

For biennials (crops that flower in year 2) or widely separated plantings, use temporal isolation: stagger planting dates so crops flower at different times. Plant one variety early, another late. Reduces cross-pollination risk.

Physical Barriers

  • Bagging flowers: Before flowers open, place organza, muslin, or paper bags around flower clusters. Prevents pollinator access. Remove bag after self-pollination (check for open anthers first).
  • Caging with screen: Build a wooden frame with fine mesh (1/16" or smaller). Enclose flowering plant. Prevents insect pollination but reduces light. Useful for small-scale work.
  • Row covers: Lightweight fabric over entire crop during flowering. Blocks insects. Remove at fruit set if crop needs pollination.

Selection Methods

Mass Selection (Simplest Approach)

Method: Grow a population of plants. Identify the best plants visually (largest, healthiest, earliest, best flavor). Save seed from them. Replant next year.

Pros:

  • Easy‚Äîrequires no special equipment or crossing techniques
  • Fast initial progress‚Äîobvious traits improve quickly
  • Maintains genetic diversity (multiple plants selected)

Cons:

  • Slow progress for hidden traits (not visible until harvest)
  • Cannot select for yield alone (final size depends on environment)
  • May inadvertently select for the worst traits if you choose immature plants

Example: Grow 100 tomato plants. At harvest, select seed from the 10 largest, earliest, best-tasting fruit. Plant those seeds next year. Repeat for 5+ generations.

Individual Plant Selection (Row Trials)

Method: Grow individual plants in separate rows. Carefully observe each plant. Keep detailed records of yield, disease resistance, maturity date, quality. Save seed only from plants meeting your criteria across multiple traits.

Pros:

  • Precise‚Äîmeasures multiple traits simultaneously
  • Selects for hidden traits (yield, disease resistance require direct measurement)
  • Creates record of genetics (know exactly which plants you selected)

Cons:

  • Labor-intensive‚Äîrequires careful observation all season
  • Small number of selected plants increases inbreeding risk (see genetic diversity section)
  • Requires good record-keeping

Progeny Testing

Method: Grow plants from different seed sources. Evaluate their offspring for hidden genetic traits (disease resistance, yield potential, storage quality). Identify the best parent plants based on offspring performance.

Use case: You've grown many squash plants. Disease resistance doesn't show until plants flower and fruit develop. You cannot select based on visible traits. Instead:

  1. Grow offspring from each parent plant in a separate plot
  2. Observe which plots resist powdery mildew
  3. Save seed from parents whose offspring showed best disease resistance
  4. Repeat‚Äînow disease resistance alleles are accumulating in your population

Progeny testing requires 2-3 extra years but is essential for invisible traits.

Positive vs. Negative Selection

Positive Selection: Choose plants with desirable traits and breed from them.

Negative Selection: Remove plants with undesirable traits and breed from the rest.

Example with beans:

  • Positive: Select largest, fastest-growing plants
  • Negative: Remove diseased, late-maturing, small-seeded plants. Keep everything else.

Negative selection is often more effective for visible defects. Combined positive + negative selection accelerates progress.

Selection Pressure and Population Size

Selection Pressure: The proportion of plants you keep for breeding. If you keep only the top 5%, selection pressure is high‚Äîrapid change but risk of losing genetic diversity. If you keep the top 50%, pressure is low‚Äîslow change but maintains diversity.

Selection Intensity Plants Selected from 100 Speed of Change Inbreeding Risk
Very High (select top 1%) 1 plant Very Fast Extreme
High (select top 10%) 10 plants Fast High
Moderate (select top 20%) 20 plants Moderate Moderate
Low (select top 50%) 50 plants Slow Low

Minimum Viable Population for Different Crops:

  • Self-pollinating crops (beans, peas, wheat): 20-50 plants. These naturally inbreed, so they tolerate small populations. However, 50+ is safer.
  • Cross-pollinating crops (corn, squash, brassicas): 100-200+ plants. Cannot tolerate tight inbreeding. Small populations rapidly lose vigor.
  • Optimal for genetic preservation: 150+ plants of any crop maintains maximum diversity and avoids inbreeding depression.

Inbreeding Depression: When you select from too few plants, genetic diversity drops. The population becomes "inbred"‚Äîgenes become homozygous, revealing hidden recessive defects. You see: reduced fertility, lower yield, weaker plants, increased disease susceptibility. It develops slowly but is permanent without introducing new genetics.

Maintaining Variety Purity

The Problem: Unwanted Cross-Pollination

If you grow two varieties of cross-pollinating crops (like two corn varieties or two squash), they will cross. The next generation shows mixed traits‚Äîneither pure variety. For breeding programs, this ruins your work.

Isolation by Distance (Physical Space)

Standard approach: Grow only one variety of a cross-pollinating crop per season. If you must grow multiple, separate them by the isolation distance in the table above (or further).

Practical considerations:

  • Wind-pollinated crops (corn, brassicas, beets) travel farther than insect-pollinated
  • Elevation differences help‚Äîpollen falls; higher plots stay pure
  • Barriers (trees, buildings, hills) block wind-pollinated pollen
  • Timing matters‚Äîearly and late-flowering varieties are partially isolated by time

Isolation by Time

Method: Plant varieties at different times so they flower at different periods.

Example with brassicas (biennial seed production):

  1. Plant Variety A in late spring (flowers in year 2, late June)
  2. Plant Variety B in early summer (flowers in year 2, mid-August)
  3. Six-week separation reduces cross-pollination risk significantly

For annual crops, use shorter season vs. longer season varieties.

Physical Barriers

Bagging Individual Flowers

Best for: Squash, cucumbers, melons‚Äîlarge flowers, easy to bag

Process:

  1. Identify female flower (has small fruit behind flower base)
  2. Before flower opens, cover with organza bag
  3. Also bag a male flower (no fruit at base) on same plant (or nearby)
  4. When female is ready (pistil is receptive‚Äîslightly wet-looking), remove bags
  5. Hand-pollinate: rub male pollen onto female stigma
  6. Re-bag immediately
  7. Mark bagged fruit with tag

Caging with Screen

Best for: Brassicas, root crops with flowers

Construction:

  • Build wooden frame around flowering plant (or multiple plants)
  • Cover with 1/16" mesh screen (prevents insect entry)
  • Access door for monitoring
  • Allow some ventilation to prevent disease

Challenge: Reduces light, can increase humidity. Works best in dry climates.

Row Covers

Lightweight spun fabric over entire crop during flowering. Blocks bees and other insects. Must remove at fruit set if crop needs pollination (then plant will auto-pollinate or wind-pollinate).

Hand-Pollination Techniques

Squash (and cucurbits) Hand-Pollination

Timing: Early morning, before flowers close. Female flowers typically open only one day.

Steps:

  1. Identify a male flower (blooming today)
  2. Remove entire male flower from plant
  3. Peel back petals to expose pollen-covered anthers
  4. Locate female flower (larger, with baby fruit at base)
  5. Use male flower like a brush‚Äîdab pollen onto female stigma (center, receptive surface)
  6. Immediately place bag over pollinated female flower
  7. Label fruit with tag indicating cross

Tomato Hand-Pollination

Tomatoes self-pollinate, but you can cross two varieties deliberately:

  1. Select young flower (not yet fully open)
  2. Carefully remove pollen sacs (anthers) with fine forceps or tweezers‚Äîthis is emasculation (removing male parts)
  3. Allow flower to mature
  4. When stigma (female part) is receptive (slightly wet), brush pollen from a different variety onto it
  5. Enclose flower in bag
  6. Tag for identification

Corn Hand-Pollination (Detasseling Method)

Corn naturally cross-pollinates via wind. To create controlled crosses:

  1. Select two varieties: "female parent" and "male parent"
  2. Plant male parent in rows
  3. Plant female parent in rows between male rows
  4. When female parent is in early flowering stage (silks not yet fully emerged), remove tassels (male flowers at top) from all female plants
  5. Mark each detasseled plant
  6. Pollen from adjacent male plants will pollinate female silks
  7. Harvest seed from female plants (all will be hybrid F1)

Roguing: Removing Off-Type Plants

Definition: Removing plants that don't match your variety standard. Essential for maintaining purity and quality.

When to rogue:

  • Early growth: Remove stunted, pale, or abnormal seedlings
  • Mid-season: Remove plants with off-type leaves, architecture, or disease susceptibility
  • Flowering: Remove plants with wrong flower color, structure
  • Fruiting: Remove plants with wrong fruit shape, size, color, or quality

Technique: Pull the entire plant (don't just pick fruit). This prevents pollen from off-type plant from pollinating your keepers.

Record Keeping for Purity

Maintain a simple log:

  • Variety name
  • Planting location (plot, row number)
  • Planting date
  • Isolation measures used (distance, time, physical barriers)
  • Neighboring varieties (potential contaminants)
  • Flowering/pollination dates
  • Roguing notes (what was removed and why)
  • Seed harvest date
  • Observed purity (any off-types in final crop?)

Cross-Pollination & Hybridization

Why Create Crosses

Deliberately crossing two varieties creates a new population with traits from both parents. This is how new varieties are developed.

Reasons to cross:

  • Combine disease resistance from Variety A with early maturity from Variety B
  • Introduce new genetic variation to a stagnant population
  • Create F1 hybrids for single-season high yield
  • Develop landrace varieties mixing local and introduced genetics

Understanding F1, F2, and Segregating Populations

P (Parental): The original two pure varieties you're crossing

F1 (First Filial Generation): The immediate offspring of P √ó P cross. All F1 plants are genetically identical heterozygotes. They often show heterosis (hybrid vigor)‚Äîlarger, healthier than parents.

F2 (Second Filial Generation): Offspring from F1 √ó F1 self-pollination or cross-pollination. F2 plants are NOT uniform‚Äîgenetics segregate. You see variation in traits.

F3, F4, F5... onwards: Continued segregation. With each generation of selection, the population stabilizes around selected traits.

Selecting from Segregating Populations

Process (simplified):

  1. Cross two varieties ‚Üí get F1 seed
  2. Grow F1 population. Observe what happens: F1s are uniform but may have unexpected traits
  3. F1 plants self-pollinate (if self-fertile) or cross with each other ‚Üí F2 seed
  4. Grow F2 from same F1 plant ‚Üí get segregating offspring (different types)
  5. Select from F2 for your target traits
  6. Grow F3 from selected F2 plants
  7. Repeat selection each generation
  8. After 6-8 generations of selection, traits stabilize (become homozygous)

Tomato Crossing Example

Goal: Combine early maturity (Parent A = 55-day variety) with disease resistance and large fruit (Parent B = heirloom disease-resistant variety, 80+ days to mature)

Year 1:

  1. Plant both parents
  2. Emasculate (remove pollen) from flowers of Parent B
  3. Cross: use pollen from Parent A on emasculated flowers of Parent B
  4. Harvest F1 seed (hybrid offspring)

Year 2:

  1. Grow out F1 plants‚Äîobserve. They'll be vigorous, uniform, intermediate maturity
  2. Allow F1 plants to self-pollinate (tomatoes naturally self)
  3. Harvest F2 seed (segregating generation)

Year 3:

  1. Grow F2 from multiple F1 plants. You'll see diversity: some early-maturing, some late; some large-fruited, some small; some disease-susceptible, some resistant
  2. Select plants that combine your target traits: early maturity + large fruit + disease resistance
  3. Save seed separately from each selected plant (maintain as individual lines)

Year 4+:

  1. Grow F3 lines from selected F2 plants
  2. Continue selection within each line
  3. After 6-8 generations, varieties become stable‚Äîtraits no longer segregate

Squash Crossing Technique

Squash crosses are controlled via hand-pollination (see Variety Purity section). If you're creating a cross:

  1. Grow Parent A and Parent B in same location
  2. Let them flower naturally
  3. Hand-pollinate selected female flowers from Parent B with pollen from Parent A
  4. Bag and label pollinated flowers
  5. Harvest F1 seed

Segregation in F2: Unlike tomato, squash F2 shows extreme segregation. Fruit shape, size, color, and flesh quality vary wildly. Requires strong selection over multiple generations to stabilize.

Corn Crossing via Detasseling

See Variety Purity section for detasseling technique. All F1 corn from detasseled plants will be hybrid (first-generation cross).

Key point: Save F1 corn seed if you want hybrid vigor that season. Do NOT save F2 corn seed (offspring of F1) for breeding‚Äîthey segregate dramatically and often perform worse than parents.

F1 Hybrid Vigor and F2 Decline

Heterosis (Hybrid Vigor): F1 hybrids are often 10-20% larger, more vigorous, more disease-resistant than parents. This is because heterozygous (mixed) genes express both alleles, sometimes resulting in superior trait expression.

F2 Decline: When you allow F1 hybrids to self or cross, offspring become homozygous again. They lose vigor. This is why F1 hybrid seed doesn't breed true‚Äîthe magic is gone.

For survival breeding: Don't depend on F1 hybrids. They're great for single-season high yield, but you cannot maintain them. Breed for stable open-pollinated varieties instead.

Stabilizing a New Variety (6-8 Generations of Selection)

The Goal: Take an F2 segregating population and breed for consistent traits until stabilized.

Process:

  • F2: Massive variation. Select best plants matching your ideal.
  • F3: Still diverse. Select within each F2-derived line. Continue selection for same traits.
  • F4: Less diversity than F3. Keep selecting.
  • F5-F6: Very little segregation. Most plants look similar.
  • F7-F8: Stable. Traits don't change year to year. Variety is established.

Minimum time: 6-8 generations means 6-8 seasons (annual crops) or longer (biennials). A new tomato variety takes minimum 6 years from cross to stable variety.

Record-keeping: Document your selection criteria each year. Know which traits you're selecting for. This creates the "variety description"‚Äîthe official definition of your new variety.

Seed Saving by Crop Type

Grains: Wheat, Barley, Oats, Rice

Type: Self-pollinating annuals

Isolation: None needed (naturally self-pollinating)

Seed Development: 6-8 weeks from flowering to mature seed

Seed Saving Steps:

  1. Allow grain to dry fully in field. Seeds should be hard‚Äîcannot dent with fingernail.
  2. Harvest entire plant when seeds are mature dry (or just before, if rain threatened)
  3. Hang bundles upside down in dry location to finish drying (2-3 weeks)
  4. Thresh: Beat dried plants to remove seed heads. Traditional method: lay on tarp and beat with stick.
  5. Winnow: Toss seed in air or use fan to blow away chaff (light debris). Seed is heavier and falls into bucket.
  6. Clean: Remove remaining debris (broken grain, weed seed) by hand-sorting or using screens
  7. Store: Dry location, sealed containers, cool temperature

Seed Longevity: 5-10 years in good storage

Legumes: Beans, Peas, Lentils

Type: Self-pollinating annuals

Isolation: None needed (though peas can cross with other pea varieties at low rate‚Äî1-3%)

Seed Development: 3-4 weeks from flower to mature pod

Seed Saving Steps:

  1. Allow pods to dry fully on plant. Seeds inside should rattle in pod.
  2. Harvest entire plant or pick individual pods when fully dry and papery
  3. Shell: Remove seeds from pods (crack open by hand or beat dried pods)
  4. Clean: Spread seeds on tray. Blow away debris or hand-pick trash.
  5. Dry: If seeds contain >15% moisture (test by trying to dent seed), spread thin and air-dry 1-2 weeks
  6. Store: Sealed containers, dry location. Can freeze if >10% moisture removed first.

Seed Longevity: 5-10 years for most beans, 3-5 years for peas

Solanaceae: Tomato, Pepper, Eggplant

Type: Self-pollinating annuals (though pepper can cross-pollinate at low rates)

Isolation: Tomato/eggplant: none. Pepper: 150-300 feet if growing multiple varieties

Seed Development: 4-6 weeks from flower to mature seed in fruit

Tomato Seed Saving (Wet Fermentation Method):

  1. Harvest fully ripe tomato (seeds are viable when fully mature)
  2. Scoop seeds and gel into small container
  3. Add small amount of water
  4. Ferment 3-5 days at room temperature. A mold layer forms‚Äîthis is normal. Stir occasionally.
  5. When fermentation is complete (mold has broken down gel), rinse thoroughly under running water
  6. Use fine screen to catch seeds while rinsing
  7. Spread cleaned seeds on paper or screen to dry completely (2-3 weeks). Stir occasionally.
  8. Store in sealed container

Pepper Seed Saving:

  1. Harvest fully ripe pepper (red or yellow for mature seeds)
  2. Cut open, remove seeds and placenta (white part)
  3. Dry on paper plate indoors, away from direct sun, for 2-3 weeks
  4. Store in sealed container

Seed Longevity: Tomato: 8-12 years. Pepper: 3-5 years.

Cucurbits: Squash, Pumpkin, Melon, Cucumber

Type: Cross-pollinating annuals

Isolation: 500-1000 feet (squash/melon), 300-500 feet (cucumber)

Seed Development: 6-8 weeks from pollination to mature seed

Seed Saving Steps:

  1. Allow fruit to fully mature on plant (winter squash: until skin hardens; melon: until stem detaches). Immature seeds are not viable.
  2. Harvest fully mature fruit
  3. Cut open, scoop out seeds and pulp
  4. Fermentation method (recommended): Place in container with water, ferment 2-3 days (breaks down seed coating)
  5. Rinse thoroughly under running water to remove fermented pulp
  6. Dry on paper or mesh, stirring occasionally, for 2-3 weeks. Should be brittle when fully dry.
  7. Store in cool, dry place

Seed Longevity: 5-10 years for squash/melon. 3-5 years for cucumber.

Brassicas: Cabbage, Broccoli, Cauliflower, Kale, Brussels Sprouts

Type: Cross-pollinating biennials (flower in year 2)

Isolation: 1000+ feet (different varieties cross easily)

Seed Development: Entire second year

Seed Saving Steps:

  1. Year 1: Grow plants, harvest heads (or leaves, buds) for eating. Leave some plants in ground to overwinter.
  2. Year 2 Spring: Plants bolt (flower). Flower buds form on stalks‚Äîthese become seed pods.
  3. Allow flowers to be pollinated by bees naturally (or hand-pollinate if desired)
  4. Seed pods form and dry on plant over summer
  5. Harvest when pods are brown and papery (late summer/fall)
  6. Dry pods completely indoors (1-2 weeks)
  7. Shell: Break open pods, remove seeds
  8. Clean: Separate seeds from pod debris
  9. Store in sealed container

Note: Brassicas require space to overwinter. In cold climates, either mulch heavily or grow in cool greenhouse. In warm climates, grow year-round (plant fall, flower spring).

Seed Longevity: 4-5 years

Root Crops: Carrot, Beet, Parsnip

Type: Cross-pollinating biennials

Isolation: 1000+ feet per crop

Seed Development: Entire second year

Seed Saving Steps:

  1. Year 1: Grow roots, harvest what you need. Select best roots (largest, healthiest, best shape/color) for seed.
  2. Store selected roots in cool place (30-40¬∞F ideal) over winter. Roots can stay in ground in mild climates if heavily mulched.
  3. Year 2 Spring: Plant stored roots. They bolt and flower.
  4. For carrot: Umbel flower clusters form. Allow insects to pollinate.
  5. For beet: Flowers on tall spike. Allow pollination.
  6. Seeds develop in small seed balls (carrot) or pods (beet)
  7. Harvest when completely dry (brown, papery)
  8. Dry indoors 1-2 weeks more
  9. Thresh: For carrot, rub dried umbels between hands to dislodge seeds. For beet, open seed balls.
  10. Clean off debris
  11. Store in sealed container

Challenge: Root crop seed production requires 2 years. For survival programs, plan carefully‚Äîallocate storage/growing space year in advance.

Seed Longevity: Carrot: 3-5 years. Beet: 4-5 years.

Alliums: Onion, Leek, Garlic

Type: Cross-pollinating biennials (or perennial for garlic)

Isolation: 1000-1600 feet (different varieties cross easily)

Seed Development: Year 2

Onion Seed Saving:

  1. Year 1: Grow onions from seed or sets, harvest bulbs. Select best bulbs (large, symmetrical, disease-free) for seed.
  2. Store bulbs in cool, dry place over winter (32-45¬∞F)
  3. Year 2 Spring: Plant bulbs. They bolt and flower.
  4. Flower heads form (tall spike with small flowers)
  5. Allow bee pollination or hand-pollinate
  6. Seeds form in pods. Harvest when pods are dry and brown.
  7. Dry completely (2-3 weeks)
  8. Thresh: Open seed pods, remove seeds
  9. Store

Garlic Seed Saving:

  • Garlic does NOT produce viable seed (in most climates). Reproduce from cloves only.
  • Save largest cloves from best bulbs for replanting
  • This is vegetative reproduction (clones parent), not genetic breeding

Seed Longevity: Onion: 1-2 years (short-lived). Garlic: cloves store 4-6 months.

Leafy Greens: Lettuce, Spinach, Arugula

Type: Mostly self-pollinating annuals (some varieties cross-pollinate)

Isolation: Generally none needed (small risk of crossing)

Seed Development: 6-8 weeks from flowering to mature seed

Seed Saving Steps:

  1. Allow one plant to bolt (stop harvesting, let it flower). Most leafy greens are harvested young, so designate specific plants for seed.
  2. Remove flowers as plant bolts to redirect energy into seed production, or just let it flower freely
  3. Seeds mature in small pods or dry on flower heads
  4. Harvest when completely dry and brown
  5. Dry indoors 1-2 weeks
  6. Thresh: Rub dried seed heads between hands
  7. Clean off debris (chaff, small trash)
  8. Store in sealed container

Seed Longevity: Lettuce: 5-8 years. Spinach: 5 years.

Seed Processing & Storage

Wet Processing (Fermentation)

Used for seeds surrounded by gel or pulp (tomato, melon, squash, cucumber, pepper).

Why ferment? Removes seed coating, kills seed-borne diseases, improves germination rate, makes cleaning easier.

Process:

  1. Extract seeds with surrounding pulp/gel into container
  2. Add water (optional but speeds fermentation)
  3. Leave at room temperature 2-5 days
  4. Mold will develop on surface‚Äînormal. This breaks down gel coating.
  5. When gel is completely liquefied, add water and stir
  6. Rinse thoroughly under running water through fine mesh screen
  7. Continue rinsing until all slime is gone and water runs clear
  8. Spread cleaned seeds on paper or mesh to dry

Dry Processing (Threshing & Winnowing)

Used for dry seeds in pods or seed heads (grains, beans, root crops, alliums).

Threshing (Separating seed from plant material)

  • Traditional method: Spread dried plants on tarp. Beat with stick or flail to dislodge seeds.
  • Walk-on method: Spread dried plants on tarp. Walk on them repeatedly to crush pods and release seeds.
  • Rub method: Rub dried seed heads between hands over bucket (works for small amounts)

Winnowing (Removing chaff and debris)

  • Wind method: Pour seeds from bucket back and forth between two containers in breeze (or with fan). Chaff blows away, seed falls into bucket.
  • Screen method: Use screens of varying sizes to separate by particle size. Large debris stays on top screen, seeds fall through.
  • Hand-sorting: For small amounts, spread on tray and hand-pick debris.

Cleaning and Grading

Remove immature, diseased, or broken seeds. This improves germination rate and prevents disease spread.

  • Spread seeds on light-colored surface
  • Remove obviously damaged seeds (broken, discolored, diseased)
  • Separate by size using screens (uniform-size seeds germinate more evenly)
  • For small amounts, hand-sort

Viability Testing (Germination Tests)

Test seed germination rate to know if storage was successful or if seed is too old to use.

Simple Test:

  1. Take 10 seeds
  2. Place on damp paper towel, fold and place in plastic bag
  3. Keep warm (70-75¬∞F) and moist for 1-3 weeks (varies by crop)
  4. Count how many germinate
  5. If 8+ germinate, seed is fine. If <4, seed is aging and should be used soon.

More formal test (if tracking inventory):

  • Test 100 seeds in controlled conditions
  • Calculate germination percentage
  • Mark seed packet with germination % and date tested

Storage Conditions: Cool, Dry, Dark

Temperature: Ideal is 32-45¬∞F (0-7¬∞C). Cool storage dramatically extends seed life (roughly, for every 5¬∞F drop, seed life doubles).

  • Room temperature (70¬∞F) is acceptable for short-term storage (1-2 years)
  • Basement or cool closet (50-60¬∞F) extends storage to 3-5 years for most crops
  • Freezer (0¬∞F) extends storage to 10+ years for properly dried seeds

Humidity: Low humidity is critical. Seeds should be dried to <10% moisture content before storage.

  • Air-dry in low-humidity location (not recommended in very humid climates)
  • Use desiccants: silica gel packets absorb moisture. Place in sealed containers with seeds.
  • Rice or powdered milk: Old method‚Äîabsorbs some moisture, but less effective than silica gel

Light: Darkness or low light is preferable. Avoid direct sunlight.

Container types:

  • Paper envelopes: Allow some air exchange but provide little moisture protection. Good for short-term (1-2 years) in dry climates.
  • Glass jars with tight lids: Excellent‚Äîseal out air and moisture. Best for long-term storage.
  • Plastic containers with tight lids: Acceptable, though plastic can allow slight moisture permeability over years.
  • Vacuum-sealed bags: Excellent for long-term. Remove air, seal, add desiccant packet.

Seed Banks and Community Seed Libraries

Purpose: Preserve genetic diversity, share seeds, provide backup supply.

Structure for small community (20-50 families):

  • Designate one or more freezers for cold storage (-10 to 0¬∞F ideal)
  • Use sealed glass jars with desiccant packets
  • Maintain detailed inventory: crop, variety, donation date, germination test result
  • Establish rules: members contribute seed from their harvests, can withdraw seed for planting
  • Test germination annually on stored seed, replace if germination drops below 70%
  • Backup: duplicate important varieties in multiple locations if possible

Record keeping:

  • Variety name
  • Donor (who contributed this seed)
  • Collection date
  • Processing method (fermented, dried, etc.)
  • Quantity stored
  • Germination test date and result
  • Storage location and conditions

Maintaining Genetic Diversity

Why Genetic Diversity Matters

A genetically diverse population can withstand environmental shocks (drought, pest outbreaks, disease) far better than a pure line. In a survival scenario, diversity is insurance.

Three risks of low diversity:

  • Inbreeding depression: Loss of vigor, reduced fertility, increased disease susceptibility
  • Monoculture vulnerability: Single disease strain wipes out entire crop (example: Great Famine)
  • Reduced adaptation: Cannot respond to changing environmental conditions

Minimum Population Sizes

Crop Type Minimum Plants for Breeding Ideal for Genetic Preservation Reasoning
Self-pollinating annual (bean, wheat, pea) 20-50 100+ Naturally inbreed, so tolerate small populations. But 100+ maintains maximum diversity.
Cross-pollinating annual (corn, squash) 100-200 300+ Cannot tolerate inbreeding. Small populations lose vigor rapidly.
Biennial (brassica, carrot, onion) 50-100 200+ Space constraints often limit size. Isolate by distance and time if possible.

Rule of thumb for small communities: If maintaining multiple crop varieties with limited land, prioritize genetic diversity over individual variety purity. A diverse population of semi-hybridized corn (from uncontrolled crossing) is more valuable than pure lines that suffer inbreeding depression.

Landrace Varieties

Definition: Locally adapted varieties that are genetically diverse populations maintained by farmers, not pure lines. Landraces are stable regionally but contain internal variation.

Advantages:

  • Adapt to local climate, soil, pests through natural selection
  • Genetic diversity provides insurance against disease/pest outbreaks
  • Already proven successful in your region (if you acquire local landrace)

How to maintain a landrace:

  • Grow 100+ plants minimum
  • Use mass selection or light individual plant selection (don't select too intensively)
  • Save seed from many plants, not just the "best"
  • Allow some random cross-pollination in cross-pollinating crops
  • Periodically introduce new genetics by crossing in other varieties (every 5-10 years)

Managing Gene Pools in Small Communities

For a survival scenario with limited land:

Strategy 1: Cooperative Breeding (Multiple Communities)

  • Each community maintains different varieties
  • Exchange seed regularly (genetic rescue)
  • Prevents inbreeding depression from small populations

Strategy 2: Staggered Crossing (Within Single Community)

  • Maintain multiple divergent lines of same crop
  • Every 5-10 generations, cross them together
  • This reintroduces genetic diversity and restores vigor

Strategy 3: Accepting Landraces Over Pure Lines

  • Don't insist on variety purity‚Äîallow natural crossing
  • Select for local adaptation, disease resistance, yield
  • Let a "landrace" develop through uncontrolled breeding
  • More resilient than pure lines

Genetic Bottleneck Risks

Scenario: You obtain one packet of seed (10 seeds) of an excellent variety. You grow them and save seed.

Problem: All future generations descend from 10 individuals. If those 10 share hidden genetic defects or lack diversity for critical traits, you're stuck with those problems forever.

Prevention:

  • When acquiring new varieties, grow multiple seed lots if possible
  • Cross different seed sources periodically (introduces new genetics)
  • Test for disease resistance before committing to long-term breeding program
  • Keep records of seed source (if landrace from geographic region, note this‚Äîgenetic diversity is built in)

Seed Exchange Networks

Purpose: Regular exchange of seed with other communities/farmers prevents local inbreeding depression.

How to organize:

  • Establish relationships with 2-3 other nearby communities (within 50-100 miles)
  • Annual exchange: swap seed of major crops
  • Compare notes on performance, share breeding progress
  • If one community's variety succumbs to disease, can restore it from partner community

Documentation: Keep seed exchange log showing what was received from whom, when, viability testing results.

Breeding for Specific Traits

Disease Resistance

Approach: Progeny testing + negative selection

Steps:

  1. Source resistance: Find or obtain a variety known for disease resistance (or showing it naturally)
  2. Grow diverse population under conditions that encourage disease development (high humidity, inoculate with pathogen if possible, use infected field)
  3. Identify plants that survive/resist
  4. Save seed from resistant plants
  5. Grow offspring the next year under same disease pressure
  6. Continue selecting for disease survivors over 3-5 generations

Caution: Select from multiple resistant plants, not just the single best. Disease resistance is often polygenic (controlled by multiple genes), and using single plant can create genetic bottleneck.

Example: Growing squash in powdery mildew-prone area. Grow 100 plants. In year 1, 70% succumb to mildew, 30% show resistance. Save seed from 20-30 resistant plants. Year 2: grow their offspring, ~60% now resistant. Continue 3-5 years. By year 5, mildew-resistant variety established.

Drought Tolerance

Approach: Selection under water stress

Steps:

  1. Establish test plot with reduced water (rain-fed, minimal irrigation)
  2. Grow diverse population
  3. Identify plants that produce acceptable yield despite drought
  4. Note which plants maintain green color, don't wilt
  5. Save seed from drought-tolerant plants
  6. Repeat under same drought conditions

Complication: Drought tolerance often correlates with later maturity (more time to accumulate water). Make sure selected variety still matures in your growing season.

Early Maturity

Why critical: Short growing season or need to escape late-season disease pressure

Approach: Direct selection for maturity date

Steps:

  1. Plant at normal time
  2. Note flowering and maturity dates for each plant
  3. Select from earliest-maturing plants (20-30% fastest)
  4. Save seed from these plants only
  5. Repeat next year‚Äîpopulation maturity date shifts earlier each generation
  6. After 4-5 generations, maturity date is 2-3 weeks earlier than original population

Trade-off: Earlier varieties often produce less total yield. This is acceptable if it allows crop to mature before frost.

Flavor and Nutrition

Approach: Sensory evaluation + analysis

For flavor (subjective):

  1. Grow diverse population
  2. Taste fruits/vegetables from many plants
  3. Record which ones taste best
  4. Save seed from good-tasting plants
  5. Have multiple people taste-test to reduce personal bias

For nutrition (requires testing):

  • If possible, analyze seed protein content (quick tests available for major crops)
  • Select plants with highest protein/mineral content
  • More feasible for grain crops and legumes

Storage Quality

Approach: Growing test + storage trial

Steps:

  1. Grow diverse population to full maturity
  2. Harvest carefully (avoid damage)
  3. Store under cool, dry conditions
  4. Periodically inspect for rot, sprouting, or deterioration
  5. Record which plants produced the longest-storing fruit
  6. Save seed from long-storage varieties

Example: Root crop storage. Grow 20 different varieties. Store roots in root cellar at 35-40¬∞F. Check monthly. Some varieties rot in 2 months, others last until spring. Select from long-storing varieties.

Pest Resistance

Challenge: Often requires controlled infestation or field observation

Approach: Selection in pest-infested area + progeny testing

Steps:

  1. Grow in area known for pest problems (Japanese beetles, corn borers, squash bugs, etc.)
  2. Observe which plants suffer least damage
  3. Confirm with progeny test next year (offspring of "resistant" plants actually show resistance)
  4. Continue selection

Note: Pest resistance is often polygenic and not well understood without detailed research. Expect slow progress. Multiple-line strategies (next section) can mitigate risk.

Multi-Line Varieties as Insurance

Concept: Instead of one pure variety, maintain 3-5 closely related lines that differ slightly in pest/disease resistance.

Advantage: If one pest strain emerges that defeats one variety's resistance, other lines still produce.

How:

  • Grow 3-5 varieties (could be landraces, or lines you've bred)
  • Plant each in separate plot
  • Rotate which variety you plant in each location each year
  • Save seed from all of them
  • If one line loses resistance to emerging pest, you still have others

This is more resilient than single-variety farming.

Record Keeping & Trial Design

Importance of Records

Without records, you cannot:

  • Know which plants you selected from
  • Repeat successful crosses
  • Evaluate progress over years
  • Train others in your breeding work
  • Share results with other communities
  • Debug problems (why did this variety fail this year?)

Field Notebook System

Setup: One notebook per field/garden per year

Record for each crop variety:

  • Variety name (or experimental line designation)
  • Plot location (row number, planting area)
  • Seed source (where it came from‚Äîown saved seed, purchased, exchange, etc.)
  • Planting date
  • Emergence date
  • Flowering date
  • Maturity date (when mature seed/fruit)
  • Height (at key growth stages if measuring)
  • Disease observations (diseases observed, severity %)
  • Pest observations (pests present, damage level)
  • Yield estimate or count (number of fruit, weight of grain, etc.)
  • Seed count (if saving seed, record grams or count saved)
  • Quality observations (taste, color, flavor, size, anything notable)
  • Problems encountered (frost damage, irrigation failure, etc.)
  • Selection decision (keep or discard for breeding next year?)

Sketch plot layout: Simple diagram showing which variety is in which location, distance between plots, barriers used, etc.

Labeling System

Use durable plant labels or sticks with crop/variety name, planting date, and plot number.

Example: "Corn A-2024" (variety A, planted 2024)

Use weatherproof paint marker on plastic labels (survive season better than pencil).

Simple Trial Plots

Control vs. Experimental

Evaluate new variety against known standard.

Setup:

  • Plant row of standard variety (your current best) ‚ÄîControl
  • Plant row of new variety being tested ‚ÄîExperimental
  • Grow side-by-side under same conditions
  • Measure yield, maturity, disease resistance, quality
  • Compare results at harvest
  • If experimental outperforms control, adopt it. Otherwise, stick with current.

Multi-Year Evaluation

One season's trial is not enough. A variety must perform well over 2-3 years.

Recommendation: Trial new variety for minimum 2 years before replacing current variety. Accounts for year-to-year variability in weather.

Measuring Yield

For grain crops:

  • Harvest by row or plant (keep separate)
  • Dry completely
  • Thresh
  • Weigh seed
  • Calculate yield per row: weight/area planted

For vegetable crops:

  • Count number of fruit (tomatoes, peppers, melons)
  • Weigh total harvest (beans, peas)
  • Measure cumulative harvest over season (lettuce, spinach)
  • Record separately for each plant/plot

Key metric: Yield per plant or per unit area. This normalizes for field variability.

Community Variety Trials

Purpose: Multiple farmers test same varieties, share results

Structure:

  • Agree on variety/varieties to trial
  • Each farmer plants control plot + trial plot
  • Each records yield, observations, quality
  • Meet at season end to compare results
  • Make collective decision: adopt or reject variety

Advantage: Tests variety across multiple soil types, microclimates, management styles. More confident evaluation than single trial.

Common Mistakes in Plant Breeding

  • Using F1 hybrid seed: Don't rely on saved seed from hybrid plants. F1 hybrids don't breed true. Always use open-pollinated or heirloom varieties for breeding programs.
  • Selecting from too few plants: Inbreeding depression from small populations is permanent and takes years to recover from. Always maintain minimum viable population size.
  • Ignoring cross-pollination: Growing multiple varieties of cross-pollinating crops without isolation results in unwanted hybridization. Ruins your breeding work.
  • Poor record-keeping: Without records, you cannot know what you did or repeat success. Document everything: seed source, selection criteria, results, decisions.
  • Expecting too much too fast: Breeding is slow. 6-8 generations minimum to stabilize a new variety. Patience is essential.
  • Selecting only for yield: A high-yielding plant in a good year might fail in a bad year. Select for multiple traits: disease resistance, drought tolerance, storage quality, nutritional value.
  • Not testing progeny: A good-looking plant might carry hidden genetic defects. Grow offspring from selected plants before committing to long-term breeding.
  • Forgetting about disease pressure: Disease resistance is invisible until exposure. If you're not selecting in disease-pressure areas, you might be inadvertently selecting for susceptibility.
  • Ignoring seed storage conditions: Poor storage (warm, humid, light) drastically reduces seed viability. Properly dried and stored seed lasts 5-10 years; improperly stored seed fails in 1-2 years.
  • Single-year evaluation: One good season doesn't make a variety. Trial for minimum 2 years before adoption. Year-to-year weather variation is huge.
