1. Overview

In a post-collapse world, libraries become the vital repositories of human knowledge. Libraries preserve technical manuals, medical references, agricultural guides, and the cultural heritage that defines civilization. A well-organized library system enables communities to access critical information rapidly, support education, and prevent the loss of essential knowledge.

This guide covers the core principles of library science adapted for offline communities: classification systems, preservation techniques, and management practices that can be maintained without electricity or digital infrastructure.

Purpose of This Guide

Learn to:

  • Organize large book collections using proven systems
  • Preserve materials for decades using simple techniques
  • Create searchable catalogs without computers
  • Manage lending systems that prevent loss and theft
  • Design library spaces for community learning
  • Preserve and transmit oral knowledge alongside written records

2. Why Libraries Matter in Post-Collapse Communities

Before digital systems, libraries were humanity's primary knowledge repositories. They served multiple critical functions that become even more important when infrastructure fails:

Knowledge Access During Crisis

When grid electricity disappears, online databases become inaccessible. Communities need instant access to medical references, food preservation techniques, construction methods, and mechanical repair manuals. A well-organized library ensures critical information is available when needed most.

Education and Youth Development

Young people growing up without formal schooling require access to educational materials. Libraries support self-education, apprenticeship programs, and skill transmission. Books provide consistent, high-quality learning resources independent of individual teachers' availability.

Cultural Continuity

Libraries preserve art, literature, history, and philosophy. These materials maintain cultural identity and prevent psychological collapse. Reading provides mental health benefits during crisis periods and connects communities to the broader human experience.

Technical Knowledge Preservation

Specialized knowledge‚Äîengineering, medicine, agriculture, metallurgy‚Äîrequires comprehensive written documentation. Libraries prevent the loss of centuries of technological development and ensure advanced skills remain transmissible across generations.

The most valuable library materials are technical references (medical texts, engineering manuals, agricultural guides), followed by educational materials, then cultural works. Prioritize acquiring these categories during the transition period.

3. Dewey Decimal System (Simplified)

The Dewey Decimal System (DDC) is the most widely adopted library classification method worldwide. While the complete system contains thousands of subdivisions, this simplified version provides enough granularity for community libraries while remaining manageable by hand.

Basic Structure

The system divides all knowledge into 10 main classes (000-900), each subdivided into 10 divisions, each subdivided into 10 sections. A three-digit number identifies the subject; decimal places allow further specificity.

Call Numbers for Community Libraries

621.3 ENG (Engineering book by someone with surname starting 'ENG') 796.33 GRA (Sports book by someone with surname starting 'GRA') 615.5 LAR (Medical reference by someone with surname starting 'LAR')

Critical Subjects for Survival Communities

  • 615 - Pharmacology & Medicine: Essential. Acquire medical reference texts, nursing guides, pharmaceutical information.
  • 621-629 - Applied Physics & Engineering: Critical. Mechanical repair, construction, water systems, power generation.
  • 631-639 - Agriculture & Animal Husbandry: Essential. Crop production, food preservation, veterinary care.
  • 641 - Food & Drink: Essential. Cooking, food preservation, nutrition, food safety.
  • 682-684 - Woodworking & Metalworking: High value. Smithing, carpentry, tool making.
  • 701-799 - Arts & Architecture: Important for structural knowledge and morale.
  • 398 - Folklore & Mythology: Preserve cultural knowledge and traditional practices.

Field Notes: Dewey Implementation

Most surviving libraries will use simplified three-digit Dewey. Don't try to apply complex subcategories unless you have specialized training or library science reference texts. The 600s (Technology) section will be your most accessed and should be organized with extra care. Consider creating a separate "Quick Reference" section with 615, 621, 631, and 641 materials for emergency access.
Don't arbitrarily create new classification numbers. Stick to standard Dewey‚Äîthis ensures any trained librarian can navigate your system and makes knowledge transferable to other communities.

4. Library of Congress Classification (Basics)

The Library of Congress (LC) system uses letters and numbers to classify materials. While more complex than Dewey, it's particularly useful for large specialized collections and is widely used in academic and research libraries.

Main Classes

CodeCategoryApplications
RMedicineMedical texts, anatomy, nursing, pharmacology
TTechnologyEngineering, construction, manufacturing, utilities
SAgricultureFarming, forestry, animal husbandry, food science
QSciencePhysics, chemistry, biology, geology, mathematics
KLawLegal systems, regulations, governance
JPolitical ScienceGovernment, politics, public administration
HSocial SciencesEconomics, sociology, education, psychology
PLanguage & LiteratureDictionaries, fiction, poetry, drama
GGeography & AnthropologyMaps, travel guides, cultural studies
E-FHistory of AmericasHistorical texts, biographies, regional history

When to Use LC vs Dewey

Use Dewey for: Small to medium community libraries (under 5,000 books), mixed collections, ease of use by untrained staff.

Use LC for: Large collections (10,000+ books), specialized libraries (medical, technical), integration with existing research library systems.

For most survival communities, Dewey Decimal is simpler to implement and maintain. Only adopt LC if you're preserving a large institutional library or integrating with existing LC-classified collections.

5. Card Catalog Construction

Card catalogs enable users to search library collections without computers. A well-designed manual card system allows rapid searching by title, author, and subject. This section covers construction and maintenance of hand-maintained catalogs.

Card Types and Formats

Standard library cards measure 3" x 5" (76mm x 127mm). This size has become standard for two reasons: it's large enough for legible handwriting and small enough to minimize storage space. Obtain acid-free cards if possible.

Card TypePurposeOrganization
Author CardPrimary record entry by author surnameAlphabetical by last name, then first name
Title CardSearchable by book titleAlphabetical by first significant word (skip "A", "The", "An")
Subject CardSearchable by topic/subject matterAlphabetical by subject heading (standardized list)
Cross-reference CardIndicates related subjectsPoints to other card locations

Catalog Card Layout

Card Writing Standards

  • Handwriting: Use black ink pen (fountain pen with permanent ink works well). Write legibly in capital letters for call numbers, title case for titles.
  • Call Number: Top left, large enough to read quickly from several feet away.
  • Author: Top line after call number. Format: Lastname, Firstname, birth-death years if known.
  • Title: Complete title as it appears on title page. Underline or mark in red for emphasis.
  • Publication: Place of publication, publisher, year. City : Publisher, YYYY.
  • Subject Headings: Use controlled vocabulary (standardized list). Underline with red pen.
  • Notes: Include edition information, illustrations, maps, diagrams, special features.

Card File Organization

Maintain three separate card files: Author File, Title File, and Subject File. Each file is alphabetically sorted in wood or cardboard card drawers. Dividers mark alphabetical sections (A-B, C-D, etc.). This provides three search pathways for users.

Field Notes: Card Catalog Maintenance

Establish monthly maintenance routine: Check for damaged cards, verify call numbers match actual book locations, update circulation status. If you acquire a new book, write one author card, one title card, and 2-3 subject cards same day. Keep blank cards and ink pens with the catalog area. Assign this task to a dedicated person or small team‚Äîconsistency matters.
If handwriting multiple copies of the same card, use carbon paper to create author and title cards simultaneously. This saves time and ensures consistency between card copies.

6. Book Repair & Preservation

Books are fragile. Improper handling and storage cause rapid deterioration. Systematic preservation extends book life from decades to centuries and prevents loss of knowledge.

Common Damage Types and Prevention

Damage TypeCausePreventionRepair
Spine BreakingForcing book fully open, droppingHandle gently, don't over-flex. Support open books.Cloth tape over spine on inside; rebind if severe
Water DamageFlooding, moisture, condensationStore in dry location (below 60% humidity). Use desiccants.Air dry flat (2-4 weeks). Press between newsprint to flatten.
Mold & MildewHigh humidity, poor ventilationMaintain 30-50% humidity. Allow air circulation. Remove moldy books.Brush dry, air in sunlight, treat with boric acid solution
Torn PagesRough handling, age-brittlenessHandle with clean hands. Support pages when reading.Japanese tissue repair: glue acid-free tissue patch behind tear
Loose BindingAge, heavy use, moistureAvoid bending, support spine during readingRebind or reinforce with book tape and cloth
Foxing (Stains)Iron oxidation in paper, mold sporesStore in stable humidity and temperature. Handle with clean hands.Cannot be reversed; prevent spread by maintaining dry conditions

Basic Repair Supplies

Maintain a book repair kit with these essentials:

  • Acid-free book tape: For reinforcing torn spines and page edges
  • Japanese tissue: Thin, strong paper for page repairs
  • PVA adhesive: pH-neutral glue (not white glue, which is acidic)
  • Bone folder: Smooth tool for creasing and folding
  • Book press or weights: Heavy objects for flattening pages
  • Acid-free cardboard: For backing and reinforcement
  • Cotton cloth: For reinforcement strips
  • Scalpel or craft knife: For precise cutting

Tear Repair Procedure

Materials needed: Japanese tissue, PVA glue, bone folder, heavy weight

  1. Clean area around tear with soft brush to remove dirt
  2. Cut Japanese tissue slightly larger than tear (1-2cm on all sides)
  3. Apply thin, even coat of PVA glue to repair tissue
  4. Place tissue over tear from inside of page (if possible)
  5. Use bone folder to smooth out air bubbles
  6. Gently close book and weight it down for 24 hours
  7. Check repair after drying; excess tissue can be trimmed

Spine Reinforcement

For books with deteriorating spines:

  1. Open book to inside cover
  2. Apply 2-3cm width book tape along entire length of spine (inside)
  3. Use bone folder to press tape firmly to prevent bubbles
  4. If external spine is damaged, reinforce from inside with cloth tape first
  5. Allow adhesive to set fully before circulation
Don't attempt major rebinding unless trained. Poor rebinding can cause more damage than the original problem. For severely damaged books, consult with experienced conservators or preserve as reference-only materials.
Create a "Repair Log" documenting what books have been repaired, the work done, and when. This prevents redundant work and tracks material condition over time.

7. Archival Storage (Temperature, Humidity, Acid-free)

Environmental conditions dramatically impact book longevity. Books stored in optimal conditions can last centuries; books in poor conditions deteriorate in decades. This section covers archival storage principles.

Ideal Storage Environment

FactorIdeal RangeAcceptable RangeConsequences of Poor Conditions
Temperature60-70¬∞F (15-21¬∞C)55-75¬∞F (13-24¬∞C)Fluctuations cause expansion/contraction; heat accelerates deterioration
Relative Humidity30-40%25-50%Too dry: embrittlement; Too wet: mold, warping, ink bleeding
Light ExposureDarkness<50 lux, no UVFading, photo-degradation of paper and inks
Air QualityDust-free, non-pollutedFiltered airDust deposits; Pollutants cause chemical deterioration

Storage Space Design

Ideal locations: Interior basement (below grade, naturally cool), interior closets (stable temperature), or specially constructed storage room.

Avoid: Attics (temperature/humidity extremes), basements prone to flooding, spaces near HVAC vents (air currents), exterior walls (temperature swings).

Shelving and Book Arrangement

  • Shelving material: Acid-free wood (maple, oak) or steel shelving. Avoid particle board (off-gasses chemicals).
  • Shelf arrangement: Store books upright when possible (prevents spine damage). Rare/valuable books may be stored flat or at slight angle.
  • Book placement: Don't pack shelves too tightly; allow air circulation. Leave 5-10cm space behind books on back wall.
  • Weight distribution: Place heavier books on lower shelves to prevent structural strain.
  • Support: Use bookends to prevent leaning and warping.

Acid-Free Storage Materials

Paper becomes brittle when exposed to acid. All materials in contact with books should be acid-free:

  • Shelving: Unfinished wood, not plywood or particle board
  • Boxes: Acid-free archival cardboard (not regular cardboard)
  • Wrapping: Acid-free tissue or muslin
  • Padding: Acid-free polyester batting or wool felt
  • Dividers: Acid-free cardboard or plastic
  • Adhesives: PVA (polyvinyl acetate), avoid rubber cement or traditional pastes

Field Notes: DIY Humidity Control

Ideal storage has stable humidity 30-40%. If space humidity fluctuates: Use passive desiccants (silica gel, calcium chloride) during humid months. Replace when saturated. Maintain air circulation with small fan. Monitor with inexpensive humidity meter ($5-10). In dry climates, reverse process: Add moisture with damp sponges in sealed containers (monitor carefully to prevent mold).

Pest and Mold Prevention

Pests: Silverfish, bookworms, and roaches consume paper. Prevention:

  • Maintain storage at 50¬∞F or below (slows insect reproduction)
  • Keep humidity at 40% or below (unfavorable to pests)
  • Inspect new acquisitions for signs of infestation before shelving
  • Quarantine infested books separately
  • Use diatomaceous earth (food-grade) around storage area perimeter

Mold: Grows above 50% humidity. Prevention:

  • Maintain humidity 30-40%
  • Allow air circulation (don't block air vents)
  • Avoid storing books in damp basements without humidity control
  • Remove any books showing mold signs immediately to prevent spread
If mold is already present in stored books, isolate them immediately. Mold spores spread rapidly to adjacent materials. Treat moldy books outdoors with careful brushing and sunlight exposure before returning to storage.

8. Lending Systems & Accountability

Libraries serve communities through lending. A good lending system ensures books circulate while remaining traceable. This prevents loss and enables recovery of missing materials.

Circulation Recording Methods

Three practical options for tracking loaned books:

Method 1: Checkout Card System (Recommended for Small Libraries)

How it works:

  • Each book contains a pocket glued inside back cover
  • Pocket contains a checkout card with call number and title
  • When book is loaned, borrower's name and return date written on card
  • Card removed and filed by return date or borrower name
  • When book returned, card reshelved in book pocket

Method 2: Borrower Register (Best for Accountability)

How it works:

  • Maintain a ledger organized by borrower name
  • Record call number, title, checkout date, and return date for each loan
  • Page per borrower or several borrowers per page depending on volume
  • Book is loaned by referencing this register only (no books from the shelf go home without entry)
  • Return is noted in the same register entry

Method 3: Library Card + Shelf Marker

How it works:

  • Each borrower receives a library card with name and ID number
  • When book loaned, card is placed on shelf where book was
  • Card remains as "placeholder" until book returned
  • Return requires finding matching book in collection and removing card
  • Excellent for visual identification of missing items

Lending Policies

Establish clear lending rules to ensure accountability:

  • Loan Duration: 14 days typical (shorter for high-demand materials)
  • Renewals: Allow 1-2 renewals if no one else waiting
  • Late Returns: Grace period (3 days) before penalty notice
  • Restricted Materials: Reference books, rare items, or critical supplies checked out in-library only
  • Overdue Recovery: Personal reminder visit, then formal community notification
  • Damage/Loss: Borrower responsible for replacement cost or repair

Field Notes: Circulation Management

Weekly: Review all current loans, identify returns due in next 7 days. Monthly: Reconcile checkout cards or register against physical inventory. Quarterly: Full inventory of collection, check for missing items, update borrower records. Keep overdue notices brief and non-punitive‚Äîmost delays are simple forgetfulness. Community cooperation matters more than enforcement.
Consider "community access libraries" where books can be borrowed with verbal agreement only‚Äîespecially critical technical materials. Trust-based systems work well in small communities and prevent knowledge hoarding during crises.

9. Reference vs Circulating Collections

Different materials serve different purposes. Reference collections support in-library research; circulating collections enable home borrowing. Strategic separation prevents loss of critical materials.

Reference Collection (Non-Circulating)

Purpose: Materials consulted for specific information that must remain available for immediate access.

Typical materials:

  • Medical reference texts (anatomy, pharmacology, emergency treatment)
  • Technical manuals and engineering handbooks
  • Dictionaries, encyclopedias, atlases
  • Rare or unique books (single copy only)
  • Government records and legal documents
  • Maps and charts
  • Indexes and bibliographies

Circulating Collection (Borrowable)

Purpose: Materials individuals take home for extended reading or study.

Typical materials:

  • Novels, poetry, fiction
  • Educational texts (if multiple copies available)
  • Technical books with multiple copies
  • Practical guides (cooking, gardening, handicrafts)
  • History and biography
  • Children's and young adult literature

Collection Development Strategy

Acquisition priorities (in order):

  1. Medical Reference: Emergency treatment, anatomy, pharmacology, nursing
  2. Technical/Engineering: Construction, mechanical repair, metalworking, electrical basics
  3. Agricultural: Crop production, food preservation, animal husbandry
  4. Educational: Mathematics, science, language, history textbooks
  5. Cultural/Literacy: Literature, poetry, history, philosophy
  6. Skills/Trades: Carpentry, masonry, blacksmithing, textile production

10. Community Reading Programs

Libraries serve cultural and educational functions beyond basic access. Community reading programs develop literacy, preserve culture, and sustain mental health during crisis.

Literacy Programs

Goal: Ensure all community members can read and write at functional level.

  • Children's programs: Story hours, learning-to-read programs, youth reading groups
  • Adult literacy: Basic reading instruction for adults who missed formal education
  • Language programs: Teaching minority languages, dialect education for cultural preservation
  • Technical reading: Helping people understand manuals, specifications, and technical documentation

Educational Support Programs

  • Study groups: Organized groups working through educational texts together
  • Apprenticeship support: Library provides reference materials for apprentices in trades
  • Research assistance: Librarian helps people find information for specific projects
  • Skill workshops: Regular classes on topics like basic medicine, food preservation, or engineering basics

Cultural Preservation Programs

  • Story hours: Community members share oral stories, traditions, and cultural knowledge
  • Poetry readings: Public readings of literary works preserve cultural traditions
  • Discussion groups: Community reading and discussion of important texts
  • Archive projects: Recording and preserving local history, family stories, traditional practices

Mental Health & Morale Programs

During crisis, libraries support psychological wellbeing:

  • Escape reading: Fiction and literature provide mental respite and stress relief
  • Quiet space: Library serves as peaceful refuge from chaos
  • Community gathering: Reading programs bring people together, reducing isolation
  • Cultural continuity: Access to cultural and spiritual texts maintains identity and hope
Make library a welcoming social space. Comfortable seating, good lighting, and regular community events make the library a gathering place that strengthens community bonds beyond its information function.

11. Oral Knowledge Preservation

Libraries preserve written knowledge, but much human learning is oral tradition‚Äîstories, skills, songs, and practices passed verbally. Systematic preservation of oral knowledge complements written collections.

Types of Oral Knowledge Worth Preserving

  • Technical skills: Descriptions of traditional techniques, tool use, material properties
  • Medical knowledge: Herbal remedies, treatment techniques, diagnostic methods
  • Agricultural practices: Planting calendars, soil management, seasonal knowledge specific to local conditions
  • Cultural stories: History, mythology, folklore that defines community identity
  • Family histories: Genealogy, ancestral stories, personal survival accounts
  • Wisdom traditions: Philosophy, spiritual practices, ethical teachings

Documentation Methods

Method 1: Transcription

Record oral knowledge in written form:

  • Conduct recorded interviews or conversations
  • Transcribe to permanent written record (handwritten or typed)
  • Organize chronologically, thematically, or by speaker
  • Store in archival folders with preservation standards
  • Index for searchability and cross-referencing

Method 2: Apprenticeship Recording

Document skills through structured teaching documentation:

  • Master craftsperson documents their knowledge while teaching apprentice
  • Create step-by-step written guides with illustrations
  • Record variation in technique, materials, and local adaptations
  • Preserve unusual or valuable techniques before they're lost

Method 3: Systematic Interview Archive

Create formal oral history project:

  1. Identify knowledge-holders in community (elders, experienced craftspeople, traditional healers)
  2. Develop interview protocol with standardized questions
  3. Conduct taped or recorded interviews
  4. Transcribe and organize materials
  5. Index by subject, interviewee, and knowledge type
  6. Make available for reference and study

Interview Protocol Template

ORAL HISTORY INTERVIEW FORM Interviewee: [Name, age, background] Date: [Date of interview] Location: [Where recorded] Interviewer: [Who conducted] Knowledge area: [Medical/Agricultural/Craft/Cultural/Other] Opening questions: - How did you learn this knowledge? - Who taught you? How long did training take? - What resources or materials are essential? - How has this knowledge changed over your lifetime? Technical questions: [Specific to knowledge area - variations, mistakes to avoid, innovations] Context questions: - When/where is this knowledge typically used? - What seasons, conditions, or circumstances affect practice? - How does this knowledge connect to other skills? Transmission questions: - Who else knows this? Have you taught others? - What's at risk of being lost? - What advice would you give to someone learning this? Closing: - Is there anything else important we should record? - Can we contact you with follow-up questions?

Field Notes: Oral History Project

Start small: Interview 3-5 community elders on topics critical to your area (food preservation, traditional medicine, local agriculture). Transcribe within 1 week while memory is fresh. File copies in multiple locations as backup. This preserves irreplaceable knowledge while honoring community elders. Over time, build archive of 50-100 interviews covering critical knowledge domains.
Pair oral history documentation with apprenticeship. When a master teaches an apprentice while documented, knowledge is both preserved AND actively transmitted, ensuring practical competence alongside written record.

12. Common Mistakes in Library Management

Learning from others' failures accelerates your success. Here are frequent errors in community library management:

Classification Mistakes

  • Inconsistent systems: Starting with one classification system then switching midway. Commit to Dewey OR LC early.
  • Arbitrary modifications: Adding non-standard call numbers or inventing new categories. Stick to standard systems.
  • Over-specialization: Creating 50 subcategories when 20 would suffice. Simplicity aids usability.
  • No authority control: Subject headings vary wildly (same topic called different things on different cards). Use standardized subject lists.

Preservation Mistakes

  • Acid-contamination: Using regular cardboard, particle board shelving, or acid-containing materials. Costs years of book life.
  • Poor storage conditions: Ignoring humidity/temperature (most common). This is the single biggest cause of deterioration.
  • DIY rebinding disasters: Attempting major repair without training, often causing more damage than original problem.
  • Neglecting inspection: Not checking new acquisitions for mold, pests, or damage before shelving.

Circulation Mistakes

  • No accountability system: Lending books without recording who has them. Makes recovery of lost items impossible.
  • Unclear lending policies: No stated loan period, late fees, or damage responsibility. Creates disputes.
  • Reference books circulating: Allowing critical reference materials to leave the library, where they're lost or not returned.
  • Poor organization of checkouts: Checkout cards scattered, register disorganized, no way to find current loans.

Collection Development Mistakes

  • Acquiring wrong books: Too many coffee table books, not enough technical references. Prioritize critical knowledge.
  • No duplicate copies: Single copy of important book means only one person at a time can access it.
  • Neglecting local needs: Acquiring books on topics irrelevant to community while missing locally essential materials.
  • Ignoring condition: Accepting water-damaged, moldy, or heavily worn books that will deteriorate further.

Staffing Mistakes

  • No documentation: All procedures exist in one person's head; library collapses when they leave.
  • Insufficient training: Library staff unclear on classification, preservation, or policies, leading to inconsistent application.
  • Overwork: One person trying to do everything; library deteriorates from fatigue and overwhelm.
  • No succession planning: Not training younger people to eventually take over library management.

Community Engagement Mistakes

  • Library as resource hoarding: Restricting access or making borrowing difficult breeds resentment and reduces usage.
  • No community input: Not asking users what materials they need; library becomes disconnected from community interests.
  • Neglecting literacy programs: Assuming everyone can read; opportunity lost to improve community literacy.
  • Poor mental health of library: Unwelcoming space, poor lighting, uncomfortable seating discourages use.
The most common and costly mistake: Not maintaining proper storage conditions (temperature/humidity). This single factor causes more book deterioration than all other factors combined. Monitor this obsessively.
Document everything in writing. Create a Library Manual covering classification system, preservation procedures, circulation policies, and disaster response. This prevents knowledge loss and enables smooth transitions when someone leaves.
