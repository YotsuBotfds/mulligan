Intelligence, Scouting & Reconnaissance

Systematic observation, patrol methods, threat assessment, and intelligence gathering for community security.

Intelligence Cycle

Professional intelligence gathering follows a systematic cycle: Requirements (define what you need to know), Collection (gather raw information), Analysis (interpret and organize findings), Dissemination (share with decision makers), and Feedback (refine collection methods based on results).

This cycle repeats continuously. Each iteration improves your understanding and response capability. Never skip analysis—raw observations without interpretation lead to poor decisions.

Patrol Types

Presence Patrol: High-visibility movement through community territory. Primary purpose is deterrence and reassurance. Regular, predictable patrols signal strength and attentiveness.

Reconnaissance Patrol: 2-3 person team moving quietly to observe and report on specific areas. Focus on gathering information about activities, threats, and changes. Do not engage unless necessary.

Listening Post: Concealed position held for 4-12 hours where personnel observe and listen for threats or unusual activity. Requires strong noise discipline and patience.

Area Patrol: Systematic sweep of defined territory using grid or spiral patterns. Ensures complete coverage and reduces blind spots.

Observation Skills

Baseline Establishment: Learn what "normal" looks like in your area—typical traffic patterns, time-of-day variations, seasonal changes, and community routines. Anomalies only stand out against a solid baseline.

SALUTE Reporting: Standardize observations using Size, Activity, Location, Uniform, Time, Equipment. This format ensures consistent, usable reporting: "Five individuals, moving northeast, near the bridge, armed with rifles, 14:30 hours."

Tracking Indicators: Look for signs of passage—disturbed vegetation, footprints, freshly broken branches, ash, cigarette butts. Learn to read ground sign and movement patterns.

Night Observation: Allow 30 minutes of dark adaptation before night operations. Use peripheral vision, which is more sensitive to motion in low light. Avoid looking directly at objects in darkness.

Counter-Surveillance

Pattern Avoidance: Never follow predictable routes or schedules. Vary your movements, timing, and methods. Predictability makes you vulnerable.

Sound Discipline: Minimize noise from equipment, movement, and communication. Use hand signals when possible. Keep voices low and speak minimally during observation.

Light Discipline: Avoid flashlights and visible light sources. Use red-light filters when light is necessary. Never shine lights in directions that might reveal your position.

Deception: Mislead potential observers about your capabilities, positions, and intentions. Use decoys, false movement, and misdirection when appropriate.

Threat Assessment Framework

Evaluate potential threats using three criteria: Capability (what can they do to us?), Intent (do they want to do it?), and Opportunity (can they access us now?). All three must be present for a real threat. A capable and willing adversary with no opportunity presents minimal immediate danger.

Threat assessment informs response options: intelligence gathering, defensive preparation, deescalation attempts, or community protection measures. Reassess continuously as conditions change.

Information Management

Map Board: Maintain an updated map showing known positions, movements, and areas of interest. Update daily with patrol findings. Use symbols for different information types (threats, resources, community zones).

Log Books: Record patrol reports, observations, and incidents chronologically. Include date, time, personnel, observations, and assessment. These logs become historical reference and help identify patterns.

Need-to-Know Compartmentalization: Share only necessary information with each person or team. Sensitive intelligence should be restricted to decision makers and affected parties. This limits damage from compromised information.
