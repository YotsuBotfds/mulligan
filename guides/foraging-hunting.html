üî¨ Universal Edibility Test

Use this systematic 8-step process when you're unsure about a plant's safety. This requires patience and can take 24+ hours, but it's your safest approach for unknown plants.

‚ö†Ô∏è IMPORTANT: If the plant shows ANY signs of poisoning during ANY stage, stop immediately and discard completely. Never taste unknown mushrooms or fungi‚Äîthis test is for herbaceous plants only.

Step-by-Step Process

Step 1: Preparation
Use only one part of the plant (leaves, stems, roots, flowers) per test. Wear gloves to avoid skin contact. Test only one plant species at a time.
Step 2: Skin Test (15 minutes)
Rub a small portion on the inside of your wrist and wait 15 minutes. If rash, burning, itching develops, reject the plant.
Step 3: Lip Test (3 minutes)
Place a tiny piece on your lip. Wait 3 minutes. If tingling, burning, or numbness occurs, reject it.
Step 4: Tongue Test (3 minutes)
Place a small piece on the tip of your tongue (don't swallow). Hold for 3 minutes. If any burning, numbing, or bitter taste, reject it.
Step 5: Chew Test (3 minutes)
Chew a tiny piece thoroughly but do NOT swallow. Spit it out. Wait 3 minutes for any reaction.
Step 6: Swallow Test (5 minutes)
If no reaction, swallow a small amount (about an almond-sized piece). Wait 5 minutes.
Step 7: Rest Period (1-6 hours)
Wait 1-6 hours without eating other foods. Watch for symptoms: nausea, vomiting, cramps, diarrhea, difficulty breathing.
Step 8: Full Consumption Test (24 hours)
If no symptoms, eat a larger portion (palm-sized) and wait 24 hours. If still fine, the plant is likely safe. However, only the tested part is confirmed safe.
‚úì If all tests pass: The plant part is likely edible, but continue with small portions until you're certain. Different parts (roots vs. leaves) must be tested separately.

üåø Edible Plant Families

Learning plant families is safer than memorizing individual species. Most plants within certain families are edible, with notable exceptions.

Safe Plant Families

Rose Family (Rosaceae)

Nearly all edible: apples, raspberries, strawberries, almonds, plums, cherries, peaches. Safe indicator: alternate leaves, flowers with five petals arranged in spirals, fleshy fruits.

Legume Family (Fabaceae)

Beans, peas, lentils, clover. Indicator: compound leaves (leaflets arranged on a stem), flowers with irregular petals (butterfly-shaped), seed pods. Nearly all legumes are safe, though some raw beans contain toxins that are destroyed by cooking.

Grass Family (Poaceae)

Wheat, rice, corn, barley, oats, bamboo shoots. Indicator: hollow jointed stems, long narrow leaves, grain-like seeds. All true grasses are edible.

Amaranth Family (Amaranthaceae)

Amaranth, pigweed. Indicator: small flowers in dense spikes, alternate leaves, edible seeds and leaves. Highly nutritious.

Mint Family (Lamiaceae)

Mint, basil, thyme, oregano, sage, lavender. Indicator: square stems, opposite leaves, aromatic when crushed, flowers in whorls. All are edible and medicinal.

Dangerous Families to Avoid

Nightshade Family (Solanaceae): Contains deadly species like deadly nightshade and poison hemlock. Only eat if 100% certain (tomatoes, peppers, eggplant are safe cultivated varieties). Wild nightshades are dangerous.
Carrot Family (Apiaceae): Includes both edible (carrots, parsley, dill) and deadly (water hemlock, poison hemlock) plants. NEVER eat wild members unless absolutely certain of identification.

‚ò†Ô∏è Poisonous Plants

Learning to identify dangerous plants is as important as identifying edible ones. When in doubt, throw it out.

Key Warning Signs of Poisonous Plants

  • Umbrella-shaped flower clusters (unless you're 100% certain it's safe, like carrot or fennel)
  • Three-leaflet arrangements with shiny coating (poison ivy, poison oak)
  • Bean or pea-like pods that are unusual or brightly colored
  • Bulbs or tubers with strong chemical smell
  • Milky sap or unusual discharge when stem is broken
  • Extreme bitterness or burning sensation on tongue

Deadly Species to Absolutely Avoid

Water Hemlock (Cicuta douglasii) - Considered North America's most toxic plant. Symptoms appear within 30 minutes: tremors, burning mouth, excessive salivation. NO antidote. Indicator: hollow stems, triangular outline, purple blotches on stems.
Poison Hemlock (Conium maculatum) - Extremely toxic; causes neurological failure. All parts deadly. Indicator: parsnip-like leaves, musty smell, purple blotches on smooth hollow stems, carrot-family appearance.
Deadly Nightshade / Belladonna (Atropa belladonna) - 2-3 berries can kill an adult. Symptoms: dilated pupils, increased heart rate, hallucinations, muscle paralysis. Black berries attractive but extremely deadly.
Ricin (Castor Bean - Ricinus communis) - Seeds contain deadly ricin toxin. Extremely potent. Shiny mottled seeds. ALL parts dangerous.
Stinging Tree (Gympie-Gympie) - If in Australia: causes severe burning pain lasting months. Tiny hairs inject toxins. Even dead plant is dangerous.
Foxglove (Digitalis) - Pretty purple/pink flowers. Causes irregular heartbeat, severe stomach pain. Often mistaken for comfrey or lettuce.

Poisonous Berries

Black Nightshade berries: Initially green, then black. Highly toxic when raw (some toxins destroyed by cooking, but not recommended).

Baneberry berries: White or red, golf-ball sized. One berry can cause cardiac arrest.

Pokeweed berries: Dark purple, attractive. Causes severe vomiting and diarrhea. Leaves only safe if boiled 3x in fresh water.

Yew berries: Red cup with black seed inside. Seed is absolutely lethal (contains taxine). Red cup may be the only non-toxic part.

Mushroom Safety Rule: When In Doubt, Throw It Out

Golden Rule: Only eat mushrooms you have 100% positively identified. There is NO universal test for mushroom edibility. A single poisonous mushroom in a meal can ruin your day or kill you. Poisonous species sometimes grow among edible species.
Common Deadly Mushrooms:
  • Death Cap (Amanita phalloides): Green/yellow/white cap. Causes liver and kidney failure. May taste good, then kill you days later.
  • Destroying Angel (Amanita virosa, bisporigera): Pure white mushroom. Similar toxins to death cap.
  • False Morel: Often confused with edible morels. Contains gyromitrin toxin causing seizures.
  • Funeral Bell (Galerina marginata): Tiny brown mushroom. Extremely toxic despite small size.

üèπ Hunting Weapons

Tools for hunting small to medium game in survival situations. All require practice for effective use.

Bow Construction

Other Hunting Weapons

Atlatl (Spear Thrower)

An ancient lever system that extends the arm and launches spears with great force. Easier to master than bow. 12-14 inch wooden lever with a cup at the end. Holds 6-8 foot spear. Can accurately kill medium game at 20-30 yards. Effective in tight forest terrain.

Sling

Leather pouch with two long cords. Whirl overhead and release one cord to launch stone projectiles. Lightweight and portable. Requires practice. Effective for small game (rabbits, birds) at 30-50 feet. Uses free resources (stones). Silent and energy-efficient.

Spear

6-10 foot shaft with sharp point. Can be thrown or thrust. Requires strong arm. Good for close-range hunting and fishing. Easier to craft than bow. Less accurate than bow but sufficient for larger prey. Dual-purpose tool for self-defense and hunting.

Bolas

Two or three weighted balls connected by cordage. Thrown at running game to entangle legs. Effective for birds and small mammals. Traditional South American weapon. Silent and reusable. Requires open area to use safely.

ü™§ Trapping & Snaring

Traps work 24/7 and conserve your energy. Effective for small game, birds, and fish. Check local regulations before deployment.

Deadfall Trap (Figure-4 Trigger)

Snare Construction

Basic Wire/Cordage Snare

Noose made from wire, vine, or twisted fibers. Set 2-3 inches above ground along narrow trails. Animal walks through noose, tightens around neck. Silent and effective for rabbits, hares, foxes. Requires minimal materials. Check regularly to minimize suffering.

Spring Snare

Bend young tree, attach noose to tension release. When animal enters snare, tree springs up, suspending prey. Prevents scavenger access. More humane as death is quicker. Effective for birds and small mammals. Requires height and the right tree spacing.

Pit Trap

Dig hole 4-6 feet deep along game trail. Cover with branches and leaves. Animal falls in, cannot escape. Effective for large game. Labour-intensive. Requires monitoring. Used historically for elephants and bears. Not suitable for small-scale survival but useful for larger game areas.

Fish Trap Design

Trap Placement Strategy

Scout First: Spend time observing game trails before setting traps. Look for worn paths, scat, broken vegetation, and signs of recent use.
Placement Tips:
  • Set traps on narrow passages between obstacles (rocks, trees, brush)
  • Place where animals naturally move, not where they might be startled
  • Face snares downwind so animal doesn't smell your human scent
  • Use natural materials for cover; animals recognize unnatural colors
  • Set multiple traps along the same trail for better odds
  • Space fish traps with current flow, at inside bends where fish rest
Bait Selection:
  • Rabbits/Hares: Clover, apple, root vegetables, or set snares on active trails without bait
  • Squirrels/Rodents: Nuts, seeds, dried berries
  • Deer: Salt licks, acorns, corn (if available), or set on regular travel routes
  • Birds: Seeds, grains, berries, insects
  • Fish: Entrails, insect larvae, small fish pieces in weir traps

üé£ Fishing Techniques

Fishing can provide reliable protein. Multiple methods increase your success rate.

Making Hooks from Natural Materials

Bone Hook: Carve bone or antler into hook shape. Create barb by leaving sharp point. Lash to line with cordage. Sharp point angles slightly inward to prevent escape.
Thorn Hook: Straighten a strong thorn, then carefully bend one end into hook. Tie to line. Less durable than bone but quickly made.
Wood Hook: Carve hardwood into U-shape with sharp barb. Requires patience but highly effective. Less likely to break than thorns.
Wire Hook (if available): Bend suitable wire (copper, iron) into hook shape. Most reliable and reusable option. Create eye for line attachment.

Fishing Methods

Hook and Line

Most direct method. Tie hook to cordage (plant fiber, twisted hair, sinew). Use live bait (insects, minnows, worms) or lures (bright objects). Cast or suspend in water. Effective for most fish species. Requires patience but rewarding. Can fish multiple lines simultaneously.

Nets

Weave net from plant fibers or cordage in mesh pattern. Cast net into schools of fish and quickly gather corners. Seine net (large net dragged through water) can be made from interconnected vines. Highly effective when fish are numerous. Labour-intensive to construct.

Fish Traps/Weirs

Covered basket traps or funnel weirs direct fish into chambers they cannot escape. Work passively day and night. Effective in streams and shallow water. Described in Trapping section above.

Spearfishing

Thrust multi-pronged spear into shallow water where fish can be seen. Requires clear water and quick reflexes. Best in clear streams and ponds. Barbed prongs prevent escape. Effective for larger fish. Quiet method that doesn't disturb other fish.

Hand Fishing (Trout Tickling)

Gently rub a trout's belly in slow motion, then grip quickly. Requires patience and stillness. Traditional method in cold streams. Effective for trout and catfish. Meditative practice. No equipment needed.

Trotlines

Long line with multiple hooks spaced along length, baited and anchored underwater. Check periodically (regulations vary by location). Set in rivers and lakes. Passive method allowing you to do other tasks. Can catch multiple fish simultaneously.

ü™ì Game Processing

Proper processing maximizes meat yield and prevents spoilage. Always prioritize quick, humane kills.

Field Dressing (Step by Step)

1. Safety First: Ensure the animal is deceased. Keep blade sharp. Work on a clean surface if possible.
2. Position the Animal: Lay on back with legs spread. Support hind legs open for access to abdominal cavity.
3. Open the Abdomen: Make a shallow cut from sternum to anus, careful not to puncture intestines. Cut just through skin and muscle.
4. Remove Viscera: Carefully pull out intestines, liver, and other organs. Save the liver (inspect for spots‚Äîif damaged, discard). Tongue and heart are edible delicacies.
5. Cool the Carcass: Prop open cavity with sticks. If possible, hang in cool area away from insects. Keep meat as clean as possible.
6. Remove the Head (optional): Cut around jaw and through neck tendons. Head provides tongue, brain, and stock. Important nutrition source.

Skinning Technique

Small Game (Rabbits, Squirrels): Hold hind legs, step on back, pull on hind legs‚Äîskin often pulls right off. Make cuts at joints to separate. Quick and efficient.
Medium Game (Deer, Elk): Hang by hind legs. Make circle cuts around legs and belly. Peel skin down like removing a shirt. Keep pulling steadily. Clean knife keeps work efficient. Entire process takes 10-15 minutes with practice.
Preserve the Hide: Salt or dry hide immediately to prevent rot. Scrape flesh from inside. Stretch and dry in shade. Can be used for clothing, cordage, or trade goods.

Butchering Into Cuts

Large Game Butchering:
  • Front Legs/Shoulders: Separate at joint. Great for stewing. Multiple small steaks.
  • Hindquarters: Largest prize. Contains backstrap (loin), sirloin, and bottom round. Can provide steaks or roasts.
  • Backstrap (Loin): Most tender meat. Premium steaks. Cook quickly with dry heat.
  • Ribs: Braise or slow-cook. Flavorful. Can make broth from bones.
  • Neck: Tough meat, excellent for stew or ground meat (if you can grind).

Organ Meats (Highly Nutritious)

Heart: One of the leanest, most nutritious meats. Slice and cook quickly. Excellent protein and iron content.
Liver: Extremely nutrient-dense (vitamin A, iron, B vitamins). Check for spots or unusual color (discard if damaged). Slice and fry. Strong flavor.
Kidneys: Rich in iron and B vitamins. Remove outer membrane. Slice and cook. Distinctive flavor. Often used in stews.
Tongue: Surprisingly tender and delicious. Peel outer layer, slice, cook. Delicacy in many cultures.
Brain: Nutrient-rich. Cook thoroughly. Traditional delicacy. Small amount per animal (serves 1-2 people).
Bone Marrow: Split bones lengthwise, scoop marrow (semi-liquid fat). Extremely energy-dense. Can be eaten raw or melted for fat.

Bone and Sinew Uses

  • Bone Broth: Simmer bones for 24+ hours to extract collagen, minerals, and nutrients. Highly nourishing base for soups.
  • Bone Tools: Sharpen into points for fishing hooks, harpoon tips, or arrowheads.
  • Bone Carving: Create ornaments, dice, or handles for tools.
  • Sinew: Dry and shred for cordage, thread for sewing leather, or bowstring material. Incredibly strong for its size.
  • Hide Glue: Boil bone and cartilage to create natural adhesive for tool construction.

Insect Preparation Methods

Boiling: Most thorough cooking method. Kills all parasites and pathogens. Collect insects in water, bring to boil for 5-10 minutes depending on size. Can eat directly or add to soups.

Frying: Cook in fat or oil. Creates crispy exterior. Flavor enhancement. Best for grubs and larger insects.
Drying: Dehydrate in sun or smoke. Creates long-shelf-life protein source. Can be crushed into powder for flour or thickener. Preserve abundant species for lean seasons.

üçÑ Mushroom Foraging

ONLY forage mushrooms you have 100% positively identified. When in doubt, throw it out. There is no universal test for edibility.

‚ö†Ô∏è CRITICAL: Many poisonous mushrooms have no unpleasant smell or taste. A single poisonous mushroom in a meal can ruin your day or kill you. Never eat a mushroom unless you are absolutely certain of its identity. Poisonous and edible species often grow in the same area and may resemble each other closely.

Clearly Identifiable Safe Species

Morels (Morchella species)

Distinctive honeycomb cap with ridges and pits. Hollow interior. Found spring (March-May in temperate zones) near ash, elm, and dead trees. Excellent culinary mushroom. Nutty, earthy flavor. Common in deciduous forests. Rarely confused with dangerous look-alikes. Highly prized.

Chanterelles (Cantharellus species)

Golden-yellow color, funnel-shaped cap, false gills (ridges), solid white interior. Summer and fall. Common in forests near moss and organic matter. Fruity, peppery flavor. Beautiful appearance aids identification. Can be eaten raw or cooked.

Puffballs (Lycoperdon & Calvatia species)

Round, ball-shaped, white or pale interior (slice to check). When young and firm, completely safe. Must be white inside with no developing mushroom structures. Found in meadows and forests. Summer through fall. Once spores develop (brown interior), inedible.

Chicken of the Woods (Laetiporus sulphureus)

Bright orange and yellow shelf fungus on dead wood (oak, hemlock). Grows in overlapping brackets. Meaty, chicken-like texture when young. Must be properly cooked (can cause digestive upset if undercooked). Spring through fall. Very distinctive appearance.

Giant Puffballs (Calvatia gigantea)

Massive round structures (up to basketball size). Pure white inside when young. Slice and cook like steak. Mild flavor. Very rare but unmistakable. Massive yield when found.

Oyster Mushrooms (Pleurotus species)

Shelf-like, growing on dead wood in clusters. White, grey, or brown depending on species. Gills run down to where stem attaches. Mild flavor. Cool to cold weather. Common and widely cultivated. Most species are edible.

Deadly Look-Alikes to Avoid

False Morels vs. True Morels: False morels have a cap attached to stem (top of cap is solid), solid interior, and some have chambers in stem. True morels have honeycomb pits that extend into the interior. False morels contain gyromitrin (unpredictable toxicity). Only eat true morels.
Poisonous Chanterelle Lookalikes: Jack O'Lantern mushrooms are slightly toxic and glow faintly in dark. True chanterelles have ridges (not true gills) and are golden-yellow. Jack O'Lanterns are thinner-capped, brighter orange, and have forked gills that reach the stem uniformly.
Destroying Angel vs. Edible Agarics: Destroying Angels are pure white with a cup (volva) at the base of the stem and a ring on the stem. Many edible mushrooms lack one or both of these features. If you see a cup-like structure, do not eat it.

General Mushroom Safety Rules

  • Rule #1: Never eat mushrooms you cannot identify with 100% certainty. Mistakes are often fatal.
  • Rule #2: Use multiple identification guides (field guides, online resources, expert consultation).
  • Rule #3: Examine multiple specimens. Collect young, mature, and older examples to understand all stages.
  • Rule #4: Note the habitat: what tree species, soil type, and season.
  • Rule #5: Cut mushroom lengthwise to examine cross-section interior for color, chambers, or unusual features.
  • Rule #6: Never eat based on smell alone. Some poisonous mushrooms smell pleasant.
  • Rule #7: Cook wild mushrooms thoroughly. Never eat raw (exceptions: some modern cultivation, certain species when fresh).
  • Rule #8: When starting, forage with an experienced forager who knows your local species.
  • Rule #9: Taste test: Even after cooking, try a tiny piece first. Wait 24 hours before eating larger amounts.
  • Rule #10: Keep samples of mushrooms you've eaten for potential poison control consultation if anything goes wrong.

üè∫ Preservation of Wild Foods

Proper preservation extends the shelf life of foraged and hunted foods from weeks to years, enabling year-round nutrition.

Drying Game Meat

Jerky Method (Most Common):

Slice lean meat thinly (¬º inch) with the grain. Remove excess fat (fat goes rancid). Salt generously (preservative). Optional: smoke for flavor and additional preservation. Hang over fire or sun-dry on racks for 7-14 days until completely brittle. Store in cool, dry place in cloth bags. Can last 1-2 months or more if completely dry. Rehydrate with water for cooking or eat directly for trail food.

Smoking Method:

Cold-smoking (below 90¬∞F) preserves without cooking. Hang meat in smoky structure for 7-14 days. Hot-smoking (above 160¬∞F) cooks while smoking. Produces ready-to-eat jerky in 6-8 hours. Smoke from hardwoods (oak, hickory, apple) imparts flavor. Salt before smoking aids preservation. Smoked meat lasts weeks to months depending on temperature and humidity.

Salting/Curing:

Pack meat in salt (dry curing) or brine solution (wet curing). Salt draws out moisture, preventing bacterial growth. Requires 1:4 salt-to-meat ratio. Rinse before use to reduce saltiness. Can preserve meat for months or years. More labor-intensive than drying but effective.

Bone Broth Storage:

Simmer bones 24-48 hours. Skim fat for cooking oil. Cool and clarify. Gelatinous broth jellifies when cold due to collagen. Store in cool cellar for weeks, or freeze if available. Highly nutritious and long-lasting. Can be rendered into solid form for portability.

Smoking Fish

Whole Fish Smoking:

Clean fish, remove guts thoroughly. Salt inside and outside. Optional: brine soak for 6-12 hours. Hang by tail in smoky structure. Cold-smoke for 1-2 weeks or hot-smoke for 6-8 hours. Smoked fish lasts weeks to months. Flavor improves with age. Especially effective for fatty fish (salmon, mackerel, herring).

Fish Jerky:

Fillet fish, remove bones. Slice thinly or in strips. Salt and smoke-dry over 7-14 days. Lightweight and portable. Excellent for travel. Can be stored many months in dry conditions. More delicate than meat jerky.

Preserving Berries

Drying:

Spread berries on cloth or racks. Sun-dry 7-14 days or use smoke/low heat (below 130¬∞F). Dried berries last 6-12 months. Lightweight for storage and travel. Rehydrate with water for eating or cooking. Excellent in winter porridges.

Fermentation:

Pack berries in jars with salt (10% by weight). Weigh down under brine. Ferment 1-4 weeks at cool temperature. Produces lacto-fermented preserves with probiotics. Can last 6-12 months. Tangy flavor becomes less sweet.

Cooking to Jam/Preserve:

Cook berries with minimal water until thickened. High sugar content acts as preservative if available, or just cook down. Cool into concentrated fruit paste. Store in sealed containers. Lasts months. Cook with honey or sugar if available for faster preservation.

Freezing (if available):

Freeze whole or prepared berries in winter cold or ice house. Lasts until next season. Thaw carefully to avoid mushiness. Use for cooking rather than fresh eating after thawing.

Acorn Processing (Removing Tannins)

Leaching Method (Most Common):

Crack acorn shells and grind nuts into flour. Soak ground acorn meal in cold water for 6-8 hours, then drain. Repeat 5-7 times until water runs clear and bitter taste is gone (usually 2-3 days total). Dry the leached flour for storage. Can then be used in breads, porridges, or baked goods. Yields ~50% of original weight after processing. Rich in carbohydrates and fat.

Boiling Method:

Boil whole acorns for 1 hour, drain tannin-rich water. Repeat 3-5 times until water is clear. Shell and grind. Faster than cold leaching (6-8 hours vs. 2-3 days) but requires more fuel/heat.

Ash Leaching (Traditional):

Mix ground acorn meal with wood ash and water to create lye solution. Leach tannins in 1-2 days. Rinse thoroughly with water to remove ash. Speeds tannin removal significantly. Traditional indigenous method.

Storage Tips: Keep preserved foods in cool, dark, dry locations. Air-tight containers prevent moisture and insects. Salt and smoke naturally prevent mold. Properly dried food lasts longest (6-12+ months). Always inspect for mold or off-odors before eating‚Äîtrust your senses. Rotate stock regularly (use oldest first).

Long-Term Storage Strategy

Year-Round Nutrition Plan:
  • Spring/Summer: Hunt actively, fish, forage fresh. Dry and preserve excess.
  • Fall: Intensive preservation. Nuts, seeds, berries dried. Root vegetables stored. Game processed and cured.
  • Winter: Live from preserved foods. Supplement with stored roots, grains, and dried items. Insect protein if needed.
  • Diversity: Mix protein sources (meat, fish, insects, nuts, legumes) to ensure complete nutrition.
  • Rotation: Eat oldest preserved foods first. Maintain cycle year to year.
