1. Malaria: Recognition and Treatment

Malaria is a parasitic infection transmitted by Anopheles mosquitoes. Recognition by fever pattern is crucial, as rapid treatment is essential for survival. Malaria kills within weeks if untreated and becomes increasingly difficult to treat as it progresses.

Types by Fever Pattern

The fever pattern is the key distinguishing feature of different Plasmodium species:

  • Tertian Fever (48-hour cycle): Fever spikes every 48 hours (every other day). Patient experiences chills for 15 minutes, fever at 40-41¬∞C for 2-3 hours, then heavy sweating and return to normal. P. vivax and P. ovale cause this pattern. Patient feels well on "off" days.
  • Quartan Fever (72-hour cycle): Fever occurs every 72 hours (every third day). Much rarer, caused by P. malariae. Extremely severe chills and high fevers.
  • Quotidian (Daily fever): Fever spikes every day. Usually indicates mixed infection or severe P. falciparum malaria. This is the most dangerous pattern.

Clinical Presentation

  • Early symptoms (Days 1-3): Non-specific fever, headache, muscle aches, fatigue, nausea. Easy to confuse with common illnesses.
  • Characteristic fever (Days 3-7): Sudden high fever (often 40-41¬∞C), violent shaking chills, severe headache, vomiting, abdominal pain. Sweat profusely until temperature normalizes.
  • Severe malaria: Cerebral malaria (confusion, seizures, coma), acute renal failure (dark urine, no urine output), severe anemia (pale, weak, breathless), pulmonary edema (difficulty breathing), metabolic acidosis (rapid weak breathing), hypoglycemia (altered mental status).
Characteristic fever patterns help identify the parasite species. Tertian fever (every other day) is most common in non-severe malaria.

Treatment Options

If available, artemisinin-based drugs are most effective. Artemisinin comes from sweet wormwood (Artemisia annua). In extremity, quinine from cinchona bark can be used, though less effective.

Artemisinin (Sweet Wormwood - Artemisia annua)

If artemisinin tablets/powder available:
Adult: 200 mg oral daily for 3 days, then complete with partner drug (if available)

Sweet Wormwood Tea (emergency preparation):
- Harvest fresh or dried leaves
- 1 tablespoon dried leaves per cup boiling water
- Steep 10 minutes, strain
- Drink 3 times daily for 5 days
- Bitter taste, but highly effective

Quinine from Cinchona Bark

Quinine Extraction (historical method):
- Powdered cinchona bark contains 5-10% quinine
- Adult dose: 650 mg (2-3 teaspoons powder) every 8 hours for 7 days
- Take with food to reduce nausea
- Tinnitus (ringing ears) is sign of adequate dose
- If tinnitus severe, reduce dose by 25%

Side effects: Hypoglycemia, cinchonism (tinnitus, deafness, blurred vision), thrombocytopenia. If available, take with glucose or food.

Supportive Care

  • Hydration: Critical. Give fluids constantly. Oral rehydration solution is ideal, but water works. Patient will be in and out of sweating phases.
  • Cooling: During high fever, use cool water sponging. Remove excess clothing. Keep in cool, shaded area.
  • Nutrition: Small frequent meals. Broths, rice, bananas. Patient will have no appetite during acute phase.
  • Anti-fever medication: Paracetamol or ibuprofen for comfort (not for routine fever reduction; fever is beneficial).
  • Prevention of complications: Watch for cerebral malaria signs (confusion, seizures, altered consciousness‚Äîrequires immediate evacuation).

2. Intestinal Parasites

Intestinal parasites affect billions globally, especially in areas without sanitation. Most are diagnosed by stool examination and can be treated with herbal or pharmaceutical remedies. Symptoms include abdominal pain, diarrhea, anemia, malnutrition, and intestinal obstruction.

Common Intestinal Parasites

Roundworm (Ascaris lumbricoides)

  • Symptoms: Abdominal cramping, bloating, occasional passage of worms in stool, growth stunting in children, malnutrition.
  • Diagnosis: Adult worms visible in fresh stool. Eggs visible under microscope in stool.
  • Severe signs: Intestinal obstruction (severe abdominal pain, inability to pass stool, vomiting), respiratory symptoms (worms migrating through lungs).

Hookworm (Necator americanus, Ancylostoma)

  • Symptoms: Severe anemia (pale, weak, shortness of breath), abdominal pain, protein loss, malnutrition. Can be fatal in severe cases.
  • Diagnosis: Hookworm larvae visible in fresh stool samples. Anemia on examination. Protein in urine (dipstick).
  • Transmission: Soil-contaminated skin contact. Preventable by wearing shoes and improving sanitation.

Tapeworm (Taenia spp.)

  • Symptoms: Passage of worm segments in stool (look like rice grains), weight loss, vitamin B12 deficiency (if Diphyllobothrium), abdominal discomfort.
  • Diagnosis: Visible segments in stool or on underwear. Eggs under microscope.
  • Transmission: Undercooked beef or pork containing cysts.

Giardia (Giardia lamblia)

  • Symptoms: Acute watery diarrhea, abdominal cramping, bloating, malabsorption of fats (oily stool), yellow color. Can become chronic.
  • Diagnosis: Cysts or trophozoites in stool samples. "Steatorrhea" (fatty stool).
  • Transmission: Contaminated water. Can be transmitted person-to-person.
Different parasites produce characteristic symptoms and microscopic findings. Identification guides treatment selection.

Treatment Protocols

Pumpkin Seeds (Cucurbita pepo)

For Roundworm and Tapeworm:
- Consume 100-150 grams (handful) of raw or slightly roasted pumpkin seeds
- Eat on empty stomach in morning
- Consume castor oil (30 ml) 2 hours later (acts as laxative to expel worms)
- Repeat for 3 consecutive days
- Safe for children and pregnant women
- Effectiveness: 70-80%

Papaya Seeds (Carica papaya)

For Hookworm and mixed infections:
- Collect seeds from ripe papaya
- Dry seeds thoroughly (3-4 days in sun)
- Grind to powder: 1 tablespoon powder = ~50 seeds
- Adult dose: 1 tablespoon powder mixed with honey, twice daily for 7 days
- Child dose: 1 teaspoon twice daily
- Can also make tea: steep 1 tablespoon crushed seeds in 1 cup water, drink morning and evening

Wormwood (Artemisia vulgaris or A. absinthium)

For Giardia and mixed parasites:
- Dried leaves: 1 teaspoon per cup boiling water
- Steep 5-10 minutes, strain
- Drink 3 times daily before meals for 2 weeks
- Bitter‚Äîcan mix with honey or other herbs
- Avoid during pregnancy
- Effectiveness: 60-75%

Garlic (Allium sativum)

For all intestinal parasites (preventive and mild cases):
- 2-3 cloves minced with food daily
- Can be made into paste: pound cloves with salt, spread on bread
- Raw garlic most effective (cooking reduces efficacy)
- Safe for chronic use
- Effectiveness: 40-60%

Stool Examination Technique

You can identify parasites with simple equipment:

  1. Collect fresh sample: Obtain stool sample, preferably within 2 hours of passage. Use clean container.
  2. Prepare slide: Mix small amount of stool (pea-sized) with drop of saline solution on glass slide.
  3. Examine: Cover with glass coverslip, examine under microscope (40x magnification) or magnifying glass (10x).
  4. Look for: Eggs (small oval structures), larvae (thin mobile structures), adult worms (rare but obvious), cysts (round structures with internal organization).
  5. Repeat: Take samples on 3 consecutive days‚Äîparasites shed eggs irregularly.

Prevention

  • Hygiene: Wash hands before eating and after defecation. Teach children especially.
  • Water treatment: Boil water, use bleach (2 drops per liter, wait 30 minutes), or ceramic filters.
  • Food safety: Cook meat thoroughly to 71¬∞C (160¬∞F) internal temperature.
  • Sanitation: Use latrines, never defecate in open areas. Properly dispose of human waste.
  • Footwear: Wear shoes to prevent hookworm transmission through skin.

3. Tropical Fevers: Dengue, Typhoid, Yellow Fever

Tropical fevers are often indistinguishable clinically but differ in transmission, progression, and outcomes. Rapid recognition is essential as some are rapidly fatal.

Dengue Fever

Transmitted by Aedes mosquitoes (day-biting, aggressive species). Incubation 3-14 days. Usually non-fatal but uncomfortable and occasionally severe.

  • Symptom pattern: Sudden high fever (39-40¬∞C), severe muscle and joint pain (break-bone fever), headache, eye pain, rash appearing 3-4 days after fever onset (starts on chest, spreads to face/limbs, spares palms/soles)
  • Course: Biphasic‚Äîfever breaks day 3-4, returns for 24-48 hours. Total 5-7 days acute illness.
  • Recovery: Weakness and depression for weeks after acute fever.
  • Severe dengue: Dengue hemorrhagic fever (DHF)‚Äîbleeding (petechiae, bruising, nosebleeds), severe plasma leakage (shock, pulmonary edema), thrombocytopenia (low platelets). Mortality 1-5% with treatment.

Typhoid Fever

Bacterial infection transmitted via contaminated food/water. Incubation 6-30 days. More serious than dengue; requires antibiotics.

  • Symptom pattern: Week 1‚Äîgradually rising fever with headache, body aches, abdominal pain, constipation (or diarrhea). Week 2‚Äîhigh sustained fever (40-41¬∞C), headache, delirium, sometimes rose spots (small rose-colored rash), hepatosplenomegaly (enlarged liver/spleen), relative bradycardia (pulse slower than expected for temperature).
  • Key differentiator: Fever is sustained (continuous high), not spiking. Patient appears toxic and confused.
  • Severe complications: Intestinal perforation (sudden severe pain, signs of peritonitis), myocarditis (heart failure), encephalitis.
  • Untreated mortality: 20-40%. With antibiotics: 1-2%.

Yellow Fever

Viral infection transmitted by Aedes mosquitoes. Incubation 3-6 days. No specific treatment; mortality 15-50% in severe cases.

  • Symptom pattern: Acute onset fever (40-41¬∞C), chills, headache, muscle pain, nausea/vomiting. Remission at day 3-4 (patient seems better). Recurrence of fever with jaundice (yellowing of skin/eyes), bleeding (hemoptysis, melena‚Äîbloody stool, epistaxis), hypotension, shock.
  • Key differentiators: Jaundice is prominent. Bleeding develops. Relative bradycardia (heart rate inappropriately slow for fever).
  • Mortality phases: 15% die during acute phase with fulminant hepatitis and hemorrhagic diathesis.

Management of Tropical Fevers

Dengue Fever

  • No specific antiviral treatment: Supportive care only.
  • Pain management: Acetaminophen preferred (avoid NSAIDs which increase bleeding risk). Maximum 3 grams daily in divided doses.
  • Hydration: Critical, especially during remission period. Monitor for signs of shock‚Äîrapid weak pulse, low blood pressure, letharness.
  • Platelet monitoring: If platelet count drops below 100,000 with bleeding signs, risk of DHF. Transfusion necessary if severe bleeding.
  • Recovery: Gradual. Avoid strenuous activity for 2-3 weeks.

Typhoid Fever

Antibiotic treatment (if available):
- Fluoroquinolone: Ciprofloxacin 500 mg twice daily for 5-7 days (first-line in most areas)
- If resistance suspected: Cephalosporin (Ceftriaxone 1-2 g daily for 7-10 days)
- Macrolide: Azithromycin 500 mg daily for 5-7 days (for drug-resistant strains)

Without antibiotics: Herbal treatment and supportive care. Mortality high.
- High fever sponging with cool water
- Frequent small meals (avoid constipating foods)
- Avoid NSAIDs (increase bleeding risk)
- Isolation precautions (patient is contagious)

Yellow Fever

  • No specific antiviral: Supportive care only.
  • Bleeding management: Avoid NSAIDs, aspirin (worsen bleeding). Use acetaminophen.
  • Liver support: Avoid hepatotoxic drugs. Maintain hydration.
  • Isolation: Patient is infective during first 3-5 days. Use mosquito netting and protective clothing around patient to prevent transmission.
  • Prognosis: If patient survives first week of hemorrhagic phase, prognosis improves significantly.

4. Vector Control Strategies

Preventing infection is better than treating disease. Vector control is critical in tropical disease prevention.

Mosquito Control

Both Anopheles (malaria) and Aedes (dengue, yellow fever) breed in water and require standing water for larvae.

Standing Water Elimination

  • Empty all containers holding water: buckets, old tires, flower pots, coconut shells, roof gutters.
  • Keep water containers covered with tight-fitting lids.
  • Clear clogged drains and gutters.
  • Fill in puddles and low-lying areas that hold water.
  • Keep vegetation trimmed around water sources (removes resting places).
  • Drain or stock fish ponds (fish eat mosquito larvae).

Bed Nets and Screening

  • Bed nets: Most effective malaria prevention. Hang net over bed, tucked under mattress. Nets treated with permethrin insecticide last 3-5 years.
  • Screen doors/windows: Prevent mosquitoes from entering homes. Can be improvised with fine mesh cloth.
  • Net repairs: A single hole allows mosquitoes entry. Repair tears immediately with needle and thread.

Plant-Based Repellents

Citronella Oil (Cymbopogon nardus):
- Extract: Crush fresh leaves, distill or soak in alcohol
- Application: 10% citronella oil in coconut oil as skin repellent
- Effectiveness: 2-4 hours per application
- Safety: Apply to exposed skin, avoid face and eyes

Neem Oil (Azadirachta indica):
- Extract: Crush or cold-press seeds
- Application: 5% neem oil in coconut oil as repellent
- Also works as larvicide: 5% neem oil kills mosquito larvae in breeding water
- Effectiveness: 3-5 hours
- Smoke: Burn neem leaves in evening to repel mosquitoes

Tick Control

  • Prevention: Wear long sleeves and long pants in endemic areas. Tuck pants into boots.
  • Body checking: Inspect entire body daily for attached ticks, especially groin, armpits, hairline.
  • Removal: Grasp tick with tweezers as close to skin as possible, pull straight out with steady pressure. Do not twist. Discard in fire or seal in container.
  • Never: Squeeze tick body, apply hot objects, or use petroleum jelly‚Äîcauses tick to regurgitate pathogens into wound.
  • Repellent: Apply permethrin (if available) or neem oil to clothing.

Fly Control

  • Sanitation: Proper waste disposal, covered latrines, no exposed food, clean living areas.
  • Screening: Keep flies away from food, patients, and wounds with screens/netting.
  • Fly traps: Improvised traps‚Äîfermented fruit in bottle with small opening, or sticky paper. Attracts and traps flies.
  • Exclusion: Keep garbage in sealed containers, bury waste promptly.

5. Water-borne Diseases: Cholera and Dysentery

Cholera

Caused by Vibrio cholerae, transmitted via contaminated water. Incubation 1-3 days. Causes massive fluid loss and can be fatal within hours without treatment.

  • Symptoms: Sudden onset of watery diarrhea (rice-water stool‚Äîclear, odorless, profuse), vomiting, severe dehydration (dry mucous membranes, sunken eyes, no urine, shock‚Äîweak rapid pulse, cool clammy skin), muscle cramps.
  • Progression: Can lose 10+ liters of fluid in 24 hours. Death occurs from shock and electrolyte depletion.
  • Mortality: 1-2% with proper rehydration treatment, 50% untreated.

Emergency Treatment: Oral Rehydration Solution (ORS)

ORS Recipe

WHO Standard Composition:
  • 1 liter clean water
  • 6 teaspoons sugar (or honey)
  • 1 teaspoon salt
  • Mix thoroughly
Administration:
  • Give patient small frequent sips‚Äî5-10 ml every 5 minutes
  • If patient vomits, wait 5 minutes then resume slower
  • Continue even after diarrhea stops
  • For severe dehydration, goal is 100 ml/kg over 4 hours
  • Total daily fluid requirement can exceed 20 liters
Signs of adequate rehydration:
  • Return of normal skin turgor (skin springs back when pinched)
  • Normal mental status
  • Adequate urine output (clear, pale)
  • Disappearance of sunken eyes

Antibiotic Treatment

If antibiotics available (adjunct to ORS, not replacement):
- Doxycycline 300 mg single dose
- Cipro 500 mg twice daily for 3 days
- Reduces diarrhea duration by 1-2 days but not essential if ORS adequate

Prevention

  • Boil water (rolling boil for 1 minute kills all cholera vibrios)
  • Bleach: 2 drops per liter, wait 30 minutes
  • Improve sanitation‚Äîisolate latrines from water sources
  • Hand washing with soap and clean water
  • Careful food handling

Dysentery

Inflammatory diarrhea caused by bacterial (Shigella, Salmonella) or parasitic (Entamoeba histolytica) infection. Characterized by bloody diarrhea.

  • Shigellosis (bacterial): Incubation 1-3 days. Acute diarrhea, blood/mucus in stool, abdominal cramping, fever. Usually self-limiting 5-7 days.
  • Amebiasis (parasitic): Incubation weeks to months. Chronic diarrhea with bloody mucus, abdominal pain, tenesmus (persistent urge to defecate). Can invade liver (liver abscess).

Treatment

Bacterial dysentery:
- Supportive care with ORS is primary
- Antibiotics if available: Cipro 500 mg twice daily for 3-5 days
- Avoid antimotility drugs (loperamide)‚Äîprolong infection and increase complications

Parasitic dysentery (Amebic):
- Herbal: Wormwood (Artemisia) tea as in parasite section
- If metronidazole available: 750 mg three times daily for 10 days
- Follow with paromomycin 25-30 mg/kg daily for 7 days to clear intestinal cysts

Common Mistakes in Parasite and Tropical Disease Management

Critical Errors to Avoid

  • Using antimotility drugs in dysentery or cholera: Loperamide (Imodium) and diphenoxylate (Lomotil) can trap pathogens, increase complications, and prolong illness. Use only after diarrhea is resolving.
  • Assuming malaria is malaria without fever pattern: Fever patterns are diagnostic. Treating all fevers as malaria misses typhoid (which requires antibiotics) and yellow fever (which is fatal untreated).
  • Delaying ORS administration in cholera: Every minute of delay increases mortality. ORS should be started immediately and continued through entire acute phase.
  • Over-treating malaria with quinine without monitoring: Quinine can cause severe hypoglycemia, cinchonism, and thrombocytopenia. Monitor for tinnitus (sign of adequate dosing) and reduce if severe.
  • Crushing infected worms instead of using anthelmintics: Mechanical trauma to worms (from purgatives without medication or physical trauma) can release parasitic antigens and cause severe allergic reactions and anaphylaxis.
  • Treating intestinal parasites without addressing underlying malnutrition: Parasites cause malnutrition; treating parasites without nutritional support fails. Provide protein, iron, vitamins during recovery.
  • Incorrect squeezing of tick during removal: Squeezing tick body can inject pathogen-laden saliva into wound, increasing infection risk. Always remove by pulling on mouth parts.
  • Starting antibiotics too early in dengue: Dengue is viral; antibiotics do not help but promote resistance. Avoid NSAIDs which increase hemorrhage risk.
  • Assuming recovered malaria patient is immune: Recovery from one malaria species does not confer immunity to others. Reinfection is common.
  • Ignoring warning signs of severe disease: Cerebral malaria, dengue hemorrhagic fever, yellow fever with jaundice, and cholera shock require immediate evacuation/advanced care. Do not delay.
