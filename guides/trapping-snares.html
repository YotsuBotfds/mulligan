Why Trapping Works

Trapping is one of the most efficient food procurement methods in a survival or low-resource scenario. Unlike active hunting, which demands constant energy expenditure, presence, and skill, traps work passively while you sleep, forage, tend crops, or perform other essential tasks.

Core Advantages

  • Zero Active Energy Cost: Once set, a trap requires no further effort until checked. You gain protein while doing other work.
  • Nocturnal & Crepuscular Animals: Traps catch prey active when hunting is difficult‚Äîdeer at night, rabbits at dusk, predators under cover of darkness.
  • Wary Animals: Foxes, bobcats, and coyotes avoid human presence; traps bypass their avoidance behavior entirely.
  • Low Skill Barrier: Trap placement is learnable; execution is more forgiving than marksmanship.
  • Scalability: Multiple traps = multiple chances of success, spreading risk across a larger area.
  • Weather Resilience: Traps work in rain, snow, and fog when hunting is compromised.

Legal Context (Historical)

Modern Legality: In contemporary society, trapping is heavily regulated by season, location, species, and trap type. Many regions prohibit snares, leg-hold traps, or certain deadfalls outright. In a post-collapse scenario, these regulations cease to apply, but understanding the *why* behind them‚Äîethical dispatch, prevention of suffering, sustainable population management‚Äîremains relevant to responsible practice.

In a functional legal system, trapping requires licenses, adherence to trap types, and daily checking (usually within 24 hours). In a survival context, these safeguards become your own moral framework.

Efficiency Metrics

  • Return on Time: 2‚Äì5 minutes to set a snare can yield 0.5‚Äì2 kg of meat over 24 hours.
  • Caloric Output: A rabbit trap line of 10 snares may produce 5‚Äì15 kg of meat per week (2,500‚Äì7,500 kcal).
  • Scalability: A trap line of 50‚Äì100 sets across a 5 km circuit yields sustained protein throughout the year.

Snares: Wire & Cordage Sets

Snares are the simplest and most effective traps for small to medium game. A snare is a loop of wire or cordage anchored to a fixed point or weighted drag, placed where an animal's head will pass through. As the animal moves forward, the loop tightens around the neck or body, restraining or killing it depending on design.

Snare Types & Mechanics

1. Simple Loop Snare (Passive)

The most basic snare: a loop of wire or cordage set at animal head height on a trail. No mechanical advantage. Effective for rabbits, hares, and young deer.

  • Construction: Wire gauge depends on target species (see below). Tie a fixed loop at one end; anchor the running loop to a staked peg.
  • Placement: On game trails, at the narrowest point of a funnel (e.g., between two rocks), or just above snow/ground level (rabbit snares sit 2‚Äì3 inches above ground).
  • Advantage: Extremely fast to set; nearly invisible when camouflaged with grass or leaves.
  • Disadvantage: Relies on animal struggling to tighten the loop; can fail if the snare is too loose or if the animal escapes sideways.

2. Spring Snare (Active‚ÄîLifts Catch)

A loop snare powered by a bent sapling or spring mechanism. When the animal triggers the snare, the spring releases and lifts the animal off the ground, preventing scavengers from accessing it before you check the trap.

  • Construction: Bend a young sapling (2‚Äì5 cm diameter) over a trigger stick arrangement. Attach cordage with the snare loop to the sapling. When the trigger releases, the sapling snaps upright, lifting the loop.
  • Advantage: Catch is suspended, protecting it from foxes, coyotes, and scavengers. Animal hangs quickly, reducing suffering. Preserved meat; no ground contact = less contamination.
  • Disadvantage: More complex to set; requires suitable saplings and a trigger mechanism (usually a figure-4 trigger).

Spring Snare with Figure-4 Trigger (Compressed State)

3. Drag Snare (Self-Anchoring)

A snare loop attached to a heavy log or weighted drag. When the animal is caught, it pulls the drag behind it, causing exhaustion and eventual immobilization.

  • Construction: Attach a snare loop to a 3‚Äì5 kg log or bundle of stones via cordage. Anchor the loop setup on the trail; the animal pulls the drag when caught.
  • Advantage: No permanent anchor needed; works in areas where you cannot drive stakes. Drag slows animal, allowing recapture over distance.
  • Disadvantage: Animal may drag the weight far from the trap site, requiring tracking. Higher injury/suffering risk if animal fights the drag for extended time.

Snare Placement Sets

Trail Set

Placed directly on an animal trail. Most basic placement. Works best in narrow, well-used paths where animals are forced to move in a predictable direction.

Funnel / Fence Set

Two lines of branches, rocks, or twigs creating a V-shaped or narrow channel that funnels the animal into the snare loop. Animals following the path of least resistance push into the snare.

Cubby Set

A small enclosure (den-like) with the snare loop at the entrance. Carnivores and curious herbivores are attracted to investigate the small opening. Effective for fox, coyote, marten, and raccoon.

Water Set

Placed at the edge of a stream or water hole. Animals must pass through the snare to drink. Highly effective in dry seasons when water is scarce.

Snare Construction Tips

Boil & Scent Removal: New wire carries human scent and shiny appearance. Boil in bark water (oak, pine, or alder bark simmered for hours) or bury in earth for 1‚Äì2 weeks. Use gloves when handling; avoid touching the loop with bare hands.
Anchor Point: Anchor must be immovable‚Äîa thick stake driven deep, a rock embedded in earth, or a tree. Test by pulling hard; if it moves, reinforce it.
Loop Shape: The loop should hang at the narrowest point of the expected neck or head. Test by holding a rabbit-sized ball (or the actual animal if available) at the set location; adjust height and position accordingly.
Camouflage: Cover the anchor, wire, and surrounding area with natural vegetation. Dust with dirt. Make the snare invisible; the path itself should be the attraction, not the device.

Deadfall Traps

A deadfall is a heavy weight (rock, log) that falls on an animal when a trigger mechanism is released. Deadfalls are effective, require no metal (purely wood/stone), and are scalable from small rodents to large game. The key is a reliable trigger mechanism and sufficient weight.

Rock Weight Specifications

The weight must be heavy enough to kill instantly or immobilize the target. A general rule: rock weight should be at least 5 times the animal's body weight.

  • Rabbit (1.5‚Äì2 kg): 8‚Äì15 kg rock (roughly 12‚Äì20 inches across)
  • Raccoon (4‚Äì6 kg): 25‚Äì35 kg rock
  • Beaver (15‚Äì25 kg): 75‚Äì150 kg rock or heavy log
  • Deer (30‚Äì150 kg): 150‚Äì300+ kg rock or balanced log trap

Figure-4 Trigger (Most Versatile)

The figure-4 is the most reliable trigger mechanism. It consists of three wooden pieces lashed or fitted together in a specific configuration.

Construction (Rabbit-Sized Example)

  • Vertical post (A): 25 cm long, 1.5 cm diameter hardwood stick, sharpened at the top
  • Horizontal bar (B): 20 cm long, 1 cm diameter, fitted into a notch carved into the top of post A
  • Bait stick (C): 15 cm long, 0.8 cm diameter, positioned at a 45-degree angle, lashed to bar B with a single figure-4 lashing

Assembly:

  1. Carve a V-notch into the top of post A (about 2 cm deep).
  2. Carve a corresponding notch into one end of bar B to fit snugly into post A's notch.
  3. Carve a shallow notch into the underside of bar B where bait stick C will rest.
  4. Position bait stick C so its free end protrudes slightly from the trigger assembly (where bait is attached).
  5. Lash bait stick C to bar B using a figure-4 lashing with thin cordage, adjusted so slight pressure on the bait stick causes release.

Figure-4 Deadfall Trigger ‚Äì Components & Assembly

How the Figure-4 Works

  1. The rock rests on top of bar B, balanced by the figure-4 assembly.
  2. An animal approaches and nibbles or pulls the bait stick (C).
  3. Pulling the bait stick applies pressure downward on the figure-4 lashing.
  4. The lashing fails (releases), causing bar B to slide out of post A's notch.
  5. The rock falls, crushing the animal beneath.

Paiute Deadfall (Hair Trigger)

A simpler, faster-setting deadfall that relies on a single bait stick balanced under a rock. Extremely sensitive‚Äîthe slightest vibration or bite triggers the fall.

  • Construction: A horizontal stick (bait stick) propped under one edge of the rock with a vertical post supporting the other side. The bait stick is notched so the rock rests in a shallow groove; pulling the bait stick causes the rock to fall.
  • Advantage: Faster to set than figure-4; minimal lashing required.
  • Disadvantage: May be over-sensitive, triggering from wind or small animals rather than your target.

Deadfall Placement & Baiting

Bait Options: Nuts, seeds, dried fruit, carrot/root vegetables, or meat scraps. For carnivores, fresh or aged meat. For herbivores, fruits and nuts attract better than meat.
  • Trail Placement: Set directly on an active game trail; the trigger assembly must be visible and accessible to the target animal.
  • Rock Stability: Ensure the rock is balanced but unstable under the trigger‚Äîif it falls prematurely, adjust the trigger sensitivity by increasing or decreasing bait stick angle.
  • Approach Path: Orient the trap so the animal approaches from a direction that leads it naturally to the bait stick (usually from upwind).

Deadfall Advantages & Disadvantages

Advantages: No metal required; can be built from field materials. Kills quickly. Single impact = immediate death (humane). Scalable.

Disadvantages: Heavy rock must be carefully positioned (injury risk during setup). Trigger sensitivity must be fine-tuned. Rock may miss, crushing only legs (non-lethal injury).

Box & Cage Traps

Box and cage traps capture animals alive, allowing live relocation, assessment, or humane dispatch. These traps are more complex to construct but highly effective and offer flexibility in handling the catch.

Simple Box Trap (Rabbits & Squirrels)

A wooden or woven box with a one-way entrance. The animal enters through a spring-loaded or hinged door but cannot exit.

Construction (Rabbit Box)

  • Box body: 30 cm long √ó 15 cm wide √ó 15 cm tall (hardwood or woven withes)
  • Entrance hole: 8‚Äì10 cm diameter, positioned 5 cm above the bottom to prevent escape
  • One-way gate: Spring-loaded or gravity-hinged flap that allows entry but not exit
  • Bait: Vegetables, clover, or hay placed inside or leading into the entrance

Operation:

  1. Place the box on a trail with the entrance facing the direction of animal approach.
  2. Create a small ramp or trail of bait leading into the entrance.
  3. The animal enters, triggering the gate closure (or gravity naturally closes the flap).
  4. Check daily; dispatch humanely or relocate.

Larger Cage Trap (Raccoon, Opossum)

A rectangular wire or wooden cage with a trigger-activated drop door. Baited with fish, eggs, or meat scraps.

Dimensions: 60 cm long √ó 30 cm wide √ó 30 cm tall (approximately). Two-section trap: one section is the cage; one section is the bait chamber with the trigger door.
  • Door trigger: Animal enters the bait chamber, presses a treadle, and the door drops, trapping it in the cage section.
  • Bait: Fish, eggs, canned cat food, or meat placed in the bait chamber to lure the animal in.
  • Advantage: Can be deployed without killing the animal; relocation possible.
  • Disadvantage: Requires frequent checking to prevent stress or exposure to other predators.

Fish Traps

Basket / Funnel Trap (Woven)

A cone-shaped woven trap with a small opening on the wide end and a narrower opening on the pointed end. Fish enter through the wide opening, navigate the funnel, and become trapped in the narrow chamber.

Fish Basket Trap ‚Äì Cross-Section View

Construction:

  • Frame: Woven willow, ash, or flexible wood withes in a cone shape (40 cm diameter at the wide end, 15 cm at the narrow end, 60 cm long).
  • Entrance funnel: Interior cone leading from the wide opening to the narrow trap chamber; prevents fish from backing out.
  • Bait chamber: A small section at the narrow end holds fish bait (fish guts, bloodmeal, or grain mash).
  • Deployment: Anchor in still pools or slow-moving sections of streams. Fish swim in through the wide opening, follow the funnel, and become trapped.

Weir Trap (Stone / Stick Wall Across Stream)

A permanent or semi-permanent wall built across a stream to funnel fish into a catching area. Ancient method, highly effective in appropriate locations.

  • Construction: Build a stone or log wall across the stream at a narrowing, leaving a small gap. Position a basket trap or net at the gap to catch fish forced through.
  • Advantage: Passive; works with water flow. Scalable to large fish.
  • Disadvantage: Alters water flow; labor-intensive to construct. May be illegal in some jurisdictions in modern times.

Bird Traps

Snare Poles

Thin cordage snares attached to a pole and positioned above branches where birds perch. As the bird lands, its foot passes through the snare loop.

Drop Nets

A net suspended above a bait pile that drops (via trigger or manual pull) when birds land to feed. Effective for ground-feeding species like quail and partridge.

Funnel Traps (Bird-Sized)

Similar to fish funnels but scaled for small birds. Woven cone-shaped trap with entrance openings leading to a dead-end chamber. Baited with seeds or grain.

Bird Trap Placement: Position along known flight paths (between roosting and feeding areas), near water, or in areas of dense cover. Best at dawn and dusk when birds are actively moving.

Leg-Hold & Body-Grip Traps

Steel traps‚Äîmanufactured or field-made‚Äîare highly effective but require care in handling, anchoring, and animal dispatch. Modern leg-hold and body-grip traps are regulated in many jurisdictions; in a survival context, improvised versions can be constructed.

Leg-Hold Traps (Steel Jaws)

A spring-loaded trap with two metal jaws that close around an animal's leg when the pan is stepped on. Holds the animal immobilized but alive.

Trap Setup

  • Bedding: Trap must rest on stable ground or a buried metal pan (prevents animal from pulling the trap upward). Dig a shallow depression, pack firm earth, and position the trap so the pan is level and sensitive.
  • Anchoring: Anchor via stake driven deep (at least 60 cm) or a drag log (10‚Äì30 kg). The anchor must be immovable; test by pulling hard.
  • Pan Tension: Adjust the trigger pan so it releases with minimal pressure (5‚Äì10 lbs for smaller game, 20‚Äì30 lbs for large game). Test by pressing gently; trap should snap shut with light force.
  • Camouflage: Cover the trap with natural material‚Äîdead leaves, soil, grass‚Äîwithout interfering with the pan mechanism.

Body-Grip Traps (Conibear-Style)

A square steel trap with two folding jaws that snap shut in a scissor-like motion. When the animal passes through the opening, pressure on the trigger frame releases the springs, and the jaws close across the animal's body (neck, chest, or spine).

Advantages & Disadvantages

Advantages: Quick kill (jaws close across spine/neck); highly effective; reliable. Animal rarely escapes once caught.

Disadvantages: Difficult to set without injury to trapper (springs are very strong). Requires practice. Heavy spring tension can mutilate pelt.

Humanely Dispatching Trapped Animals

Ethical Responsibility: An animal caught in a leg-hold trap is frightened and potentially injured. Dispatch must be swift and with minimal additional suffering. This is a moral imperative, not a legal nicety (in a post-collapse context, law enforcement is absent, but ethics remain).

Methods:

  • Blow to the head (blunt trauma): Strike the back of the skull with a heavy object (rock, hammer, thick stick). Requires accuracy and force. Effective but distressing if you miss.
  • Spine dislocation (cervical dislocation): For small animals (rabbits, foxes up to 10 kg), hold the animal firmly, place one hand on the back of the head, the other on the lower back, and apply a quick upward and twisting motion. Severs the spinal cord instantly. Requires strength and practice.
  • Exsanguination (bleeding out): Cut the carotid arteries and jugular vein on both sides of the neck. Animal loses consciousness in seconds; death occurs in minutes. Requires sharp knife and steady hand. Preferred method for larger animals (for clean pelts, preferred for bleeding before field dressing).
  • Captive bolt (if available): A mechanical device that fires a bolt into the brain. Humane, but requires a captive bolt gun (rare in survival scenario).
Pelt Preservation: If you intend to preserve the pelt (fur, hide), dispatch via exsanguination (bleeding) is preferable‚Äîminimal trauma to hide, and blood is drained before skinning.

Trap Line Management

A productive trap line requires planning, consistency, and adaptation. A trap line is a circuit of traps checked daily or every other day, covering a defined territory (typically 3‚Äì10 km depending on trap density).

Route Planning

  • Follow Water: Most animals use water sources daily. Streams, ponds, and springs are travel corridors. Plan your route to check sets along water.
  • Elevation Changes: Animal populations shift with season and elevation. In winter, concentrate traps in valleys and dense cover. In warmer months, higher elevations and open areas.
  • Distance & Time: A 5 km circuit with 20‚Äì30 traps should take 45 minutes to 1.5 hours to check, depending on terrain.
  • Safety Margins: Never plan a trap line so remote that checking requires a full-day hike in bad weather. Frostbite or hypothermia are risks if you're stranded.

Daily Checks

In a modern legal context, traps are checked within 24 hours (often daily) to prevent unnecessary suffering and to prevent scavengers from destroying the catch. In a survival context, daily checks prevent meat spoilage and scavenger interference.

Check Timing: Early morning is ideal‚Äîyou avoid diurnal scavengers and can dispatch any catch before it's stressed by heat. In winter, morning checks reduce the risk of overnight predator activity damaging a fresh kill.

Scent Elimination

Animals have keen olfactory senses. Human scent on traps reduces effectiveness.

Pre-Treatment:

  • Boil in Bark Water: Simmer new wire, traps, or metal components in water infused with oak, pine, or alder bark for 2‚Äì4 hours. The tannins neutralize metallic scent and add a natural odor.
  • Bury in Earth: Bury traps and wire in moist earth for 1‚Äì2 weeks. Natural decomposition and soil bacteria reduce scent.
  • Smoke in Fire: Expose traps to smoke from a smoldering fire (pine, cedar, or juniper). Animals associate this scent with natural hazard, not human presence.

During Placement:

  • Wear gloves (hide scent).
  • Avoid touching the snare loop or trigger mechanism with bare hands.
  • Do not urinate or spit near traps.
  • Approach traps from downwind if possible.

Trap Rotation & Preventing Trap-Shyness

Animals learn. If a trap has not produced a catch in 1‚Äì2 weeks, it may have been discovered and avoided by local animals.

  • Rotate trap locations: Move traps every 2‚Äì3 weeks to fresh trails or areas.
  • Refresh the area: If a location has produced well, clear away old debris and rebait to reinvigorate it.
  • Move sets after a catch: An animal caught at a location often triggers other animals to avoid that spot. Move the trap 5‚Äì10 meters away or to a different trail.
  • Seasonal patterns: Shift trap lines with seasonal animal movements (higher elevations in summer, valleys in winter).

Fur & Pelt Processing

A fur-bearing animal is valuable beyond meat‚Äîthe pelt can be tanned into leather, the fur used for warmth or trade. Proper processing preserves the hide and maintains pelt quality.

Skinning Techniques

Case Skinning (Small Mammals)

Used for rabbits, squirrels, marten, fox, coyote. The hide is removed as a tube (case) with fur intact on the outside.

  1. Position: Hang the animal upside down by the hind legs.
  2. Initial cuts: Make a small incision around each hind leg (just above the foot). Cut down the inside of each leg toward the tail.
  3. Peel: Working downward, peel the skin off the hind legs. The skin should separate naturally from muscle with gentle pulling.
  4. Remove tail: Cut the tail bone at the base; the skin covering the tail stays attached to the body skin.
  5. Continue peeling: Work toward the front legs and head, peeling the case downward as if removing a sweater.
  6. Front legs: Bend the front legs backward and cut around each foot; pull the case over the head.
  7. Final step: Careful cuts around the eyes and ears ensure the case is removed intact with all fur.

Open Skinning (Larger Animals)

Used for beaver, deer, larger canines. The hide is opened via a belly incision and removed in large sections.

  1. Belly cut: Make a long incision from the sternum to the tail base (centered on the belly).
  2. Peel sections: Separate hide from the sides, pulling carefully to avoid fur removal or hide damage.
  3. Leg removal: Cut around each leg at the foot; peel the hide away from the limbs.
  4. Head & neck: Careful cuts around the ears, eyes, and muzzle ensure the head hide is removed intact.
  5. Result: An open hide in several large sections.

Fleshing (Removing Meat & Fat)

After removal, the raw hide must be cleaned of meat, fat, and connective tissue. This is done on a fleshing beam (a curved or angled wooden structure).

Fleshing Beam:

  • Construction: A log or wooden beam, slightly curved, positioned at a 45-degree angle. The hide is draped over the beam, fur-side down.
  • Tools: Fleshing knife (a dull-edged, blunt blade‚Äînever sharp, as sharp blades cut the hide). Scrape downward using body weight; do not slash.
  • Process: Work methodically across the entire hide, removing all meat, fat, and tissue until the hide surface is clean and pale.
Save the meat: Fleshing scraps are edible and nutritious. Trim clean meat for cooking; discard purely fatty tissue (though fat can be rendered separately).

Stretching & Drying

After fleshing, the hide must be stretched and dried to prevent shrinkage and promote preservation.

Wire/Wood Stretchers

  • Rabbit case: Insert a wire or wooden spreader inside the case (cross-shaped) to hold the skin taut and flat as it dries.
  • Beaver / larger: Pin the hide (with wooden pegs or leather thongs) to a wooden frame, stretching it evenly in all directions.
  • Deer hide (open): Peg the hide to the ground (or a frame) with the flesh side exposed to air, ensuring even drying without curling.

Drying Conditions

  • Temperature: Cool (below 20¬∞C is ideal). Heat causes hide to become brittle and fur to slip.
  • Ventilation: Good air circulation is essential. Hang in a well-ventilated shed, basement, or outdoor shelter protected from rain and direct sun.
  • Time: Drying takes 2‚Äì4 weeks depending on size and humidity. Hide should be stiff and paper-like when fully dry.
  • Protection: Keep away from insects (especially flies and beetles). Cover with cheesecloth if needed; allow air movement.

Storage & Tanning Referral

Once fully dried, hides can be stored indefinitely in a cool, dry place. For use as leather (softened, wearable material), the hide must be tanned. See leather-tanning.html for detailed tanning processes (vegetable tanning, brain tanning, oil tanning).

Pre-Tanning Storage: Keep dried hides in a rodent-proof container, away from moisture and heat. Dry hides are stable indefinitely; tanning can be done months or years later.

Pest Trapping & Control

Beyond food procurement, trapping addresses pests‚Äîrodents raiding grain stores, moles destroying garden beds, gophers killing crops. Effective pest control combines trapping with exclusion and environmental management.

Rats & Mice

Snap Traps

Spring-loaded traps that snap shut on the animal's back or neck. Simple, reliable, and lethal.

  • Baiting: Peanut butter, dried fruit, nuts. Set the bait on the trigger.
  • Placement: Along walls and near holes. Rats run along edges; place traps perpendicular to the wall.
  • Trigger: Set sensitivity so minimal pressure triggers the trap. Test with a pencil before deploying.

Bucket Traps (Rolling Log)

A large bucket (5-gallon or larger) positioned over water, with a ramp and a rolling rod or log spanning the bucket's opening.

  1. Fill bucket 1/3 with water.
  2. Position a wooden dowel or log across the bucket's rim (balanced, not fixed).
  3. Create a ramp from the surrounding surface to the rod.
  4. Bait the rod with peanut butter or grain.
  5. Rat climbs the ramp, walks onto the rolling rod, loses balance, and falls into the water.
  6. Water drowning is efficient; rats cannot climb out of a slick bucket.

Placement Strategy

  • Position traps along walls, near food storage, and near entry holes.
  • Rats avoid exposed areas; traps must be against walls or in dark corners.
  • Multiple traps in a single area increase catch rate.
  • Check daily and dispose of dead rodents to prevent odor and secondary scavenger issues.

Moles & Gophers

Tunnel Traps (Scissor-Jaw)

Traps designed to fit inside burrows. When the animal passes through, the trap snaps shut.

  • Identification: Mole tunnels are shallow, visible ridges; gopher holes are deeper and less visible.
  • Placement: Set trap directly in the tunnel, bracing it firmly so it does not shift.
  • Bait: Not necessary; the animal is caught as it travels through.

Flooding

Introducing water into tunnels forces animals to surface, where they can be dispatched or relocated.

Stored Grain Protection

Prevent rodent infestation of grain stores by combining physical barriers with natural repellents.

Physical Barriers:

  • Sealed containers: Store grain in metal or heavy plastic bins with tight-fitting lids. Rodents cannot chew through metal; plastic must be 3 mm or thicker.
  • Elevated storage: Keep bins off the ground on wooden pallets or shelves (minimum 30 cm height) to prevent burrowing access.
  • Traps around bins: Maintain a perimeter of snap traps or bucket traps around the storage area.

Natural Repellents:

  • Diatomaceous earth (food-grade): Dust around and within grain containers. Diatoms are sharp-edged microscopic shells that damage insects' and small mammals' exoskeletons. Non-toxic to humans; effective against rodents and grain pests.
  • Bay leaves: Scatter throughout grain stores. Rodents dislike the smell and taste. Replace every 2‚Äì3 months.
  • Essential oils: Peppermint, cedar, and clove oils on cotton balls placed in and around grain stores. Repel rodents via smell.

Garden Pest Fencing & Exclusion

  • Rabbits: Hardware cloth fence, minimum 60 cm height, 15 cm buried underground to prevent burrowing. Gates must be secure; rabbits will exploit gaps.
  • Deer: Fencing minimum 2 meters high or multiple overlapping fences (deer jump, but rarely both). Electric fencing is effective if power is available.
  • Gophers/Moles: Raised garden beds with hardware cloth floor (minimum #1/4 inch mesh) prevents tunneling access.
  • Insects: Row covers (lightweight fabric) over plants; secure edges to prevent entry. Remove once plants flower (if pollinators are needed).

Fishing Traps & Passive Fish Procurement

Like land traps, fishing methods can be passive‚Äîlines and nets that work while you attend other tasks. Trotlines, limb lines, and jug fishing are ancient techniques requiring no electricity or active skill once deployed.

Trotlines (Main Line with Baited Hooks)

A long mainline anchored at both ends, with multiple shorter lines (dropper lines) and baited hooks attached at intervals along its length.

Construction:

  • Mainline: Strong cordage (3‚Äì5 mm diameter) or light rope, 20‚Äì50 meters long depending on water width and desired fishing density.
  • Dropper lines: Shorter lines (20‚Äì40 cm) tied at 1‚Äì2 meter intervals along the mainline. Tie using a clove hitch or surgeon's knot.
  • Hooks: Size #4 to #8 (depending on fish species). Tie using a clinch knot or improved clinch knot.
  • Anchors: Heavy rocks, stakes, or weighted bags at both ends. Ensure the mainline is taut but allows for water current movement.

Baiting & Checking:

  • Bait: Small fish, worms, crayfish, insects, or bloody meat. Change bait every 12‚Äì24 hours to maintain effectiveness.
  • Checking: Check every 12‚Äì24 hours. Caught fish deteriorate quickly; daily checks minimize spoilage.
  • Maintenance: Remove caught fish, rebait hooks, and inspect the line for damage or tangling.

Limb Lines (Single Hooks on Overhanging Branches)

Individual baited hooks suspended from overhanging branches above deep water holes.

Setup:

  1. Tie a length of cordage (30‚Äì50 cm) to a branch or exposed root hanging over water.
  2. Tie a hook to the free end of the cordage.
  3. Bait the hook with small fish, worms, or meat.
  4. The hook hangs just above (or touching) the water surface.

Advantages:

  • Minimal gear; requires only cordage and hooks.
  • Multiple limb lines can be set along a stream quickly.
  • Catches fish as they surface to feed on floating insects or bait.

Jug Fishing (Floating Bait Lines)

Baited hooks attached to floating jugs. The jugs drift or are anchored in a fishing area; caught fish pull the jug, making the catch visible.

Construction:

  • Float: Empty plastic jug, sealed beer bottle, or wooden float (carved from cork or light wood).
  • Anchor line: Heavy cordage from jug to bottom (anchoring the jug in place) or allowing drift.
  • Dropper lines: Shorter lines tied to the jug's side, each with a baited hook.
  • Spacing: Hooks spaced 30‚Äì60 cm apart to avoid tangling.

Advantages & Challenges:

  • Advantage: Works in current; jug movement attracts fish via vibrations. Jug is visible from distance, making catch obvious.
  • Challenge: Drift jugs must be tracked; risk of loss in strong currents or wind. Anchored jugs require suitable depth and bottom.

Net Construction (Gill Nets)

A gill net is a mesh wall suspended in water. Fish swim into the net and become entangled by their gills, unable to back out.

Homemade Gill Net (Hand-Knotted):

  • Materials: Strong, thin cordage (waxed linen, nylon, or plant-based twine). Minimum 20 meters for a useful net.
  • Frame: Anchor line (top and bottom) and side lines, creating a rectangular frame. Mesh is knotted within the frame.
  • Knot method: Use sheet bend or surgeon's knot to create uniform mesh. Spacing determines mesh size.
  • Time: A 10√ó3 meter net by hand takes 20‚Äì40 hours of knotting. Pre-made nets are preferable if available.

Deployment & Checking:

  • Set net perpendicular to water flow, anchored at top and bottom.
  • Check every 6‚Äì12 hours. Fish in the net deteriorate quickly; long intervals result in spoilage and lost meat.
  • Carefully extract fish by backing them out of the mesh (pushing tail-first through the opening, following the direction they entered).
Gill Net Legality: In many modern jurisdictions, gill nets are illegal or heavily restricted (overfishing threat). In a survival context, they are invaluable; in a legal framework, understand the restrictions.

Fish Trap Combinations

Multiple passive methods can work together. A trotline set in the same area as basket traps and limb lines creates redundancy: different traps catch different species and sizes, maximizing yield from a single fishing area.
