Orthodontic Appliance Types

Fixed Appliances (Braces)

Brackets (small squares bonded to tooth) connected by wire (archwire). Wire is bent to correct tooth position; tension guides teeth into alignment. Bands (rings cemented to molars) provide additional anchorage. Components: brackets, archwires (various materials/sizes), ligatures (ties holding wire to bracket), bands, elastics/rubbers. In survival: if patient has braces, manage complications; removal requires professional care.

Removable Appliances

Dentures, retainers, aligners. Can be removed by patient. Advantage in survival: patient can remove for cleaning, eating, emergency situations. Disadvantage: effectiveness depends on compliance (wearing as prescribed).

Appliance Failure Points

Brackets: adhesive failure (bond breaks), bracket crack/breakage. Wires: breakage, kinking, unbending. Bands: cement failure, band slippage. Elastics: degradation, loss. Survival scenario: minimize damage, improvise temporary solutions, accept some discomfort for time period until professional care available.

Wire Management

Wire Breakage

Signs: visible wire break (usually midway along wire), increased discomfort, loss of tooth control. Causes: trauma, material fatigue, corrosion. Management: remove wire segment (if possible without damaging tooth/bracket). Improvise temporary replacement: flexible wire (guitar string, fishing line, thin metal wire if available) threaded through brackets to maintain some position control. Apply topical anesthetic if sharp wire causing irritation.

Protruding Wire

Sharp wire end extends beyond bracket, irritates lip/cheek. Management: 1) Identify protrusion point (ask patient, visually confirm). 2) Protective covering: apply wax (if available) or soft material to cover sharp end. 3) Bend back: if safe, gently bend wire back with pliers (if tool available) to eliminate protrusion. 4) Accept temporary irritation if bending impossible; topical treatments (anesthetic, lip balm) provide comfort.

Loose/Unbent Wire

Wire slips out of brackets, loses tension (no longer applies force). Management: rethread wire through brackets if possible, reactivate (rebend) to position. If cannot accomplish: maintain with temporary wire to prevent complete loss of position control. Remove wire entirely if completely non-functional (patient may tolerate better than sharp irritation).

Bracket and Band Repair

Bonded Bracket Failure

Bracket separates from tooth surface; adhesive fails. Signs: bracket loose but still attached by wire, bracket visible space from tooth, bracket pain when chewing. Causes: inadequate initial bonding, high shear forces, decay under bracket. Management: 1) Do NOT force removal (damages tooth). 2) Stabilize with temporary wire if mobility high. 3) If causing pain: consider extraction of tooth (if acceptable in survival), or accept temporary discomfort. 4) Seek professional re-bonding when possible.

Bracket Breakage

Bracket physically breaks (crack, missing piece, slot damaged). Signs: bracket damage visible, wire won't stay secure, sharp fragment if broken piece present. Management: remove sharp debris (if loose), stabilize remaining bracket with wire, wrap tooth/bracket area if sharp edges cutting mouth tissue. May need extraction if bracket severely damaged and patient in pain.

Band Looseness

Molar band (cemented ring) loosens; cement fails. Signs: band mobility, sometimes food trapping underneath, possible sensitivity. Management: avoid sticky foods, use floss carefully to avoid dislodging further. If band completely loose: remove and rinse/dry tooth (prevents decay under band). If band stays in place: apply temporary cement (zinc oxide eugenol) to reseat if bond loose. Professional re-cementation needed long-term.

Pain Management

Pressure Soreness (Normal with Adjustment)

After initial placement or wire tightening: teeth ache, tender to chewing. Usually resolves in 3-5 days. Management: NSAIDs (ibuprofen 400-600mg every 4-6 hours), soft foods, warm salt water rinses, topical anesthetic rinses if available. Wax over brackets if irritating soft tissue.

Ulceration from Irritation

Sharp wire/bracket rubs creating open sore on lip/cheek/tongue. Management: 1) Identify sharp component (bend back or cover). 2) Topical treatment: anesthetic ointment (benzocaine), antimicrobial if available (chlorhexidine rinse), honey (natural antibacterial). 3) Protective covering: beeswax, soft wax, or improvised soft material over sharp edge. 4) Avoid irritating foods/beverages. 5) Warm salt water rinses (1/2 teaspoon salt per 8 oz water) promote healing.

Severe Pain

Possible causes: bracket/wire pressing on exposed dentin, abscess (dental infection), root exposure. Management: NSAIDs, topical anesthetic, assessment for infection (check for abscess signs: swelling, fever, increased pain). If infection suspected: antibiotics, possible extraction. If exposed dentin: apply desensitizing paste (if available), avoid acidic foods.

Emergency Procedures

Bracket Removal

If bracket causing severe pain/damage and cannot be managed conservatively: removal necessary. Method: 1) Gentle pressure with instrument to dislodge adhesive (do NOT pry aggressively—risks enamel fracture). 2) Once separated: scrape off remaining adhesive with plastic instrument. 3) Polish enamel to smooth. 4) Risk: residual adhesive roughness (may need professional polish later), but preserves tooth integrity for future re-bonding.

Wire Removal Entirely

If wire cannot be managed: remove completely. Method: 1) Cut wire segments between brackets (use wire cutter if available, or file through). 2) Remove segments carefully. 3) Clean remaining adhesive/wire from brackets. 4) Accept loss of orthodontic correction temporarily; will need professional replacement post-survival.

Extraction if Necessary

If tooth with bracket/appliance causing intolerable pain, mobility excessive, or infection present: extraction may be only option. Post-extraction: appliance on surrounding teeth will be affected; redistribute force/remove if necessary. Accept that orthodontic treatment disrupted; focus on infection/pain control.

Oral Hygiene with Appliances

Cleaning Around Brackets

Braces trap food/plaque. Inadequate cleaning → rapid decay. Technique: 1) Brush: use soft brush, angle 45° toward gum, small circular motions around bracket. 2) Floss: specialized floss threader or waxed floss passed between teeth/bracket. 3) Water irrigator (if available) flushes debris. 4) In survival: maintain best possible cleaning (brush after meals, improvise floss from cloth if needed).

Plaque Accumulation Risk

Orthodontic patients high-risk for cavities if hygiene poor. In survival with limited toothpaste: use whatever available (sea salt paste, baking soda paste, improvise). Rinse frequently with salt water. If plaque accumulation heavy: accept increased cavity risk; prevention focus where possible.

Periodontal Complications

Gum disease (gingivitis, periodontitis) more common with braces (mechanical irritation, difficult cleaning). Signs: red/swollen gums, bleeding with brushing, recession. Management: improve hygiene, anti-inflammatory rinses (salt water), floss around braces. If periodontal infection develops: treat with antibiotics, possible appliance removal if infection severe.

Common Complications

Allergic Reactions

Nickel (in some wires/brackets) causes allergic reactions in sensitive individuals. Symptoms: red rash in/around mouth, local swelling, itching. Management: remove/replace offending appliance component if possible (use stainless steel alternative if available), antihistamines (diphenhydramine 25-50mg), topical corticosteroid cream if available. If severe reaction: remove appliance temporarily.

Appliance Damage from Trauma

Sports injury, fall, blow to mouth: brackets/wire may break, teeth displace. Management: assess for tooth fracture/displacement (see first aid), stabilize appliance if functional, replace broken components if possible. Accept temporary derangement; focus on tooth/oral tissue integrity.

Decay Under Appliance

Brackets/bands harbor bacteria; poor cleaning → cavities form. Signs: white spots (demineralization) around brackets, visible cavity, pain. Management: improved hygiene going forward, topical fluoride rinse if available, accept cavity and address once appliance removed. Prevention: maintain meticulous cleaning during orthodontia.

Critical Orthodontic Safety

  • Sharp wire = laceration risk. Never leave protruding sharp wire untreated; cover or bend back immediately.
  • Swallowed bracket/wire = choking/GI injury risk. Ensure small components secured; if swallowed, monitor for symptoms (abdominal pain, difficulty swallowing); seek professional evaluation if symptoms develop.
  • Infection around appliance = rapid spread risk. Treat infections aggressively with antibiotics; swelling with fever indicates systemic infection risk.
  • Pressure necrosis from tight appliance = possible enamel/root damage. Adjust tension if patient reports escalating pain; do not continue increasing force blindly.

Common Orthodontic Care Errors

  • Aggressive bracket removal: Damages enamel. Use gentle pressure, patience, avoid prying.
  • Wire tension over-tightening: Excessive force causes pain, root resorption, necrosis. Adjust cautiously; patient discomfort guides limits.
  • Ignoring infection signs: Swelling, fever, pain = infection. Treat with antibiotics, assess for abscess drainage.
  • Poor hygiene acceptance: Assume brushing/flossing will be done; it often isn't. Educate, monitor, provide tools if possible.
