Snare Designs & Setup

Snares capture game by strangulation or entanglement. Effective snares require proper placement on animal trails and careful trigger design to avoid premature release.

Basic Wire Snare

Most reliable design for small game (rabbits, squirrels) and medium game (foxes, coyotes).

Construction

  1. Obtain flexible wire or heavy-gauge wire from fence, springs, or other sources
  2. Fashion a loop approximately 6 inches in diameter for small game, 8-12 inches for larger game
  3. Create loop using eye splice knot (thread wire back through itself)
  4. Attach standing snare wire (main support) using fixed loop or by threading through anchor point
  5. Adjust loop size to slightly larger than animal's head to ensure easy entry but secure capture
  6. Stiffen loop with wood spreaders if necessary to maintain circular shape

Placement

  • Position snare on active animal trails where tracks are clear and frequent
  • Set snare at height appropriate for species: 4-6 inches for rabbits, 8-10 inches for foxes
  • Position just behind bend in trail where animals naturally brush vegetation
  • Secure support wire to immovable object (tree, rock) at least 3-4 feet high
  • Create channel funnel with sticks to guide animals directly through snare
  • Ensure snare moves freely—any resistance indicates improper setup

Deadfall Snare Hybrid

Combines snare trigger with deadfall weight for more effective large game capture.

  • Use heavy log or stone as deadfall (50+ pounds for large game)
  • Trigger snare releases deadfall when animal enters
  • Requires precise trigger mechanism to function—practice with mock animals
  • Position directly on trails to maximize contact probability

Test snare triggers before setting—they should release immediately on contact. Sticky snares miss animals that could otherwise be captured.

Deadfall Traps

Heavy weights crush or pin game animals. Effective for medium to large game (deer, boar, large birds). Requires significant preparation but highly effective once set.

Figure 4 Deadfall

Classic trigger design using three wooden pieces arranged to hold heavy log.

Construction

  1. Prepare three wooden pieces: main vertical support (6-8 inches long, 1-2 inches diameter), horizontal bait stick (3-4 inches), and diagonal trigger stick (8-10 inches)
  2. Carve flat surfaces on each piece for binding points
  3. Lash vertical support to horizontal bait stick with cordage, creating 90-degree angle
  4. Position diagonal trigger stick supporting the whole structure with single endpoint resting on bait stick
  5. Arrange structure so removal of bait stick causes immediate collapse
  6. Select heavy log or stone weighing 50-200 pounds depending on target game
  7. Position weight to crush animal—height should be 2-3 feet above ground for medium game

Baiting

  • Use fresh meat, organ tissue, or strong-smelling bait
  • Place bait where animal must disturb trigger mechanism to access it
  • Small game: dried meat, nuts, seeds
  • Large game: organ tissue (liver, heart), carrion
  • Position bait to maximize trigger contact probability

Stone Deadfall

Uses stacked stones or flat rock instead of log—effective in rocky terrain where heavy logs unavailable.

  • Stack stones 2-3 feet high using support stick method
  • Ensure stability—unstable stack may collapse accidentally
  • Arrange stones to crush target zone (head and neck for largest kill probability)
  • Test stability before leaving trap unattended

Deadfalls are indiscriminate—cover or mark to prevent accidentally triggering on non-target animals or humans.

Pit Traps & Concealment

Excavated pits capture or impale falling animals. Effective for large game but requires significant labor and careful construction.

Basic Pit Trap

Simple excavated hole with concealed top and sharpened stakes at bottom.

Construction

  1. Select location on active game trail in soft ground
  2. Excavate pit 4-6 feet deep, 3-4 feet wide—dimensions vary by target species
  3. Sharpen wooden stakes (3-4 inches diameter) to point using knife or axe
  4. Install stakes at pit bottom with points upward, lashing together for stability
  5. Create camouflage covering using branches, leaves, and grass
  6. Use light wood frame covered with branches and vegetation to create realistic appearance
  7. Ensure covering supports animal weight briefly before breaking (requires testing)

Pit Maintenance

  • Check pits daily for captured animals or accidental collapse
  • Refurbish coverings if damage detected or after rain
  • Relocate inactive pits as animal movement patterns change seasonally
  • Fill and mark inactive pits to prevent injury to humans or unintended animals

Funnel Traps

Use natural or constructed barriers to channel animals into confined kill zone.

  • Build fence or barrier guiding animals along narrow corridor
  • Position snares, deadfalls, or pit traps at corridor endpoint
  • Most effective when aligned with existing travel corridors
  • Requires less immediate labor than individual pit traps

Pit traps are dangerous—clearly mark all pits to prevent humans falling. Maintain careful records of pit locations.

Animal Tracking Basics

Reading tracks, scat, and sign allows hunters to locate game, assess health and size, and predict movement patterns.

Sign Interpretation

  • Fresh tracks: Clear edges, displaced soil still settling, predator scent trails present
  • Aged tracks: Blurred edges, debris settled, edges hardened
  • Scat (droppings): Reveals diet, digestive health, and approximate time since passage
  • Rubs & marks: Antler rubs indicate dominant male activity (autumn/rut season)
  • Browsing damage: Stripped bark, broken branches indicate recent feeding
  • Bedding areas: Flattened grass, worn spots where animals rest regularly

Tracking Technique

  1. Begin tracking immediately after observing fresh sign
  2. Follow trail slowly, reading terrain variations and track age
  3. Assess animal's mental state: alert movement creates irregular trail, calm movement shows regular pattern
  4. Note directional changes indicating awareness of danger
  5. Evaluate feeding stops suggesting animal's distraction opportunities
  6. Predict destination based on terrain analysis and behavior pattern

Track size increases during wet soil conditions—actual animal may be smaller than track suggests. Fresh muddy tracks show clean edges; aged tracks show degradation and soil settlement.

Hunting Methods

Various techniques allow hunters to approach, sight, and take game animals. Success requires patience, skill development, and understanding of animal behavior.

Stalking

Approaching animals by following their trail or movement pattern.

  • Move slowly and quietly, placing feet carefully to avoid sudden noises
  • Position self downwind so animal cannot scent you
  • Stay below skyline to avoid silhouetting yourself
  • Use terrain features for cover—ridges, trees, rocks
  • Pause frequently to scan for animals and listen for sounds
  • Stop if animal becomes alert—remain motionless until it relaxes or moves away
  • Approach from sides or behind—animals have limited rear vision

Ambush/Waiting

Position at likely feeding, watering, or shelter locations and wait for animals to approach.

  • Identify high-traffic areas using track and scat analysis
  • Select positions offering clear view and good cover
  • Avoid positioning directly upwind from expected approach
  • Minimize movement and noise—maintain stillness for extended periods
  • Best during dawn and dusk when most animals are active
  • Position yourself on trails with natural funnels restricting animal movement

Driving

Multiple hunters work together to move game toward waiting hunters or into ambush positions.

  • Requires coordination among team members
  • Some hunters make noise to flush animals forward
  • Hunters positioned ahead wait silently for fleeing animals
  • Effective in dense brush or forest
  • Dangerous—requires clear communication to prevent shooting towards other hunters

Calling

Using animal vocalizations or distress sounds to attract game.

  • Mimic prey animal distress sounds (wounded rabbit calls work well)
  • Use predator calls during predator hunting season (coyote howls)
  • Mimic mating calls during rut season (deer rutting calls)
  • Position downwind of calling location
  • Stop calling periodically to listen for responses
  • Have weapon ready—animals may approach quickly once called

Extreme noise discipline required during hunting—silent stalking demands that all communication be nonverbal. Even whispered words may spook game.

Field Dressing

Removing internal organs immediately after kill preserves meat quality and prevents spoilage from bacteria and body heat.

Preparation

  • Ensure animal is completely deceased before beginning field dressing
  • Position carcass with hind legs downhill if possible to aid drainage
  • Establish clean workspace—use clean knife and hands
  • Wash hands and knife in clean water if available
  • Gather containers for organ tissue (liver, heart may be eaten)

Step-by-Step Process

  1. Position carcass: Lay animal on back with legs spread, expose belly area
  2. Initial incision: Starting below ribs, make incision from breastbone to rear vent, cutting only through skin and muscle (avoid piercing organs)
  3. Expose cavity: Carefully enlarge opening to see internal organs
  4. Remove trachea/esophagus: Cut windpipe and food pipe, let organs fall forward into pelvic cavity
  5. Remove intestines: Carefully cut and pull intestines from body cavity, exposing liver and other organs
  6. Remove liver: Inspect liver for spots (indicates disease)—if healthy, retain for eating
  7. Remove heart/lungs: Cut connecting blood vessels and membranes, remove chest organs
  8. Remove digestive tract: Cut at anus and carefully remove all intestinal tissue
  9. Final cleaning: Wipe interior cavity with clean cloth or grass
  10. Cool carcass: Prop open to allow air circulation and cooling

Post-Dressing Care

  • Protect carcass from insects using cloth covering (allows air circulation)
  • Keep meat as cool as possible—shade is essential in warm weather
  • Do not wash interior cavity in warm weather—moisture promotes bacterial growth
  • Transport to butchering location as quickly as possible
  • Field dress large game within 30 minutes of kill to prevent spoilage

Liver examination reveals animal health—spotty liver indicates disease, liver should be smooth and dark burgundy colored.

Butchering & Meat Processing

Breaking down carcass into usable cuts requires anatomical knowledge and sharp tools. Proper technique maximizes meat yield and quality.

Tools Required

  • Large butcher knife (8-10 inches, sharp blade)
  • Small boning knife (3-4 inches) for detail work
  • Meat saw (for bone cutting) or hand saw
  • Cutting surface (clean board, stretched hide)
  • Containers for different cuts and organ meats
  • Salt and spices for immediate preservation if needed

Basic Butchering Steps

  1. Remove skin/hide: Cut along belly and legs, carefully peel skin away from carcass (save for hide processing)
  2. Remove limbs: Cut legs at joint where they attach to body, work blade to separate cleanly
  3. Separate meat from bone: Follow natural seams between muscle groups
  4. Trim fat: Remove fat layer as desired (trim excessive fat to prevent rancidity)
  5. Cut into portions: Steaks from hindquarters (perpendicular to bone), roasts from shoulder and neck
  6. Debone for storage: Remove meat from bones for easier storage and reduced space requirements
  7. Save organ tissue: Heart, liver, kidneys (if healthy) for immediate consumption or preservation

Cut Descriptions

  • Steaks: 1-2 inch thick cuts perpendicular to bone, suitable for grilling or pan frying
  • Roasts: 3-5 pound sections suitable for slow cooking, stewing, or braising
  • Ground meat: Trim pieces run through grinder or pounded/chopped finely for sausages, patties, or stews
  • Organ meats: Liver (most nutritious), heart (tough but flavorful), kidneys (strong flavor), tongue (tender if properly prepared)
  • Stock bones: Save marrow bones for boiling into nutrient-rich stock

Storage Immediately After Butchering

  • Salt heavily or smoke immediately to prevent spoilage
  • Hang in cool location (50-60°F) with good air circulation for short-term aging
  • Process into preserved form (drying, smoking, salting) within 24-48 hours if refrigeration unavailable
  • Keep raw meat separate from other foods to prevent cross-contamination

Ensure sharp tools—dull knives slip and cause injuries. Extreme care required when handling sharp instruments during extended butchering sessions.

Hide & Pelt Preparation

Treating hides properly produces usable leather and fur for clothing, containers, and trade. Improper handling results in hair loss and hide deterioration.

Fresh Hide Handling

  • Remove hide immediately after killing, while body heat aids separation
  • Begin skinning at lower legs and work toward head
  • Minimize hair loss by careful peeling rather than cutting (except where attachment is strong)
  • Avoid contaminating flesh side with hair
  • Keep hide cool and away from direct sunlight

Hide Fleshing

Removing muscle and fat tissue from inner hide surface.

  1. Stretch hide on frame or smooth surface with flesh side up
  2. Use dull tool (bone scraper, smooth rock) to remove tissue without puncturing hide
  3. Work from center outward in consistent strokes
  4. Remove all muscle, fat, and tissue to clean white surface
  5. Rinse thoroughly in cold water
  6. Stretch again to ensure even thickness

Hide Tanning

Traditional methods use brain, plant materials, or salt to preserve hides.

Brain Tanning (Most Accessible)

  1. Soak hide in cold water for 24 hours
  2. Wring out excess water
  3. Prepare brain solution: crush animal brains in water (ratio 1:1 brain to water)
  4. Work brain solution into hide thoroughly by hand
  5. Let sit for 12-24 hours
  6. Wring and smoke hide over cool fire (100-120°F) for 4-8 hours
  7. Repeat soaking, brain treatment, and smoking for even penetration
  8. Stretch hide frequently during process to maintain suppleness

Salt Tanning (Drying)

  • Salt hide heavily on flesh side immediately after fleshing
  • Roll flesh-side in and store in cool, dry location
  • After 1-2 weeks, shake off salt and dry completely
  • Produces durable if somewhat stiff leather
  • Resoak and work to soften if needed

Fur/Hair-On Pelts

  • Follow same fleshing procedure for hair-on pelts
  • Use salt tanning to preserve fur without complex processing
  • Do not soak excessively—moisture causes hair slippage
  • Dry fur-on pelts completely before storage
  • Store in cool, dry location away from insects and rodents

Brain tanning produces softest, most workable leather. Multiple tanning cycles improve suppleness—well-worked hide becomes increasingly soft with use.

Common Hunting & Trapping Mistakes

  • Poor trap placement: Traps on wrong trails or at wrong height catch nothing—scout thoroughly before setting
  • Inadequate deadfall weight: Undersized deadfall may kill only small game intended for larger species
  • Contaminated bait: Human scent on bait repels cautious animals—handle minimally with clean hands
  • Inadequate concealment: Visible hunters spook animals before approaching—blend with environment completely
  • Hunting into wind: Animals detect scent from upwind hunters—always position downwind
  • Excessive movement: Any movement spooks alert animals—remain perfectly still for extended periods
  • Neglected carcass care: Unclean kill or delayed field dressing spoils meat—work quickly and carefully
  • Poor butchering technique: Careless cutting wastes meat and contaminates with bone fragments—learn proper technique first
  • Inadequate hide protection: Exposed hides deteriorate rapidly—protect immediately and process within days

Never assume trap is safe—always approach from behind and probe first with stick. Escaped animals may be aggressive. Use extreme caution with large game in traps.

Safety Precautions

  • Firearm safety: Always point weapon in safe direction, keep finger off trigger until ready to shoot, never climb with loaded firearm
  • Animal danger: Wounded animals are extremely dangerous—approach from behind, never from front
  • Trap safety: Mark all trap locations clearly, check traps regularly, cover pit traps to prevent accidental falls
  • Hunting partners: Never shoot in direction of other humans—verify target and surroundings completely
  • Knife safety: Maintain sharp tools only—dull knives slip and cause injury; always cut away from body
  • Contamination: Wash hands after handling raw meat, keep separate tools for different foods
  • Spoiled meat: Discard meat with foul smell, discoloration, or slimy texture—food poisoning risk is severe
  • Overexertion: Hunting and butchering are physically demanding—stay hydrated and take breaks
