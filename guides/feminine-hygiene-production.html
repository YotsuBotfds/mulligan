1. Overview: Critical Health Need Post-Collapse

Feminine hygiene products are essential medical supplies that communities depend on for health, dignity, and productivity. In collapse scenarios, commercial menstrual products become unavailable, making the ability to manufacture alternatives a vital community skill.

Approximately 26% of the population menstruates, and without proper supplies, health consequences range from embarrassment and social isolation to severe infections and anemia. This guide covers manufacturing sustainable, effective alternatives using available materials.

A single person may require 50-200 menstrual products per year depending on cycle length and flow. Community manufacturing can produce this economically using basic materials.

Why This Matters

  • Menstrual insecurity reduces school attendance and work productivity by 10-25%
  • Untreated infections from inadequate hygiene threaten maternal health and fertility
  • Psychological impact of period poverty extends beyond physical health
  • Reusable products reduce waste and material dependency over time
  • Manufacturing creates local economic opportunity and skill transfer

Product Types Covered

  • Reusable cloth pads: Most practical, durable, and economical option
  • Menstrual cups: For people with moderate-heavy flow and comfort with insertion
  • Menstrual discs: Alternative to cups; similar function, different shape
  • Emergency alternatives: When primary products unavailable
Historical context: Before 1920s, menstruating people used cloth rags, often called "monthly cloths," washed and reused. These worked effectively for centuries and remain practical today.

Prerequisites

  • Basic sewing skills (straight seams, simple patterns)
  • Access to natural fibers (cotton, bamboo, wool) or textile scraps
  • Sewing equipment (needle, thread, scissors)
  • Materials for closures (snaps, buttons, elastic)
  • Understanding of menstrual health and hygiene
  • For cups/discs: latex or silicone, molding equipment, sterilization capability

2. Reusable Cloth Pad Construction

Cloth pads are the most accessible and sustainable menstrual product, consisting of multiple layers providing absorption, protection, and comfort. A single pad can be used 2-3 years with proper care.

Construction Steps: Standard Regular Pad

Cut pattern pieces: body (9" √ó 2.5"), wings (1.5" √ó 1.5" each), accounting for seam allowances (1/2" recommended).
Prepare all fabric pieces: soften new fabrics by washing if possible; pre-shrink to prevent later warping.
Layer absorbent materials: stack 2-4 layers of cotton flannel, bamboo fleece, or cotton batting; pin together.
Create moisture barrier: if available, add thin waterproof layer (oilcloth, thin plastic, or 100% cotton outer shell acts as minor barrier).
Assemble: place absorbent stack between soft top fabric and waterproof back layer; pin all edges.
Attach wings: position each wing on sides at pad center; insert between top and back layers before sewing.
Sew edges: use strong straight stitch along all perimeter edges; reinforce corner areas with diagonal stitching.
Install closures: attach snaps or buttons to wing ends; ensure spacing matches typical underwear. Test closure snap/button.
Finish edges: trim loose threads; seal any fraying with careful hand-stitching or fray-check if available.
Final check: verify absorbent layers stay intact during use; test moisture wicking and barrier function.
For first-time pad makers, construct a test pad using scrap fabric. Test for absorbency, comfort fit, and leak potential before making final versions with good materials.

  • Cost: Nearly free (repurposed materials)

4. Sewing Techniques & Pattern Construction

Precision in pattern construction and sewing ensures reliable product performance and comfortable fit.

Creating Your Pattern

Draft a paper template for repeatable accuracy:

Draw main pad body on paper: 9" length √ó 2.5" width for regular pad. Use ruler for straight edges.
Round the corners slightly (1/4" radius) to prevent sharp points and improve comfort.
Mark center line along length; mark wing positions (1" from ends on each side).
Add seam allowance lines (1/2" around all edges) or mark on fabric directly.
Test pattern on scrap fabric first; verify all measurements before cutting good materials.
Save pattern for future use; trace onto sturdy paper or cardboard for durability.

Essential Sewing Techniques

Straight Stitch (Primary seam)

  • Purpose: Connect layers and create strong seams
  • Stitch length: 2-3mm (small stitches = stronger seams)
  • Tension: Balanced; thread should be invisible inside seam
  • Position: 1/2" from fabric edge for seam allowance
  • Reinforcement: Backstitch at start/end 2-3 times to lock threads

Box Stitch (Reinforcement at corners)

  • Purpose: Prevent seam failure at stress points
  • Method: After straight stitching, stitch a small box (1/2" √ó 1/2") at each corner, then stitch an X diagonally
  • Location: At all four corners of main pad body
  • Benefit: Distributes stress, significantly increases pad longevity

Zig-Zag Stitch (Finish edges, prevent fraying)

  • Purpose: Seal cut edges, prevent unraveling
  • Stitch width: 3-4mm; length 2-3mm
  • Application: Optional but recommended for durability
  • Note: Hand-sewing alternative is careful whip-stitching of folded edges

Sewing Process: Step-by-Step

Prepare workspace: organize all fabric pieces, thread colors (match top or blend), needles, scissors.
Pin layers: assemble all layers (top, absorbent, barrier); pin every 2-3" around perimeter.
Mark seam line: use chalk or pencil to draw 1/2" from edge; provides guide for straight stitching.
Sew perimeter: start at one corner; backstitch 2-3 times; straight stitch entire perimeter along marked line.
Reinforce corners: box stitch at each corner; add diagonal X for maximum strength.
Finish edges: trim loose threads; apply zig-zag stitch if available or hand-finish with careful stitching.
Press seams: if iron available, press flat to set stitches and improve appearance.
For hand-sewing without machine: use small running stitch (not larger than 1/8") for strength. Take multiple passes over stress areas for durability similar to machine sewing.

Snap Installation (Sew-Through Method)

Determine closure position: wings should snap at approximately center-front of underwear for secure hold. Test positioning on actual underwear.
Mark snap locations on wings: typically 2-3 snaps per wing set (one on each wing tip, possibly one center).
Reinforce snap points: add small scrap of sturdy fabric behind snap point area (sew square piece 1.5" √ó 1.5" on wing underside) to prevent tearing.
Install snaps: follow snap manufacturer instructions for thread holes. Sew securely with multiple stitches around each hole.
Test fit: attach to underwear; verify closure is snug and secure; adjust if necessary.

Button Closures (Alternative)

Traditional method; requires more space and dexterity to use but available historically:

  • Advantages: Simple to repair or replace; visible closure allows easy checking
  • Disadvantages: More time to fasten/unfasten; buttons may come loose with repeated washing
  • Button type: 4-hole or shank buttons; sew firmly with doubled thread
  • Button spacing: Center one button on wing tip; optional additional button on wing edge
Snap closures are historically accurate (early 1900s onward) and extremely durable. Test snaps can be sewn on repeatedly without damage; pads often outlive snaps and can be re-snapped.

6. Washing & Sanitization Protocols

Proper washing extends pad life (5-10 years) while maintaining hygiene. Bacterial growth in menstrual products can cause infections if not managed correctly.

Pre-Washing Management

Fresh Pads (During Use)

  • Rinse under cold running water immediately after removal to prevent blood setting
  • Soak overnight in cool water with 1-2 tablespoons salt (antimicrobial) or white vinegar (1:10 ratio)
  • Never use hot water initially; heat sets blood proteins into fabric
  • Squeeze gently; don't twist or wring

Storage Before Washing

  • Keep pads in breathable container (cloth bag, bucket with ventilation)
  • Never store sealed in plastic; promotes bacterial growth and odor
  • Add 1-2 essential oil drops (lavender, tea tree) or baking soda to reduce odor
  • Wash every 2-4 days; don't allow pads to soak longer than overnight

Washing Methods

Hand Washing (Most Thorough)

Pre-soak: soak pads 2-4 hours in cold water with salt or vinegar (helps loosen blood).
Scrub: gently scrub stains with soft brush or cloth using cool water; work in circular motions.
Wash: use mild soap (no bleach or harsh chemicals); wash gently by hand or gentle rubbing motion.
Rinse thoroughly: rinse 3-4 times with cool water until water runs clear; ensure no soap residue.
Dry: hang to air-dry in sunlight if possible (natural bleaching kills bacteria); takes 12-24 hours.

Machine Washing

  • Settings: Cold or warm water (not hot); gentle cycle; low spin
  • Detergent: Mild, fragrance-free; avoid bleach (damages fabric) and softener (reduces absorbency)
  • Frequency: Can combine pads with regular laundry but wash more frequently than regular clothes
  • Mesh bag: Place pads in mesh laundry bag to protect snaps and prevent tangling

Drying and Storage

  • Drying: Air-dry completely before storing (12-24 hours); sunlight preferred for bacterial control
  • Never tumble dry: Heat damages elastic and water-resistant layers
  • Storage location: Cool, dry, dark place (shelf or drawer); moisture promotes mold
  • Storage container: Cloth bag or ventilated container; check monthly for mold
  • Shelf life: Properly stored pads last 5-10 years; some last 20+ years
Mold in fabric is health hazard. If mold spots appear, soak affected area in vinegar, scrub gently, and expose to direct sunlight for 6+ hours. If mold returns, discard pad for health safety.

Sterilization (Optional, for disease prevention)

If community member has infectious disease or immune compromise:

  • Boiling: Boil pads in water for 20 minutes (may damage some materials)
  • Bleach solution: Soak 5-10 minutes in 1:10 diluted bleach solution, then rinse thoroughly
  • Sun exposure: Lay pads in direct sunlight for 6+ hours (less aggressive sterilization)
  • Vinegar: Not sterilizing but reduces many pathogens when soaked 30 minutes
Sunshine is remarkably effective at reducing bacteria and odor while being gentle on fabrics. Even cloudy days provide some UV benefit; weekly sun exposure keeps pads fresher and reduces need for harsh chemicals.

Material Selection

Latex (Natural Rubber)

  • Properties: Flexible, warm feel, biodegradable
  • Sourcing: Latex sheets or latex molding compound
  • Allergies: 1-3% population has latex allergy; medical concern
  • Durability: 5-7 years with proper care

Silicone (Medical-grade preferred)

  • Properties: Highly flexible, excellent chemical resistance, hypoallergenic
  • Sourcing: Medical-grade liquid silicone rubber (LSR) or silicone sheets
  • Temperature tolerance: -60¬∞C to +200¬∞C (survives sterilization)
  • Durability: 10+ years
Standard craft silicone (non-medical grade) may leach chemicals into menstrual fluid. Use only medical-grade silicone for vaginal products.

Basic Cup Molding Method (Latex Dipping)

Most accessible DIY method requires latex liquid and a form:

Create mold form: use plaster or clay to sculpt bell-cup shape matching target dimensions; must be smooth with no undercuts.
Prepare latex: obtain latex compound (available from specialty suppliers); follow safety instructions (work in ventilated space).
Dip process: heat latex to working temperature (~70¬∞C); dip form in latex slowly, allowing coating to build layer by layer.
Build thickness: repeat dips 10-15 times, allowing drying between layers; target final thickness 1-2mm (0.04-0.08 inches).
Create stem: attach small latex stem or fashion from excess material at cup base; sand smooth.
Cure fully: allow 24-48 hours complete curing in warm, dry location.
Remove from mold: carefully peel latex cup from form; may require lubrication (talc powder, corn starch).
Finish: trim excess material, sand all edges, test for leaks by filling with water.
Sterilize: boil in water for 20 minutes before first use; allows initial venting of any chemical odors.

Mold Form Creation

  • Materials: Clay, plaster, or machined forms
  • Smoothness: Critical; any surface irregularities transfer to final cup
  • Release coating: Apply talc or corn starch to mold for easier removal
  • Size accuracy: Measure mold carefully; small variations (1-2mm) affect cup function

Disc Construction (Latex)

Fabricating discs requires different technique than cups:

Create flat disc mold: use smooth, flat surface (glass, polished wood, or silicone); mark target diameter (typically 60-65mm for regular).
Shape edge rim: disc must have firm edge rim to maintain shape during insertion; thickness ~2-3mm at rim.
Latex application: coat mold with latex, building thickness gradually; keep center thin (0.5-1mm) for flexibility, rim thicker (2-3mm).
Create grooves (optional): add subtle groove pattern on interior (2-3mm apart) to improve gripping and user control.
Cure and remove: allow 24-48 hours curing; remove carefully by rolling off mold.
Test: disc should be flexible enough to fold for insertion but maintain disc shape under normal pressure.

Traditional Cloth Disc Alternative

For situations where latex/silicone unavailable:

  • Material: Sturdy cotton fabric (chambray, denim-weight) with multilayer absorbent center
  • Size: 4-5" diameter circular pad with curved edges
  • Design: Can be used like large pad or positioned at cervical opening
  • Advantages: Easily manufactured from cloth pads; familiar materials
  • Disadvantages: Lower capacity than molded disc; less reliable retention

Sphagnum Moss Preparation

Harvest from wetland areas; look for light-colored, fluffy moss (usually near water or constantly moist ground).
Wash thoroughly in cool water to remove dirt and debris; squeeze gently to remove excess water.
Spread on cloth to air-dry completely (12-24 hours); ensure bone-dry before use.
Fluff gently by hand, breaking apart compressed sections; looseness improves absorbency.
Layer between cloth pieces: place 1-2 inches of fluffed moss between top cotton layer and waterproof backing.
Secure edges: hand-sew or tie to prevent moss from shifting.
Change every 3-4 hours; sphagnum is naturally antimicrobial and odor-resistant.

Belt/Attachment Systems (No Underwear)

In resource scarcity, pads might be worn with belt rather than underwear:

  • Materials: Cloth strip (1" wide), tied around waist at hips
  • Attachment: Pad sewn to belt or secured with buttons/snaps
  • Positioning: Keep pad centered; test for comfort before use
  • Advantage: Can be used without underwear; allows air circulation
  • Disadvantage: Less stable than underwear-based system
Emergency solutions should be temporary. Inadequate menstrual management (frequent leakage, inadequate change frequency) increases infection risk significantly. Return to proper pads/cups as soon as possible.

11. Menstrual Health Education

Knowledge about menstrual function, health, and self-care prevents complications and supports overall wellbeing. Community education breaks stigma and improves health outcomes.

Menstrual Health Basics

  • Normal menstruation: Not painful; some discomfort acceptable but debilitating pain is not normal
  • Cycle control: Stress, illness, nutrition, exercise all affect cycle temporarily; permanent changes warrant investigation
  • Hygiene principle: Keep pads/products clean and change regularly; external genital washing ok but internal douching harmful
  • Nutrition impact: Iron-rich diet critical for menstruating people (blood loss = iron loss); deficiency causes fatigue, weakness

Menstrual Cycle Phases and Self-Care

Understanding cycle phases helps optimize self-care and activity planning:

Phase 1: Menstruation (Days 1-5, typically)

  • Body state: Shedding uterine lining; hormone levels low
  • Energy: Often lower; fatigue common
  • Self-care: Rest, warmth, iron-rich nutrition, light movement
  • Product needs: Regular to heavy absorbency depending on flow

Phase 2: Follicular Phase (Days 1-13, typically)

  • Body state: Estrogen rising; preparing for ovulation
  • Energy: Increasing; mood improving
  • Self-care: Increase physical activity, social engagement, projects
  • Product needs: Light to regular absorbency (decreasing over phase)

Phase 3: Ovulation (Day 14, typically)

  • Body state: Hormone surge; release of egg
  • Energy: Peak energy and social confidence
  • Self-care: Peak time for important tasks, exercise, social events
  • Product needs: Minimal (may have slight discharge)

Phase 4: Luteal Phase (Days 15-28, typically)

  • Body state: Progesterone rising; body preparing for period or pregnancy
  • Energy: Gradually declining; may increase again briefly before period
  • Self-care: Self-care focus; organizational work; comfort activities
  • Product needs: Minimal until period begins
Menstrual health education should emphasize that cycles vary by person: 21-35 days all normal, 2-7 days all normal. Individual patterns matter more than textbook averages.

Red Flags: When to Seek Health Care

  • Sudden cessation of periods (not pregnancy) with no other cause
  • Periods becoming extremely heavy or duration increasing significantly
  • Severe pain that prevents normal activity or doesn't improve with rest/heat
  • Cycle length changing dramatically (from 28 to 60+ days, for example)
  • Unusual discharge: foul odor, cottage-cheese consistency, heavy greenish discharge
  • Persistent fatigue despite adequate nutrition and rest (possible anemia)
  • Fever during or after period (possible infection)

12. Storage & Distribution for Community Wellbeing

Organized storage and thoughtful distribution systems ensure products reach those in need reliably and respectfully.

Storage Best Practices

Environmental Conditions

  • Temperature: Cool, stable (15-25¬∞C ideal); avoid heat which damages elasticity
  • Humidity: Dry environment; moisture promotes mold and material degradation
  • Light: Dark storage preferred (UV can degrade some materials)
  • Airflow: Good ventilation prevents odors and mold

Inventory Organization

  • Categorize by type: Separate light/regular/heavy pads; track cups and discs separately
  • Label clearly: Mark pad absorbency level on each unit
  • Track expiration: While cloth pads don't "expire," latex may degrade after 5-7 years; note manufacturing date
  • Quantity tracking: Maintain inventory log; reorder before depletion

Distribution Models

Community Distribution Hub

Centralized location for all menstrual products; open to all who need them:

  • Advantages: Consistent supply; education opportunity; breaks stigma; builds community connection
  • Challenges: Requires safe space; may cause embarrassment for some users
  • Solution: Private dispensing area; trained staff; respectful approach

Private Distribution (Discreet Delivery)

Personal delivery to individuals, especially those unable to access hub:

  • Advantages: Reaches isolated or homebound individuals; maintains privacy
  • Challenges: Requires volunteer delivery staff; higher logistics
  • Solution: Trained volunteers; regular delivery schedule; respectful approach

School/Institution Supply

Partner with schools, workplaces, shelters to provide products on-site:

  • Advantages: Reaches vulnerable population; improves attendance/productivity
  • Challenges: Requires institutional cooperation; storage in facilities
  • Solution: Secure, discreet dispensing locations; education programs

Education Component of Distribution

Effective distribution pairs products with education:

  • Product instruction: How to use cups, discs, cloth pads; what to expect
  • Health information: Normal menstruation; when to seek help; health monitoring
  • Hygiene education: Proper cleaning, storage, use; infection prevention
  • Normalization: Menstruation is normal, healthy, nothing to be ashamed of

Community Production Training

Teach members to manufacture products locally, creating economic opportunity and self-sufficiency:

  • Sewing workshops: Teach cloth pad construction; materials sourcing
  • Material sourcing: Identify local cotton, hemp, wool sources
  • Business model: Small-scale pad production for income or community supply
  • Quality standards: Consistency, durability, health safety
Historically, menstrual product production was home/community-based. Skills to manufacture supplies locally create both health security and economic resilience in communities.

13. Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

Learning from others' errors ensures product quality and customer satisfaction.

Design and Construction Errors

  • Mistake: Insufficient absorbent layers; pads leak within 2-3 hours
  • Result: Customer embarrassment, leakage onto clothing, lost confidence
  • Prevention: Test absorbency before finalizing design; layer 3-4 flannel or 2 batting + 1 flannel minimum
  • Mistake: Waterproof layer too effective; traps moisture and heat causing skin irritation
  • Result: Discomfort, maceration, possible yeast infection from moisture buildup
  • Prevention: Choose breathable barrier (tight cotton) or high-quality PUL; ensure adequate ventilation
  • Mistake: Wings too small or snaps misaligned; pad shifts during wear
  • Result: Leakage, discomfort, user abandons product
  • Prevention: Test fit on actual underwear; ensure snaps positioned for secure hold; wings 1.5-2"+ wide

Sewing Quality Issues

  • Mistake: Weak stitching; threads break or seams separate after first wash
  • Result: Pads fail prematurely; layers separate; product unusable
  • Prevention: Use strong thread (polyester or cotton-wrapped); small stitch length (2-3mm); reinforce corners with box stitch
  • Mistake: Loose or unraveling edges; fibers escape into sensitive areas
  • Result: Irritation, discomfort; edges fray further with washing
  • Prevention: Finish all edges with zig-zag stitch or careful hand-stitching; trim loose threads immediately

Material Selection Errors

  • Mistake: Using synthetic fabrics directly against skin; causes irritation and heat buildup
  • Result: Discomfort, potential allergic reaction, reduced product use
  • Prevention: Use only natural fibers (cotton, bamboo, hemp) for skin contact; synthetic only for moisture barriers
  • Mistake: Skimping on absorbent materials to reduce cost; using thin single-layer padding
  • Result: Inadequate absorption; customer dissatisfaction; premature replacement cost
  • Prevention: Use appropriate layers for intended absorbency; educate customers on product tiers (light/regular/heavy)

Hygiene and Sanitation Errors

  • Mistake: Not sanitizing cups/discs before first use; mold spores or manufacturing residue present
  • Result: Possible infection or allergic reaction on first use
  • Prevention: Always boil new products 20 minutes before first use; include instructions with product
  • Mistake: Inadequate washing instructions provided to customers
  • Result: Improper care reduces product life; staining and odor problems
  • Prevention: Include detailed care sheet with each product; cover cold water rinsing, gentle washing, proper drying

Fitting and Sizing Errors

  • Mistake: One-size-fits-all approach; standard pad dimensions don't work for all bodies
  • Result: Discomfort, leakage, product abandonment by users with smaller or larger frames
  • Prevention: Offer multiple sizes; teach body awareness (no "standard" body size); customize if possible
Collect feedback from actual users. What works in theory sometimes needs adjustment based on real-world use. Iterate based on feedback to continuously improve products.

Distribution and Access Errors

  • Mistake: Making products difficult to access or obtain (shame-based gatekeeping)
  • Result: Some people avoid seeking products; health and wellbeing suffer
  • Prevention: Make access easy, nonjudgmental, dignified; normalize menstruation; remove barriers
Never make menstrual products conditional on anything (attendance, behavior, etc.). Menstruation is a biological function, not a privilege. Products should be freely available to anyone who menstruates.
