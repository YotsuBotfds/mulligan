Foundations

Foundation Types

Slab-on-Grade Foundation

A concrete slab poured directly on the ground, typically 4-6 inches thick. Best for dry, stable ground with good drainage. Advantages: economical, easy to construct, suitable for smaller buildings. Disadvantages: difficult to repair utilities, susceptible to frost heave in cold climates, moisture issues if drainage is poor.

Slab Composition: Typically 4-6" of concrete over 4" of gravel base. Perimeter should be below frost line or isolated thermally. Pour monolithic (all at once) for smaller buildings.

Crawlspace Foundation

Concrete footings below frost line with stem walls creating an accessible 18-48" crawlspace. Allows utility access and installation. Requires proper ventilation or encapsulation. Standard for moderate climates.

Crawlspace Details: Footings must extend below frost line. Stem walls are typically 8-12" above grade. Vapor barrier on floor essential. Natural ventilation (1 sq ft per 150 sq ft of crawlspace) or encapsulation with dehumidification recommended.

Pier & Post Foundation

Individual concrete piers or posts supporting a wooden structure. Excellent for difficult terrain, sloped sites, and off-grid scenarios. Allows water drainage underneath.

Post Sizing: 4x4 or 6x6 posts on 4-6" concrete piers. Posts must be set on concrete isolated from soil. Posts should be pressure-treated or naturally rot-resistant wood. Space typically 4-8 feet apart depending on load.

Stone Foundation

Traditional method using stacked stone and mortar. Excellent for off-grid scenarios where stone is available locally. Requires solid limestone or granite blocks.

Stone Work: Use stones 18-36" long, larger stones at bottom. Mortar mix: 1 part lime : 2-3 parts sand. Lay roughly level, fill voids with smaller stone. Height depends on structure, typically 18-48" above grade.

Site Preparation

Clear vegetation and topsoil from the building area. Remove at least 6-12 inches of organic material and stockpile for later landscaping. Establish proper drainage patterns - slope should fall away from building at 5-10%. Compact subgrade soil in 4-6 inch lifts with a hand tamper or machine.

Drainage Considerations

  • Surface Drainage: 5-10% slope around foundation, extending 4-6 feet from building
  • Subsurface Drainage: Perforated drain pipe around footing in high water table areas, leading to daylight or French drain
  • Frost Line: Varies by region: 12-18" for mild climates, 36-48" for cold climates. Foundations must extend below to avoid frost heave
  • Moisture Barriers: Vapor barrier between concrete and building materials

Curing & Strength Development

  • Initial Set: 24-48 hours (surface can be walked on)
  • Formwork Removal: 7 days minimum for most applications
  • Load Application: Wait 14-28 days before applying structural loads
  • Full Strength: Reaches ~90% strength at 28 days; continues gaining strength slowly for months
  • Curing Method: Keep concrete moist during first 7 days - cover with plastic, wet burlap, or spray daily in dry climates

Rebar Placement

#4 rebar (1/2" diameter) is standard for residential. Space 12-18" apart in each direction for slabs. In footings, place one bar near bottom center. Use chairs or concrete spacers to maintain 2-3" clearance from bottom surface. Top bars in slabs go 2" from top surface.

Rebar Sizing: #3 (3/8") for light duty, #4 (1/2") standard, #5 (5/8") for heavy loads. Lap splices 40x bar diameter (40" for #4).

Timber Framing

Post and Beam Basics

Post and beam construction uses large timber posts (typically 4x4 to 8x8) spaced 8-16 feet apart, supporting large beams that span between posts. This is one of the oldest and most practical framing systems. Posts carry vertical loads to foundation; beams carry horizontal loads between posts. This system allows flexible interior spaces with fewer interior walls.

Beam Sizing Principles

Beam size depends on: span (distance between supports), spacing of posts (tributary load), species of wood, and desired deflection. Larger spans require larger beams. Greater spacing between posts increases beam load.

Traditional Joinery

Mortise and Tenon Joint

A strong structural joint where a projecting tenon (male part) on one timber fits into a mortise (female hole) on another. Used for post-to-beam connections and heavy timber work.

Mortise Size: Typically 1/3 width of post or beam. Depth = 1.25x width. Tenon should fit snugly in mortise with hand pressure (not hammered). Gap should be less than 1/32".

Lap Joint

Two timbers lap over each other by removing half the thickness of each member where they cross. Creates a flat connection suitable for bracing and horizontal connections.

Lap Joint Construction: Mark half the thickness of each piece. Cut removal carefully, test fit. Fasten with wooden pegs or mortises perpendicular to joint. Very effective for wind bracing.

Traditional Pegging

After cutting joints, drill perpendicular holes through the connection and drive hardwood pegs (1-1.5" diameter). Pegs lock the joint and allow for seasonal wood movement. Oak pegs are traditional; whittle to slight taper for easier driving.

Structural Principles

  • Load Path: Vertical loads flow down posts to foundation; lateral loads brace against diagonal members or shear walls
  • Grain Direction: Strength is greatest along the grain (parallel). Perpendicular loads weaken connection
  • Seasoning: Green (wet) wood shrinks as it dries. Design connections to accommodate 5-10% shrinkage across grain
  • Deflection: Beams bend slightly under load. Limit deflection to span/240 for comfort (1" deflection for 20' span)

Raising Techniques

Large timber frames must be raised carefully to avoid injury and damage. Traditional methods include: block and tackle or hand winches, lever and fulcrum systems (gin pole with tripod and pulley), or modern machinery. Assemble frame on ground with all joints fitted, test assembly to ensure all mortises fit properly. Mark all connections and joints clearly. Use wooden wedges to hold frame plumb during raising.

Safety Note: Large timber raising is a controlled group activity. Assign clear roles, use proper equipment rated for load, secure temporary bracing, maintain constant communication. Never raise timber alone.

Roof Truss Design

Wall Construction

Stud Framing - 16" On-Center (OC)

Standard framing uses 2x4 or 2x6 studs spaced 16 inches apart (measured center-to-center). 2x4 is adequate for single story or upper floors; 2x6 provides better insulation value and some structural advantage. Bottom plate nailed to rim beam or foundation. Top plate(s) carry loads and tie wall together. King studs extend full height; trimmer studs support window/door headers. Cripple studs fill space above windows and doors.

Stud Framing - 24" On-Center (OC)

Wider spacing reduces material but requires stronger studs (2x6 minimum) and closer engineering attention. Acceptable for secondary walls, upper floors, or low-load situations. Reduces thermal bridging by 33% compared to 16" OC. Headers must be sized for 24" spacing (larger beams needed). Not recommended for high wind or snow areas.

Wall Sheathing

  • Plywood (1/2" or 5/8"): Provides shear bracing and weather resistance. Exterior grade. Nailed 6" on edges, 12" field
  • OSB (Oriented Strand Board): Similar performance to plywood but less moisture-resistant. Cheaper option
  • Board Sheathing: 1x6 or 1x8 boards, typically diagonal, provide shear bracing and traditional appearance. More material and labor but excellent performance
  • Gypsum Sheathing: Interior only, provides fire resistance and straightness
  • Mineral Fiber: Breathable, excellent for moisture management in mixed climates

Wall Bracing Against Racking

Walls must resist lateral forces (wind, seismic). Methods include:

  • Diagonal Sheathing: 1x6 boards at 45¬∞ angle, very effective
  • Plywood Sheathing: Nailed properly acts as shear wall
  • Diagonal Bracing: 2x4 braces from top of wall to bottom at corners, angle-braced or let-in
  • Shear Walls: Full-height walls with plywood, critical for lateral load resistance

Vapor Barriers and Moisture Management

Vapor barrier placement: Place on warm side of insulation (interior in cold climates, exterior in hot-humid climates). Typical materials: polyethylene film (6 mil minimum), kraft paper facing on batts, or smart membranes that adjust to humidity.

Moisture Management: Incomplete or poorly placed barriers trap moisture and cause rot. Better to have slightly permeable assembly that dries than fully sealed walls. Provide air gap or ventilation path on cold side of insulation in cold climates.

Roofing Systems

Roof Types and Geometry

Gable Roof (Pitched)

Two sloped sides meeting at ridge, creating triangular end walls. Most common, excellent water shedding, easy framing. Rafters slope equally from ridge to exterior walls. Gable ends can be vented for attic ventilation.

Shed Roof (Single-Slope)

Single sloped surface. Economical, simple framing, good for additions. Requires careful flashing where it meets other structures. Common on outbuildings.

Hip Roof

Four sloped sides with no gable walls. More wind-resistant than gable, more complex framing. Hips run from corners to ridge. Common in high-wind areas and provides shelter on all sides.

Roof Sheathing

  • 1x6 or 1x8 Boards: Traditional, allows drying, 1/4" gaps for seasonal expansion. Excellent for metal roofing and shingles
  • 1/2" or 5/8" Plywood: Standard modern sheathing, fast installation. Needs underlayment for shingles
  • Metal Decking: Corrugated or standing seam, self-supporting, excellent for snow shedding

Roofing Materials

Metal Roofing

Galvanized steel, aluminum, or copper panels. Lifespan: 40-70 years. Excellent water shedding, lightweight, durable. Low maintenance. Superior for off-grid (no moss/algae in rain collection). Standing seam (concealed fasteners) or exposed fastener systems. Cost: $8-14/sq ft installed.

Asphalt Shingles

Standard composition shingles, 20-25 year lifespan. Three-tab or architectural styles. Economical ($3-8/sq ft). Requires underlayment. Not ideal for off-grid water collection (granules and chemicals).

Wood Shingles

Cedar or redwood, 20-40 year lifespan if maintained. Beautiful appearance, excellent insulation. Requires annual maintenance (moss treatment), fire risk in dry climates. Cost: $10-15/sq ft.

Thatch

Traditional material using straw, reed, or wheat. 30-60 year lifespan with skilled maintenance. Excellent insulation (R-20+). Regulates humidity naturally. Requires specialized craftsperson. Cost: $15-25/sq ft. Not permitted in many fire-risk areas.

Green/Sod Roof

Growing medium and plants on roof. Excellent insulation, manages stormwater, thermal mass. Requires strong structure (40-150 psf load). Complex drainage and irrigation systems. Best in cool, moist climates. Cost: $20-30/sq ft.

Flashing Details

Flashing prevents water intrusion at roof penetrations and edges. Metal (aluminum, galvanized steel, or copper) flashing directs water away from joints.

  • Ridge Flashing: Caps ridge line, laps down both slopes
  • Valley Flashing: Guides water down valleys where slopes meet
  • Chimney Flashing: Complex multi-piece flashing (base, cricket, sides, top)
  • Vent Flashing: Pipe collars direct water around penetrations
  • Edge Flashing: Drip edge and gutter flashing

Ridge Vents & Attic Ventilation

Proper ventilation removes heat and moisture from attic space. Continuous ridge vent (1x metal cap with perforations) paired with soffit vents provides convective circulation. Minimum 1 sq ft net free area per 150 sq ft attic. In sealed attics (spray foam), ventilation not needed but air sealing critical.

Snow Load Considerations

Snow load varies by region (25-200 psf depending on climate). Steeper roofs (9:12 pitch and steeper) shed snow naturally. Roof framing must support snow load + dead load. Consider snow guards or roof rakes in areas with heavy snow to prevent dangerous slide-offs.

Wind & Snow: High-wind and snow-load regions require stronger framing, closer rafter spacing, and enhanced attachment methods. Consult local codes and engineer designs.

Earthen Building Methods

Cob Construction

Cob is a mixture of clay, straw, and sand formed into thick walls (12-18") by hand or foot treading. No molds needed. Excellent thermal mass, breathable, uses on-site materials.

Cob Recipe (by volume)

  • Base mix: 3 parts sand : 1 part clay : 1 part straw (long fiber)
  • Preparation: Test soil first to find clay content. Mix sand and clay, add water, tread barefoot for 1-2 hours
  • Application: Forms built 12-18" thick, hand-packed in lifts. Each lift dries 2-3 weeks before next layer
  • Plastering: Finish with lime plaster (see plaster section), 2-3 coats, 1/2" each
  • Thickness: 12-18" walls provide R-3 to R-6 but mainly valued for thermal mass (slow heating/cooling)

Adobe Brick Construction

Sun-dried bricks made from clay, straw, and sand. Blocks are uniform, stackable, can be made on-site. More structured than cob.

  • Standard: 4" x 6" x 12" or 4" x 8" x 16"
  • Weight: 30-40 lbs per brick (handles with two hands)
  • Drying: 3-6 weeks in sun, stack with 1" gaps for air circulation
  • Strength: 50-300 psi, varies with clay content and curing

Adobe Mortar Recipe

Same as adobe mix but with less straw (2-3%), applied 1/2-1" thick between bricks. Lime can be added (10% by volume) for durability in wet climates.

Rammed Earth

Clay-rich soil compacted in formwork layers. Very dense, strong (200-400 psi), excellent thermal mass. Requires substantial formwork but minimal material processing.

  • Soil Ratio: 5-15% clay, 85-95% sand/gravel, minimal silt
  • Compaction: Layer 4-6" lifts, compact with tamper to 95%+ density
  • Forms: Reusable plywood forms, held with external bracing
  • Finish: Same plaster as adobe and cob, 1-2" thick
  • Drying: 1-3 months before removing forms

Earthbag Building

Load-bearing walls using soil-filled polypropylene bags stacked in a running bond. Quick, uses on-site soil, stable, earthquake-resistant due to weight and flexibility.

  • Soil Mix: 80% sand, 20% clay, compact into bags
  • Bag Size: 14" x 18" (used flour or grain sacks) or specialized earthbags
  • Stacking: Stack like bricks, running bond pattern, barbed wire between courses for shear
  • Cols: Posts at openings, tied into bags with wire
  • Roof Connection: Bond beam (filled bags or concrete) at top ties to roof framing
  • Finish: Plaster exterior and interior, typically 1-2" thick

Straw Bale Construction

Load-bearing walls using densely compressed straw bales (700-1400 lbs per bale). Excellent insulation (R-20 to R-40 depending on density and orientation), low cost, sustainable.

  • Bale Dimensions: 14-16" width x 18-24" height x 36-48" length (varies by source)
  • Density: Minimum 7 lbs per cubic foot for structural bales (dense bales stronger)
  • Foundation: Must be elevated 12-24" above grade (moisture protection). Concrete or stone preferred
  • Stacking: Running bond pattern, pin with wooden stakes or rebar every 4' vertically
  • Top Plate: Bond beam or pressure-treated plate distributes roof load
  • Plaster: 2-4" total (1-2" coats each side) of clay-sand plaster for breathability
  • Window/Door Frames: Rough openings framed before bale stacking, or frame cut after stacking

Lime Plaster & Renders

Traditional finish for earthen buildings. Breathable, self-healing, regulates humidity. Superior to cement for earthen walls.

Moisture Management in Earthen Buildings

Earthen materials naturally regulate humidity (absorb and release moisture). Proper design prevents saturation:

  • Foundation Height: 12-24" above grade minimum
  • Roof Overhang: 2-3' on all sides to keep rain off walls
  • Drainage: Slope ground away from building, gutters recommended
  • Vapor Permeability: Use breathable plasters and paints; avoid plastic vapor barriers
  • Interior Finish: Earth plaster or lime plaster interior maintains breathability

Joinery & Woodworking Fundamentals

Essential Joints and Applications

Mortise and Tenon Joint

A mortise (female socket cut into one piece) receives a tenon (male projection cut on another piece). One of the strongest joints in woodworking, used for frames, posts to beams, and heirloom furniture.

Cutting: Mortise is typically 1/3 width of piece, depth 1.25x width. Tenon shoulders are 1/4" (gives 1/2" thickness on 1" lumber). Mortise chisel, tenon saw for cutting. Test fit without glue, should require hand pressure to fit.

Dovetail Joint

Interlocking tails and pins. Appears frequently in drawer fronts and corner joints. Very strong, visible beauty in fine woodworking. Requires careful layout and hand tools.

Proportions: Tails (the angled parts) are typically cut at 7-8¬∞ angle from vertical (1:8 ratio). Pins approximately half the width of tails. Layout on piece, scribe between pieces to match, careful cutting with dovetail saw.

Lap Joint

Two pieces overlap, each with half thickness removed where they cross. Simple, strong, commonly used in frames and structural bracing. Can be: - Cross lap (perpendicular pieces) - Half lap (end of one piece meets side of other) - Full lap (end to end, overlapping)

Dado Joint

A groove cut across the grain of one piece; another piece fits into groove. Commonly used for shelves in cabinets. Strong and straightforward.

Proportions: Dado depth is 1/4 to 1/3 of board thickness. Width matches thickness of shelf/insert (usually 3/4"). Dado saw or router with straight bit. Test fit with pressure, not glue fit.

Rabbet Joint

An L-shaped cut on edge or end of piece. One piece's edge fits into rabbet. Simpler than dado, suitable for case backs and some frame joints.

Box Joint (Finger Joint)

Interlocking rectangular "fingers" of equal size. Very strong and striking in appearance. Common in fine boxes and drawers. Requires careful layout and precise cutting.

Hand Tool Techniques

  • Saws: Tenon saw for joinery (14-16" blade, fine tooth). Keep saw vertical to grain. Let gravity do work, don't force. Japanese pull saws excellent but require different technique
  • Chisels: Sharp chisels are essential. Strike with mallet perpendicular to blade, never hammer parallel. Pare with hand pressure for fine cuts
  • Planes: Bench plane (4 or 5) for smoothing. Keep blade sharp, set finely. Plane with grain when possible. Block plane for end grain
  • Marking: Knife lines superior to pencil for joinery (thinner, more accurate). Marking gauge for parallels, combination square for angles

Grain Direction and Tearout

Grain runs generally along tree growth: Look for direction of raised grain (flooring pattern). Plane/chisel with grain direction (uphill, away from you) to avoid tearout. Reverse grain (direction changes) requires lower angle or scraping. On cross-grain (end grain), always support with sacrificial wood block behind to catch tearout.

Wood Finishing for Off-Grid Use

  • Linseed Oil (raw or boiled): Traditional finish, water-resistant, brings out grain. Multiple coats, wipes dry. Slow dry, risk of spontaneous combustion (used rags should be spread or submerged)
  • Tung Oil: Similar to linseed but faster drying, harder finish. More water-resistant than linseed
  • Natural Wax: Beeswax or carnauba wax, soft finish, pleasant smell. Limited water resistance, needs frequent renewal
  • Shellac: Natural resin from lac beetle, beautiful finish, easily repaired. Poor water resistance, alcohol soluble (affected by spills)
  • Earth Pigments: Iron oxides, natural clays mixed with oils for colored finishes without synthetic dyes

Form Building

Forms must be rigid, watertight, and capable of supporting weight of wet concrete (150 lbs per cubic foot). Materials: plywood (3/4" minimum), dimensional lumber 2x4 or 2x6, metal forms. Brace externally with diagonal supports spaced 2-3 feet. Support interior walls to prevent bowing. Coat forms with oil or release agent for easy removal.

Safety: Concrete is heavy (150 lbs/cu ft). Undersized forms or inadequate bracing cause failure and serious injury. Consult engineer for large pours.

Concrete Curing Timeline

  • 0-24 hours: Initial setting, concrete hardens but remains vulnerable
  • 24-48 hours: Can be walked on, remove non-critical forms
  • 7 days: Remove formwork from vertical surfaces, 70% strength achieved
  • 14 days: Safe for light loads, formwork removal from beams acceptable
  • 28 days: Full design strength reached. Standard reference point for concrete strength
  • 30-90 days: Continues slowly gaining strength
  • Moisture Curing: Keep concrete moist (wet burlap, plastic sheeting, spray mist) for first 7 days. Faster drying reduces strength by 10-15%

Basic Brick Laying

Bricks laid in running bond (offset) pattern with mortar joints. Typical brick: 3-5/8" x 2-1/4" x 7-5/8". Mortar joint: 3/8" typical.

Brick Laying Steps

  1. Prepare bed of mortar, 3/8" thick, on course below
  2. Butter end of brick with mortar
  3. Press brick firmly, creating 3/8" joint on all sides
  4. Level brick using level and wooden mallet
  5. Use trowel to strike joint flush or recessed
  6. Continue course, maintaining plumb and level

Stone Wall Construction

Stacked stone with mortar, or dry-stacked (no mortar). Wider at base, tapers toward top. Stones should be relatively flat with largest face exposed. Range: 2-3 feet thick at base for 12-15 foot wall.

  • Coursed Stone: Stones sorted by height, laid in roughly horizontal lines. More finished appearance, faster laying
  • Random Stone: Irregular sizes stacked tightly. More labor-intensive but striking appearance
  • Dry Stack: No mortar, reliant on stone fit and weight. Very durable long-term if well-built. Slower, more skillful work
  • Larger Stones: Use corner stones for strength, place largest stones at base and at corners
  • Height: Typical walls 18-48" above grade, thicker at bottom for stability

Chimney Building

Chimneys must provide draft (natural convection), resist thermal stress, and prevent smoke/gas leakage. Complex structure with multiple components:

  • Firebox: Cast iron, steel, or masonry combustion chamber. Sloped back wall (45¬∞) directs smoke toward throat
  • Throat: Narrows toward flue, critical transition. Damper installed here or above
  • Smoke Chamber: Expands from throat to flue, directs smoke laterally to prevent downdraft
  • Flue: Vertical passage carrying smoke, typically 4-8" diameter. Must be smooth, insulated from exterior
  • Flue Sizing: Roughly 1 sq inch of flue per 10 sq inches of fireplace opening
  • Height: Should extend 3+ feet above roof ridge for proper draft
  • Cleanout: Access door at bottom for ash removal and cleaning

Doors & Window Construction

Simple Door Construction

Solid Wood Door (Frame and Panel Style)

Traditional construction with wooden frame and infill panels. Strong, beautiful, repairable.

  • Frame: 1x4 or 1x6 rails and stiles (horizontal and vertical pieces). Mortise and tenon joints
  • Panels: 3/4" or 5/8" solid wood, or plywood veneer. Panels float in grooves (dado) in frame, allowing expansion
  • Thickness: Standard 1-3/4" interior doors, 2-1/4" exterior for insulation
  • Dimensions: Standard interior 32" or 36" wide, 80" high. Exterior 36" or 42" wide for accessibility
  • Stiles/Rails: Bottom rail thicker (2-3") for strength. Rails spaced roughly equal, more panels add strength

Board and Batten Door

Vertical wooden boards held together by horizontal battens and diagonal bracing. Simple, rustic, very strong.

  • Boards: 1x6 or 1x8 vertical lumber (soft or hardwood)
  • Battens: 1x4 horizontal pieces, 2-4 battens typical, spaced equally
  • Braces: Diagonal 1x4 braces prevent racking, angle from top to bottom on hinge side
  • Fastening: Mortise and tenon joints, or hand-hammered nails through battens into boards
  • Thickness: 1-3/4" to 2-1/4" depending on board size

Door Hanging and Hardware

Hinges

  • Butt Hinges: Standard, recessed into door and frame edge. Size 3-4" for residential doors. Typically 2-3 hinges per door
  • Heavy-Duty Hinges: For thick doors, exterior doors, high-traffic. 4-5" or larger
  • Strap Hinges: Traditional, surface-mounted. L-shaped or long straps distribute load. Excellent for rustic/period doors
  • Spacing: Hinges placed 10-12" from top and bottom, third hinge centered on tall doors

Door Hanging Process

  1. Check door fits in opening (1/8" gap all around typical)
  2. Mark hinge positions on door (typically 10-12" from top/bottom)
  3. Score hinge outline with knife, chisel out recess to hinge thickness
  4. Screw hinge to door, test against frame opening
  5. Mark frame, chisel recesses, screw hinges to frame
  6. Hang door, check swing and operation

Essential Hardware

  • Locks/Latches: Mortise locks (heavy-duty, built into door), key-in-knob locks, traditional latch with thumb-piece
  • Door Closers: Hydraulic closers pull door closed, adjustable speed. Or traditional springs
  • Kick Plates: Protection for lower door, prevents scuffing
  • Strike Plates: Reinforce frame around latch. Essential for security and durability
  • Door Stops: Prevent door slamming against wall, wooden blocks nailed to floor or wall

Window Frame Building

Basic Window Frame Components

  • Sill: Horizontal piece at bottom, sloped for water drainage, typically 1-1/4" thick
  • Head Jamb: Horizontal top piece
  • Side Jambs: Vertical pieces
  • Frame Thickness: 1-3/4" to 2-1/4", depends on wall cavity depth
  • Mortise Joints: Frame assembled with mortise and tenon joints at corners

Sash (Moving Part) Construction

Sash is the window part that opens and closes. Double-hung windows have two sashes (top and bottom move). Casement windows have one or two sashes that pivot.

  • Stiles/Rails: Vertical and horizontal pieces forming frame. 1x2 or 1x3 typical
  • Glazing Rabbet: Groove cut in inside edge, holds glass or thick putty
  • Muntins: Small pieces dividing glazing into smaller panes. Mortise and tenon joinery
  • Joint Location: Rails and stiles meet with mortise and tenon, muntins mortised into rails and stiles

Glazing & Glass Installation

Glass thickness: 3-4mm (1/8-5/32") for single pane. 6mm (1/4") for larger panes or safety. Insulating units use two panes with air gap.

  • Setting Glass: Bed of putty or glazing compound in rabbet, glass pressed firmly, another bead of putty/compound holding glass
  • Putty Recipe (traditional): Linseed oil putty (whiting + linseed oil + white lead or titanium dioxide)
  • Installation: Push putty into rabbet with glazing knife, overlap rabbet edge. Seal top edge with beveled putty stroke
  • Curing: Allow 2-3 weeks for putty to set before painting

Weatherstripping

Seals gaps around doors and windows, prevents air infiltration and weather penetration.

  • Felt Strip: Inexpensive, pressed into grooves or nailed to frame edge. Lasts 3-5 years
  • Rubber/Plastic Bulb: Compressible, more effective seal. 5-10 year lifespan
  • Metal/Vinyl Tape: Adhesive-backed, pressed into place. Moderate durability
  • Traditional: Oakum (tarred hemp fiber) caulked into gaps, painted over. Very long-lasting

Screen Construction

Simple frames with mesh screening for ventilation while excluding insects.

  • Frame: 1x1 or 1x1-1/4" wood (softwood typical, or hardwood for durability). Mortise and tenon corners
  • Screening: Galvanized or plastic-coated wire mesh, 16-18 mesh typical. Aluminum mesh for coastal areas
  • Installation: Mesh stretched tight, stapled or splined into frame groove (screen wire held by spline/cord)
  • Frame Size: Slightly smaller than window opening, hung on light hinges or friction fit

Structural Engineering Basics for Off-Grid Builders

Load Types

Dead Load

Permanent weight of structure: roof material, framing, walls, floors. Does not change. Typical residential: 20-40 psf for roof, 50-100 psf for floors.

Live Load

Temporary, moving loads: snow, people, furniture, vehicles. Varies by use: - Floor: 40 psf residential, 100 psf commercial - Roof (not sloped): 20 psf residential - Snow: 25-200 psf depending on climate

Wind Load

Lateral pressure from wind. Varies by location (exposure, elevation, terrain). Typical residential: 80-120 mph design wind. Creates uplift on roofs and lateral pressure on walls.

Seismic Load

Earthquake forces transmitted through structure. Depends on region and building mass. Well-braced, tied-down structures perform better.

Beam Sizing Principles

Bending Stress

A beam under load bends, creating compression on top and tension on bottom. Deeper beams (greater moment of inertia) resist bending better. Span is squared effect: double the span requires approximately 8x greater beam strength.

Deflection

Beams bend under load - limit deflection to span/240 for comfort. A 20-foot span should deflect no more than 1 inch. Deeper beams reduce deflection.

Shear

Internal forces trying to slice beam. Critical near supports. Wood has good shear strength perpendicular to grain.

Load Paths and Lateral Bracing

Vertical Load Path

  • Roof loads accumulate on rafters/trusses
  • Flow down to top plates of walls
  • Through studs to bottom plates
  • Into rim beam or sill
  • Down to foundation posts or footings

Lateral Bracing Against Wind and Seismic

  • Shear Walls: Walls with plywood sheathing nailed properly act as rigid panels resisting lateral motion
  • Diagonal Bracing: 2x4 diagonals at corners or let-in bracing transfer lateral loads to foundation
  • Roof-to-Wall Connection: Hurricane ties or rafter ties anchor roof to walls, prevent roof uplift and separation
  • Wall-to-Foundation: Bolts anchor sill plate to foundation, prevent sliding
  • Floor System: Adequate nailing and blocking prevent diaphragm action (floor flexing and causing walls to rack)

Safety Factors

Structural design applies safety factors to allow for material variation, unknown conditions, and human error.

  • Wood Bending: Design stress typically 1200-1800 psi, actual failure stress may be 2x higher
  • Compression: Design stress 400-600 psi parallel to grain, depends on species
  • Standard Factor of Safety: 1.5-2.0 typical (design load x 1.5-2 approaches failure stress)
  • Snow Load: Ground snow load adjusted upward for roof loading (roof factor 0.8-1.2)

Engineering Judgment for Off-Grid Builders

  • Local Experience: Study what works in your area/climate. Local buildings (barns, houses) show proven designs
  • Oversizing: When in doubt, slightly oversize beams and connections. Extra 10-20% wood costs little but provides margin
  • Testing: Test questionable connections with slow load application before full building load
  • Inspection: Periodically inspect connections and joints for movement, splitting, or separation
  • Document:**Strong connections and bracing details matter more than marginal oversizing. Clear load paths prevent failure

Alternative Building Materials

Compressed Earth Blocks (CEB)

Overview

Compressed Earth Blocks (CEB) are a sustainable, low-cost alternative to fired brick. They use local soil compressed mechanically to create building blocks. CEB requires no firing, making it ideal for off-grid communities.

Soil Selection

  • Ideal Composition: 5-10% clay, 80-90% sand/silt, minimal organic material
  • Clay Test: Mix soil with water; if it forms a firm ball when dry, clay content is adequate
  • Sand Content: Higher sand content (coarser) reduces shrinkage and cracking
  • Organic Matter: Screen out roots, vegetation, and debris
  • Salt Content: Avoid saline soils (coastal regions) as they degrade blocks over time

CEB Production Steps

  1. Excavate and Prepare: Remove topsoil, excavate subsoil 3-5 feet deep
  2. Dry and Crush: Spread soil in sun for 2-3 weeks, then crush with mechanical beater or tractor
  3. Sieve: Pass through 3mm sieve to remove stones and large particles
  4. Mix Water: Add 6-12% water by weight, mix thoroughly (several passes through machine)
  5. Compress: Feed into manual or mechanical block press. Each block requires 300-800 psi compression
  6. Cure: Stack blocks in shade for 3-7 days, then in sun for 2-4 weeks before use

CEB Block Specifications

Standard Dimensions: 35 x 23 x 14 cm (14 x 9 x 5.5 inches) or similar regional standards

Weight: 4-6 kg per block when dry

Strength: 2-3 MPa (minimum 1 MPa acceptable), tested at 28 days cure

Density: 1600-1900 kg/m¬≥

Building with CEB

  • Mortar: Same soil mix (without straw) or weak cement mortar (1:8 cement:sand)
  • Courses: Lay blocks running bond pattern, minimum 12mm mortar joints
  • Reinforcement: Add bamboo or wire reinforcement horizontally every 4-6 courses in seismic areas
  • Plaster: Apply earth plaster (clay:sand:straw) or lime mortar over blocks for protection
  • Wall Height: Single-story walls: up to 4 meters, multi-story requires engineering analysis

Adobe Brick Making

Adobe Composition Ratios

Traditional adobe requires precise ratios of clay, sand, and straw. Variations by region and soil type:

Component Low-Clay Soil Medium-Clay Soil High-Clay Soil
Clay (by weight) 8-12% 12-18% 18-25%
Sand (by weight) 75-85% 70-80% 65-75%
Straw (by weight) 2-3% 3-4% 4-5%
Water (as needed) 15-20% 18-22% 20-25%

Adobe Production Process

  1. Clay Test: Mix subsoil with water in ratio 1:2. Let settle 24 hours. Clay settles bottom layer, sand above, silt top. Measure each layer to calculate clay percentage.
  2. Excavate and Stockpile: Expose subsoil, separate from topsoil. Stockpile for weathering (1-2 months freezing/thawing breaks up clay)
  3. Sift and Separate: Remove stones, roots, debris. Screen sand to <2mm diameter
  4. Mix Dry: Combine clay soil and additional sand (if needed) in proper ratios. Mix thoroughly with hoe or tractor
  5. Add Water and Straw: Gradually add water while mixing. Straw should be evenly distributed (use 4-8 inch chopped lengths)
  6. Mix Thoroughly: Tread mixture with hooves (cattle/horses) or mechanical mixing until uniform, no streaks of clay or sand
  7. Mold into Forms: Press mixture into wooden brick molds (standard: 4 x 8 x 16 inches or regional equivalent). Overfill and strike level with screed board
  8. Cure in Shade (7-10 days): Stack bricks on edge in shaded location, allow air circulation between rows
  9. Cure in Sun (2-4 weeks): Move to sunny location, lay flat or stacked, until hard and light in color

Adobe Quality Indicators

  • Color: Uniform tan/orange (light clay content) or tan/brown (high clay). White streaks indicate uneven mixing.
  • Drop Test: A fully cured brick dropped from shoulder height should break, not shatter into powder
  • Durability: Adobe bricks last 100+ years in dry climates; protection from rain essential in wet climates

Thatched Roofing

Overview

Thatching is an ancient roofing technique using dried vegetation (straw, reed, or palm) bound in layers. It provides excellent insulation (R-value 3.5-4.0 per inch) and is completely renewable.

Materials

  • Primary Material: Wheat straw (ideal), rye straw, water reed (more durable), or palm thatch
  • Binding: Hazel or willow withes (twisted branches), or tarred twine
  • Fixings: Wooden pegs or metal hooks to secure thatch to rafters
  • Tools: Thatching knife (sickle-shaped), beater, needles, shears

Roof Structure Requirements

  • Pitch: Minimum 45¬∞ (4.5:12 rise:run) recommended for thatch drainage
  • Rafters: Standard rafter spacing, solid sheathing optional (thatch creates weather barrier itself)
  • Eaves Projection: 18-24 inches minimum to protect walls from rain splash
  • Ridge Height: Higher ridge allows deeper thatch layers and extended lifespan

Thatching Process

  1. Prepare Material: Thresh and clean straw. Soak reeds 24 hours if very dry. Bundle straw in 2-3 inch diameter bundles (called "yealms")
  2. Secure First Layer: Starting at eaves, lay thatch bundles horizontally, overlapping butt ends. Secure with wooden pegs or hooks driven through withes into rafters
  3. Compress and Pack: Use beater (wooden paddle) to compress and pack each layer. Thatch compresses to roughly 1/3 its original volume as it settles
  4. Layer Progression: Overlap each layer 6-8 inches over the previous layer (like roof shingles). Each bundle is combed out at the top edge to create uniform appearance
  5. Repeat to Ridge: Continue layering until roof reaches ridge. Total thickness typically 12-18 inches
  6. Ridge Treatment: Create decorative ridge cap, securing with withes or metal strapping
  7. Final Combing: Comb entire roof surface to expose fibers and create uniform appearance

Lifespan and Maintenance

  • Wheat Straw: 20-30 years, requires ridge re-thatching every 10-15 years
  • Water Reed: 40-60 years, more durable but harder to harvest locally
  • Maintenance: Annual inspection for holes, bird damage, or settling. Small repairs (patching) extend life

Chimney and Fireplace Construction

Chimney Design Principles

  • Chimney Effect (Draft): Hot gases rise due to temperature difference between interior flue and exterior air. Draft creates suction that pulls air through fireplace and fuel bed.
  • Draft Calculation: Static pressure (inches of water column) = 0.01944 √ó chimney height (feet) √ó (density difference)
  • Minimum Height: 10-12 feet above firebox, or at least 2 feet above roof peak (whichever is higher) to prevent backdrafting
  • Flue Sizing: Cross-sectional area = fireplace opening area √∑ 10-15. For 36 x 30 inch fireplace (90 sq in), flue = 6-9 sq inches

Masonry Fireplace and Chimney Construction

  1. Foundation: Extend below frost line, support weight of entire chimney (typically 12,000-15,000 lbs for single stack)
  2. Firebox: Interior chamber where fire burns. Sloped back wall and side walls direct heat forward and upward
  3. Firebox Dimensions (for 36-inch hearth width):
    • Opening: 36 inches wide x 24 inches high (approx)
    • Back Wall Depth: 20-24 inches
    • Back Wall Slope: Slant forward at 45¬∞ angle starting 20 inches up from hearth
    • Throat Height: 6-8 inches above damper (constricts opening)
  4. Damper: Metal device in throat above firebox, allows manual control of draft. Seals when fireplace not in use. Length should equal or exceed throat width.
  5. Smoke Chamber: Space above damper, sloped to meet flue. Allows smoke to rise smoothly without turbulence
  6. Flue Lining: 8-inch diameter (or equivalent rectangular) clay tile or brick-lined flue. Must be continuous from smoke chamber to above roof peak
  7. Mortar: Use fireplace mortar (high-clay, low-sand, withstands thermal cycling). Standard masonry mortar fails due to temperature expansion/contraction
  8. Thermal Mass: Thick walls (at least 8 inches) retain heat and release it slowly. For overnight heat retention, 12-16 inches preferable

Chimney Draft Troubleshooting

  • Poor Draft Causes: Insufficient height, flue too large, air leakage, roof penetration height too low, fireplace opening too large, blocked flue
  • Backdrafting: Smoke enters room instead of rising. Often caused by competing exhausts (bathroom fans, range hoods, clothes dryers). Add makeup air or reduce competing drafts.
  • Over-drafting: Excessive draft pulls smoke out rapidly, losing heat. Reduce height or create draft control damper

Natural Insulation Materials

Straw Bale Insulation

  • Material: Compressed straw from grain harvest (wheat, rye, rice)
  • R-Value: R-2.5 to R-3.6 per inch when installed perpendicular to wall (compressed)
  • Installation: Stack bales horizontally or vertically within wall framing. Bales compress to roughly 50% of original volume under load
  • Advantages: Renewable, low embodied energy, excellent sound damping, moderately good vapor permeability
  • Disadvantages: Flammable (requires fire-retardant treatment), susceptible to rodent damage, settles over time
  • Protection: Apply plaster or cladding to both sides. Plaster creates fire barrier and weather protection.

Earth/Rammed Earth Insulation

  • Rammed Earth Walls: Compacted earth mixture in formwork. High thermal mass (slow response to temperature changes) provides stable interior temperature
  • Thermal Mass Formula: Heat capacity ‚âà 0.2 BTU/lb¬∞F for earth. A 12-inch earth wall stores significant heat (example: 1,000 sq ft at 12 inches = 150+ tons, stores ~30,000 BTU per ¬∞F temperature rise)
  • R-Value: R-0.08 to R-0.15 per inch (low insulation but excellent thermal mass)
  • Better for: Passive solar homes in moderate climates. Stores daytime solar heat, releases at night

Moss and Seaweed Insulation

  • Material Source: Harvested from forests or coastal areas
  • R-Value: R-3.0 to R-3.5 per inch (comparable to fiberglass)
  • Installation: Loosely packed into wall cavities or compressed into batts
  • Advantages: Renewable, non-toxic, good moisture regulation
  • Disadvantages: Labor-intensive harvesting, limited in most regions, settling over time

Foundation Drainage Systems

Moisture Problems and Solutions

  • Hydrostatic Pressure: Water in soil exerts pressure on below-grade walls. Prevent with perimeter drainage systems
  • Capillary Rise: Moisture wicks up foundation walls from soil. Breaks contact with earth‚Äîcritical for wood structures
  • Groundwater: High water table or spring water requires active drainage to prevent saturation

Perimeter Drain Installation

  1. Drainage Trench: Excavate around foundation perimeter, minimum 12 inches from foundation, down to footing bottom
  2. Filter Cloth: Line trench bottom and sides with geotextile (prevents soil clogging)
  3. Drainage Pipe: 4-inch perforated pipe (holes face down) laid in trench. Slope minimum 1% (1 foot drop per 100 feet)
  4. Gravel: Cover pipe with 2-3 inches of coarse gravel (prevents silt entry)
  5. Backfill: Backfill trench with remaining soil or sand/gravel mix
  6. Discharge: Drain to daylight (downslope exit) or sump basin

Interior and Exterior Moisture Control

  • Exterior Grade: Slope ground away from foundation, minimum 1% slope for 10 feet (1-foot drop per 100 feet horizontal)
  • Gutters and Downspouts: Collect roof water, extend downspouts 10 feet from foundation (prevents water from concentrating around footing)
  • Interior Sump Basin: If below water table, install sump pit with pump. Removes groundwater that enters anyway.
  • Vapor Barriers: 6-mil polyethylene under slab (below grade) or 4-mil plastic sheet on crawlspace floors

Window and Door Framing

Rough Opening Sizing

Rough opening (R.O.) must be larger than window unit to allow installation and adjustment:

  • Width R.O.: Unit width + 1.5 inches (0.75 inches each side for shims and adjustment)
  • Height R.O.: Unit height + 1.5 inches (0.5 inches below sill, 1 inch above header for shims)
  • Example: 36 x 48 inch window unit requires 37.5 x 49.5 inch rough opening

Header Sizing

Header spans opening, supports load above. Size depends on load and span:

3-4 feet Light Moderate 2x6
4-5 feet Moderate Heavy 2x8
5-6 feet Heavy Very Heavy 2x10

Installation Details

  • Flashing: Metal or rubber flashing above window to shed water away from wall
  • Sill Pan: Sloped flashing below window opening, drains water to exterior
  • Sealant: Use exterior-grade caulk (silicone or polyurethane) around window unit perimeter after installation
  • Shims: Insert wood shims around window frame to ensure square and plumb, then nail studs through shims
  • Air Sealing: Foam or caulk between shims and rough framing to eliminate air leakage
  • Interior Trim: Install interior casing after drywall. Casing covers gap between drywall and window frame.

Yakisugi (Shou Sugi Ban) - Charred Wood Preservation

Yakisugi is a traditional Japanese technique for preserving wood by surface charring. The charred surface provides natural fire and rot resistance without chemical treatments.

Process

  • Char Depth: Burn surface to 1/8 inch (3mm) depth using blowtorch or open flame
  • Temperature: Surface reaches 800-1000¬∞F (425-540¬∞C); interior wood remains cool
  • Cooling: Allow to cool naturally (10-30 minutes depending on wood thickness)
  • Brushing: Brush away loose char with stiff wire brush to reveal underlying char layer
  • Finishing: Optional: apply linseed oil or finish to stabilize char and enhance color

Benefits

  • Fire-resistant: char layer resists ignition better than bare wood
  • Rot-resistant: eliminates food source for wood-eating organisms
  • Insect-resistant: char layer hostile to termites and beetles
  • Weathering: char weathers to silvery-gray patina naturally
  • No chemicals: purely mechanical treatment, no toxic preservatives

Applicable Wood Types

Works well on any wood, but traditionally used on cedar, pine, and larch. Hardwoods are more difficult to char evenly.

Durability

Properly charred surfaces last 10+ years without additional treatment in exposed conditions. Re-charring can be done every 10-20 years to refresh protection and appearance.

Roof Pitch for Snow Load

Roof pitch (slope) affects both water drainage and snow load capacity. Steeper roofs shed snow faster and carry less snow weight; shallow roofs accumulate heavy snow and require stronger framing.

Snow Load Recommendations

  • Minimal Snow (0-20 lbs/sq ft annual): Pitch 3:12 to 4:12 acceptable; water shedding is primary concern
  • Moderate Snow (20-50 lbs/sq ft): Minimum 6:12 pitch recommended; allows snow to slide off before accumulating excessive weight
  • Heavy Snow (50+ lbs/sq ft): Minimum 8:12 to 12:12 pitch; steeper is better to prevent accumulation
  • Design Snow Load: Check building codes for your region; these specify the 50-year snow load (largest snowfall expected in 50 years)

Roof Pitch Notation

Pitch expressed as "rise:run" (e.g., 6:12 means 6 inches of rise for every 12 inches of horizontal distance).

  • 4:12 = 18¬∞ slope
  • 6:12 = 27¬∞ slope
  • 8:12 = 34¬∞ slope
  • 12:12 = 45¬∞ slope

Framing Strength for Snow Load

Deeper roof trusses or rafters are required for heavy snow loads:

  • Light snow: 2x6 or 2x8 rafters 16" on center
  • Moderate snow: 2x8 or 2x10 rafters 16" on center, or 2x6 at 12" on center
  • Heavy snow: 2x10 or 2x12 rafters 12-16" on center, engineered trusses

Critical: Consult a structural engineer or follow engineered truss specifications for your snow load. Under-framing leads to roof collapse.

Chimney Draft Calculation

Chimney draft (natural air flow) depends on the height of the chimney and its diameter. Proper draft ensures complete combustion and prevents backdrafting.

Draft Requirements

  • Minimum chimney height: 15 feet total (from grate to cap exit)
  • Typical wood stove: 8-inch diameter pipe minimum
  • Fireplace: 12-inch square or 10-inch round minimum
  • Effective height: Vertical distance from firebox to chimney exit; horizontal runs reduce effective height

Draft Force (Inches of Water Column)

Adequate draft is approximately 0.05-0.10 inches of water column (measured with a draft gauge). Too much draft wastes heat; too little causes smoke backup.

Approximate draft at various heights (with 8" pipe diameter):

  • 15 feet height: ~0.08 inches WC (adequate)
  • 20 feet height: ~0.11 inches WC (good)
  • 25 feet height: ~0.14 inches WC (excellent)

Factors Affecting Draft

  • Temperature difference: Hotter chimney = better draft; insulated chimneys perform better
  • Wind: Can help (updraft) or hinder (downdraft); cap design and location matter
  • Leaks: Cracks or loose joints reduce draft; seal all connections with high-temperature mortar or cement
  • Obstruction: Creosote buildup, nests, or debris reduce draft; annual cleaning essential

Rule of thumb: Every additional 5 feet of height improves draft proportionally. Problems with insufficient draft usually indicate too-short chimney.

Wind Bracing for Wood Frame Structures

Walls must resist lateral (sideways) wind forces. Unbraced frames can collapse in moderate winds. Diagonal bracing, plywood sheathing, and proper connections provide necessary resistance.

Wind Bracing Methods

Diagonal Let-In Bracing

  • Material: 1x4 or 2x4 lumber running diagonally from top of wall to bottom
  • Angle: 45¬∞ to 60¬∞ from horizontal (steeper = stronger)
  • Spacing: Place bracing on all exterior walls, approximately every 25 feet of wall length
  • Installation: Notch (let-in) into studs so brace is flush with exterior surface; nail with minimum 3 nails per stud intersection

Plywood Sheathing

  • Most effective: Plywood nailed every 6 inches on edges and 12 inches in field
  • Strength: 1/2" CDX plywood provides equivalent bracing to diagonal let-in bracing over same area
  • Combined: Plywood + diagonal bracing provides redundant protection and highest wind resistance

Oriented Strand Board (OSB)

  • Less effective than plywood for bracing (weaker in shear)
  • Acceptable in low-wind areas if properly fastened

Connection Details

  • Foundation to frame: Anchor bolts every 4-6 feet prevent walls from lifting in wind
  • Roof to walls: Hurricane ties or rafter ties prevent roof from separating in uplift
  • Wall to wall: Corner bracing critical at intersections; doubled studs at corners

Gable vs. Hip Roofs

  • Gable roofs: Triangular end walls act as a sail, creating high wind stress; require bracing of gable end
  • Hip roofs: No exposed gable; all slopes drain wind pressure; generally more wind-resistant

Earthquake-Resistant Joinery

In seismic regions, connections must allow slight movement without failure. Flexible, well-fastened connections perform better than rigid joints.

Key Principles

  • Redundancy: Multiple fastening points; if one fails, others carry load
  • Flexibility: Slight movement is preferable to sudden failure
  • Metal fasteners: Nails and bolts with washers distribute load

Traditional Mortise-and-Tenon Joinery

  • Strength: Interlocking wood-to-wood connection is inherently strong
  • Improvement: Add wooden pegs (fox wedging) through the mortise-tenon to lock the joint against racking
  • Advantage: All-wood construction (no metal fasteners) is traditional and allows flexibility

Modern Bolted Connections

  • Bolts with large washers: Distribute load over wider area than nails
  • Spacing: Multiple bolts (minimum 2) at each joint prevent rotation
  • Flexibility: Slightly oversized bolt holes allow limited movement without binding

Recommended Details for Seismic Areas

  • Beam-to-post: Mortise-and-tenon with pegs, or bolted steel brackets on both sides
  • Foundation to frame: Anchor bolts embedded in concrete, threaded to wood sill plate
  • Diagonal bracing: Reduces lateral movement and distributes seismic forces

Design Principle: A flexible building that sways slightly in an earthquake survives. A rigid building that won't move cracks and fails catastrophically. Design for flexibility, not rigidity.
