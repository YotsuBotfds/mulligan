Electrical Fundamentals

Ohm's Law & Basic Relationships

Ohm's Law is the foundation of electrical theory and describes the relationship between voltage, current, and resistance in any circuit.

Ohm's Law
V = I √ó R
V = Voltage (volts), I = Current (amps), R = Resistance (ohms)

Voltage is electrical pressure measured in volts (V). It represents the potential energy per unit charge.

Current is the flow of electrical charge measured in amperes or amps (A). It represents how many electrons are moving.

Resistance is opposition to current flow measured in ohms (Œ©). Materials with high resistance convert electrical energy to heat.

Power calculation: P = V √ó I (watts). This tells you how much energy is being consumed or produced.

AC vs DC Current

DC (Direct Current) flows in one direction constantly. Voltage and current remain relatively constant. Used in batteries, solar panels, and most off-grid systems. DC is safer for low-voltage applications and easier to store in batteries.

AC (Alternating Current) periodically reverses direction, typically 50-60 times per second (50/60 Hz). Used in grid power and generators. AC transmits more efficiently over long distances due to voltage transformation via transformers.

Conversion between AC and DC requires rectifiers (AC to DC) or inverters (DC to AC). Off-grid systems typically generate or store DC power and convert to AC for household loads.

Circuit Types

Series Circuits

In series circuits, components are connected end-to-end in a single path. Current is the same throughout but voltage divides among components. Total resistance is the sum of all resistances: R_total = R1 + R2 + R3. If one component fails, the entire circuit breaks. Series connections are useful for voltage addition (batteries) but poor for reliability in household systems.

Parallel Circuits

In parallel circuits, components are connected across the same voltage source with multiple current paths. Voltage is the same across all branches, but current divides. Total resistance is lower than any single branch: 1/R_total = 1/R1 + 1/R2 + 1/R3. If one branch fails, others continue working. Parallel connections are used for household wiring, battery banks, and solar panels.

Series-Parallel Combinations

Most practical systems combine series and parallel configurations. For example, battery banks often arrange cells in series to increase voltage and then parallel multiple strings to increase capacity while reducing internal resistance.

Grounding & Safety

Grounding provides a safe path for fault currents to reach earth, preventing shocks and fires. A proper ground connection to earth rod or water pipe is essential for any electrical system, especially in off-grid installations.

Three-wire systems include: Hot (carries current), Neutral (return path at earth potential), and Ground (safety path). Ground fault circuit interrupters (GFCIs) detect current leakage and shut down power in milliseconds, providing critical protection in wet areas.

Bonding connects all metal frames and enclosures to ground to prevent dangerous voltage buildup. Main breaker switches and disconnect points must be properly bonded.

Electrical Wiring & Repair

Wire Splicing Techniques

Twisted & Soldered Splice

Strip 1-2 inches of insulation from each wire. Twist exposed conductors together tightly in a spiral, creating mechanical strength. Apply rosin-core solder (not acid-core) with a hot iron until solder flows smoothly over the joint. Allow to cool. Wrap with electrical tape, starting below the original insulation and overlapping upward to create a waterproof seal. This creates a strong, reliable connection suitable for permanent installations.

Wire Nuts (Crimp Connectors)

Strip 3/4 inch of insulation from each wire. Twist conductors together by hand to hold them in position. Screw a wire nut over the twisted bundle, turning clockwise until tight but not overtightened. Test by tugging on each wire‚Äîit should not pull free. Wire nuts are quick and reversible, ideal for temporary connections or splice-and-junction situations. Always verify the nut size matches wire gauge and count.

Crimped Connectors

Use proper crimping tools matched to wire gauge and connector type. Strip wire to depth marked on connector barrel. Insert wire fully until it bottoms. Crimp firmly with correct tool. Crimp connectors create consistent, strong connections. Ring terminals provide reliability for permanent connections; spade terminals allow disconnection; butt splices splice equal-gauge wire. Always use heat-shrink tubing over the crimp for weatherproofing in outdoor applications.

Switch & Outlet Installation

Single-Pole Switch

A single-pole switch controls a load from one location. At the circuit breaker, identify the hot wire (black) feeding the switch. Connect hot wire to brass terminal on switch, load wire to the other brass terminal. Connect neutral (white) and ground (green/bare) to their respective terminals or wire nuts. The switch operates by breaking/making the hot connection. Install in electrical boxes with proper strain relief.

Outlet Installation

Outlets have brass terminals (hot), silver terminals (neutral), and green (ground). Current flows from hot through the outlet load to neutral. Always verify polarity: hot to brass, neutral to silver, ground to green. Install outlets with long slot facing neutral. Test with multimeter before energizing. Daisy-chain outlets by connecting power into one and output from another‚Äîall loads share the same circuit protection.

Circuit Breaker Troubleshooting

Circuit breakers protect wiring from overcurrent that causes fire. If a breaker trips repeatedly, it indicates overload or short circuit. Check the load on that circuit‚Äîturn off all devices and reset breaker. If it holds, devices were overloaded. If it trips immediately, there's a short circuit. Turn off all outlets on that circuit, reset breaker, then methodically re-enable devices to find the problem.

A tripped breaker has a switch between ON and OFF positions. To reset: switch fully to OFF, then firmly to ON. If breaker won't stay ON or trips when nothing is connected, the breaker is faulty and must be replaced by a qualified electrician.

12V DC Off-Grid Systems

Off-grid cabins and remote equipment typically use 12V DC systems powered by solar panels, batteries, or generators. 12V DC is safer than AC at these voltages and doesn't require inverter losses.

System Layout

A basic system includes: solar array (source), charge controller (regulates charging), battery bank (storage), DC loads (lights, pumps), and inverter (for AC loads if needed). Battery cables must be oversized (typically 2 AWG or larger for distances over 10 feet) to prevent voltage drop. Use a main disconnect switch between battery and system. Include blocking diodes on solar inputs to prevent backfeeding. Install fuses near battery terminals rated slightly above expected current.

Solar Panel Wiring

Series Configuration

Connecting solar panels in series adds their voltages while maintaining current. Two 12V panels in series produce 24V. Each panel receives current through its positive terminal and outputs to the next panel's negative terminal. Advantage: smaller wire gauge (reduced loss). Disadvantage: shading one panel reduces entire string output. Use series when space is limited or to match controller input voltage.

Parallel Configuration

Connecting panels in parallel maintains voltage while adding current. Two 12V panels in parallel produce 12V at double the current. Connect all positive terminals together and all negative terminals together through combiner box. Advantage: partial shading affects only that panel. Disadvantage: larger wire gauge required. Use parallel for distributed arrays where shading varies or when reliability is critical.

Series-Parallel Arrays

Create strings (series), then parallel the strings. Two strings of two panels each (2√ó2 configuration) adds flexibility and balances voltage/current. Example: Two 12V panels in series = 24V; four strings in parallel = 24V at 4√ó current. This provides redundancy and flexibility for system expansion.

Battery Bank Configuration

Lead-acid batteries are series-connected to increase voltage. A 48V system uses four 12V batteries in series. Capacity is measured in amp-hours (Ah). To increase amp-hour capacity without changing voltage, parallel battery banks are used, but each parallel string should have identical state of charge.

Inverter Selection

An inverter converts DC power to AC. Pure sine wave inverters produce AC identical to grid power, suitable for sensitive electronics and induction motors. Modified sine wave inverters are cheaper but create a stepped waveform that can damage some equipment and is noisier. Choose pure sine for critical loads.

Sizing: Inverter rated power (watts) must exceed peak load. Check continuous power and surge power. A microwave might draw 1500W continuous but 2000W surge. Choose inverter rated for at least 125% of peak load. Battery voltage must match inverter input (12V, 24V, or 48V). Higher voltage systems use smaller wire gauges.

Install a battery disconnect switch between battery and inverter. This allows safe shutdown for maintenance and protects against parasitic drain when equipment is not in use.

Small Engine Repair

4-Stroke Engine Operation

Most modern small engines (generators, mowers, chainsaws) use 4-stroke cycles. The engine cycles through four strokes per combustion event, requiring two full crankshaft rotations.

Intake Stroke

Piston moves downward, intake valve opens, drawing fuel-air mixture into the cylinder. Volume increases, pressure drops, mixture flows in naturally. Exhaust valve remains closed.

Compression Stroke

Piston moves upward, both valves closed, compressing the fuel-air mixture. Compression ratio determines final pressure‚Äîtypically 8:1 to 10:1. High compression increases efficiency but requires higher octane fuel. Just before top dead center, spark plug ignites the mixture.

Power Stroke (Combustion)

Combustion pushes piston downward with tremendous force, doing useful work. Both valves remain closed to contain pressure. This is the only stroke that produces power.

Exhaust Stroke

Piston moves upward, exhaust valve opens, pushing combustion gases out the muffler. Exhaust valve closes at bottom dead center. Cycle repeats.

Carburetor Cleaning & Adjustment

The carburetor mixes fuel and air in the correct proportion for combustion. Dirty or misadjusted carburetors cause hard starting, rough running, and poor power.

Carburetor Cleaning Procedure

  1. Drain fuel tank completely (dispose safely)
  2. Remove carburetor from engine using appropriate wrenches
  3. Disassemble carefully‚Äîmark part positions with a photo
  4. Soak all metal parts in carburetor cleaner for 15-30 minutes
  5. Use compressed air to blow out all passages; do NOT use wires that could damage precision passages
  6. Inspect float valve for sticking or debris
  7. Reassemble with new gasket if original is damaged
  8. Reinstall on engine with fresh fuel

Idle Speed Adjustment

Locate idle speed screw (usually marked L for low speed). Start engine and let warm up 30 seconds. Turn screw clockwise until engine almost dies, then back out 1/4 turn. Engine should idle smoothly without stalling. High-speed adjustment screw (H) should only be adjusted with a tachometer by a professional.

Spark gap: Proper gap is critical (typically 0.030" or 0.76mm). Use feeler gauge to measure and adjust by bending electrode carefully. Remove and clean plugs by scrubbing with wire brush or sandblasting. Replace after 100+ hours or if electrodes are damaged.

Fuel System Troubleshooting

Fuel Filter & Sediment

Dirty fuel is the most common cause of small engine problems. Gasoline absorbs water over time, creating varnish. Check fuel filter‚Äîshould be translucent with minimal debris inside. Replace if dirty. For stored engines, use ethanol-free fuel or add fuel stabilizer to prevent gum formation.

Fuel Line Check

Pinch fuel line with vice-grips near carburetor. If fuel squirts out when line is released, fuel is reaching carburetor. If not, check filter and trace line for cracks. Fuel lines degrade from sunlight and heat; replace with approved replacement hose, not vinyl tubing which fuel will dissolve.

Compression Testing

Low compression indicates worn piston, rings, or valve problems. Remove spark plug. Screw compression tester into spark plug hole. Crank engine through 4-5 complete strokes. Read gauge at peak pressure. Most small engines should show 60-100 psi depending on design.

If compression is low, add a small amount of oil through spark plug hole and retest. If pressure increases significantly, rings are worn. If it remains low, valves are stuck or seal is broken. A single weak compression reading indicates a cracked head or gasket issue.

Oil Change Procedures

  1. Warm engine for 30 seconds to loosen sludge
  2. Stop engine and locate oil drain plug (usually on crankcase bottom)
  3. Place drain pan under plug and remove plug slowly
  4. Allow oil to drain completely (5+ minutes)
  5. Reinstall drain plug with new washer if provided
  6. Locate oil filter (if equipped) and remove with filter wrench
  7. Coat new filter rubber seal with fresh oil, screw on hand-tight only
  8. Add correct amount of specified oil grade (check manual‚Äîtypically 10W-30 or SAE 30)
  9. Run engine 30 seconds, stop, wait 5 minutes, check level on dipstick
  10. Adjust to full mark if needed
Dispose of used oil properly. Never pour into ground, drains, or trash. Take to recycling center. Used oil is valuable and recyclable.

Governor Adjustment

Governors maintain constant engine speed under varying load by adjusting fuel flow. A hunt/surge problem (speed wavers up and down) indicates governor malfunction. Check for loose linkage, bent control rods, or contamination on governor spring. Throttle response should be smooth without hunting or stalling under load.

Plumbing Fundamentals

Pipe Types & Materials

PVC (Polyvinyl Chloride)

Plastic pipe used for cold water and drain/waste/vent (DWV). Advantages: inexpensive, lightweight, corrosion-proof, easy to cut and fit. Disadvantages: cannot handle hot water above 140¬∞F, brittle in cold, UV sensitive outdoors. Use Schedule 40 for general plumbing, Schedule 80 for higher pressure. Join with solvent cement.

CPVC (Chlorinated PVC)

Modified PVC handling hot water up to 180¬∞F. More expensive than PVC but withstands hot water for supply lines. Same joining methods as PVC. Good choice for solar hot water systems where temperature can exceed standard PVC limits.

Copper Pipe

Metal pipe used for water supply (hot and cold). Advantages: durable, reliable, antimicrobial. Disadvantages: expensive, requires soldering, can develop pinhole leaks in acidic water. Types: K (thick, flexible, used for underground supply), L (medium, most common for residential), M (thin, cheaper, less reliable). Joined by soldering with silver solder (>95% copper), making permanent leak-free joints.

PEX (Cross-linked Polyethylene)

Flexible plastic tubing for both hot and cold water supply. Advantages: flexible (easier routing), freeze-resistant, good for solar systems, quick installation with mechanical fittings. Disadvantages: slightly more expensive than PVC, fitting variety can be confusing, not suitable for UV exposure. Join with crimp or compression fittings. Can be expanded for temporary pressure testing then returns to size when pressure released.

Galvanized Steel

Iron pipe with zinc coating used in older systems. Advantages: strong, reliable. Disadvantages: heavy, requires threading, rust develops over 20-30 years creating reduced water flow. Still functional but difficult to repair‚Äîthreading requires professional equipment. Avoid new installations of galvanized pipe for water supply.

Joining Methods

Solvent Cement (PVC/CPVC)

Clean and deburr pipe ends with plastic cleaner. Apply thin coat of cement to inside of fitting and outside of pipe. Push pipe into fitting with slight twist to distribute cement. Hold 30 seconds. Cure time is 2 hours before pressurizing. Solvent cement creates a permanent fusion joint‚Äîcannot be disassembled. Proper technique prevents weak or leaking joints.

Soldering (Copper)

Clean pipe and fitting interiors with emery cloth until shiny. Apply flux (acid or no-clean type) to all mating surfaces. Heat pipe and fitting with torch until joints visibly darken. Touch silver solder to joint‚Äîit should flow around smoothly from capillary action. Remove heat, allow to cool naturally without moving joint. Test with gentle pressure. A proper solder joint is shiny silver and airtight. Undersizing or skipping flux creates weak brittle joints.

Mechanical Fittings (PEX)

Compression fittings screw together with a ferrule that squeezes onto pipe, creating watertight seal. Cut pipe square with sharp tool. Insert ferrule, then nut, onto pipe. Screw nut onto body until snug, then turn 1/2 additional turn. Over-tightening breaks ferrule, under-tightening leaks. Crimp fittings require specialized crimping tool and dies matched to tubing size. Properly crimped joints are permanent and reliable.

Water Pressure Basics

Static pressure is pressure in a system at rest. Dynamic pressure is pressure during water flow. Normal residential pressure is 40-80 psi. Pressure less than 20 psi indicates supply problem. Pressure above 100 psi can damage fixtures and waste water through leaks.

Pressure drop occurs as water flows through pipes due to friction. Longer runs and smaller diameter pipes create more drop. At 50 feet with 1/2" pipe, flow of 5 gpm causes approximately 5-8 psi drop. Oversizing pipe diameter reduces loss‚Äî1" pipe causes less than 1 psi drop for same flow.

Gravity-Fed Water Systems

Off-grid systems often rely on gravity. Water stored at height creates pressure based on elevation difference. One foot of elevation difference creates 0.433 psi of pressure. A 25-foot elevation difference creates approximately 10 psi‚Äîadequate for household use.

Storage tank sizing: Calculate daily needs (typically 50-100 gallons per person) and elevation of water source. For solar-powered pumps, larger storage allows running pump during peak sun hours and storing for evening/cloudy day use. Tank material should be food-grade plastic, never previously used for chemicals or fuel.

Slope/Pitch: Drain pipes must slope downward at minimum 1/4" drop per 8 feet of horizontal run (3% slope). This allows solids to flow without settling. Too steep causes water to run faster than solids. Measure slope by leveling, marking 8 feet distance, and measuring height difference.

P-Traps & Venting

P-trap is a U-shaped section of pipe under sinks and fixtures that holds water, creating a seal that prevents sewer gases from entering. The trap must be positioned correctly‚Äîvent arm (from trap to vent) should slope upward at least 1/4" per foot toward the vent stack. Trap seal should be 2-4 inches of water depth.

Venting allows air into drain pipes, preventing vacuum that slows drainage. Without vent, draining creates suction that can pull trap seals dry, allowing gases to enter home. Vent pipes rise through roof above roofline (minimum 6 inches above) and terminate open to atmosphere. Separate vent pipe required for each fixture.

Common Leak Repairs

Pinhole Leaks in Copper

Small leaks require immediate repair‚Äîshut off water supply. Dry pipe completely with cloth or heat gun. Clean area with emery cloth. Apply epoxy putty to hole, following package directions. Most epoxy putties need 24 hours curing before pressurizing. Alternatively, solder a small copper patch over hole. Pinhole leaks often indicate water quality issues (acidic/corrosive water)‚Äîconsider filtration to prevent future leaks.

Split PVC Pipe

Cracks in PVC from freezing or impact require replacement. Shut off water. Cut out damaged section with hacksaw, leaving clean edges. Dry completely. Use slip couplings or compression couplings to splice in new section. Solvent cement in cold weather may not cure properly‚Äîwork in 50¬∞F+ temperatures or allow extended cure time. Insulate outdoor pipes to prevent freezing damage.

Loose Fitting Leaks

For threaded connections, drain system and use two wrenches‚Äîhold fitting body with one, turn nut with other. Shut off water first. Wrap thread tape (Teflon) around male threads, 3 wraps clockwise. Reassemble and tighten. Do not over-tighten brass fittings which crack easily. For compression fittings, hand-tight plus 1/2 turn usually suffices. If still leaking, replace ferrule.

Mechanical Systems

Bearings

Types of Bearings

Ball bearings: Hardened steel balls roll in grooved races, minimizing friction. Used in electric motors, pumps, fans. Handle radial (perpendicular) and some axial (thrust) loads. Most common type. Can be open or sealed.

Roller bearings: Cylindrical rollers instead of balls, supporting heavier loads over larger contact area. Used in heavy machinery and automotive applications. Handle high radial loads but poor for axial loads.

Tapered roller bearings: Conical rollers mounted in angled races. Excellent for both radial and axial loads. Common in wheels and gear drives.

Plain bearings (bushings): Simple metal-lined holes relying on lubrication film. Used in lower-speed, lower-load applications. Cheaper but require frequent maintenance.

Bearing Replacement

Worn bearings cause noise, vibration, and heat. Replace before catastrophic failure. Remove old bearing with bearing puller tool‚Äînever strike directly with hammer which damages races. Clean bore and shaft carefully. Press new bearing squarely onto shaft using bearing press or careful hammer taps on installation collar. Bearings must slide smoothly onto shaft with slight force; if excessive resistance, verify bore diameter and bearing size match. After replacement, rotate shaft smoothly by hand before operating.

Improvised Lubrication

Ball bearings require specific grease formulations (lithium, synthetic, etc.) for reliability. When proper grease is unavailable, motor oil can temporarily keep bearings functional for low-speed operation. Do NOT overfill‚Äîexcess lubricant heats and breaks down. For emergency repairs, clean bearing with light oil, add small amount of grease, and monitor temperature carefully. Plan for proper bearing maintenance as soon as supplies are available.

Gears & Mechanical Advantage

Gear Ratios

When two gears mesh, the ratio of teeth determines speed and torque multiplication. Smaller input gear driving larger output gear reduces speed but multiplies torque. Ratio = output teeth √∑ input teeth. Example: 20-tooth gear driving 60-tooth gear = 3:1 reduction (3 input rotations = 1 output rotation, torque multiplied √ó3).

Mechanical Advantage Calculation

Mechanical Advantage
MA = Output Force √∑ Input Force
For gears: MA = Driven gear teeth √∑ Driver gear teeth

Pulleys & Belt Drives

Pulleys transfer power via belts. Diameter ratio determines speed and torque. Small pulley driving larger pulley reduces speed. Belt speed (feet/minute) = (diameter in inches √ó RPM √ó 3.14) √∑ 12. Proper tension is critical‚Äîtoo loose causes slipping and heat, too tight overloads bearings.

V-belts have tapered grooves and sit in matching pulleys, providing excellent grip. Flat belts grip by friction alone. Timing belts have teeth for precise power transmission without slipping, used in gates and machinery requiring synchronization.

Belt tension testing: Apply side force at belt midspan. Belt should deflect approximately 1/2" at 10 pounds force. Adjust by moving pulley positions or tightening idler pulley. Worn belts slip noticeably and should be replaced. New belts may be tight initially and settle after a few minutes of running.

Chain Drives

Chains provide positive (no-slip) power transmission over distance. Chain pitch (roller spacing) must match sprocket teeth. Sprocket size ratio determines gear reduction, same as gears: 15-tooth driving 45-tooth = 3:1 reduction.

Chain tension: Free sag at midspan should be 1/2" to 3/4" for horizontal runs. Too tight loads bearings and snaps chain; too loose causes jumping and noise. Tighten by adjusting sprocket position or using chain tensioner. Inspect for rust and wear‚Äîrusty chain requires cleaning and relubricating. Chain that won't fully seat on sprocket has stretched and must be replaced.

Lever Systems & Force Multiplication

Simple levers provide mechanical advantage for human-powered work. The lever ratio determines force multiplication.

Lever Mechanical Advantage
MA = Effort arm length √∑ Load arm length
Example: 4-foot effort arm √∑ 1-foot load arm = 4:1 advantage (lift 400 lbs with 100 lbs effort)

Class 1 levers (fulcrum in middle) change force and direction. Class 2 levers (load in middle) always advantage human (like wheelbarrow). Class 3 levers (effort in middle) always disadvantage human but provide speed multiplication.

Hydraulic Principles

Pascal's Law: Pressure applied to confined fluid transmits equally throughout. Hydraulic systems use this principle to multiply force. Small piston (small area) applying pressure creates large force on large piston (large area).

Hydraulic Force
F = P √ó A
Force (pounds) = Pressure (psi) √ó Area (square inches)

Example: 100 psi applied to 2 sq. in. pump piston produces 200 pounds force. If connected to 10 sq. in. cylinder piston, force is 1000 pounds. Hydraulic systems also provide torque multiplication and smooth power delivery. Maintenance requires clean fluid, proper seals, and leak-free connections. A single leak reduces system pressure and force output.

Welding & Metal Joining

Arc Welding Basics

Arc welding uses electrical arc between electrode and workpiece to create intense heat (7000¬∞F+) that melts metal, fusing pieces together. Shielding gas (or flux coating) protects molten metal from oxygen and nitrogen in air which would weaken the weld.

MIG Welding (Metal Inert Gas)

Semiautomatic process using continuous wire electrode and inert gas shield (argon/CO2 mixture). Easiest to learn. Wire feed and travel speed must be properly balanced. Too fast melts through; too slow creates cold joints. Suitable for thin to medium steel and aluminum. Creates smooth bead with minimal spatter when properly adjusted.

Stick Welding (SMAW - Shielded Metal Arc Welding)

Electrode rod coated with flux provides shielding. Heat-resistant flux melts, creating gas shield and slag coating. Works in wind and outdoors better than MIG. Slower than MIG and requires skill to maintain proper arc length and travel speed. Slag must be chipped off after cooling. Excellent for thick steel and field repairs.

TIG Welding (Tungsten Inert Gas)

Nonconsumable tungsten electrode with separate filler rod, using inert gas shield. Most precise and strongest welds. Difficult to learn‚Äîrequires coordinating electrode, filler rod, torch movement, and foot pedal simultaneously. Used for critical welds and thin materials.

Weld Quality Factors

Penetration: Weld must melt base metal, not just electrode. Undercut (groove in base metal) or incomplete fusion (electrode not melted into base) creates weak joints. Reinforcement: Weld bead should be slightly crowned; too flat may indicate incomplete penetration. Appearance: Consistent ripple pattern indicates good travel speed and technique. Porosity (small holes from gas), spatter (metal droplets), and cracks indicate process problems.

Oxy-Acetylene Cutting & Brazing

Oxy-Acetylene Cutting

Torch mixes oxygen and acetylene gases, creating 3000¬∞F flame. High-pressure oxygen jet oxidizes and blows away heated steel. Can cut through thick steel (1 foot+) quickly. Requires proper technique to maintain perpendicular torch and steady travel speed. Creates 1/4" kerf (width of cut). Releases intense heat and molten metal‚Äîrequires fireproof clothing and careful material placement.

Brazing

Joining process using brass or bronze filler metal between parts. Filler melts (1100-2000¬∞F) but base metal remains solid‚Äîcreating mechanical bond rather than fusion. Weaker than welding but works on dissimilar metals. No shielding gas needed. Good for jewelry, electronics, and emergency repairs. Heat must be applied evenly; filler should flow by capillary action. Insufficient heat leaves cold joint; excessive heat burns base metal or filler.

Soldering

Joining method using low-melting filler (below 840¬∞F melting point). Common solders are lead-free (tin/silver) or lead-containing for different applications. Electronics use fine rosin-core solder; plumbing uses silver solder or plumbing solder. Flux helps solder flow and prevents oxidation. Cold solder joint (insufficient heat or flux) creates unreliable connection prone to failure. Good solder joint is shiny and smooth.

Riveting

Rivets permanently join two or more pieces of sheet metal or thin material. Rivet is inserted through aligned holes. Head on one end is backed up while opposite end is hammered, upsetting metal and creating permanent lock. Blind rivets (pop rivets) use special tool to collapse rivet stem from outside‚Äîuseful when one side is inaccessible. Riveted joints can be as strong as welds if properly installed. No heat required.

Thread Repair

Stripped Threads in Holes

When threads are stripped and bolt spins without tightening, options include: (1) use next size larger bolt if hole can be drilled bigger, (2) use thread-locking compound (Loctite) which thickens fastener to create grip in stripped threads, (3) install threaded insert (helicoil) which provides new threads, or (4) fill hole with epoxy and redrill/tap new hole.

Stripped Bolt Threads

If bolt threads are stripped, replace with new bolt. If bolt cannot be removed (stuck), apply penetrating oil and allow to soak. Heat carefully with torch to expand metal and break corrosion. Turn gently; sudden force snaps the bolt. If snapped, use easy-out (reverse spiral drill) or drill out and use bolt of next size.

JB Weld & Epoxy Repairs

Two-part epoxy putty (JB Weld) bonds metal, plastic, and ceramic. Mix equal parts, knead until uniform color, then apply to damaged area. Conforms to shape, cures rock-hard. Works on cracks in metal, breaks in plastic, and holes. Not suitable for high-temperature or high-pressure applications. Cure time is 24 hours for full strength. For larger holes, undersized metal or plastic backing is often needed to contain epoxy until cured. Thin epoxy (liquid form) fills cracks better than putty but requires containment.

Alternative Energy Systems

Solar Panel Fundamentals

Solar photovoltaic (PV) panels convert sunlight directly to electrical current via semiconductor effect. A single solar cell produces approximately 0.5 volts under load; panels series-connect cells to produce 12V, 24V, or 48V nominal outputs. Panel output depends on sunlight intensity, temperature, and angle to sun.

Sizing a Solar Array

Calculate daily energy need in watt-hours. Example: 10 amps √ó 12 hours daily = 120 amp-hours √ó 12 volts = 1440 watt-hours needed. Account for inefficiencies (70% system efficiency) and weather variation (use 4-6 peak sun hours average). In above example: 1440 Wh √∑ 0.7 efficiency √∑ 5 peak hours = 410 watts array capacity minimum. Choose panels slightly larger than calculation to account for aging and seasonal variation.

Panel Angle Optimization

In Northern Hemisphere, panels should face south. Optimal tilt angle varies by latitude: approximately equal to latitude for year-round use (45¬∞ latitude = 45¬∞ tilt), or latitude + 15¬∞ in winter, latitude - 15¬∞ in summer for adjustable systems. Dust and snow reduce output‚Äîclean panels in spring and after major dust events. Annual output varies ¬±10-15% based on weather patterns.

Charge Controller Selection

PWM Controllers (Pulse Width Modulation)

Simple, inexpensive controllers that switch charging on/off rapidly to match battery voltage. Solar panels must match or slightly exceed battery voltage. Example: 12V panels for 12V battery. PWM efficiency is 70-80%. Good for small systems and budget installations.

MPPT Controllers (Maximum Power Point Tracking)

Advanced controllers that track solar panel peak power point and convert voltage via buck-boost converter. Allow higher voltage solar panels to charge lower voltage batteries. Example: 60V panels charging 24V battery system. MPPT efficiency is 95%+. Cost more but deliver 20-30% more power in real-world conditions, especially in partial shade or cold weather. Ideal for large systems where extra 25% output justifies higher cost.

Controller Features

Protection features include: temperature compensation (adjusts charge voltage for ambient temp), low-voltage disconnect (prevents over-discharging battery), high-voltage disconnect (prevents overcharging), and load control (can power DC loads directly). Display shows real-time voltage, current, and power. Data logging tracks daily/monthly production. Proper controller prevents battery damage and extends system lifespan.

Wind Power Fundamentals

Wind turbines capture kinetic energy and convert to electrical power. Power output depends on wind speed cubed: doubling wind speed increases power 8√ó. Minimum viable wind speed is approximately 10 mph continuous (9-13 mph varies by turbine design). Tower height is critical‚Äîwind speed increases with height; 30-foot tower is minimum for reliable generation.

Micro-turbine sizing: Small 1-2 kW turbines serve households in consistently windy locations. Output varies daily and seasonally‚Äîaverage output (usually 25-35% of rated capacity) matters more than peak rating. Wind systems work well paired with solar‚Äîwind often better in winter when solar is weakest.

Micro-Hydro Power

Flowing water's gravitational potential energy drives generator. Requirements: elevation drop (head), water flow rate, and reliable source. Power calculation: Power (watts) = flow rate (gpm) √ó head (feet) √∑ 11.8 √ó efficiency (typically 60-75%). Example: 50 gpm drop at 100 feet = 50 √ó 100 √∑ 11.8 √ó 0.65 = 275 watts available.

Micro-hydro is among most reliable renewable sources‚Äîprovides consistent daily power if water supply is steady. Seasonal flow changes affect output. Systems use Pelton wheels or Turgo runners for small high-head applications, or cross-flow turbines for larger lower-head applications. Intake must be screened, penstock (pipe) sized for minimal friction loss, and discharge designed to prevent erosion.

Battery Types for Storage

Lead-Acid Batteries

Flooded lead-acid: Most economical. Open vents require maintenance‚Äîadd distilled water as needed. Hydrogen gas from charging requires ventilation. Deep-cycle rated for repeated discharge, not starting batteries. Typical lifespan 5-10 years. Cost approximately $0.50-1.00 per watt-hour.

AGM (Absorbent Glass Mat): Maintenance-free, sealed, no venting required. Can be installed in any orientation. Accepts higher charge rates. Cost slightly more. Lifespan similar to flooded.

Gel batteries: Thick gel electrolyte resists vibration and freezing better. More expensive. Require careful charge voltage control or premature failure.

Lithium Batteries

Lithium-iron-phosphate (LiFePO4) offer high energy density, no maintenance, longer lifespan (10+ years, 3000+ cycles), and 85%+ depth of discharge usable. Cost is 3-5√ó lead-acid but price declining. No thermal runaway risk like lithium-ion. Better for new installations if budget allows. Management electronics required (BMS - battery management system).

Deep-Cycle vs Starting Batteries

Deep-cycle designed for repeated discharge to 80% capacity. Thick plates tolerate cycling. Starting batteries designed to deliver high current briefly then recharge fully. Thin plates damaged by deep cycling. Never substitute starting battery for deep-cycle storage‚Äîshort lifespan results.

System Monitoring

Multimeter: Check voltage and current at key points. Battery voltage indicates state of charge (12V = depleted, 14.4V = full for lead-acid). Solar input current shows if panels are producing. Load current shows consumption.

Shunt ammeter: Permanently installed device measures battery current (charging or discharging) and calculates amp-hours. Tracks energy flow. Important for identifying loads exceeding sustainable production.

Kill-a-watt meter: Portable device measures AC power, showing watts and energy used. Identify heavy loads draining battery unnecessarily. Replace or manage high-draw devices.

Monitor battery temperature, voltage, and charge/discharge rate. Morning voltage should be 2-3 volts higher than evening (proper night recovery). If not charging adequately, check solar output, controller settings, and wiring. If battery draining overnight, find phantom loads.

Tool Maintenance & Sharpening

Edge Tool Sharpening (Axes, Chisels, Planes)

Understanding Edge Geometry

Sharp edges have included angle (angle between surfaces) typically 20-35 degrees. Thinner angle (20¬∞) is sharper but dulls faster. Thicker angle (35¬∞) is more durable. Curved profile (convex) is stronger than flat ground edge. A properly sharpened tool requires minimal pressure and produces clean shavings.

Axe Sharpening

Use medium-coarse whetstone or file angled at 20-30 degrees. Stroke from spine (back) toward edge, lifting at end of stroke. Repeat on opposite side. Count strokes to maintain symmetry. Never sharpen on a grinder‚Äîexcessive heat softens metal, removing hardness. Finish with fine stone or leather strop. Convex edge (gently curved) is best for axes. Never allow edges to touch during storage.

Chisel & Plane Sharpening

Flatten back on coarse stone (do NOT assume flat). Remove mill marks. Angle stone at 25 degrees and sharpen bevel with figure-8 motion. Refine on medium stone, then fine stone. Test sharpness by shaving arm hair or cutting paper‚Äîshould not require pressure. A dull plane creates tearout and requires excessive force. Maintain straightness by checking back on flat stone regularly‚Äîhollowed backs make impossible to sharpen.

Saw Sharpening & Setting

Hand Saw Sharpening

Handsaw teeth have three angles: cutting angle (bevel), set (alternating side-to-side bend), and rake (forward lean). Dull teeth require excessive pressure. File each tooth at consistent angle, typically 60 degrees. File from gullet (valley) toward point. Count file strokes to maintain uniformity. File straight across for crosscut saws, angled for ripsaws. Every 5-10 sharpenings, re-set teeth (bend slightly outward) to create kerf wider than blade, preventing binding.

Circular & Band Saw Blade Maintenance

Circular blades cannot be resharpened‚Äîreplacement is standard. Band saws can be sharpened but require skill and special equipment. Tension must be correct (usually 20,000-30,000 psi depending on blade)‚Äîcheck with tension gauge. Too loose causes tracking problems and blade jump; too tight causes rapid dulling and breakage. Keep blade guards in place‚Äîdull blades cause kickback.

Drill Bit Sharpening

Drill bits dull from use, requiring increasing pressure. Professional sharpening services are available but expensive. Basic resharpening: hold bit in vise, file back flank (relief) behind cutting edge at shallow angle (~10 degrees). Maintain point centering by filing symmetrically. File only the relief, not the cutting edge directly. For emergency field repairs, a light touch on grinder can extend life temporarily.

File Maintenance

Files become clogged with material (especially aluminum), reducing cutting action. Clean with wire brush across teeth. For persistent buildup, use chalk on file teeth before use‚Äîchalk fills teeth and prevents sticking. A clogged file cuts poorly and should be cleaned before discarding. Very fine files can be submerged in vinegar briefly to remove rust. Never strike file‚Äîfiles are hardened and brittle; impact causes chipping.

Tool Handle Replacement

Axe Handle Replacement

Remove old handle by cutting through eye (hole in head) with hacksaw, then splitting with chisel. Drill out remnants. Select replacement handle matched to head weight. File handle slightly to fit tight. Tap head up handle with hammer until seated. Drill retaining hole and install steel wedge, driving until tight. Wrap with cordage or hose clamp to prevent splitting from vibration. Allow wood to season 2-3 weeks before full-power use.

Hammer Handle Replacement

Remove old handle by drilling out retaining pin or cutting handle near head. Press off head in vise carefully. Size new handle to fit eye. Install in eye and install retaining pin through hole. Trim excess handle flush. For old sledges, wrap handle with rawhide or rope to prevent splintering and provide grip.

Proper Tool Storage

Keep cutting tools edge-up in racks or wooden blocks‚Äînever edge-down on metal surface. Moisture causes rust; store in dry location with desiccant if necessary. Oil blades lightly for long-term storage. Hang power tools on wall racks or store in cases with desiccant. Batteries should be removed from cordless tools for storage longer than 1 month. Sharpen tools before storing for next season rather than storing dull tools.

Vehicle Repair Basics

Tire Changing & Patching

Tire Change Procedure

  1. Position vehicle on flat surface, engage parking brake, turn on hazard lights
  2. Remove wheel cover if equipped
  3. Using lug wrench, loosen (but don't remove) all lug nuts while tire is on ground
  4. Place jack under frame near wheel, raise until tire barely clears ground
  5. Remove lug nuts completely, set aside in wheel cover or container
  6. Grip tire and pull straight toward you, then rest against body for stability
  7. Align holes on replacement tire with studs, push onto studs until flush
  8. Replace lug nuts by hand first, then tighten with wrench in star pattern (opposite lugs)
  9. Lower vehicle completely to ground
  10. Using calibrated wrench, tighten all lug nuts to specified torque (typically 80-100 ft-lbs)
Keep wheels balanced by rotating every 6,000-8,000 miles: front-left to rear-right, front-right to rear-left, move rears to front.

Tire Repair

Small punctures (less than 1/4") in tread can be patched. For temporary field repair, use tire plug kit: locate puncture, clean hole, insert rubber plug with tool, trim excess. Holds 50+ mph indefinitely if done properly. For permanent repair, professional vulcanizing patch is superior.

Sidewall punctures cannot be reliably patched and require replacement. Bulges, cracks, or tread wear below 4/32" depth require replacement. Check age even if tread is good‚Äîtires older than 10 years should be replaced.

Brake Pad Replacement

Brake pads wear gradually, reducing friction. When brake pedal feels soft or requires harder pressure, pads may be worn. Inspect: remove wheel, look at pad thickness. Minimum thickness is typically 3mm (1/8"). Pads nearly touching rotor (metal disc) or grinding sound indicates urgent replacement.

Procedure: Remove wheel. Remove caliper bolts (usually two). Slide caliper aside. Press out old pads. Install new pads, ensuring proper orientation (friction material faces rotor). Reinstall caliper. Pump brake pedal several times to seat pads. Bleed air from brake system if pedal is soft. Front pads wear first; rear pads last much longer.

Battery & Charging System Diagnosis

Battery Testing

Voltmeter test: With engine off, 12V battery should read 12.6 volts. Below 12.4V indicates discharged state. Under 11.5V indicates failed cell. After charging, should reach 13.2V. After 5 minutes running, should drop to 13.5-14.5V (alternator regulating). Remains at 12.0V while running indicates bad alternator.

Cold cranking amps (CCA) rating indicates cold-start ability. 600-800 CCA typical for cars, higher for trucks. Discharged battery cannot provide adequate CCA for starting.

Alternator Diagnosis

With engine running, alternator should maintain 13.5-14.5V at battery. If voltage stays at 12-13V, alternator is failing. If jumps to 15V+, regulator is bad. Test alternator output with ammeter: should produce 30-100 amps depending on design. Listen for mechanical noise (failing bearing) or smell of burning (failed diode). Alternator output decreases with age and heat.

Radiator & Cooling System

Leak Detection & Repair

Small pinhole leaks in radiator can be temporarily sealed with Stop Leak additive (temporary only). Visible leak requires repair. Drain coolant into container. Identify leak location. For small holes, epoxy putty works temporarily. For larger damage, radiator must be replaced or professionally repaired.

Never work on hot radiator‚Äîallow engine to cool 30+ minutes. Pressure cap can explode from steam if opened hot. Refill with proper coolant type (green, orange, pink, or blue‚Äînever mix types). Add distilled water if coolant unavailable in emergency (but coolant is preferred for freeze/boil protection).

Thermostat Diagnosis

Engine temperature gauge climbs slowly above normal (especially during idle), or overshoots then drops (surging), may indicate stuck thermostat. Feel upper radiator hose‚Äîshould be hot when engine is warm. If cool, thermostat is stuck closed, blocking coolant flow. Replacement requires draining coolant, removing housing, and installing new thermostat with fresh gasket.

Belt Replacement

Serpentine belt drives alternator, water pump, power steering, and air conditioning. Cracking, fraying, or oil-soaked appearance indicates replacement needed. Squealing during acceleration may indicate worn belt slipping on pulleys.

Replacement: Note routing diagram or take photo before removal. Loosen idler pulley bolt, move pulley to reduce tension, remove old belt. Install new belt, ensuring proper routing. Tension should be approximately 1/2" deflection with hand pressure at midspan. Over-tension damages bearings; under-tension causes slipping and heat.

Oil & Filter Change

Consult manual for correct oil type and capacity. Warm engine (5 minutes running), then stop and wait 5 minutes for oil to drain. Locate oil drain plug (usually on oil pan). Drain into container. Remove old filter with filter wrench (filter only needs 3/4 turn‚Äîhand-tight no more). Coat new filter rubber seal with fresh oil, screw on by hand until seal touches, then turn 1/2 additional turn. Add correct oil amount. Run engine 30 seconds, then check level on dipstick and adjust if needed.

Fuel System Basics

Fuel pump feeds fuel through filter to carburetor or fuel injectors. Listen for fuel pump buzz when key is turned to ON (before starting). If no buzz, pump may be failed. Check fuel pressure with pressure gauge at Schrader valve‚Äîtypical pressure is 3-7 psi for carburetor, 35-60 psi for injectors.

Fuel filter (replaceable element) should be replaced every 15,000-30,000 miles. Clogged filter starves engine and reduces power. Smell of fuel in cabin or fuel smell under vehicle indicates leak‚Äîlocate and repair before driving further.

Jump Starting Procedure

  1. Position donor vehicle next to dead battery (parallel, 18 inches apart)
  2. Turn off both vehicles and all electrical loads
  3. Attach positive (red) clamp to dead battery positive terminal
  4. Attach other positive clamp to donor battery positive
  5. Attach negative (black) clamp to donor battery negative
  6. Attach final negative clamp to unpainted metal on dead vehicle engine (NOT battery negative‚Äîavoids sparks)
  7. Start donor vehicle, let run 2-3 minutes at 1500 rpm
  8. Start dead vehicle
  9. Once running, remove clamps in reverse order
  10. Keep jumping vehicle running for 20+ minutes to recharge battery
Never jump-start with frozen battery or battery showing leaks. Never smoke or create sparks near battery (explosive hydrogen gas). Wear eye protection in case of acid splash.

Troubleshooting Methodology

Systematic Diagnosis Approach

A structured approach to problem-solving saves time and prevents wasting effort on random guesses. Follow this logical process:

1. Define the Problem Precisely

What exactly is not working? Is it intermittent or constant? What were recent changes? Did it fail suddenly or gradually? Gather specific observations rather than vague descriptions. Example: "Motor won't start" is vague. "Motor cranks slowly, dim dashboard lights, clicking at starter" is specific and diagnostic.

2. Gather Information

Inspect visually for obvious problems: loose wires, corroded connections, leaks, damage. Smell for burning (electrical) or fuel odors. Listen for unusual sounds (grinding, knocking, buzzing). Feel temperature (hot is not normal unless expected). Review any warning lights or error codes. Ask when problem started relative to maintenance or weather changes.

3. Form Hypotheses

Based on symptoms and information, list possible causes in order of probability. Most failures come from simple causes (dead battery, clogged filter, loose connection) far more often than complex failures. Prioritize easy-to-check hypotheses before time-consuming ones.

4. Test Systematically

Test highest-probability causes first. Eliminate possible causes through testing, narrowing scope. If test is inconclusive, do not move on‚Äîverify result or test differently. Document test results. This prevents later confusion about what was actually checked.

5. Implement Solution

Once cause is identified, implement most appropriate repair. For intermittent problems, monitor closely after repair to verify solution. For safety systems, verify repair does not create new hazards.

6. Verify & Document

Test repaired system under normal operating conditions. Verify improvement and absence of new problems. Document repair method for future reference or warranty purposes.

Using a Multimeter

Voltage Measurement

Set selector dial to appropriate voltage range (DC for batteries/circuit, AC for wall outlets). Touch black probe to ground/negative, red probe to test point. Reading shows potential difference. Measuring across battery: positive to positive terminal, black to negative terminal. Measuring across component: probes on either side of component. Voltage drop across functioning component indicates current flow through load.

Current Measurement

Multimeter in current mode acts as short circuit‚Äîmust be placed IN SERIES with load, not across it. Break wire, insert meter to measure current flow. Always start on highest range to prevent meter damage from overcurrent. Current draw indicates component is receiving power and working.

Resistance Measurement

Set dial to ohms (Œ©). DISCONNECT POWER before measuring resistance. Measuring resistance with power on damages meter. Probes across component show resistance. Zero ohms indicates short circuit (wires touching). Infinite ohms (shows OL) indicates open circuit (break in wire). Resistance between expected values indicates component is functioning. Unexpected resistance suggests problem.

Continuity Testing

Some multimeters have continuity mode (usually indicated by diode symbol). Beeps when resistance is very low (near zero). Useful for quickly testing whether wire is broken, switch is stuck open, or connection is complete. Continuity tests closed switch (conducts) as "good" and open switch as "bad."

Reading Wiring Diagrams

Wiring diagrams use standardized symbols to show electrical connections. Learn common symbols: battery (long and short parallel lines), light (circle with X), switch (circle with break line), resistor (zigzag), wire (solid line), wire splice (dot at intersection), ground (three horizontal lines), fuse (circle with line through it).

Circuit tracing: Follow line from positive terminal through all components back to negative. Understand series vs parallel connections. Identify where voltage might be present or absent depending on switch positions. Use diagram to predict what multimeter should read at various points if circuit is functioning properly.

Common Failure Patterns

Complete system failure Power supply (battery/generator dead), main fuse blown, emergency shutoff activated Check fuse, battery voltage, kill switch position
Intermittent operation Loose connection, corroded connector, marginal component, temperature-dependent issue Wiggle connectors, measure resistance at connections, monitor under load
Reduced power/speed Low voltage, high resistance in circuit, clogged filter, mechanical friction Check voltage throughout circuit, measure current, inspect filter, test load

Improvised Repair Techniques

When Proper Parts Are Unavailable

Wire substitution: Use whatever gauge is available if proper size is out. Thinner wire overheats; thicker wire works but wastes weight. Minimize distance to reduce voltage loss.

Fuse replacement: A blown fuse indicates circuit problem, not normal wear. Find and fix cause before replacing. If proper fuse is unavailable, next smaller amperage provides temporary protection (slightly reduced function). Never exceed fuse rating or fire risk increases. Larger amperage defeats protection.

Connector repair: If connector is broken, splice wire with soldering or crimping. Solder joint is permanent; crimp allows future disconnection. Ensure insulation is complete and weatherproof.

Gasket substitution: Rubber gaskets fail from age/weather. Improvised gasket from rubber sheet, gasket cord, or even duct tape (temporary only) maintains seal. Ensure surfaces are flat and clean. New gasket material should be available at hardware/farm supply stores in emergency prep.

Maintenance Prevention Approach

Regular maintenance prevents most failures. Keep maintenance log documenting: oil changes, filter replacements, fluid levels, belt/hose inspections, battery testing, and service intervals. Replace consumables before failure: belts at 50,000 miles, spark plugs at recommended interval, air filters annually, fuel filters every 15,000-30,000 miles.

Seasonal preparation: winterize before freezing (heaters, coolant concentration). Before critical season (harvest, winter), fully service equipment. Inspect for cracks, corrosion, loose fasteners. Budget for parts replacement before they fail catastrophically.

Keep spare parts on hand for critical systems: extra spark plugs, fuses, belts, oil/filters, brake pads. These cost $20-50 but save hours of downtime when needed. Store in cool, dry location, protected from moisture and UV.
