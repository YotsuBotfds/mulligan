Latrine Construction

Proper human waste management prevents contamination of water sources and reduces disease transmission. Critical in any sustained survival or emergency situation.

Latrine Site Selection

  • Minimum 50 feet downslope from water sources (streams, wells, springs)
  • Minimum 50 feet downslope from camp areas
  • In areas where water naturally flows away from camp
  • Not in flood-prone areas (flooding spreads pathogens)
  • Not in areas with high groundwater table (contaminates water)
  • Multiple latrines if camp has 10+ people (reduces disease spread)

Cat Hole Method (Small Groups)

  1. Dig hole 6-12 inches deep, 100+ feet from water or camp
  2. Use for single bowel movement
  3. Cover completely with soil after use
  4. Reuse hole until multiple visits, then create new hole
  5. Suitable for short-term camps (less than 1 week)

Pit Latrine (Extended Camps)

  1. Dig pit 3-4 feet deep, minimum 6x6 feet if multiple users expected
  2. Deeper pits last longer (accommodate more use)
  3. Surround with screen/fence for privacy and to prevent animals from using
  4. Build superstructure: frame with seat hole (toilet seat height)
  5. Cover when not in use - prevents insect breeding, contains smell
  6. When full (2 feet of space left), close pit and create new one

Latrine Superstructure

Construct simple seat using wood or plastic over hole. Four posts support frame at toilet seat height (45cm). Seat hole 8-10 inches diameter. Build privacy structure (walls) around seating area. Roof protects from rain, provides shade.

Handwashing Station

Place near latrine. Simple container with water and cloth/leaves for drying. Users wash hands after latrine use (critical for disease prevention). Clean water bucket changed daily. Soap if available, but water alone adequate if used with vigorous scrubbing.

Closing Pit Latrines

When pit full or camp relocating, close latrine properly. Cover with soil layer (1+ feet), then leaves and branches on top. Mark clearly (don't want future accidents). Can be used again after several months of decomposition if needed.

Portable Toilet Systems

Bucket toilet with detachable liner: uses large plastic bucket with tight-fitting seat. Line with plastic bag (doubled for strength). Waste deposited in bag, sealed, and buried away from water sources. Reusable system useful for extended camps.

Waste Management

All waste properly managed prevents disease and environmental contamination.

Organic Waste (Food Scraps)

Proper disposal prevents attracting animals and spreading disease. Bury food waste minimum 100 feet from camp and water sources. Bury 1+ foot deep (deep enough that animals cannot dig up). Meat scraps buried deeper (attract more animals). Grains and vegetables safer to dispose of in open compost.

Ash Disposal

Fire ash is generally safe. Can be scattered around latrine (provides odor reduction). Small amounts can be used as fertilizer (contains minerals). Large amounts collected and buried if space available. Never ash around water sources (affects pH).

Animal Waste

If animals in camp (pack animals, guard dogs), collect waste in designated area away from camp and water. Bury similar to human waste. More frequent burial if high volume. Regular removal prevents disease spread.

Wastewater (Greywater)

Water used for cooking, washing, cleaning contains pathogens and organic matter. Strain out large particles, then dispose far from water sources. Can be spread over ground away from camp (biological organisms in soil help decompose). Never pour directly into water sources.

Trash Management

Minimize trash production (no packaging materials in wilderness). Burn paper and cardboard when safe. Bury non-combustible items (metal, glass, plastic). Never leave trash scattered - attracts animals, causes injury, creates environmental damage.

Water Treatment for Sanitation

Clean water is foundation of health in survival situations. Multiple treatment methods work together.

Water Source Hierarchy

  1. Rain collected from clean surfaces (least treatment needed)
  2. Springs with fast-flowing water (moderate treatment)
  3. Wells and deep groundwater (moderate treatment, but usually good quality)
  4. Streams and rivers (heavy treatment required)
  5. Stagnant water sources (heavy treatment, less desirable)

Pre-Filtration

Remove sediment and large particles before main treatment. Strain through cloth into second container. Repeat if water very cloudy. Pre-filtration reduces load on main treatment system, increases effectiveness.

Boiling

Rolling boil 1 minute (3 minutes at high elevation). Kills all pathogens. Does not remove sediment or chemicals. Most reliable method. Fuel cost only limitation.

Chemical Treatment

Iodine tablets or chlorine dioxide tablets. Follow package instructions. Wait required contact time (varies by water temperature). Good for emergencies, travel. Less suitable for large-scale water treatment.

Filtration Maintenance

Bio-sand filters require 1-2 weeks to establish biofilm. Initial filtration less effective. Continue boiling or chemical treatment until confident in filter quality. Filters never disinfect - must combine with boiling or chemicals.

Storage of Treated Water

Store in clean, sealed containers. Prevent contamination from insects or animals. Treated water remains safe weeks to months in cool, dark, sealed storage. Cover containers tightly. Change water if tastes bad or appears cloudy.

Personal Hygiene

Individual hygiene habits prevent disease transmission to others and reduce personal infection risk.

Handwashing

Most important hygiene practice. Wash hands: after latrine use, before eating, before food preparation, after handling animals. Vigorous washing with water for 20+ seconds removes pathogens. Soap if available (cuts through oils, increases pathogen removal). Cold water adequate - temperature less important than mechanical scrubbing.

Body Washing

Regular body washing removes pathogens, dead skin cells, and prevents infection. Full body wash every 2-3 days if water available. Daily washing of face, hands, and genital area maintains basic hygiene. Can use clothing to scrub or rough cloth for friction.

Dental Hygiene

Prevents infections, decay, tooth loss. Chew on small twigs (frayed end acts as toothbrush). Plant based mouthwashes: salt water, herbal infusions (peppermint). Flossing prevents infection between teeth (use plant fibers or improvised string). Avoid sugary foods that promote decay.

Wound Care

Clean wounds with boiled water. Remove debris with cloth. Apply pressure if bleeding. Keep wounds clean and dry. Cover if in dirty environment. Check daily for signs of infection (redness, swelling, pus, heat). Treat infection early with herbal poultices or boiling water cleansing.

Menstrual Hygiene

Challenges in survival situations. Cloth wrappings can be washed and reused (boil to sterilize). Improvisations from clean cloth. Change materials regularly. Dispose of used materials by burying in designated waste area. Blood attracts animals - double-bury materials and protect camp perimeter.

Clothing Maintenance

Clean clothes reduce pathogen load. Wash clothes regularly in clean water. Sun exposure kills many pathogens (air dry on ground or hang in sun). Rotate clothing so each piece dries between wearing. Avoid excessively sweaty conditions when possible (increases skin bacteria).

Disease Prevention

Preventing illness is easier than treating disease. Proper sanitation prevents majority of illnesses in survival situations.

Contaminated Water Diseases

Diseases spread by fecal contamination: cholera, typhoid fever, dysentery (bacterial and amoebic), hepatitis A, giardiasis, cryptosporidiosis. All prevented by proper water treatment (boiling or chemicals) and handwashing. Symptoms: severe diarrhea, dehydration, abdominal pain. Treatment: rehydration, electrolyte replacement if severe. Can be fatal if untreated.

Diarrheal Disease Management

Maintain hydration: drink small frequent amounts of clean water. Avoid dairy and high-fat foods. Eat bland foods if tolerating: boiled grains, broths, salt. Rest. Most infections self-limit in 3-5 days. Seek help if severe (bloody diarrhea, fever above 39°C, signs of dehydration).

Skin Infections

Prevented by keeping skin clean and dry. Scrubbing removes pathogens. Boil any contaminated cloth. Treat cuts/scrapes promptly. Avoid swimming in dirty water. If infection develops (red, swollen, warm, pus): clean frequently, apply warm compresses, use herbal poultices. Watch for spreading infection.

Respiratory Infections

Less directly related to sanitation but spread by coughing/sneezing. Prevent by: spacing people apart when sick, covering mouth when coughing, not sharing eating/drinking utensils. Treatment: rest, fluids, warmth. Herbal teas (ginger, chamomile) provide comfort.

Parasite Prevention

Soil-transmitted parasites from walking barefoot or contaminated food. Prevention: wear shoes, don't defecate in areas near camp, boil water, cook food thoroughly. Treatment difficult in survival - prevention critical.

Camp Sanitation Protocols

Group living requires coordinated sanitation practices to prevent disease outbreaks.

Zone Establishment

  • Living zone: sleeping, cooking, eating areas (upwind from latrines and waste)
  • Water zone: water collection and treatment areas (upwind from latrines)
  • Sanitation zone: latrine and waste disposal area (downwind, downslope)
  • Work zone: equipment repair, resource processing (separate from living)

Water Storage and Handling

Separate drinking water from washing water. Drinking water in clean, covered containers. Washing water can be reused for multiple tasks. Greywater (from washing) disposed away from living area. All water handling performed by designated person (reduces contamination risk).

Food Preparation Safety

  1. Prepare food in designated area (separate from sanitation zone)
  2. Cook all meats thoroughly (kills parasites)
  3. Boil water used in food preparation
  4. Clean utensils and cookware after each use
  5. Store food in containers protecting from animals and insects
  6. Discard suspicious food (unknown origin, unusual smell)

Pest Control

Flies and mosquitoes spread disease. Minimize standing water (breeding grounds). Cover food and water containers. Keep garbage buried and decomposing far from living areas. Smoke from fire deters insects. Mosquito netting on sleeping areas if materials available.

Daily Sanitation Routine

  • Morning: water collection and treatment
  • Before meals: hand washing station availability check
  • After meals: immediate cleaning of utensils and cookware
  • Evening: latrine/waste area inspection and closure
  • Night: verifying all food stored and protected

Sick Individual Isolation

If individuals show symptoms of contagious disease: isolate from main group if possible. Separate eating/sleeping areas. Dedicated handwashing before contact with others. Quarantine until symptoms resolve plus 2-3 additional days.

Handwashing and Personal Sanitation Stations

Proper handwashing infrastructure supports sustained good hygiene practices.

Handwashing Station Setup

Construction

Simple design: bucket elevated on frame at comfortable height. Bucket has small spout or hole for water to flow. Basin or trough below catches runoff (prevent swampy ground). Soap if available, otherwise use friction and running water. Cloth for drying (air dry preferred to shared cloth).

Water Management

Change bucket water daily. Use basin water for multiple hand washings before changing. Minimize water use in scarce water situations (still use but careful measurements). Soap enhances cleanliness but friction alone adequate with warm water and vigorous scrubbing.

Soap Alternatives

Wood ash mixed with water creates alkaline solution similar to soap. Plant-based soaps from crushed soapwort or similar plants. Regular soap if available. Even without soap, vigorous water washing removes most pathogens through mechanical action.

Handwashing Technique

  1. Wet hands thoroughly
  2. Apply soap (if available)
  3. Scrub all surfaces: palms, backs, between fingers, under nails (30 seconds)
  4. Rinse thoroughly with running water
  5. Dry on clean cloth or air dry

Pay extra attention to under-nail area where pathogens accumulate. Scrub nails against palm (helps dislodge bacteria).
