1. IPM Philosophy: The Prevention-First Approach

Integrated Pest Management (IPM) is a holistic, science-based approach that prioritizes prevention and minimal intervention over pesticides. Rather than declaring war on all insects, IPM seeks to maintain pest populations below economically damaging levels while protecting human health and the environment.

The IPM Hierarchy

IPM Core Principles

  1. Prevention First: The most effective and economical pest management strategy is preventing pests from becoming problems. Use resistant plant varieties, maintain crop rotation, improve soil health, and remove pest habitat.
  2. Regular Monitoring: Scout gardens and storage areas weekly. Early detection of pest problems allows for minimal intervention and lower treatment costs.
  3. Establish Action Thresholds: Don't treat at the first sign of pests. Determine acceptable pest population levels for your crop or situation before deciding to intervene.
  4. Intervene Minimally: Start with the least toxic, most targeted approach. Hand-pick pests, use physical barriers, encourage beneficial insects before considering any pesticide.
  5. Escalate Only as Needed: If low-impact methods aren't effective, move to biological controls, then physical controls, then targeted organic pesticides as a final resort.
  6. Record Everything: Keep detailed notes on pest problems, treatments applied, and results. This data helps you make better decisions in future seasons.

The Economic Threshold

Not all pests require treatment. The economic threshold is the pest population level at which damage costs exceed treatment costs. For example, a few aphids on your tomato plant may cause minimal damage and cost more to treat than the lost yield. Understanding this threshold prevents unnecessary pesticide applications.

Garden pests range from tiny soft-bodied insects to large beetles. Identification is crucial for effective control. Below is a comprehensive guide to common garden pests with damage signs and organic controls.

Hand-Picking Protocol

Hand-picking is one of the most effective and immediate controls for larger pests. Scout plants early in morning or evening when pests are most active. Wear gloves and collect pests in a bucket of soapy water. For leaf-eating larvae, look on the underside of leaves near damaged areas. For beetles, check under leaves and along stems. Hand-picking works best when pest populations are low‚Äîuse this as an early intervention strategy.

Timing of Monitoring

Scout your garden weekly during the growing season. Early morning (6-8 AM) and evening (6-8 PM) are best for finding most pests active. Keep a simple log: date, plant, pest observed, damage level (none/light/moderate/heavy), and action taken. This record helps you predict problem timing in future seasons.

3. Stored Grain Protection: Preserving Your Harvest

Stored grain and seeds are vulnerable to insects, mold, and rodents. Proper storage is the foundation of grain protection. The goal is to create an environment where pests cannot survive or reproduce.

Storage Container Requirements

  • Food-Grade Containers: Use food-grade plastic buckets with tight-fitting lids, glass jars with seals, or metal cans. Containers must be truly airtight to prevent pest entry and moisture infiltration.
  • Size Considerations: Smaller containers allow you to rotate stock more efficiently. A 5-gallon bucket holds approximately 35-40 pounds of grain.
  • Location: Store in a cool, dry place away from moisture and direct sunlight. Basements, root cellars, or climate-controlled areas are ideal. Temperature below 50¬∞F significantly slows insect reproduction.

Organic Preservation Methods

Diatomaceous Earth (Food-Grade)

Food-grade diatomaceous earth (DE) is the powder of fossilized diatoms with microscopic sharp edges that damage insect exoskeletons without toxicity to humans. Mix thoroughly with grain at a rate of 1 tablespoon per 5 gallons (or 1% by weight). DE works by dehydration and is effective against nearly all grain storage pests. It remains effective indefinitely if the grain stays dry. Never use pool-grade DE‚Äîit's toxic.

Bay Leaves

Bay leaves contain volatile oils that repel grain storage pests, particularly flour beetles and weevils. Place 5-10 fresh bay leaves per 5-gallon bucket. The effect is repellent rather than lethal, so combine with other methods. Replace bay leaves every 6 months as potency fades.

Freezing

Freezing grain for 48 hours at -4¬∞F or below kills all life stages of grain storage insects. If you have access to a commercial freezer or deep winter temperatures, freeze grain in sealed bags before long-term storage. Thaw slowly to prevent moisture condensation. This method works well for smaller quantities.

Food-Grade Nitrogen Flush

Nitrogen gas displaces oxygen, killing any insects present and preventing new infestations as long as the container remains sealed. Commercial grain storage operations use this method. For small-scale use, purchase nitrogen in spray cans designed for food storage. Follow product instructions carefully. This is the most foolproof method if you can source nitrogen safely.

Storage Rotation and Inspection Schedule

FIFO Principle: Implement First-In-First-Out storage. Use oldest stored grain first, adding new harvest on top or in new containers. This naturally rotates stock and prevents long-term storage of older grain.

Time Period Inspection Action What to Check
At Storage Thorough inspection Moisture content (should be 12-14%), insects, mold, temperature
Monthly Visual inspection Container seals, any signs of pest entry, moisture condensation
Quarterly Sample check Open container, look for insects, smell for mustiness, check moisture
Seasonally Full assessment Review all containers, check temperature/humidity, assess for any spoilage
Before Use Final check Inspect grain appearance, smell, taste small amount to ensure quality

Signs of Grain Spoilage

  • Musty, moldy smell or sour odor
  • Visible mold (white, green, or black fuzzy growth)
  • Grain clumping or moisture
  • Insects visible in grain or containers
  • Webbing inside container (sign of moth infestation)
  • Discoloration or grain darkening
  • Heat generation in grain mass (sign of mold or fermentation)

Health Alert: Mycotoxins

Moldy grain can develop mycotoxins, especially aflatoxins produced by Aspergillus molds. These are carcinogenic and can cause serious health issues. Never consume moldy grain. Discard any grain showing mold growth. Prevent this through proper moisture control and airtight storage.

4. Rodent Control: Effective and Safe Methods

Rodents (primarily mice and rats) damage stored food, gnaw on structures and wiring, and carry serious diseases. A comprehensive rodent control strategy combines physical exclusion, removal, and continuous monitoring.

Rats vs. Mice: Identification Differences

Characteristic Mice Rats
Body Length 3-4 inches (head to rump) 7-9 inches (head to rump)
Tail Thin, as long as body, hairless Thick, shorter than body, scaly
Ears Large relative to head Small relative to head
Droppings Rice-grain sized (3-8mm), pointed ends Larger (10-18mm), blunt ends, darker

Gnaw Marks Fine scratches and gnaw marks Large, splintered gnaw marks

Physical Traps: The Most Effective Control

Snap Traps

Traditional snap traps are highly effective and kill rodents instantly. Set them along walls where rodents travel, or near food sources. Bait with peanut butter, chocolate, or dried fruit. Check traps daily. Wear gloves when handling dead rodents. Dispose of dead rodents in sealed bags in the trash or bury deep (at least 3 feet) away from gardens. Snap traps cost pennies and outperform most other methods.

Bucket Traps (DIY)

A simple, effective trap can be made with a 5-gallon bucket. Create a ramp leading to the bucket lip using a stick or wooden board. Place peanut butter or grain on the bucket edge. Rodents attempting to reach the bait fall into the bucket. Add a few inches of water to the bottom for a quick death, or keep dry and dispose of the rodent away from your property. This trap works for both mice and rats and can catch multiple rodents. Check daily.

Cats: Natural Predators

Cats are exceptional rodent hunters, particularly mousers. A single cat can significantly reduce mouse populations around a homestead. Barn cats or outdoor cats particularly excel at this. They provide continuous, non-chemical control without human effort. Cats are especially effective in grain storage areas and around structures. The downside is commitment to cat care, vaccination, and potential loss to disease or predators.

Exclusion: Prevention is Best Control

Seal ALL holes greater than 1/4 inch diameter. Mice can enter through openings as small as a dime (3/8 inch), and rats need only 1/2 inch. Inspect your building thoroughly:

  • Foundation cracks and holes
  • Spaces around pipes and utility lines entering the building
  • Gaps around doors and windows
  • Damaged or missing weatherstripping
  • Holes in screens or vents
  • Gaps in siding or trim

Sealing Materials: Use hardware cloth (1/4-inch mesh), steel wool mixed with caulk, or expanding foam followed by hardware cloth. Rodents cannot chew through these materials. Avoid using plastic or rubber alone‚Äîrodents will gnaw through them.

Remove Food Sources

  • Store all grain, seeds, and dry food in rodent-proof containers with tight lids
  • Clean up spilled grain immediately
  • Elevate stored food off the ground on platforms or shelves
  • Never leave pet food out overnight
  • Keep compost bins away from structures and animal-proof
  • Remove fallen fruit and vegetables from gardens
  • Eliminate water sources‚Äîfix dripping taps, cover water containers

Rodent-Borne Diseases: Know the Risks

Hantavirus

Spread through contact with rodent droppings, urine, or saliva, or by inhaling aerosolized virus. Causes severe respiratory illness in humans. No vaccine exists. Never sweep or blow away rodent droppings. Instead, spray them with disinfectant, let sit 5 minutes, then carefully wipe up with disposable cloths. Wear gloves and a mask. Most common in western North America from deer mice.

Plague (Yersinia pestis)

Transmitted through flea bites on infected rodents or direct contact with infected tissue. Causes bubonic or pneumonic plague. Still occurs in rural western North America. Eliminate rodent and flea populations. Wear gloves when handling dead rodents. Seek medical attention for fever or swollen lymph nodes after rodent exposure.

Leptospirosis

Spread through contact with rodent urine contaminating water or soil. Causes flu-like illness and can progress to kidney or liver failure. Wear gloves and boots when handling rodents or contaminated areas. Avoid wading in water contaminated with rodent urine. Wash hands thoroughly after exposure.

Salmonella & Other Bacteria

Many bacteria colonize rodent intestinal tracts and are shed in droppings. Practice strict hygiene: wear gloves, wash hands thoroughly, disinfect contaminated areas, and avoid touching eyes, mouth, or nose until hands are cleaned.

Safe Rodent Handling

  • Always wear disposable gloves when handling dead or live rodents
  • Use a broom and dustpan to pick up rodents; never use bare hands
  • Place dead rodents in sealed plastic bags before disposal
  • Dispose of rodents away from food preparation areas and water sources
  • Disinfect traps and contaminated surfaces with bleach solution (1:10 bleach:water)
  • Wash hands and arms thoroughly with soap and water after handling
  • Never eat, drink, or smoke while handling rodents

5. Insect Pest Prevention: Keeping Flying Insects at Bay

Flying insects like mosquitoes, flies, gnats, and wasps transmit diseases, bite, and spread filth. Prevention focuses on physical barriers, repellents, and elimination of breeding habitat.

Physical Barriers

Window and Door Screens

Quality screens with fine mesh (18-20 per inch) keep out mosquitoes and many small flying insects. Repair holes and tears immediately. Ensure screens fit tightly in frames with no gaps. Use weather-stripping around doors to eliminate gaps when closed. Install screens on all windows and doors, especially bedrooms and sleeping areas.

Mosquito Nets

Bed nets, particularly when treated with permethrin, provide excellent protection while sleeping. Hang nets so they don't touch skin. Tuck edges under the mattress for complete coverage. Nets are essential in areas with dengue fever, malaria, or other mosquito-borne diseases. Quality nets last many years.

Chemical Repellents

Citronella and Lemongrass

Citronella oil from lemongrass repels mosquitoes and some biting flies. Burn citronella candles, use essential oil diffusers, or plant citronella grass and lemongrass around outdoor areas. The scent provides protection within a few feet. Reapply or refresh regularly as scent fades. Natural but less effective than synthetic repellents.

Permethrin for Clothing

Permethrin is a synthetic pyrethroid insecticide that is safe for humans when applied to clothing and gear (not skin). Treatment of shirts, pants, socks, hats, and mosquito nets significantly reduces bites. Apply permethrin spray to clothing and allow to dry (2-3 hours) before wearing. Effectiveness lasts through multiple washings. This is highly effective against mosquitoes, ticks, and many biting insects. Not recommended for young children under professional guidance.

Eliminate Breeding Habitat

Standing Water

Mosquitoes lay eggs in standing water‚Äîeven tiny amounts. Weekly, drain and eliminate all standing water:

  • Empty buckets, flower pots, and plant saucers
  • Keep gutters clean and ensure water flows freely
  • Fill or drain low spots in yards
  • Keep birdbaths fresh and clean (change water every 2-3 days)
  • Cover water storage tanks and barrels with screens
  • Clear clogged drains immediately

Preventing standing water is the single most effective mosquito control. One female mosquito can produce hundreds of offspring, so eliminating breeding sites prevents future populations.

Professional Habitat Management

  • Keep grass mowed short (mosquitoes rest in tall grass and brush)
  • Remove dense vegetation near seating areas
  • Ensure good air circulation‚Äîmosquitoes fly poorly in moving air
  • Remove leaves and debris where flies breed
  • Keep compost bins covered and away from living areas
  • Remove food sources‚Äîkeep garbage sealed and dispose of frequently

Natural Mosquito Predators

Dragonflies are voracious mosquito predators, consuming hundreds daily. Create dragonfly habitat by maintaining shallow ponds or wetland areas. Bats also consume enormous quantities of mosquitoes (see Biological Controls section). Fish in ponds, particularly mosquito fish (Gambusia affinis), eat mosquito larvae.

6. Termites & Wood Pests: Protecting Structural Integrity

Wood-eating insects like termites, carpenter ants, carpenter bees, and wood-boring beetles can cause serious structural damage. Prevention is far more cost-effective than repairs.

Borate Treatment for Wood

Boron compounds are toxic to insects but safe for humans when properly applied. Borate treatment penetrates wood and remains effective indefinitely if the wood stays dry. Apply borate solution (like Timbor or similar products) to:

  • All exposed wood in crawl spaces
  • Sill plates and foundation wood
  • Wood in contact with soil
  • Roof trusses and attic wood
  • Interior framing in high-risk areas

Borate is applied as a liquid spray or mist that soaks into wood. Follow product directions for application rates and safety. Once dry, borate provides permanent protection (as long as wood remains dry). This is one of the most effective and least toxic wood protection methods available.

Physical Barriers and Prevention

Raised Foundations

Elevating buildings on concrete piers or posts with 18+ inches of clearance between wood and soil makes termite access difficult. Inspect regularly for mud tubes‚Äîthese are tunnels termites build from soil to wood. Any mud tubes should be destroyed and areas treated with borate or diatomaceous earth.

Moisture Control

Wood-eating insects thrive in damp conditions. Prevention includes:

  • Proper grading so water drains away from foundation
  • Gutters and downspouts directing water 5+ feet from building
  • Crawl space ventilation to keep air dry
  • Vapor barriers in crawl spaces
  • Interior dehumidification if needed
  • Immediate repair of roof leaks

Wood Selection

Some wood species have natural resistance to insect attack:

  • Excellent Resistance: Eastern Red Cedar, Black Locust, Osage Orange (extremely durable)
  • Good Resistance: White Oak, Chestnut, Redwood, Western Red Cedar
  • Poor Resistance: Pine, Spruce, Fir, Hemlock (need protection)

Use naturally resistant woods for foundation work, sill plates, and ground contact. Treat pine and other susceptible woods with borate for structural elements.

Detection and Monitoring

  • Inspect crawl spaces and basements quarterly for mud tubes, wood damage, or insects
  • Look for termite swarmers (flying reproductives) in spring‚Äîa sign of active colony nearby
  • Check for wood damage by probing with a knife‚Äîdamaged wood is soft and crumbly
  • Listen for termite sounds (faint clicking) in walls at night in quiet conditions
  • Look for carpenter ant trails‚Äîthey're active primarily at night
  • Check for wood-boring beetle exit holes (small round holes with fresh sawdust nearby)

When to Call Professionals

If you discover active termite infestations or significant wood damage, professional pest control is often necessary. Termite colonies can number in the millions and cause extensive damage before discovery. A professional inspection and treatment plan may be more cost-effective than attempting DIY control.

7. Companion Planting for Pest Control

Strategic plant placement can repel pests, attract beneficial insects, and create a more balanced garden ecosystem. While scientific evidence for some companion planting is limited, generations of gardeners have observed that certain plants reduce pest problems when grown near vulnerable crops.

Trap Cropping Strategy

Some plants, particularly nasturtium, attract pests strongly enough that they prefer them to your main crops. Plant trap crops as a sacrificial border around vulnerable plants. When pest populations concentrate on trap crops, remove and destroy them (or hand-pick pests). This reduces overall pest pressure on your food crops.

Flower Power for Beneficial Insects

In addition to pest-repelling plants, plant flowers that provide nectar and pollen for beneficial insects:

  • Alyssum, buckwheat, fennel, Queen Anne's lace, sweet clover, yarrow
  • Sunflowers, zinnias, cosmos, and other open-faced flowers
  • Native wildflowers and flowering shrubs

Blooming plants ensure beneficial insects have food throughout the season, maintaining populations to control garden pests.

8. Biological Controls: Nature's Pest Managers

Biological controls use living organisms‚Äîbeneficial insects, parasitoids, predators, pathogens, or nematodes‚Äîto control pest populations. This is the foundation of IPM and often the most sustainable long-term approach.

Beneficial Insects

Ladybugs (Coccinellids)

Ladybugs, both adults and larvae, are voracious aphid hunters. A single ladybug consumes 50-60 aphids daily. They also eat mites, scale insects, and small caterpillars. Encourage ladybugs by planting pollen and nectar-rich flowers. Purchase ladybug larvae (more effective than adults, which often fly away) from specialty suppliers and release in early morning when temperatures are cool. One ladybug per 3-4 plants is a typical ratio.

Lacewings (Chrysopidae)

Green and brown lacewing larvae are fierce predators of soft-bodied insects including aphids, mealybugs, scales, and small caterpillars. An individual larva consumes 200+ aphids before pupating. Adults feed on pollen, nectar, and honeydew. Purchase eggs or larvae and release in affected areas. Lacewings are particularly effective against aphids and whiteflies. They're hardy and establish well in most gardens.

Parasitic Wasps (Braconidae, Ichneumonidae)

These tiny wasps (often 1/4-inch or smaller) lay eggs inside pest insects. Developing wasp larvae consume the pest from inside, killing it. Different wasp species target different pests‚ÄîTrichogramma wasps target eggs, Braconid wasps parasitize caterpillars and aphids, and Ichneumon wasps target beetles and flies. These wasps are not aggressive to humans and cause no stings. Purchase species-specific parasitoids and release at appropriate times (when target pests are present). Many parasitoids establish permanently in gardens.

Praying Mantis

Mantises are general predators eating a wide variety of insects. While they're impressive hunters, they're less selective than specialist predators and will consume beneficial insects. They're useful in large gardens with diverse insect communities. Purchase mantis egg cases in fall or spring and attach to shrubs or tall plants. They'll hatch and hunt throughout the season.

Birds as Pest Controllers

Birds consume enormous quantities of insects. A single bird can eat thousands of insects during breeding season. Encourage birds by:

  • Planting native shrubs and trees for shelter and food
  • Providing bird feeders during winter
  • Maintaining water sources (bird baths)
  • Reducing pesticide use (birds can't survive in poisoned environments)
  • Installing nest boxes appropriate for local bird species

Martin Houses for Mosquitoes

Purple martin birds consume thousands of mosquitoes nightly during breeding season (June-August in most regions). Install proper martin houses (not sparrow nests‚Äîmartins need specific housing) in open areas 15+ feet high. Success depends on: location (open area, away from trees), proper house design with correct hole sizes and spacing, and annual maintenance. Once established, a martin colony provides excellent mosquito control.

Bats: The Night Shift Pest Control

A single bat consumes 1000+ mosquitoes and flying insects per night. Bats are the most efficient natural mosquito control available. Encourage bats by:

  • Installing bat boxes at 15+ feet height in open areas
  • Maintaining native trees and shrubs for shelter
  • Providing water sources
  • Avoiding pesticides (bats eat insects, accumulating toxins)
  • Not disturbing caves or roosts

Bat boxes require specific design for local species. Research your region's bat species and install appropriate houses. A single box may attract 50-100 bats, providing exceptional pest control. Bats also pollinate plants and eat crop-destroying pests like corn earworm moths.

Entomopathogenic Nematodes

Beneficial nematodes (Steinernema and Heterorhabditis species) are microscopic parasites that kill grub larvae, beetle pupae, and other soil-dwelling insects. Apply as liquid suspension to soil when temperatures are appropriate (usually spring or fall). Nematodes seek out pests, enter through body openings, and reproduce inside the host, killing it. One application can provide season-long control of grubs, which prevents adult Japanese beetles and other beetles from emerging.

Establishing Beneficial Insects

Success with purchased beneficial insects requires: 1) Release when target pests are present in significant numbers (beneficial insects need food to establish), 2) Avoid pesticides for 2-3 weeks before and after release, 3) Provide pollen and nectar plants, 4) Maintain moisture (many beneficials need water), 5) Release in early morning or evening when cooler. Multiple releases over the season work better than single releases.

9. Natural Pesticide Recipes: Last Resort Treatments

These recipes are for organic pesticides derived from plants or minerals. Use only after prevention, monitoring, and biological controls have been tried. All pesticides, even natural ones, should be applied carefully to avoid harming beneficial insects, pets, or humans. Always spray in early morning or evening when beneficial insects are less active.

Neem Oil Spray

For: Aphids, whiteflies, mites, mealybugs, scale insects, caterpillars, fungal diseases

Ingredients

  • 1 tablespoon cold-pressed neem oil
  • 1 teaspoon liquid dish soap
  • 1 quart warm water

Instructions

  1. Mix neem oil and dish soap in a small container, stirring well
  2. Add the mixture to warm (not hot) water and shake vigorously for 3 minutes
  3. Pour into spray bottle
  4. Apply to all plant surfaces, including leaf undersides, until dripping wet
  5. Spray in early morning or late evening when beneficial insects are less active
  6. Repeat every 7-14 days as needed, but not more than twice weekly
  7. Do not apply in temperatures above 85¬∞F or in direct sun

Safety Notes

  • Neem oil is non-toxic to humans and mammals in small amounts, but avoid skin contact and inhalation
  • Wear gloves and avoid breathing spray mist
  • Do not apply to flowers in bloom‚Äîharmful to bees
  • Avoid application for 2 weeks after releasing beneficial insects
  • Some plants (like impatiens and certain ferns) may be sensitive; test on small area first
  • Neem oil has a strong garlic-like odor

Garlic-Pepper Spray

For: Aphids, beetles, caterpillars, mites, thrips, whiteflies

Ingredients

  • 5-6 garlic cloves, chopped
  • 2-3 fresh hot peppers (or 1 teaspoon cayenne powder)
  • 1 tablespoon dish soap
  • 1 quart water

Instructions

  1. Combine garlic and peppers in a blender with 1 cup water
  2. Blend until liquefied, creating a thick paste
  3. Add mixture to remaining 3 cups water and let steep for 24 hours
  4. Strain through cheesecloth, pressing solids to extract all liquid
  5. Add dish soap to help spray adhere to leaves
  6. Pour into spray bottle and apply to affected plants
  7. Repeat weekly or after rain as needed

Safety Notes

  • Irritating to eyes and mucous membranes‚Äîwear gloves and avoid touching face
  • Wash hands thoroughly after application
  • Do not apply to flowers or immediately before bee activity
  • Can be phytotoxic (damage plants) at high concentrations; test on small area first
  • Store in refrigerator for up to 1 week; freezing extends shelf life to 3 months

Insecticidal Soap Spray

For: Soft-bodied insects (aphids, mealybugs, scales, spider mites, whiteflies, thrips)

Ingredients

  • 1 tablespoon pure castile soap (Dr. Bronner's or similar)
  • 1 quart water

Instructions

  1. Mix soap and water in spray bottle
  2. Shake well before each use
  3. Spray directly on affected plants, coating all surfaces
  4. Repeat every 5-7 days as needed until pests are controlled
  5. Spray in early morning or late evening
  6. Do not apply in direct sunlight or temperatures above 85¬∞F

Safety Notes

  • Soap works by breaking down insect exoskeletons‚Äîsoft-bodied insects are particularly vulnerable
  • Non-toxic to mammals, but avoid skin and eye contact
  • Some plants are sensitive to soap; test first (particularly ferns, impatiens, and certain succulents)
  • Do not use dish detergent‚Äîit's too harsh and may damage plants
  • Do not apply when plants are stressed by heat or drought
  • Safe around beneficial insects if applied late evening when they're less active

Pyrethrin Spray (Chrysanthemum Extract)

For: Beetles, caterpillars, flies, lice, mosquitoes, fleas, whiteflies, thrips

Ingredients

  • Fresh or dried pyrethrum flower petals (Chrysanthemum cinerariifolium)
  • Water
  • Optional: 1 teaspoon dish soap

Instructions

  1. Harvest pyrethrum flowers when blooming, dry completely
  2. Grind dried flowers into fine powder using mortar and pestle
  3. Mix 2 tablespoons powder with 2 cups water and let steep for 24 hours
  4. Strain through fine cloth or filter paper
  5. Add dish soap to help spray stick to insects
  6. Apply to affected plants, spraying thoroughly on all surfaces
  7. Repeat every 5-7 days as needed

Safety Notes

  • Pyrethrin is derived naturally from chrysanthemum flowers but is still an insecticide
  • Low toxicity to mammals when used as directed, but avoid skin contact and inhalation
  • Highly toxic to aquatic organisms‚Äîdo not spray near water sources or allow runoff
  • May cause allergic reactions in sensitive individuals; wear gloves and protective clothing
  • Toxic to bees‚Äîapply in early morning or late evening only
  • Breaks down rapidly in sunlight (beneficial for environment, but requires frequent reapplication)
  • Never apply pyrethrin to food crops less than 3 days before harvest
  • Do not use on pets or humans without veterinary/medical supervision

Rotenone Dust (Derris Root Extract)

For: Beetles, caterpillars, flies, lice, mites, thrips, weevils

Ingredients

  • Rotenone powder (from derris root), 1-5% concentration
  • Optional: finely ground diatomaceous earth for dilution

Instructions

  1. For liquid spray: mix 1-2 tablespoons rotenone powder with 1 quart water
  2. Add 1 teaspoon dish soap to help suspension adhere to leaves
  3. Shake vigorously before each application
  4. For dust application: use rotenone dust directly or dilute with food-grade diatomaceous earth
  5. Apply dust in early morning when plants are damp with dew
  6. Repeat every 7-10 days as needed

Safety Notes

  • Rotenone is plant-derived but highly toxic to aquatic life‚Äîdo not allow runoff to streams or ponds
  • Moderately toxic to mammals; avoid skin contact, inhalation, and eye exposure
  • Highly toxic to bees‚Äîapply in evening only, well before bee activity
  • May cause respiratory irritation‚Äîwear mask when applying dust form
  • Avoid applying to food crops less than 7 days before harvest
  • Breaks down in sunlight within 3-4 days
  • Not recommended for use around children or pets without careful precautions
  • Some jurisdictions restrict or ban rotenone use due to environmental concerns

Tobacco Tea (Nicotine Extract)

For: Aphids, beetles, caterpillars, flea beetles, mites, thrips, whiteflies

Ingredients

  • Loose tobacco or dried tobacco leaves, 2-3 tablespoons
  • Water, 1 quart
  • Optional: 1 teaspoon dish soap

Instructions

  1. Place tobacco in a container and pour hot (not boiling) water over it
  2. Cover and let steep for 24 hours
  3. Strain through cheesecloth, pressing to extract all liquid
  4. Dilute the concentrated tea 1:1 with water (1 cup tea + 1 cup water)
  5. Add dish soap if desired to help spray adhere
  6. Spray affected plants, coating thoroughly
  7. Repeat every 5-7 days as needed

Safety Notes

  • Nicotine is a potent insecticide but also a poison to humans and animals
  • Do not ingest under any circumstances
  • Wear gloves when handling tobacco and spray‚Äînicotine absorbs through skin
  • Avoid breathing smoke or spray mist; wear a mask
  • Toxic to bees‚Äîapply in early morning or late evening only
  • Can accumulate in soil with repeated applications; limit to 2-3 applications per season
  • Do not apply to edible crops less than 3 days before harvest
  • Wash hands and exposed skin thoroughly after application
  • Seek medical attention immediately if ingested; nicotine is highly toxic
  • Many jurisdictions restrict or prohibit tobacco-based pesticides due to health risks

General Spray Application Tips

  • Always spray in early morning (6-8 AM) or late evening (after 6 PM) when beneficial insects are least active
  • Avoid applying when temperatures exceed 85¬∞F‚Äîsprays may damage plants or evaporate too quickly
  • Don't spray when rain is expected within 24 hours (spray will wash off)
  • Coat all plant surfaces, especially leaf undersides where pests hide
  • Alternate different spray types to prevent pest resistance development
  • Never mix different pesticides unless specifically directed; combinations can create dangerous reactions
  • Always test on a small plant section first before full application to check for plant damage
  • Store concentrated sprays in labeled bottles in a cool, dark location
  • Keep all pesticides away from children, pets, and food preparation areas

10. Household Pest Management: Common Indoor Pests

Household pests contaminate food, damage property, and transmit diseases. Control focuses on elimination of pests and sanitation to prevent future infestations.

Ants

Borax + Sugar Bait

A simple, effective ant killer combines borax (a mineral salt) with powdered sugar. Ants carry the sweet bait back to their nest, poisoning the colony. Mix 1 teaspoon borax with 3 teaspoons powdered sugar and 1 cup water. Soak cotton balls in the mixture and place near ant trails. Keep out of reach of children and pets. Ants will disappear in 5-7 days as the colony is eliminated. This method kills the queen, preventing ant reproduction.

Preventing Ant Infestations

  • Seal food containers with tight-fitting lids
  • Clean up food crumbs and spills immediately
  • Take out garbage regularly and use sealed cans
  • Seal cracks and crevices where ants enter
  • Remove water sources (fix leaky pipes, dry sinks before bed)
  • Elevate pet food bowls on platforms and remove uneaten food

Fleas

Diatomaceous Earth for Flea Control

Food-grade diatomaceous earth kills fleas at all life stages. Dust affected areas (pet bedding, carpets, furniture) with a light, even coating. The microscopic sharp edges of diatom shells damage flea exoskeletons, causing death by dehydration. Leave in place for 24-48 hours, then vacuum thoroughly. Repeat weekly for 3-4 weeks to ensure all fleas are eliminated (including those emerging from eggs). DE is non-toxic to pets and humans but should not be inhaled in large quantities.

Comprehensive Flea Management

  • Treat all pet bedding weekly in hot water and heat-dry
  • Vacuum carpets and furniture daily, disposing of vacuum contents immediately
  • Bathe pets regularly with flea shampoo during infestations
  • Consider professional flea treatments or veterinary-prescribed medications for pets
  • Treat yards (particularly shaded areas where fleas thrive) with appropriate pesticides
  • Prevent reinfestation by treating pets monthly with flea prevention products

Lice (Head and Body)

Mechanical Removal with Fine-Tooth Comb

The safest and most non-toxic treatment for head lice is mechanical removal. Using a fine-tooth louse comb (finer than normal combs), comb through damp hair section by section. Wipe the comb on a cloth or paper towel and examine for lice or nits (lice eggs). Repeat daily for 10-14 days to ensure all lice and newly hatched nymphs are removed. This method is time-consuming but effective and requires no chemicals.

Vinegar Treatment Enhancement

Vinegar's acidity may help loosen nits from hair shafts. After combing, rinse hair with vinegar (diluted 1:1 with water), then comb again. This step improves mechanical removal efficiency. Wash hair with regular shampoo after treatment.

Comprehensive Lice Management

  • Wash all bedding, clothing, and towels in hot water and heat-dry
  • Soak combs and hair brushes in hot water for 10 minutes
  • Do not allow head-to-head contact with infested individuals
  • If chemical treatment is necessary, use permethrin-based products (non-prescription) or prescription medications (pyrethrin derivatives or permethrin)
  • Treat all household members and close contacts simultaneously
  • Repeat treatment 7-10 days after initial application to catch any newly hatched lice
  • Continue combing for 2-3 weeks even after visible lice are gone

Bed Bugs

Heat Treatment: Most Effective Method

Bed bugs cannot survive temperatures above 120¬∞F. Heat treatment is the most reliable method for complete elimination:

  • Whole-House Heat: Professional heat treatment raising indoor temperatures to 120-135¬∞F for several hours. Kills all bed bugs and eggs. Most effective but expensive ($1000-5000).
  • Room-by-Room: Using space heaters or portable heating units to raise room temperature to 120¬∞F for 1-2 hours. Multiple treatments may be necessary.
  • Item Treatment: Seal infested items (clothing, bedding) in black bags in direct sunlight for several days. Temperature inside bags exceeds 120¬∞F on sunny days.
  • Freezer: Items can be frozen at 0¬∞F for 4+ days. Place in freezer with a thermometer to verify temperature. Not practical for larger items.

Supplementary Treatments

  • Vacuum infested areas thoroughly, dispose of vacuum contents in sealed bags
  • Wash all bedding and clothing in hot water (120¬∞F) and heat-dry on high
  • Encase mattresses in bed bug-proof covers (prevents reinfestation from mattress)
  • Seal cracks and crevices where bed bugs hide using caulk
  • Remove clutter where bed bugs hide (piles of clothing, papers, boxes)
  • Consider professional pest control if DIY treatment fails

Pesticide Resistance in Bed Bugs

Bed bugs have developed resistance to many common insecticides. Chemical treatments alone are often ineffective. Heat treatment combined with mechanical removal and sanitation is more reliable than pesticide sprays. If using pesticides, alternate different chemical classes to reduce resistance development.

Flies and Gnats

Traps and Prevention

  • Fly Traps: Yellow sticky traps attract flying insects. Place near windows or problem areas. Simple, safe, and effective for light infestations.
  • Fruit Fly Traps: Small bowls of apple cider vinegar with a drop of dish soap. Flies are attracted to vinegar and drown. Replace daily.
  • Drain Treatment: Use a drain brush or drain snake to remove buildup in sink drains where fly larvae develop. Flush with hot water.
  • Screens: Install and maintain fine-mesh screens on all windows and doors.

Sanitation

  • Do not leave food out; cover dishes or place in sealed containers
  • Take out garbage daily, especially food waste
  • Keep fruit in sealed containers in the refrigerator
  • Clean drains regularly; flies breed in organic buildup
  • Eliminate standing water in plant saucers and containers
  • Compost food scraps in sealed bins, not open piles

Professional Help When Needed

For severe infestations of any household pest (termites, bed bugs, cockroaches), professional pest control is often necessary. Licensed pest control professionals have access to more effective treatments, can apply them safely, and provide warranties on their work. Don't hesitate to call professionals when DIY methods fail.
