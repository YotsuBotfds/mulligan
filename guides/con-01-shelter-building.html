Shelter Fundamentals

A shelter's primary functions are wind protection, rain protection, heat retention, and pest exclusion. The most effective shelters use natural materials found within 100 meters of the site, minimizing the energy expended on construction.

Key principles for all shelters: insulate from ground moisture (use dead leaves, pine needles, or bark as barriers); create wind-blocking entry systems (angled doors reduce draft); maximize heat reflection (light-colored interior surfaces); and ensure adequate drainage around the shelter footprint.

Construction timeline varies dramatically by shelter type: debris shelters (30 minutes), lean-tos (2-3 hours), A-frames (4-6 hours), log cabins (40-80 hours), and sod houses (80-120 hours).

Site Selection & Preparation

The shelter site determines 50% of construction success. Evaluate terrain, weather patterns, proximity to resources, and natural hazards before breaking ground.

Ideal Site Characteristics:

  • Elevation: Slightly elevated to avoid water pooling; high ground prevents flash flood inundation
  • Drainage: 3-5 degree slope away from structure; avoid low spots that collect runoff
  • Wind: Natural windbreaks (dense trees, rock formations); avoid ridge tops and narrow valleys that funnel wind
  • Water: 30-50 meters from source (reduces insect breeding and contamination risk)
  • Trees: Hardwoods for structural material; deadfall for fuel; southern exposure for winter warmth
  • Sun: South/southeast facing for warmth and drying; north side protected from winter wind
  • Hazards: Avoid dead trees (widow makers), avalanche paths, flood-prone areas, animal migration routes

Clear vegetation from 10-meter perimeter to prevent fire spread. Remove rocks and roots from floor area. Create a 1-meter wide water drainage ditch upslope if on sloping terrain.

Tip: Test soil drainage by digging a 30-cm hole and filling with water. If water drains within 4 hours, drainage is adequate. If it pools, relocate site or build raised platform.

Debris Shelters

The debris shelter is the fastest emergency shelter, constructed in 20-30 minutes with zero tools. Effective in temperatures down to -15°C with proper construction.

Construction Steps:

  1. Find ridgeline material: Locate a branch 2-3 meters long, 3-5cm diameter. This becomes your ridge pole.
  2. Position support: Wedge branch 1 meter high between two trees or on rocks. Ensure solid, level support.
  3. Add framework: Lean sticks (1-2cm diameter, 2-meter lengths) along ridge pole at 30cm intervals, creating A-frame profile.
  4. Layer leaves: Pile leaves, pine needles, bark, and debris 30cm thick over frame. This is critical for insulation.
  5. Create entrance: Leave 1-1.5 meter opening at downwind end. Create baffle using branches to prevent wind entry.
  6. Floor insulation: Layer leaves 30cm deep inside shelter before entering.
  7. Seal gaps: Use mud, leaves, and bark to fill all openings except ventilation gap (5cm at peak).
Safety Warning: Carbon monoxide buildup is fatal in completely sealed shelters. Always maintain 5-10cm ventilation gap at peak. Never fully seal an enclosed shelter.

Insulation value: A well-constructed debris shelter with 30cm leaf layer provides R-value of approximately 3-4. Combined with body heat, sufficient for survival temperatures above -10°C when properly sized (2.0m x 1.5m x 1.2m).

Lean-To Structures

Lean-tos provide more protection and longevity than debris shelters while remaining simple to construct (2-4 hours). Effective for 2-5 day occupation.

Standard Lean-To Design:

Dimensions: 3-4 meters long, 2-2.5 meters deep, 1.8 meters tall at back, 1.2 meters at front. This 30-degree pitch sheds rain efficiently while maintaining usable interior height.

Construction:

  1. Frame posts: Two sturdy posts (10cm diameter, 2-2.5 meters tall) set 3 meters apart. Dig 50cm deep for stability.
  2. Ridgeline: Span thick branch (8-10cm diameter) between post tops, lashing with cordage using square lashing or mortise joints.
  3. Roof support: Lean poles (4-6cm diameter) at 40cm intervals from ridge to front stake (1.2-1.5 meters forward). Lash every intersection.
  4. Roof material: Layer bark sheets overlapping like shingles. Seal gaps with clay or mud. Thatch is alternative (see specialized section).
  5. Back wall: Build substantial wall 1-1.5 meters tall to protect against wind and reflect heat from fire.
  6. Floor: Create slight slope (10cm drop over 2 meters) to shed water. Layer with leaves or branches.
  7. Fire placement: Position 1.5 meters in front of shelter, with reflector wall behind fire to direct heat inward.

Weatherproofing:

Lean-tos are vulnerable to wind-driven rain. Create a 30cm extension below the roofline at the front. Install a gutter-style channel along the base to direct water away. Seal all gaps between roofing material with clay-grass mixture.

Tip: Construct the back wall from stone or heavy timber rather than sticks. A stone wall 1.5m high retains heat throughout the night and provides wind protection far superior to wooden walls.

A-Frame Shelters

A-frames provide excellent weather protection and heat retention while remaining simple to construct. Can house 2-4 people for 1-2 weeks with proper materials. Complete construction in 4-8 hours.

Design & Dimensions:

Standard A-frame: 4 meters long, 2.5 meters wide base, 2.2 meters peak height. The 45-60 degree roof pitch sheds snow and rain efficiently while providing acceptable headroom.

Construction Sequence:

  1. Cut ridge poles: Two 4-meter poles (6-8cm diameter) for roof; two 2.5-meter poles (4-6cm diameter) for sides.
  2. Construct frame: Create two A-shaped trusses at each end (7 pieces per truss). Lash together using square or diagonal lashing.
  3. Connect trusses: Run ridge poles between truss peaks. Secure with lashing at 1-meter intervals.
  4. Add purlins: Attach 3-4 horizontal poles per side (perpendicular to ridge) at 50cm intervals for roof sheathing support.
  5. Roof sheathing: Layer bark or wooden shingles, overlapping 15cm. Seal gaps with clay and grass.
  6. Gable ends: Close triangular ends with sticks and mud/clay, or leave partially open for ventilation.
  7. Floor: Raise 30cm above ground using cross-logs. Layer with insulation material (leaves, straw, branches).
  8. Entrance: Cut 1-1.5 meter opening at one gable end. Create wooden door or hang bark/hide covering.

Interior Improvements:

  • Sleeping platform: Build elevated platform (60cm high) along one side. Provides insulation and moisture separation.
  • Storage shelf: Attach branch shelf 1.5 meters high at opposite end for equipment and supplies.
  • Fire pit: Dig 30cm deep, line with rock. Position at shelter entrance with reflector wall behind it.
  • Smoke hole: Create 30cm opening at roof peak for ventilation and smoke exit. Install removable cover for rain protection.

Log Cabin Construction

Log cabins provide permanent shelter with excellent insulation and weatherproofing. Require 40-80 hours of skilled labor but house 4-6 people indefinitely with proper maintenance.

Site & Foundation:

Clear a level platform 5x5 meters minimum. Build foundation of stacked stone 40cm high around perimeter (prevents rot and rodent entry). Alternative: dig post holes 80cm deep for corner posts.

Log Selection:

  • Species: Hardwoods (oak, maple) for structural posts; softer woods (spruce, pine, aspen) for walls acceptable if well-drained site
  • Diameter: 15-25cm minimum for stability; larger logs reduce gaps requiring chinking
  • Length: Cut to precise lengths before stacking; 20cm longer than final wall width to account for corner overlap
  • Quality: Select straight logs. Curved or branched logs create gaps and weak points.

Building Process:

  1. Corner joinery: Use saddle notch, V-notch, or full dovetail joint at corners. Saddle notch is fastest; dovetail is strongest.
  2. First course: Lay two full-length logs (north-south orientation) on foundation. Level with shims. Check square using 3-4-5 triangle measurement.
  3. Subsequent courses: Alternate perpendicular logs. Notch each log to nest tightly over log below. Notch depth typically 40-50% of log diameter.
  4. Chinking: Pack gaps between logs with clay, grass, and straw mixture. Apply 5cm interior, 8cm exterior for weather protection.
  5. Wall height: Stack to 2.2-2.4 meters for interior head clearance. Typical residential cabin uses 12-15 courses.
  6. Window/door openings: Frame openings before laying logs. Use doubled logs above openings as lintels.

Roof Installation:

Construct roof frame on top course before raising final logs. Typical gable roof with 40-degree pitch. Attach ridge pole to peak rafters. Layer rafters at 50cm intervals. Cover with bark shingles or sod.

Interior Systems:

  • Flooring: Install split-log flooring (flat side up) on floor joists set into grooves on foundation logs. Create 2-3mm gaps for expansion.
  • Insulation: Stuff clay-straw mixture into 5cm gaps between interior chinking and exterior logs. Creates R-value of 2-3 with 10cm thickness.
  • Caulking: Seal all exterior gaps with birch bark strips, then clay mortar. Reapply annually.
Tip: Construct a temporary cabin (20 logs, minimal chinking) first as proof-of-concept and practice. Then build permanent cabin with higher standards. This approach saves material and reduces mistakes.

Sod House Building

Sod houses (soddies) utilize earth blocks for insulation. Best built in prairie regions with thick grass cover. Provide R-value of 4-6 and house 4-8 people permanently.

Material Preparation:

Sod blocks: Cut 30cm x 60cm x 10cm blocks from prairie soil with mature grass roots. Roots bind soil together. Stack with grass-side-up in walls. Allow to dry 4-6 weeks before use.

Foundation & Walls:

  1. Trench foundation: Dig 60cm deep, 40cm wide perimeter trench. Fill with stacked stone or adobe blocks to ground level.
  2. First course: Lay sod blocks grass-side-down. Pack clay between courses. Offset joints like brickwork.
  3. Wall thickness: Build 60cm thick walls minimum (3 blocks deep). Thickness provides structural strength and insulation.
  4. Height: Stack to 2.2 meters. Walls compress approximately 10% over first year; account during construction.
  5. Openings: Frame windows and doors with sturdy timber. Use doubled logs above openings as structural support.

Roof Construction:

Sod house roofs must support heavy loads (roof + sod cover = 2-3 tons per 10 square meters). Use robust timber frame with rafters at 30cm intervals. Cover with sod blocks for insulation, waterproofing, and fire protection.

Interior Finish:

  • Plastering: Apply clay plaster (3cm thick) to interior walls to seal dust and insulate further. Lime wash for whitening and antimicrobial properties.
  • Flooring: Stone, packed clay, or split logs on timber joists.
  • Root cellar: Dig 1.5-meter-deep storage pit in floor for food preservation. Maintain 2-5°C temperature year-round.

Insulation Systems

Insulation is critical for survival in cold climates. Understanding R-values and installation methods determines whether a shelter provides life-saving protection or merely wind-break.

Natural Insulation Materials & R-Values:

MaterialThicknessR-ValueNotes
Dry leaves30cm3-4Settles 30-40%; use 40cm initially
Pine needles30cm3.5-4.5Best insulation; naturally antimicrobial
Straw/hay30cm3-4Moisture absorbs; requires protection
Wood fiber25cm3-4Chipped bark; lower settling rate
Sawdust25cm2.5-3.5Settles significantly; requires compression
Sod/turf20cm3-4Heavy; requires structural support
Clay-straw mix20cm2-3Best for walls; compacts over time

Installation Methods:

Blanket method: Layer insulation material 30-50cm thick over roof structure. Most effective for simple structures. Requires frame strong enough to support weight.

Sandwich method: Insulation between two layers of bark or wooden slats. Prevents material from settling. Uses 20% less material but requires more labor.

Fill method: Pack insulation into wall cavities during construction. Gaps between logs and interior/exterior facing. Requires 10-15cm depth.

Tip: Double-layer insulation using two methods (e.g., sandwich roof + fill walls) provides additive protection. A shelter using both techniques maintains 5-10°C higher interior temperature with same size fire.

Weather Protection Systems

Wind Protection:

Wind increases heat loss exponentially. A 20°C shelter in calm conditions drops to 5°C in 30 km/h wind without windbreak. Strategies: orient entrance downwind, create multiple baffles, build wind-break fence, construct partially underground, use vegetation as wind-break.

Rain & Snow Management:

  • Roof pitch: Minimum 30 degrees for rain shedding; 45+ degrees for heavy snow regions
  • Eaves: Extend 40-60cm beyond wall to protect walls from driven rain and snow accumulation
  • Gutters: Channel water 2+ meters from shelter base. Trench with 2-degree slope away from structure
  • Snow load: In heavy snow regions, construct roof to support 150+ kg per square meter. Use rafters at 30cm maximum spacing.

Moisture Management:

Interior moisture condenses on cold walls, creating damp conditions. Solutions: ventilation (5-10cm opening), elevated sleeping platform (warmest micro-climate), clay-grass plaster (absorbs humidity and releases slowly), bark flooring (allows air circulation).

Fire Safety:

Maintain 1-meter minimum clearance between fire and any shelter material. Create rock-lined fire pit with raised lip to prevent embers spreading. Keep water and sand nearby. Establish emergency exits from all sleeping areas.

Common Mistakes & Solutions

Mistake 1: Insufficient Insulation Depth

Error: Building shelter with 15-20cm leaf layer, expecting sufficient warmth. Results: interior temperature only 5-10°C above ambient. Survival time limited to 4-8 hours below freezing.

Solution: Use 30-40cm minimum insulation. Freshen insulation weekly as material compacts and settles. Monitor interior temperature with hand/arm test—should feel cool but not cold.

Mistake 2: Inadequate Drainage

Error: Building on level ground without water channel. First rain creates pooling around shelter base, soaking floor insulation and walls. Shelter becomes unusable within 24 hours.

Solution: Dig 1-meter-wide water channel 50cm upslope of shelter. Slope ground away minimum 1 meter in all directions at 3-5 degree angle. Install interior floor slightly raised (5-10cm) above outside ground level.

Mistake 3: Inadequate Ventilation

Error: Sealing shelter completely for warmth. Carbon monoxide accumulates, causing lethargy, unconsciousness, death within hours. Most dangerous in shelters with fires (logs, charcoal, candles).

Solution: Maintain 5-10cm ventilation opening at peak at all times. Create chimney-like effect by positioning vent above fire smoke path. Never fully seal shelter with occupants inside.

Mistake 4: Weak Structural Joints

Error: Using nails, improvised ties, or inadequate lashing. Shelter collapses under snow load, high wind, or occupant weight on platforms.

Solution: Use square lashing or mortise joints for all critical connections. Test structural integrity before occupation by applying weight (jump test) and pushing on walls.

Mistake 5: Exposed Ground Floor

Error: Sleeping directly on earth without insulation barrier. Ground draws body heat rapidly. Hypothermia risk even in relatively warm shelters.

Solution: Always create 30cm minimum insulation layer between body and ground. Use leaves, pine needles, dry branches, or elevated platform. Refresh bedding material every 2-3 days as compression reduces insulation value.
